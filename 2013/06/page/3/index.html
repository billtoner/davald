Get an education

June 14, 2013 by davald

Or, perhaps not.

A couple of op-ed columns in this morning’s New York Times, one by David Brooks the other by Paul Krugman, address the plight of workers, albeit from completely different perspectives. Brooks bemoans the changing mores of the wealthy, suggesting that “seemliness” has given way to more conspicuous, in-your-face consumption. Krugman invokes the Luddites, suggesting that most of us are similarly situated in the modern economy.

Brooks begins with a story of Walter Judd, who would serve 20 years in Congress after becoming a doctor. Denied financial assistance by his father, Judd was able to work his way through college cleaning dishes for the local Y.M.C.A. That, of course, would be impossible to pull off today, since tuition costs greatly exceed the wages of washing. (Note: I was able to pay for a university education by cleaning the slopes of the football stadium and washing pots and pans at a fraternity. But that was a long time ago.) After acknowledging the remarkableness of Judd’s story, Brooks then writes, curiously:

More important, that people then were more likely to assume that jobs at the bottom of the status ladder were ennobling and that jobs at the top were morally perilous. That is to say, the moral status system was likely to be the inverse of the worldly status system. The working classes were self-controlled, while the rich and the professionals could get away with things.

I think that Brooks is too quick to generalize from an inference about Judd and washing dishes. First off, it seems that Judd had little choice but to find work if he was to complete college. Second, it’s clear that Judd was not content to stay in the kitchen; he aspired to become something much more, including a national political figure. That Judd parlayed his physician’s degree into missionary work suggests the importance of religion in his life, which is one of Brooks’s principal themes. The presumed moral precepts that may have governed or guided Judd’s career, from college to Congress, had their roots in the Bible, Brooks avers. Maybe. From this, Brooks laments:

Over the years, religion has played a less dominant role in public culture.

It’s quite possible that the rich don’t bother going to church any more, if they ever did, but it’s quite a stretch, I think, to say that “religion has played a less dominant role in public culture.” Has he forgotten about the rabid Republicans of the Christian right, who appear to have gained a foothold in the party, essentially determining the outcomes of primary elections?

One thing is clear, however. There can be no more Walter Judds, or yours truly, for that matter. Religion or no, part-time menial labor cannot purchase a college degree. Which brings me to Krugman.

The Princeton professor wonders if we have been forced into neo-Luddite situations. He quotes from the originals:

“How are those men, thus thrown out of employ to provide for their families?” asked the petitioners. “And what are they to put their children apprentice to?”

Today’s youth are urged to go to college to gain new or different abilities. But at what cost? As Brooks points out, the path of Walter Judd is no longer available. Instead, as Krugman reminds us:

…we go deep into debt to acquire the skills we’re told we need, only to learn that the economy no longer wants those skills…

Krugman’s prescription will surely be denounced by the religion-soaked conservatives.

Education, then, is no longer the answer to rising inequality, if it ever was (which I doubt).

So what is the answer? If the picture I’ve drawn is at all right, the only way we could have anything resembling a middle-class society — a society in which ordinary citizens have a reasonable assurance of maintaining a decent life as long as they work hard and play by the rules — would be by having a strong social safety net, one that guarantees not just health care but a minimum income, too. And with an ever-rising share of income going to capital rather than labor, that safety net would have to be paid for to an important extent via taxes on profits and/or investment income.

Not going to happen.

Are taxes a form of austerity?

June 13, 2013 by davald

Matthew Yglesias writes:

In fact the general trend over the past several years among people on the left to start using “austerity” as a meaningless signifier for “policies I don’t like” has had an unfortunate negative impact on the quality of discourse. When state governments cut back social services in the face of falling tax revenue and rising employee health care and pension costs, that is a kind of austerity. But the available alternative of raising taxes is also a form of austerity.

I have been using ‘austerity’ to refer to ‘cutbacks in government spending,’ especially acute among the states, which typically have to balance their budgets. In the face of recession-triggered reductions in revenues, they have chosen to reduce expenditures on public services rather than raise taxes, a politically difficult choice.

But I have to wonder how raising taxes is necessarily a form of austerity. Yglesias seems to be suggesting that austerity can imply economic contraction. If so, then the answer depends.

If governments raise taxes on the wealthy, say the top one percent, would that in itself retard economic growth? It could if the dollars the rich pay in taxes were otherwise spent on productive activities. Yet the rich spend a disproportionately lower percentage of their income than the Rest of Us, who can use every penny we make. Here’s Joseph Stiglitz, writing for Vanity Fair:

The relationship is straightforward and ironclad: as more money becomes concentrated at the top, aggregate demand goes into a decline. Unless something else happens by way of intervention, total demand in the economy will be less than what the economy is capable of supplying—and that means that there will be growing unemployment, which will dampen demand even further. In the 1990s that “something else” was the tech bubble. In the first dec­ade of the 21st century, it was the housing bubble. Today, the only recourse, amid deep recession, is government spending—which is exactly what those at the top are now hoping to curb.

Also, we know that during the 50s and 60s the marginal tax rate at the federal level was much, much higher than it is today. Nevertheless, the economy grew at a far faster clip than it does currently when tax rates are low.

So, I would argue that increasing taxes on the wealthy, either by raising the marginal rate or by instituting Robert Frank’s consumption tax, could actually spur economic growth by stopping the flow of dollars from the spending bottom to the hoarding top. Here’a s chart I’ve used before from the Federal Reserve showing the Gini index and the velocity of money, the latter decreasing over time with increased wealth concentration.

 

During the relative halcyon days of the post-war era, the federal government balanced its budgets, collecting sufficient revenues to meet expenses, which continued to grow. With the fall in tax rates on both personal and corporate income, revenues have declined but federal spending has not, leaving us with a series of deficits and higher debt.

I was begot

June 13, 2013 by davald

In this second installment of an elder’s story (the first is here), I’ll talk a bit about my father, who passed away from cancer in 1998 at the age of 76. Were it not for this dreaded disease I’m certain he’d still be playing golf, which was his passion.

He was a member of Tom Brokaw’s “greatest generation” having fought in World War II. Here’s a picture of him looking rather scared in uniform.

He was a pilot for what was then called ‘the Army-Air Force.’ Assignments took him over the Mediterranean, where I presume he bombed and shot at targets, mostly German or Italian. I’m sure that he felt lucky to have survived. He no doubt shared that feeling with his brothers, both of whom came home in one piece, more or less.

In all my conversations with my father and uncles there was no discussion of the Great War. There was nothing about exploits or near-misses or the fact that they must have been frightened at times beyond description. I gather that my dad was the least decorated of the two, as the photo above attests. After my uncles’ deaths, I came across a couple of items describing achievements and acts of heroism of J.B., on the left, and Gene, in the center, the latter receiving a purple heart, as I recall.

With the war over my father wound up near West Point, about 60 miles north of Manhattan. There he met an attractive young secretary who worked for a general and they soon married.

Notice the smile on my dad’s face. It looks forced, and it’s a pose evident in many of the photos I have of him. As I said, there was a marriage.

A year or so later I became the first born of Donald and Florence Aldrich. That was in April of 1947. My father traveled to the San Francisco Bay area to join his brothers, all in search of employment. My mother and her three-month-old son—that would be me—took a non-stop flight aboard a propeller-powered DC 9. 

We would establish a household in a North Richmond rental, part of a large apartment complex populated by young ex-military and their wives. We lived on the bottom floor of a four-unit building that was connected to a line of others along one side of the street.

Precious, isn’t he. I would guess several pounds and months out of the womb. And here is where we lived. Dad, Mom, sister Nancy, and I sitting on the front porch of the Richmond apartment.

 

I have only two distinct memories of the place. One involved a fire that had started in an adjacent unit. No injuries, as I recall, but the event seemed to cause much commotion. The other was about a neighbor’s dead cat, which I discovered in the gutter. I nobly reported the event to the owner. The other memories were vaguer, having to do with climbing up and down stairs to visit the Catanesis, who would become life-long friends. I was told years later that my parents would spend many a weekend evening partying with the neighbors while we were presumably asleep. For shame.

My mother, Florence Dingee, was born and raised in upstate New York. Here is a picture of her parents. Proper.

 

I never saw Grandpa Dingee, though Grandma visited us in the East Bay on a couple of occasions. I do recall the fateful phone call to my mother from one of her siblings announcing the news of Grandpa’s death. The only time, really, that I saw her cry, and there would be several opportunities to do so, including Nancy’s death.

It’s a cruel thing to move far away from your parents and families, especially with a new baby in tow. Back then, travel was either expensive or lengthy, though my parents did drive all the way from California to New York, a journey of some five days. That was when we children were left in the care of my two great aunts, the one resembling a linebacker.

Aunt Nell

Another coincidence

June 13, 2013 by davald

Readers of this blog know that I spend quite a bit of time on what I call ‘the Great Divergence,’ a period beginning in the mid-1970s and continuing to the present. It has been marked by growing inequality, certainly, with the rich becoming richer and the poor poorer and more plentiful. A number of other metrics correlate with the decline of the Rest of Us and the ascendancy of the monied interests. These include, for example, the increasing financialization of the economy, the declining share of labor, diminished GDP from year to year, and a steadily falling labor participation rate among male workers. We can add another.

Bloomberg News reports that Americans’ dissatisfaction with Congress has reached another new low. The most recent Gallop Poll reveals that only ten percent of us are “satisfied” with federal lawmakers. The rating reached its highest level in 1973, at 42 percent.

Financial innovation

June 13, 2013 by davald

Thomas Edsall writes for the New York Times today on “dirty money politics.” As you might infer, the politics are murky and hardly transparent.

The flow of cash through organizations asserting tax-exempt status under section 501(c)(4) of the federal tax code has been rising exponentially, from just $5.2 million in 2006 to $310.8 million in 2012.

The appeal of the 501(c)(4) classification is secrecy. Donors do not have to be disclosed. The law governing the status, however, stipulates that political activity cannot be a group’s “primary” activity. But groups’ leaders and lawyers have found ways to skirt the prohibition. Edsall provides an illustrative example:

The phrase “not its primary activity” has been interpreted by campaign finance lawyers to mean that a 501(c)(4) organization can spend no more that 49.9% of its money on political activity, according to [Sheila] Krumholz [of the Center for Responsive Politics].

In a process she refers to as “money churning,” a hypothetical tax-exempt organization, let’s call it the Good Government Coalition, has $10 million in revenues. The G.G.C. fulfills its obligation to spend just over half its money on non-political activity by giving $5 million plus $1 to another tax-exempt social welfare organization with an ambiguous name, the Liberty Bell Alliance. G.G.C. can now spend what it has left, $4,999,999 on political activity. The Liberty Bell Alliance, which now has $5 million plus $1, can spend just under half, $2,499,999, on political activity. The net result is that of the original $10 million, instead of only $4,999,999 going to political activity, $7,499,998, or 75 percent of the original $10 million, can be spent on politics.

Of course, the example has real-life counterparts, mostly among conservative groups, which have vastly outspent Democratic-leaning organizations. Edsall includes this chart from the Center for Responsive Politics:

Edsall concludes:

The current political activities of large numbers of 501(c) organizations in no way constitute the kind of charitable work for which the public would grant favorable tax status as a reward. One of the reasons the people involved with political nonprofits — operatives and donors alike — love secrecy is that they fear public repercussions if their activities have to be conducted in the open. It is by now abundantly clear that abuse of the 501(c)(4) loophole corrupts and corrodes a campaign-finance system that was hardly a model of rectitude to begin with.

To paraphrase Apple’s Phil Schiller, “Charity, my ass.”

Economics matters

June 12, 2013 by davald

A few professional economists don’t like what they see in the mirror. They decry the sorry state of their discipline, whose practitioners predict badly as they offer erroneous advice to those in a position to make a difference.

So why should economics, of all the academic pursuits, currently summon our attention? After all, we don’t seem to mind that philosophers disagree sharply on some rather profound concepts, from the nature of knowledge to how we should behave. Admit it, you probably haven’t read a philosopher recently and feel that you haven’t missed much. Even scientists, those presumably in pursuit of verifiable truths about the world, differ on significant topics, including global warming, and yet we give them a pass, if we pay attention at all.

I suggest that economics matters now more than ever because many of us are concerned about broad and deep unemployment amidst wasted lives, and, more to the point, we believe that the government can play a vital role in ameliorating the situation. No academic field comes to mind in addressing these problems.

But economists themselves do us no favor when they fail to reach consensus on both description and prescription. They cannot agree on what ails us, nor do they present a unified front on what to do. Still others decline to proffer corrective policies at all.

Part of the reason, I suspect, takes us back to the post-war decades wherein Keynesianism held sway. I should clarify. Economists both in and out of government believed that the government could fine-tune the economy to smooth out what were assumed to be natural business cycles. If GDP seemed to be trending down, then open up the fiscal spigots. When inflation reared its ugly head, raise the cost of money. For a while the minor adjustments to the accelerator and brake appeared to work.

However, Berkeley economist Christine Romer concluded (pdf) that such government interventions, both monetary and fiscal, created a juggernaut of leap-frogging expectations between prices and wages, culminating in the stagflation period of Jimmy Carter’s latter years in office and the beginning of Reagan’s term. The Federal Reserve, led by Paul Volcker, sharply increased the federal funds rate, which quickly dampened inflation but at the cost of higher unemployment.

 

Following the grand Volcker intervention, the idea that the economy could be tamed by the government became anathema. Indeed, the less the government interfered in markets the better. Economic actors, guided by their own preferences and rational expectations, should be free to choose.

This attitude remains dominant, even following the collapse of the housing bubble and the major disruption of Wall Street, whose exuberantly wrong-headed bets necessitated the mother of all subsidies. Policy makers both here and in Europe stubbornly cling to the false dichotomy between public and private, insisting that the former’s role in the latter is perniciously counterproductive. Moreover, federal governments should never spend more than they take in and when they do all the world’s problems ensue.

In Europe, especially, fiscal consolidation reigns as the antidote to the spendthrift ways that ushered in the Great Recession. This is no time for easy money or public programs to put people back to work. Tighten your belts.

Yet, fiscal austerity hasn’t worked. It has failed to restore investor confidence or spur economic activity, as its proponents promised. Instead, it has had the predictable effect of contracting the economy, consigning millions of people to undeserving and unwanted idleness.

We might excuse political leaders for tightening their governments’ belts. After all, most are not professional economists. But they never lack for economists who provide them with intellectual cover, and it is those economists who deserve opprobrium.

Oxford economist Simon Wren-Lewis writes in his blog today:

I am not disdainful of those in 2010 who thought austerity was necessary because either a debt crisis was around the corner, or economic recovery had been assured, and have subsequently done what Keynes suggested should be done when the evidence becomes clearer. I think they were wrong back then, and said so, but it was an understandable mistake, and even the best economists make mistakes. But I’m afraid to continue in 2013 to advocate a course of action which anyone can see is doing immense harm to so many people is just inexcusable.

We are left, then, with a profession that came to disdain the teachings of John Maynard Keynes, though many surely misunderstood him, while adopting a “free-to-choose” orthodoxy championed by Milton Friedman and his saltwater acolytes. This is unfortunate for the Rest of Us.

The transformation from Nixon’s “we are all Keynesians now” to free-market apologists has coincided with the gradual eroding of income of the bottom 90 percent, rapidly escalating private debt, collapsing public infrastructure and services, and the increasing financialization of the economy, even as economic activity stagnates.

Cynics would suggest that money tends to have its way. With the financial sector siphoning off an ever-increasing share of total wealth, a resurgent stock market, and extremely low inflation, one might conclude that things are as they should be—meaning the Rest of Us just don’t count.

Education fads

June 10, 2013 by davald

The title may be redundant, as anyone who has been a teacher can attest. One thing remains constant, however. There is always a crisis in education. There’s also money, which creates cottage industries preaching this or that “program,” with gullible school boards and principals consuming with insatiable appetites.

When I taught, and I use the term reluctantly, my principal desperately tried to find something that would work in my classrooms, since I was a novice and not having much success with 40+ students at a time. She discovered a hybrid pedagogical approach, we might say. It involved dividing up the classes into teams. Each team would compete with the others. But within the teams cooperation would rule. Moreover, each team would be comprised of a share of the brightest, the dimmest, and the in between. Students would cooperate so as to compete, assuming that two disparate motivations would magically combine to achieve the desired effect, namely academic learning. Let’s just say that in my incapable hands, another program collapsed into oblivion.

In today’s New York Times we learn that ability grouping has made a quiet comeback, after being quashed some decades ago, allegedly for damaging children’s self-esteem. After all, who wants the world to know that you’re dumb? So, students were lumped together in elementary classrooms, receiving the same instructions all the time. Of course, problems arose. The quick kids got it, then looked for things to do, much of it mischievous. The slow students never could, so they also acted out. Those in the middle struggled along mostly learning, and it was to those in the middle the teacher directed her lessons. This wasn’t working, which explains the resurgence of grouping children by ability. The Times quotes one educator on the difficulties of whole-class teaching:

“My instruction aimed at the middle of my class, and was leaving out approximately two-thirds of my learners,” said Ms. Sears, a fourth-grade teacher at Woodman Park Elementary in Dover, N.H. “I didn’t like those odds.”

The Times article also talks about the not-so-subtle influence of high-stakes testing. Teachers size up their students’ capabilities rather quickly. They know which students are bright and which are hopeless. They also identify those children who are on the cusp of getting it. With some added attention these struggling students could improve enough to pass the test, and it’s the damn tests that count, after all.

Now I would not call ability grouping a fad or a program. It just makes sense, though it’s hardly easier for the classroom teacher, who must plan separate strategies for lessons to accommodate the different abilities among her students.

There are real fads and programs, however, and a visit to today’s classrooms would reveal an alphabet-soup array of them. I’ll mention three that are prominent in my wife’s school: RTI, AVID, and CIA. In turn, each of these programs comes by way of a separate cottage industry, which charges money for both materials and teacher-instruction, usually in the form of extended conferences housed in resort-like destinations.

In reading about Finland’s approach to education I was struck by the distinction between “renting” and “owning.” The programs mentioned above are “rented” by school districts for a substantial fee, usually. No one in the schools “owns” the programs, since they are not the product of teachers working with their students and with each other. Finnish teachers are trained every bit as much as doctors and lawyers before they enter the profession. That training concentrates on research. So that when teachers assume responsibility for educating pupils, they have a solid grasp of what works and what doesn’t. Also, and most important, the teachers themselves, either individually or collaboratively, design their own curricula. They “own” the programs they create.

This past Sunday the New York Times featured an article by Andrew Hacker and Claudia Dreifus on Common Core Standards. This is the mother of all programs, and it’s being rented by just about every state in the union.

By the 2014-15 academic year, public schools in 45 states and the District of Columbia will administer Common Core tests to students of all ages.

When helping my wife clean out her classroom over the weekend at the end of her last school year, she showed me just a couple of binders filled with Common Core Standards, these on reading and literature. Hacker and Dreifus have identified over 1,300 such standards across the disciplines, from math to reading. Many an educator can abandon sleeping pills for one of the binders. A few pages will do.

WHAT became the Common Core began quite modestly. Several years ago, many organizations, including the National Governors Association and the Council of Chief State School Officers, whose members are top-ranking state education officials, independently noticed that the content and scoring of high school “exit” tests varied widely between states. In 2006, for instance, 91 percent of students in Mississippi passed a mathematics exit exam on the first attempt, while only 65 percent did so in Arizona. At the same time, students’ performance on the National Assessment of Educational Progress often differed from the state results.

This was not just embarrassing: it looked unprofessional. The governors and the school chiefs decided to work together to create a single set of standards and a common grading criteria. Private funding, led by some $35 million in grants from the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, allowed the coalition to spread its wings. Aligning tests became an opportunity to specify what every American child should know.

You can guess who also embraces Common Core. Yep. Arne Duncan, who wants all children to “race to the top.” All they need to do is pass the tests aligned with the 1,300 skills and standards.

I predict that this mother of all programs will be another colossal failure, but not before billions of dollars have been spent and countless teachers have been drummed out of the profession, many of their own volition. They, like my wife, will refuse to participate in creating a nation of “imbeciles,” as Noam Chomsky suggests.

Finland’s policy makers ultimately concluded that improving their public schools had to begin and end with teaching. So, they set out to develop the best and the brightest teachers on the planet, then gave them the resources needed, both within and outside the schools, to produce a nation of academic achievers.

The Finns understand that impoverished children make for poor learners. We Americans, on the other hand, believe that we can rent our way out of poverty. Not going to happen.

I’ll give Hacker and Dreifus the last words:

For our part, we’re tired of seeing teachers cast as scapegoats, of all the carping over unions and tenure. It is time teachers are as revered in society as doctors or scientists, and allowed to work professionally without being bound by reams of rules.

Still, there’s an upside to the Common Core’s arrival. As the public better appreciates its sweep, there is likely to be much discussion about schools and what we want them to do. Ideally, this will involve a reconsideration of the contours of knowledge and the question of how we can become a better-educated nation.

Leaks

June 10, 2013 by davald

The government spies. I know, that shatters the earth. Governments lie, too, as I.F. Stone declared, after decades of revealing the deceptions. So we should not be surprised that the current executive branch, led by an erstwhile defender of privacy and liberties, should both eavesdrop on its citizens then initially deny the same.

Throughout this latest scandal I’m reminded of the television program Person of Interest. A brilliant computer scientist designed an incredibly sophisticated program that instantly sifts through terabytes of data to reveal “a number,” the lone identity of someone who is either about to be harmed or will perpetrate a despicable act against another. Perhaps the premise is not so far fetched, as we’re learning of late.

And speaking of identity, the individual who spilled the beans on the National Security Agency has come forward with an admission, also telling us that he is willing to face the consequences of his actions. And we know that there will be consequences.

Have you noticed, then, that the nefarious deeds revealed rather quickly fade, replaced by an obsessive focus on the leakers? We soon lose sight of the pernicious spying, or the “secret” conversation about evil intentions and conduct, to focus exclusively on those who exposed the shenanigans in the first place. I have in mind, for example, the following:

  • Nixon going after Daniel Ellsberg
  • the Republicans going after Jim McDermott when he distributed a purloined tape recording of misdeeds
  • the government’s over-the-top prosecution of both Private Bradley and Julian Assange in the Wikileaks revelations

We consumers of news gladly allow the message to change from bad people doing bad things to pursuing the whistleblower. Why, he broke the law. He captured the conversation illicitly. They broke national security.

Next scandal.

An elder’s personal story

June 7, 2013 by davald

Watching the interview with singer Patty Griffin about her newest album, American Kid, I concluded that I’ve been remiss in my duty toward history. I’ll explain.

Ms. Griffin gave some thought to the imminent passing of her father, which led to several songs for the album, aided and abetted by Robert Plant, formerly of Led Zeppelin. She spoke of “elders” and how we tend to ignore them, often by sequestering them in places like Florida, where they go to die. Instead, we should encourage our elders to tell their stories, which would enrich our own lives.

I am an elder—officially. While nothing monumental has occurred in my life so far and there is no chance that I’ll have a page on Wikipedia, I begin here a sporadic account of yours truly—as a record for those who survive me, my children and grandchildren, in particular. For the casual reader, I promise nothing of significance, nothing that will excite or enthrall. Truth be told, you’ll find it rather boring. But, hey, it gives me something to do as the clock ticks away.

As with most biographies, we begin with my ancestors. Since they evidently told no stories, I don’t have much to report. I do have some pictures, however.

Let’s start with my father, who would have been 92 years old today. Here’s some proof that he was born. Note the date.

 

Donald J. Aldrich, my father, was born in Elgin, Illinois, to William Hartsford Aldrich and Mary Louise (Devine) Aldrich. My grandfather was born in Iowa, I’m told. Here’s a photo, the oldest I have, showing W.H. with his sister Jessie, whom I never met and knew nothing about.

A rather striking figure, don’t you think? I’m told that his father came from money, which probably accounts for the professional photograph. My great grandfather, Avery, also struck quite a pose.

My grandfather’s mother belonged to the Dennis family. Again, I know very little about her, other than her divorce from Avery after the birth of my grandfather. Here’s a photograph of Mr. Dennis, who was W.H.’s maternal grandfather. (I could have used “begets,” but that’s already been done.)

The Dennises had a farm. Here’s proof:

The Dennises may have had money, too, judging by the number of farmhands.

I mention the money angle because family lore suggests that W.H., my paternal grandfather, was something of a black sheep. He was more Bohemian than Rockefeller, a reported ancestor. Indeed, William Hartsford Aldrich became a political radical, essentially denouncing property; he never owned real estate and referred to Republicans with the adjective “damn.” We might get a sense of why he chose a different path by looking at his mother and aunt below.

Run for the hills, Grandpa! These women meant business.

W.H. learned a trade. He would support himself and family as a linotype operator, and, I’m told, a darn good one. Here’s a photograph of him working for a local newspaper. My grandfather appears on the far left.

My paternal grandparents, surprise, met then got married. I’ll show you a delightful photograph of the young couple, first.

They were married in Chicago, where they would live for several years.

Now, the announcement says that they moved first to Racine, Wisconsin. News to me. I presume that my grandfather’s job was there, but he would eventually work for one of the Chicago major dailies, before an extended strike forced him to go west, where he joined his three sons after the war.

My paternal grandmother, who was called “Molly,” was born in DeKalb, Illinois, of an Irish immigrant, John Devine, and a woman from New Jersey, named Ellen Dolan. Here’s Molly’s birth certificate.

My grandmother had two sisters and a brother, Uncle Ed. He was a bit daft, shall we say, though not always so. I’ll say a bit more about him later. But first I’ve got to show you this picture of W.H. and the three sisters.

Molly is at the far left. Aunt Margaret is in the middle, with Aunt Nell on the right. (Nell was a battle axe, having forced me to eat corn flakes with water—no milk or sugar—during a brief baby-sitting experience. As you can see, she could have played a linebacker for the Bears.)

I’ll sign off now, but will return with more pictures and narrative. Oh, about Uncle Ed.

The story goes that he was a bright, outgoing young man who fell head over heels for a woman. They became engaged, then she broke it off. That was literally too much for Ed, who suffered a mental breakdown. He never recovered, and was essentially in the care of my two great aunts above. I recall his sitting in front of the television watching a ball game eating soup, which he slurped messily. I think I have a picture of him in his later years. Here is it. He’s in the middle. Spoiler alert: that’s my mother on the far right. Aunt Margaret is just behind Uncle Ed’s left shoulder. I have no idea who the others are.
