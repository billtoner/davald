Up, down, or just staying the same

January 15, 2013 by davald

I routinely check the stock markets, though I have no direct investments in equities. I do so because, rightly or wrongly, I view the Wall Street trajectories as a gross indicator of economic health. Intuitively I sense that if the Dow Jones and S&P are moving upward at a steady pace, things are generally good. If they’re trending downward, people are feeling less hopeful about the economy.

John Maynard Keynes offered his opinion in The General Theory:

Even outside the field of finance, Americans are apt to be unduly interested in discovering what average opinion believes average opinion to be; and this national weakness finds its nemesis in the stock market. It is rare, one is told, for an American to invest, as many Englishmen still do, ‘for income’; and he will not readily purchase an investment except in the hope of capital appreciation. This is only another way of saying that, when he purchases an investment, the American is attaching his hopes, not so much to its prospective yield, as to a favourable change in the conventional basis of valuation, i.e. that he is, in the above sense, a speculator.

The American is “attaching his hopes” to a belief that others will increase the value of his holdings. If more people buy a particular stock, the price will rise, since shares are finite. The trick is to “get in” when the price is low then wait until it rises before cashing out.

A friend of mine recently purchased about 80 shares of Apple stock. As I recall he bought when the price was around $550 a share. Quickly he “made” several thousand dollars, as the value appreciated. But timing is everything. This morning Apple is trading at around $490.

Let’s look at the Dow Jones Industrial Average since 1950.

We’ll note that in the post-War decades before Ronald Reagan assumed office, the stock market barely moved, either up or down. Then things changed, dramatically so. Stock prices began to rise every year in exponential fashion. They did so especially during Clinton’s last term.

Part of the reason for the change must surely be the amount of economic output going to capital and the declining fortunes of labor. With so much money consolidated in the hands of a few, where to put it? The rich, as a rule, don’t particularly care to put their dollars in mattresses. They want their cash to grow. So they invest, with the hope, really, that value appreciates.

Yet, investing in the stock market is not for the faint-hearted. If we plot the annual change in the Dow we get a chart like this:

That red line is the linear trend, which is about nine percent. If one has money to invest and can be patient, buying stocks eventually rewards the purchaser over time.

Now back to the stock market as a gross economic indicator. The economy continues to sputter; and since Reagan, annual growth rates have steadily declined. Here’s a chart comparing the changes in DJIA and real GDP.

While the market fluctuates markedly, real GDP not so much. However, and as I’ve noted previously, the trend in GDP is downward. Indeed, sputtering economies are the new norm.

Not much there to get excited about. And if my intuitions are correct, then I am not surprised that stock markets are also sputtering. The reigning pessimism is manifest in both economic output and Wall Street metrics.

My biggest concern is that the numbers will stay modest for a very long time. Should that occur, millions will continue to suffer for lack of work and thus income.

Yet the rich continue to do well, and since they’ve accumulated so much wealth they have acquired the political power to match. So they are not likely to advocate policies that will improve matters for the Rest of Us. Indeed, many of them have demonstrated an open hostility to “the moochers,” otherwise known as “the 47 percent.” At the very least, we’re irrelevant.

This is tragic, not only because so many continue to suffer, but also because of Americans’ profound ignorance. Consider this one simple statistic. Ninety percent of U.S. households have incomes below $100,000 a year. Yep. If your family draws down a bit more than that, you’re in the top 10 percent. Now that doesn’t seem like a whole lot of money to me. It does not bespeak tropical vacations or second homes along beaches. That kind of money is not usually associated with a Mercedes in the garage or fancy meals each week. We’re talking the basics and perhaps something left over for rainy days.

I suppose, since the Rest of Us outnumber the rich, that a more representative government could be had, one that worked for rather than against our interests. I’m not holding my breath.

Building less

January 14, 2013 by davald

Fewer people employed in construction. No surprise there, given the collapse of the housing bubble and the severe overhang of both stock and debt. We would also expect spending to be down in this sector as well.

Consider this excerpt from a recent study (pdf) on infrastructure spending:

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The goal of this report is to understand the short- and long-term effects of public infrastructure spending on the U.S. economy, as well as to contribute new suggestions towards alternative financing of future road construction.

Estimated Short-Run Effects

  •  In the short-run, a dollar spent on infrastructure construction produces roughly double the initial spending in ultimate economic output.
  •  The biggest effects of infrastructure spending occur in the manufacturing and business services sectors.
  • In better economic times, spending on infrastructure construction generates a larger return. Yet even in a recession, the overall effects of initial spending still double output as they ripple through the economy.

Estimated Long-Run Effects

  • Over a twenty-year period, generalized ‘public investment’ generates an accumulated $3.21 of economic activity per $1.00 spent.
  •  Over twenty years, investing $1.00 in highways and streets returns approximately $0.35 in tax revenue to federal and state/local governments, of which $0.23 specifically accrues at the federal level.
  • Over twenty years, investing $1.00 in sewer systems and water infrastructure returns a full $2.03 in tax revenue to federal and state/local governments, of which $1.35 specifically accrues at the federal level.

Despite the need for and desirability of spending on infrastructure, Republicans resist any and all fiscal stimulus. They say, in their defense, that they just don’t like government debt. Nor do they care much for government at all, save for the jobs they hold within it.

But look at the above numbers. Every dollar the government spends on building roads or schools, for example, adds two dollars to GDP. A stronger economy yields higher government revenues, thereby reducing deficits. Since Washington can borrow money at essentially zero cost to fund infrastructure improvements, we seem to have a no-brainer.

All of which suggests that Republicans lack more than a brain.

A few brain farts short of victory

January 14, 2013 by davald

Puget Sound came to a freezing halt yesterday morning. Between the hours of 10 and 1:30 p.m., sports fans and their suffering others sat before television screens anxiously riveted to the fortunes of the Seattle Seahawks. In the end, and I mean the very bitter end, hopes were dashed, quickly replaced by thoughts of next year.

Pete Carroll’s decisions during the game disqualify him for coach of the year, or anything else. His only claim to fame this season was selecting Russell Wilson as his starting quarterback, after the team signed Aaron Rodgers’s backup to a multi-million-dollar contract. Were it not for the rookie QB at the helm, the Seahawks may have been scrambling to win the cellar, a tough competition among two other hopefuls, St. Louis and Arizona.

As for the decisions, what, pray tell, was he thinking? Perhaps that was the problem. When careful thought was required, Carroll suffered brain farts. It’s fourth-and-one in the red zone. Behind by two scores, the coach elected to “go for it.” Except that his team wasn’t ready, with a mismatch at the line. So, rather than take the sure three points from a field goal, the Hawks ran the ball and actually lost a yard. The Falcons took possession then marched up field for a score, putting Seattle down 20-0. Oh, oh. Had Carroll done the right and obvious thing, the Seahawks, as it turns out, would have won the game, perhaps handily.

Then at the end of the first half, with his team trailing 27-7, Carroll took his last time out, denying the Hawks yet another opportunity to put points on the board. Into the locker room they went, much further behind than in last week’s game against the Redskins. But with Wilson, no opponent’s lead is safe.

I can only imagine what D.C.’s fans were going through in that second half, as different from the first as Bill Walsh is from Pete Carroll. Russell Wilson proved once again that he deserves the Rookie-of-the-Year award, if not also MVP. He marched and threw and ran, taking big chunks out of the Redskins’s margin. In our brief story, we are setting things up for one last brain fart.

Miraculously, the Seahawks score a touchdown, achieving a tie. The extra point is good. Seattle is ahead by a single point with only 31 seconds to go in the game! Victory in hand, right?

Let’s keep in mind that the Seattle defense sucked in the first half then held the Redskins to nada in the second. But that last brain fart is looming as time ticks away.

First Carroll elects to kick the ball deep, allowing a runback that consumed almost 30 yards in just six seconds. A completed pass puts the Redskins into Seattle territory. On their last play from scrimmage, the Redskins would throw to the most obvious man on earth. That would be fellow former-Bear Tony Gonzalez, the planet’s best tight end—ever. He caught the ball, against a linebacker! No contest; a poor coaching decision not to have a safety also covering the future Hall-of-Fame inductee. Time out.

Out trots the Redskins’s place kicker. Now we witness the last episode of mental flatulence. Carroll would choose to call a timeout himself. You know, to “ice” the kicker. Silly stuff. The ball was snapped anyway, and, guess what? The ensuing kick sailed wide right. But Washington gets a do-over. The next try would arch its way through the middle of the goal posts. Game over. The Seahawks lose 30-28.

Damn.

UK: a “natural” experiment

January 11, 2013 by davald

Britain’s political leaders assumed office with the stated intention of reining in debt. So they slashed federal funding. In so doing, they promised, the economy would actually grow. They relied on a firm belief that what causes output to expand is “confidence.” If capitalists were assured that the government would keep its fiscal house in order, then they would buy bonds and invest in productive pursuits.

Well, Cameron’s Tory regime definitely cut spending. But the promise of prosperity remains a very empty one.

Writing for the London Review of Books, John Lanchester calls the Cameron-Osborne policies “a failure.” (Subscription may be required to read article online.)

Saying ‘I told you so’ is supposed to be near unbeatable fun, so it’s disappointing to report that, in the case of the government’s handling of the British economy, speaking for myself, no fun is being had. As George Osborne’s autumn statement made clear, the scale and speed and completeness with which things are going wrong are numbing. The Tories went into the 2010 election with a manifesto commitment to reduce the structural deficit – the amount by which the government’s spending in any given year exceeds its income, excluding temporary effects from the downturn. The first point in their economic policy read as follows: ‘We will safeguard Britain’s credit rating with a credible plan to eliminate a large part of the structural deficit over a Parliament.’ How? It’s on the next page: ‘We will cut government spending to bring the deficit down and restore stability.’

I showed this chart previously. It compares real GDP of the UK and the U.S. since the Great Recession, or roughly the end of 2007. Lanchester cites the Office of Budget Responsibility’s figures, which show that the UK economy will contract again this year.

Lanchester discusses “the multiplier.” If the government spends a dollar, does the economy grow by that same amount, or does it either contract or expand by a greater amount? Likewise, if the government cuts spending, is there a one-to-one correspondence in GDP? The International Monetary Fund recently concluded that government austerity measures, in the UK especially, have a negative multiplier above unity. That is, for every dollar the government cuts spending, there is more than a dollar contraction of GDP.

In a previous post I included a link to a rough transcript prepared by Berkeley economist Brad DeLong of a four-member panel discussion. One of the members, economist Valerie Ramey, argued that government spending had a multiplier of less than one. Therefore, she concludes, fiscal stimulus would actually harm the economy. But if this were true, then how to explain what’s going on in the UK? Why hasn’t austerity done what its proponents promised? Could it be that the IMF is right and Ramey is wrong?

We can appreciate that the answer is important. If Ramey is correct, then the government really can’t do anything to stimulate the economy, contra John M. Keynes. If she is wrong and the multiplier is above unity, then the federal government can improve economic output. Or, as we’re seeing in Britain, contracting federal expenditures can actually retard GDP.

Lanchester provides an interesting discussion of a related topic, the velocity of money. Here’s how he tells it:

Imagine for a moment that you come across an unexpected ten pounds. After making a mental note not to spend it all at once, you go out and spend it all at once, on, say, two pairs of woolly socks. The person from the sock shop then takes your tenner and spends it on wine, and the wine merchant spends it on tickets to see The Bitter Tears of Petra von Kant, and the owner of the cinema spends it on chocolate, and the sweet-shop owner spends it on a bus ticket, and the owner of the bus company deposits it in the bank. That initial ten pounds has been spent six times, and has generated £60 of economic activity. In a sense, no one is any better off; and yet, that movement of money makes everyone better off. To put it another way, that first tenner has contributed £60 to Britain’s GDP. Seen in this way, GDP can be thought of as a measure not so much of size – how much money we have, how much money the economy contains – but of velocity. It measures the movement of money through and around the economy; it measures activity. If you had taken the same ten quid when it was first given to you and simply paid it into your bank account, the net position could be argued to be the same – except that the only contribution to GDP is that initial gift of £10, and if this behaviour were replicated across the whole economy, then the whole economy would grind to a halt. And that, broadly speaking, is what is happening right now. People are sitting on that first tenner.

I have suggested elsewhere that inequality reduces the velocity of money. The lower classes, in economic terms, tend to spend most, if not all, of what they make. The rich buy what they need and want and still have a surplus, which they park somewhere and otherwise withhold from circulation.

Here’s a chat that I’ve used before. It shows the Gini index and the velocity of money. As we see, as the country got poorer it spread less of its money around.

 

So, we could increase economic activity by reducing inequality. We could help reduce inequality by putting more people to work in better-paying jobs. Since the private sector isn’t doing very well on this front, we must turn to the government. We’ve got a lot of things that need fixing around here.

Giving a damn

January 10, 2013 by davald

Yesterday I referenced a forum of economists, which included Paul Krugman. I mentioned a division between the panelists according to compassion, or lack thereof. To wit, and we might expect this from someone who calls his blog “The Conscience of a Liberal,” Krugman frets over so many people being still unable to find work. He adds that not only are their lives ruined but the future health of the economy will be adversely effected.

On her blog this morning Digby decries the pervasive indifference to the unemployed. She writes:

It remains one of the strangest and saddest aspects of our current economic debates that nobody seems to care all that much about our still painfully high unemployment.

The official statistics should trouble us.

 

There are many people in Congress and in academia who would have us believe that the economy will eventually right itself, if left to its own devices. In other words, the government should stay the hell out of the way and let “market forces” do their thing. Indeed, government meddling only makes matters worse. And, by the way, forget all that stuff by Keynes.

But the above chart suggests that we’re in a very unique period, vastly different from what the country experienced from the late-60s to the Great Recession. In particular, a whole lot of people are out of a job and they’ve been unemployed for far longer than “normal.”

We can see from the chart below that more and more people were joining the labor force during the post-War years.

 

But I hasten to add that the chart misleads. If we divide the labor force by sex, we see a different story.

First the men:

 

As you might have guessed, women have increased their participation rate.

 

Digby links to a Huffington Post article about the long-term unemployed. Here’s an interesting point:

Jesse Rothstein, an associate professor of public policy and economics at the University of California, Berkeley, found in a 2011 research paper that the recent regimen of extended benefits did indeed keep people from giving up their job search as quickly as they otherwise might have.

Did you catch that? Extended unemployment benefits correlate with recipients continuing to look for work. This belies the myth propagated by the Right that giving money to the unemployed undermines their incentive to find a job.

It’s all Keynes’s fault

January 10, 2013 by davald

James Buchanan, who won a Nobel Prize in economics, blamed Keynesian economic policies for the eventual disconnect between federal expenditures and revenues—otherwise known as deficits. Buchanan championed smaller governments, because he believed that larger governments enabled corruption, bribery, extortion and those things we call earmarks, subsidies, and tax breaks, all of which essentially define the status quo. His recent death prompted an obituary in the New York Times.

Economist Dean Baker chided the Gray Lady for failing to expose defects in Buchanan’s views. He charges the Times with “rewriting history” as regards the effects of Keynesian policies. In particular, the post-War decades before the Great Divergence were a period of relative fiscal balance, marked by adherence to the Keynesian notion that federal governments have an important role in the national economy.

I’ve written often about these “relative halcyon days” of fiscal probity cum continuing economic growth. Some have called this era “the Great Moderation.” We were a more egalitarian society then, and workers’ productivity gains closely matched wage appreciation.

We’ll note that after Ronald Reagan entered the White House federal receipts started lagging behind outlays, creating annual deficits and an accumulating debt. The one exception occurred during Clinton’s last term, when revenues actually exceeded expenditures, resulting in budget surpluses that G.W. Bush quickly squandered, as the above chart clearly demonstrates.

How did the economy do during those post-War years? Take a look.

These numbers tend to refute Buchanan’s central thesis, at least as characterized by the Times. After all, according to Baker, even R.M. Nixon proclaimed that “we are all Keynesians now.” Reagan didn’t get the memo. But he did get an airport.

Yet, I find nothing wrong with this excerpt from the obituary:

But Dr. Buchanan contended that the pursuit of self-interest by modern politicians often led to harmful public results. Courting voters at election time, for example, legislators will approve tax cuts and spending increases for projects and entitlements favored by the electorate. This combination can lead to ever-rising deficits, public debt burdens and increasingly large governments to conduct the public’s business.

Indeed, he said, governments had grown so vast and complex that it was no longer possible for elected officials to make more than a fraction of the policy decisions that genuinely affect the people. Thus, he said, much discretionary power is actually held by civil functionaries who can manipulate priorities, impose barriers to entitlements and pressure legislators for rules and budgets favorable to their own interests.

Sounds about right.

Bonds whiffs

January 9, 2013 by davald

The baseball writers snubbed him, along with the other still-living players eligible for this year’s Hall of Fame induction. The reason for the grand no-vote was obvious: steroids, whether real or imagined. In deciding, the voters had to consider “integrity” and “character.”

In the case of Barry Bonds, no baseball writer liked him, and he returned the favor. Though he was not convicted of using performance-enhancing drugs, the mostly white baseball world simply didn’t care for the bulked version, which gave writers the excuse to deny black Bonds entry.

Some comparisons may be in order. What did the “skinny” Bonds do versus the new and improved edition?

 

Bonds played 22 years, roughly half at around 190 pounds, the other between 206 and 228. Had he averaged 30.4 HR for 22 years he would have wound up with 668, just a few more than his godfather, Willie Mays, but far short of Hank Aaron’s 755. During those “bulk” years Bonds increased his annual output by 28 percent. Had he been forever bulky, he might have hit 100 more four-baggers than Aaron, or 855—statistically speaking.

Hitting a baseball is difficult. The best players hit safely less than a third of the time. You don’t win elections with those numbers. Nor could you stay in the NBA shooting only 30 percent.

In his leaner years, Bonds hit above .300 five times and averaged .288. The heavier Bonds averaged .309, for a seven-percent improvement. He hit .370 in 2002 at the age of 37. The year before he knocked 73 balls over the fence, a single-season record. Does a stronger player improve his ability to make contact? I doubt it.

Also, Bonds hit 46 home runs as a skinnier player; he weighed 185. Both his batting average and home-run production increased with age as well as with weight. Could it be that he just got better through years of practice and in the weight room and that drugs may have had little to do with his success?

Well, the writers appeared to have focused exclusively on the taint, denying Barry Bonds et al. their due. The voters clearly have no idea what talent is involved in doing well on the diamond, whether one is skinny or bulked.

When economists gather

January 9, 2013 by davald

Paul Krugman and three other economists formed a panel over the weekend in San Diego. Ostensibly the goal was to extract answers to four or five questions. As it happened, each panelist proffered his or her views on the role of government in the economy, whether in good or bad times. The substitute moderator, Brad DeLong, who teaches macroeconomics at Berkeley, provides a “rough” transcript of the event, which is well worth your time.

Yet, I must add that I am not any more confident in the economics profession now than I was before, a small increment above zero. Krugman and DeLong have argued and argue here that the government should be stimulating the economy by spending on useful projects. Other panelists, most notably Valerie Ramey, counter that the multiplier effect of fiscal expansion is less than zero. She is joined in this view by Harald Uhlig.

A cursory reading of the statements reveals a fundamental divide between Krugman and the others. The difference is compassion, for lack of a better word at the moment. Krugman believes that our collective failure to address the immediate problems of high un-and under-employment means not only long-term disaster for the overall economy but also tragedy for those directly affected. At the end of the Q&A one senses strong exasperation on his part for what he surely holds to be wrongheaded arguments by his colleagues, who exhibit the sympathy of a prune.

If people attended the forum in hopes of finding wisdom via consensus they were sorely disappointed. The state of macro is very muddy indeed.

Sad.

That said, one obvious point made by Prof. Ramey. If we could significantly reform the health care system in the U.S. we would get close to solving the fiscal mess. As I’ve written on these pages, the U.S. spends far more per capita on health care but achieves less aggregate benefit.

Where Ramey gets into trouble, in my opinion, is by drawing a simplistic conclusion from headline data. To wit, she concludes that U.S. government spending on health care must be inefficient, since Canada spends the same amount as a percentage of GDP as we do (roughly eight percent) but 100 percent of Canadians are covered, whereas only 25 percent of Americans are. If our federal government were to cover all U.S. citizens, then health care spending would rise dramatically, she opines.

I see two problems with this extrapolation. First, Ramey ignores the whole idea of insurance, something that Ben Franklin understood over two hundred years ago. All Canadians belong to one giant pool of risks. The healthy therefore subsidize the relatively unhealthy. In America, at least prior to the Affordable Care Act, government programs covered those who lack private health insurance because they have none through their employer or have pre-existing conditions the carriers refuse to cover. As a result, publicly funded health care is more expensive here than in Canada. Second, Ramey ignores the inherent inefficiencies of the private sector, with its multiple providers and high administrative costs. Also, no need for advertising budgets in universal health care systems.

Before leaving, I refer to a comment made by Prof. Krugman. He tells the audience:

The basic story—at least as many of us see it—is that we had this really, really dramatic shock to private spending. This is the private-sector financial deficit: gross private domestic investment minus gross private domestic saving as a share of potential GDP as estimated by the CBO.
