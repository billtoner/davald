Baker to Brooks, come in

July 15, 2011 by davald

Dean Baker, one of the few economists to predict the rising housing bubble and the implications of its collapse, blogs at the Center for Economic and Policy Research. Today he takes on David Brooks, which has become a cottage industry, along with exposing George F. Will’s crazy ideas on climate change.

Baker writes:

However, it seems that no one told Brooks that the problem is not that people in the United States are getting too much care, the problem is that we are paying too much for the care we get. The United States pays close to twice as much for its drugs, its doctors, its medical equipment as people in other wealthy countries. As a result, our per person health care costs are more than twice the average of other wealthy countries, even though they all enjoy longer life expectancies. If we paid the same amount per person for our health care as people in other wealthy countries, then we would be looking at long-term budget surpluses, not deficits.

This means that Brooks’ discussion of our willingness to die when life loses its joys is beside the point. The choices around the end of life are important and difficult, but that is not our health care cost problem. Our health care cost problem is the cesspool corruption that we rely upon for our health care.

Brooks has made a huge step forward by recognizing that the fiscal problem is not one of government spending generally, but rather spending on health care. Now he has to make another huge step forward to recognize that our health care system is a money pit that is better at transferring money to providers than giving care to the public.

Sounds pretty simple, the way Baker puts it. The cynic in me suggests that it’s not in Washington’s nature to follow common sense; much better to obfuscate and complicate. There’s where the money is.

Technology musings

July 15, 2011 by davald

I’ve come across a few articles of late proclaiming the rise of mobile computing in the enterprise. One pundit suggested that whoever rules mobile computing will eventually rule IT departments, since mobile computing is expected to overtake the desktop in the foreseeable future.

IT departments have been predictably averse to innovation, preferring the tried and true for a couple of reasons: (1) purported security concerns; and (2) entrenched platform bias. The first is legitimate. The second wrong-headed.

For any of you who have spent some time working in a medium- to large-size business you’re no doubt familiar with the conventional computer structure. You, as a client, have a cubicle, most likely, furnished with desks, drawers, shelves, and the ubiquitous tower computer-cum-display-cum keyboard. In turn, your central processing unit (CPU) is connected to a server infrastructure via Ethernet cables. The software applications, overwhelmingly Microsoft, reside on your CPU’s hard drive. There you may also store files or keep them on any number of servers, usually with peculiar names. You’ll have access to the Internet and email.

Increasingly, more and more of your time is spent in front of the computer display, hands fixed above or resting upon the keyboard, eyes glued to the screen. More often than not you’re engaged in email exchanges with other employees or individuals and groups external to the organization. You have occasion to write and read many emails. Some have attachments, either produced in Microsoft’s office suite programs (e.g., Word, Excel) or transformed into portable document format (pdf). You may, from time to time, access corporate data, which you then manipulate (not necessarily a pejorative term) for, say, report purposes.

It’s not unusual to walk through the labyrinth of cubicles to see dozens of ensconced employees doing pretty much the same thing: typing or reading at their desks. Even employees in adjacent cubicles will use email to communicate with each other rather than chat face to face.

There are meetings, of course. A small group of employees seated around one or more tables with an overhead screen at one end of the room and a projector at the other. Someone in the group has a laptop or a desktop system, physically tethered to the projector. That person will run the slide show, the lingua franca of all modern organizations. The group members will gaze at the screen while the presenter literally reads from the slides as they appear in order onto the screen. It’s not unusual to be provided with a printed copy of the presentation. Sounds like fun? You know the answer.

Let’s try to envisage a new paradigm of corporate communications.

Every employee will have a portable computer that fits into a pocket or in the palm of a hand—think smartphone and tablet. Gigabytes of data in various forms are instantly available on these seemingly miniature devices. That information is stored in “a cloud” and everything on the smart devices is automatically synced with the cloud. Make a change on your tablet and it’s automatically available on all your portable devices. Want to show other employees what’s on your tablet, say, then simply display the information on one of many LCDs scattered around the workspaces. No need for wires; the information is sent wirelessly.

Much work done in organizations is project-based. That means teams of employees, probably from different departments representing different organizational functions. Collaboration is critical, necessitating increased contact, either physical or virtual. Each team member, under the new paradigm, will have instant access to the same information via the cloud-cum-smart devices. Instead of employees passively gazing at a PowerPoint presentation, each will interact with the other co-creating the project-based material and outcomes.

It’s easy to imagine that the traditional desktop computer infrastructure and those thousands of wires will go the way of the dinosaur. Instead all will be wireless and data will be stored in the cloud rather than reside on cubicle hard drives or even laptops. That information will be available 24/7 to any employee with the requisite credentials. Communication will be seamless between internal and external customers, using web-based apps, virtual face-to-face exchanges (e.g., Apple’s Facetime) via smart devices, and virtual conferencing involving multiple parties.

I’ve had this hunch for some time. Microsoft, which has coasted on its Office and Windows application licenses, raking in billions of dollars a quarter, will either recognize the shifting paradigm or not. Its track record on innovation is hopeless, so even if Mr. Balmer finally gets it, it will be too late. The new technology giants are Google and Apple, with the former aping many of the strategies of Redmond including market fragmentation (running software on multiple, disparate devices made by a multitude of different companies). Apple, as always, works to integrate the software with the hardware, a model scoffed at by both Bill Gates and Steve Balmer.

But consider this piece.

Apple currently dominates the tablet market, having sold 25 million iPads to date and possibly more than 14 million more iPads in the third quarter. But one analyst believes Apple will sell a billion of them.

OK, OK. It’s clear that Apple’s iOS is beyond successful, and threatens to dominate global mobile sales in the foreseeable future.

The reason this is significant is that as Apple comes to dominate mobile computing, mobile computing is increasingly dominating computing in general.

Over the next five years, we’ll see more people using phones and tablets as their main computing devices. And I think we can also expect to see current desktop platforms like Mac OS X, Windows and Linux replaced by touch-friendly interfaces like iOS, Android, Metro and so on.

In other words, Apple’s iOS is poised to take over mainstream computing.

If Apple should prevail, we may finally be able to say goodbye to paper and those insufferable PowerPoint presentations. Kicking and screaming to the new paradigm will be the IT staff, all too comfortable with their legacy Windows’ systems.

We’ll see.

What now, Mariners?

July 14, 2011 by davald

With the second half of the season underway, what can or should the Mariners do? If they honestly believe that they are still in the hunt, they’ll likely make different choices as we near the July 31 trading deadline than if they are convinced that yet another season is headed into the toilet.

As of this writing, the team is 7.5 games back of division-leading Texas, which has eked out a one-game lead over the closing Angels, who began the season mired in last place. In order to compete in the West, the Mariners will have to beat both of these teams, which is a tall order, indeed. We need remember that just before the All-Star break the Halos swept the Mariners in a four-game series.

We should also keep in mind that the M’s have the worst hitting team in baseball, and this dismal fact is on display almost every game. The Angels beat the M’s with the long ball, and Texas has even more firepower. Despite being among the best, the Mariner hurlers make mistakes from time to time. Texas and Anaheim exploit those errors with four-bangers. In reverse, the M’s either let the center-cut fastball slip harmlessly into the catcher’s mitt or they manage a weak out. Yep, these Mariners just can’t hit worth a darn.

But, we hear, there is potential. There’s always potential. Greg Halman and Dustin Ackley show early promise, with both hitting above .300. Then it’s downhill from there. Perennial all-star Ichiro Suzuki is batting a career-low .270 and may not record 200 hits for the first time in the bigs. Justin Smoak lingers at 12 homeruns, tied with catcher Miguel Olivo for the team lead. Yet their averages are .229 and .223, respectively. Gold-glover Gutierrez has shown some recent spark at the plate, but is still far below the Mendoza line at .187. Brendan Ryan, after a superlative May, has seen his average plummet; he’s hitting .252. Old reliable Adam Kennedy is doing slightly better at .259.

Because the Mariners have a topnotch pitching staff most of their games are close. However, when the opposition averages more runs per nine innings than you, chances are you’ll lose more games than you’ll win.

I’m going out on a very short limb to suggest that the Mariners are unlikely to be challenging either Texas or Anaheim in September. So, if I were the general manager, here is what I’d do:

  1. Jettison Chone Figgins and Jack Cust. In eight years with the Angels, Figgins batted 0.291, with an on-base percentage of .363. So far with the Mariners, Figgins is hitting an anemic 0.238, with an OBP of .308. When the Figgins deal was first announced, I said “kudos” to GM Jack Zduriencik. Who would have thought that Figgins would just collapse upon moving to Seattle. (Second thought, how many players have plunged into the performance black hole we natives call ‘the Emerald City’?) As for Cust, over his career he’s struck out 39 percent of the time he comes to the plate. (Figgins’ number is 15 percent.) His 162-game seasonal average for home runs is 26; this year he’s hit just three.
  2. Jack Wilson is another acquisition I initially supported, with good reason. Seven years ago he made the All-Star team as a shortstop, batting .308. His lifetime fielding percentage is .977. (Current shortstop Brendan Ryan is a hair better at .979.) But with Wilson’s myriad injuries and placing himself indefinitely in manager Eric Wedge’s doghouse for volunteering to quit in the middle of a game for “the team’s sake,” Wilson’s value to the M’s is nearly zero.

Here’s the problem, which you can anticipate: the Mariners won’t get very much for these players, unless there really is something to the black-hole phenomenon, in which case, once these players leave town they’ll blossom again. Try that for a negotiating tactic; actually sell Seattle as the nonpareil talent-sucking vortex. But I jest.

The players that would fetch big money or quality players are pitchers you’d need to compete, if not now then eventually. What a dilemma: weaken the pitching staff in the hope of landing some bats.

Speaking of bats, Safeco Field might as well have force fields for fences. Balls don’t fly away; they die in outfielders’ gloves. This is not Texas or Anaheim. So, I would focus on acquiring contact hitters with higher OBPs and more productive outs. The stadium nearly dictates a station-to-station approach. Forget about the long ball. (Which is why I thought Figgins would be a good fit. Oh, well.)

The Mariners have a pretty good reputation for developing talent in their farm system. And it’s interesting to note that their AAA team, the Tacoma Rainiers, has almost a team makeup the reverse of the M’s: good hitting, poor pitching. Just look at these stats. Five players are hitting .335 or above. Kyle Seager was leading the team at .455 before he was brought up a couple of weeks ago. Several on this list have been with the M’s, only to fail miserably. Yet, as soon as they return to Tacoma, they resume hitting the cover off the ball. The difference has to be the quality of pitching in the majors vs. the minors.

Now, are these Tacoma hitters really that good so that the only thing they lack to succeed in the bigs is experience hitting top-caliber pitching? To a fault, perhaps, I think the Mariners’ brass has been patient with the likes of Michael Saunders, Matt Tuiososopo, and Ryan Langerhans. None did well up here. Mike Wilson didn’t get a fair shake this year; he’s hitting .345 with the Rainiers.

At the risk of condemnation, I’m going to wade into dangerous territory. It’s about Ichiro. Both his hitting and his fielding have suffered this year, and his batting average is trending down. While this shouldn’t surprise us, after all he’s 37, it brings up an obvious question: Could another team realize more value from Ichiro than the M’s?

What gives me pause, is that Ichiro is still quite popular in Seattle, for good reason. Also, the team’s Japanese ownership, I gather, would never consent to trading him. These qualities, however, combined with declining skills create an obstacle. There are only three outfield positions, and the Mariners appear to have some real natural talent waiting to fill those slots. Should management conclude that the team can’t compete for the top rung, then it would seem appropriate to bring up the hitters from Tacoma to gain experience against major-league pitching.

But what about their hitting instructor, Chris Chambliss? Last year, during the team’s 101-loss season, the hitting coach was fired. Will Chambliss also be sacrificed? He could certainly hit as a long-time major leaguer, finishing with a career average of .279. (He hit a home run off me when he was at UCLA and I at Cal.) Hitting and coaching don’t always go together, however.

Charlie Lau is considered one of the best hitting instructors in major league history, yet his career average was only .255. One characteristic of the “Charlie Lau swing” is removing the top hand from the bat after contact with the ball. Ted Williams scoffed at this method, and the Splendid Splinter was not only one of the top five hitters to ever play the game he was perhaps the most earnest student of hitting, even after he’d long hung up his cleats.

I mention these two individuals to suggest that hitting has many theories and few good practitioners. Moreover, it does not follow that those who have excelled at the plate can succeed as instructors—or vice versa.

It’s obvious, though, that whatever Chambliss is telling and showing these young talents isn’t translating. One of the M’s’ problems is that they quickly fall behind in the count, letting the best pitches (which could be “mistakes”) go by for called strikes. Also, lacking plate discipline, the now-behind hitters easily fall victim to the down-and-out slider. How many times have we seen Olivo, or Peguero, or Gutierrez swing wildly at a pitch far off the plate or in the dirt?

As a former pitcher I can assure the reader how important it is to get ahead in the count. The fast-ball first-pitch strike is strategy number one. So why don’t the Mariner hitters realize this? Why do they let so many good pitches go? If Chambliss is telling his charges to be “more aggressive,” it isn’t sticking.

Enough of this. The M’s aren’t going anywhere. Let’s trade or sell the deadwood, keep the pitching, and bring up the bats from Tacoma. But I’d also recruit a different hitting coach. Sorry, Chris. Unfortunately, Charlie Lau died a long time ago. So did Ted Williams.

Maybe there’s someone down in Tacoma.

We’d like everything for free, thank you.

July 13, 2011 by davald

The NY Times‘ David Leonhardt pens a nice column revealing Americans’ insatiable desire for free lunches. We like all the good stuff, and will resist efforts to diminish them, but we’re sure as heck not going to pay for them. He writes:

Unfortunately, this nostalgic view depends on a misunderstanding of the budget. It imagines a budget in which the United States indefinitely has the world’s highest medical costs, its largest military, an aging population and, nonetheless, taxes that are among the world’s lowest. Economists have a name for that combination: a free lunch.

Members of Congress behave irresponsibly, reflecting their constituencies. They evidently believe that they can get away with such gimmicks as pledging not to raise taxes or even allow shameful subsidies to elapse. Why? Besides strict adherence to a loopy ideology I think the principal reason is that each of them are nearly anonymous to the whole. That is, being one of 435, as in the House, or even one of 100, as in the Senate, provides cover for the ridiculous. No single member, after all, is responsible to the whole.

But simple logic tells us that the aggregation of individual irresponsibility yields collective irresponsibility. I suggest that the only individual in D.C. who may genuinely feel some responsibility to the whole—as in country—is the president. Yet he is clearly hamstrung by the collective irresponsibility of the other branches. He can’t accomplish much of anything without the backing of Congress; and this Congress has no intention of acceding to presidential victories.

An obvious example of the irresponsibility is screaming bloody murder about the horrific federal debt (not really) while simultaneously refusing to raise taxes that would eliminate the debt. (Recall that the country was on a path to retire its debt, had the Clinton-era tax rates continued.)

Playing in Arizona

July 12, 2011 by davald

Dave Zirin tells us that he will refuse to watch tonight’s All Star game on television. But if he lived within a day’s drive of Phoenix, he’d show up to protest. Why?

Well, unless you’ve been living under a rock during the last year or so you are aware of that state’s draconian anti-immigrant law, the one that’s currently being challenged in the courts. After the bill was signed into law by Arizona’s governor, Zirin tried to draw attention to the xenophobic racists of Arizona. Zirin:

Over the last year, civil rights organizations, politicians, sportswriters and baseball players have asked Major League Baseball Commissioner Bud Selig to move Tuesday’s 2011 All-Star Game out of Arizona. He chose not to listen and now I choose not to watch.

Some players—quite some time ago—said that they would refuse a trip to Phoenix as a statement of solidarity with the Hispanic population, the target of the anti-immigrant law. Among them were Adrian Gonzalez and Heath Bell. At the time of their announcement they were teammates in San Diego. Now Gonzalez is playing with Boston and leading the American League in batting. Both Gonzalez and Bell were selected for the All Star game. Yet, last night there was Gonzalez participating in the home run derby and Bell chumming it up with ESPN panelists.

Since the law’s enactment groups have filed legal challenges. Indeed, the US Department of Justice sought an injunction. Just before the law was to take effect, a court blocked its implementation. Arizona has been unable to overcome the court’s decision for the most part, although parts of the law are still in effect.

The players association initially condemned the law, but has since retreated. Gonzalez, perhaps the most outspoken of the players last year, has evidently changed his mind. A few civil rights groups have indicated that they will protest outside the stadium tonight.

In the months following Arizona’s law other states have passed even worse pieces of anti-immigrant legislation. Alabama, always known for its dedication to civil rights, has the most egregious such law in the land. Evidently that state’s legislators weren’t at all rattled by threatened boycotts.
