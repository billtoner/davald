The one percent

April 12, 2011 by davald

Joseph Stiglitz provides a nice overview of the current state of inequality in America. He begins with some provocative words:

Americans have been watching protests against oppressive regimes that concentrate massive wealth in the hands of an elite few. Yet in our own democracy, 1 percent of the people take nearly a quarter of the nation’s income—an inequality even the wealthy will come to regret.

But will they?

While Congress has always been beholden to wealthy interests, today it has been completely captured by the One Percent, who also happen to control 40 percent of the nation’s wealthy. Indeed, Stiglitz tells us, Congress itself is populated by members of the One Percent. This would suggest that we cannot rely on Congress to do our bidding. What about us? Stiglitz opines:

Some people look at income inequality and shrug their shoulders. So what if this person gains and that person loses? What matters, they argue, is not how the pie is divided but the size of the pie. That argument is fundamentally wrong. An economy in which most citizens are doing worse year after year—an economy like America’s—is not likely to do well over the long haul.

Stiglitz then offers several reasons why this is so. Most important is that the growing inequality has created two different societies: the One Percent and the Rest of Us. He concludes:

As we gaze out at the popular fervor in the streets, one question to ask ourselves is this: When will it come to America? In important ways, our own country has become like one of these distant, troubled places.

Alexis de Tocqueville once described what he saw as a chief part of the peculiar genius of American society—something he called “self-interest properly understood.” The last two words were the key. Everyone possesses self-interest in a narrow sense: I want what’s good for me right now! Self-interest “properly understood” is different. It means appreciating that paying attention to everyone else’s self-interest—in other words, the common welfare—is in fact a precondition for one’s own ultimate well-being. Tocqueville was not suggesting that there was anything noble or idealistic about this outlook—in fact, he was suggesting the opposite. It was a mark of American pragmatism. Those canny Americans understood a basic fact: looking out for the other guy isn’t just good for the soul—it’s good for business.

The top 1 percent have the best houses, the best educations, the best doctors, and the best lifestyles, but there is one thing that money doesn’t seem to have bought: an understanding that their fate is bound up with how the other 99 percent live. Throughout history, this is something that the top 1 percent eventually do learn. Too late.

Here we go again

April 11, 2011 by davald

We faithful Mariners fans are admonished to be patient with the current bunch. After last year’s awful season, the club is in a “rebuilding mode,” which is a euphemism for crappy. So far, the M’s have not disappointed; they are playing terribly, and not having such a good time of it.

What is it about management obsessed with conversion? Last year, long-time third baseman Chone Figgins switched roles with long-time second baseman Jose Lopez. It didn’t work. Lopez was traded and Figgins is now back at third, although he’s still hitting like a third grader.

This year new manager Eric Wedge ordered all-star shortstop Jack Wilson to second. The club acquired Brendan Ryan from the Cardinals. He’s an admitted head case with an anemic bat but possesses a superb glove. He would replace Wilson at short. Wilson didn’t like it one bit. Nevertheless, he swallowed his pride and worked hard at turning the double play from his new position.

When Wilson committed two egregious errors in one inning, he evidently took himself out, embarrassed and disheartened. Wedge, attempting to provide cover for his errant second baseman, told the press that Wilson was “hazy,” having been knocked to the ground by a sliding opponent. However, Wilson told the press that the manager had removed him. This understandably ticked off Wedge, who described Wilson’s actions as “unspeakable.”

Wilson is still in the doghouse, having ridden the bench consecutive games. But he leads the club in hitting, which admittedly isn’t saying all that much. His replacement at short, Ryan, is batting a frigid .143—190 points behind Wilson.

The Mariners also acquired veteran second baseman Adam Kennedy, whom I’ve always liked. He replaced Wilson at second. He, too, is hitting .333.

So, if I were manager I would reinstall the better-hitting Wilson at short, keep Kennedy at second, and consign Ryan to the bench. Yet, one couldn’t stop there and expect the club to improve. There are miles to go before we’ll see that, and those miles are the opposing players circling the bases.

Waxing sacred—for a moment

April 11, 2011 by davald

In reading Alex Ross’s latest essay in the New Yorker I was struck by one of his comments.

Recently, I listened to around fifty of the cantatas during a thousand-mile drive in inland Australia, and, far from getting too much of a good thing, I found myself regularly hitting the repeat button. Once or twice, I stopped on the side of the road in tears.

Ross was listening to Bach compositions. They are sublimely beautiful.

Indeed, I find many passages in the classical genre that stop me in my tracks. For example: the closing bars of the first movement of Berlioz’s Symphonie Fantastique; a portion of the final movement of Beethoven’s Ninth; the last minute or so of the first movement of Saint-Saens’s Third Violin Concerto; and old Latin hymns from my Catholic youth. This brief list is hardly exhaustive.

Ross recommends that his readers acquire John Eliot Gardner’s collection of Bach’s sacred music, which includes his cantatas and the passions of saints Matthew and John. The operative term here is ‘sacred.’

Some of us find the divine, or sacred, in ordinary lives. I have a different take.

It seems to me that artists of previous periods, both visual and musical, sought to create sacred images and sounds. I suspect that they did so in part to provide a contrast between the muck and mayhem of the human condition and some notion of perfection—namely, God. In fashioning the sounds and images, the sky was literally the limit; to be “on high” was a metaphor for heaven, the venue of saints and angels. Cathedrals reached for the sky, as did the eyes of the painters’ subjects.

The British philosopher Thomas Hobbes spoke of “the life of man, solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short.” How depressing. While Hobbes offered the state to impose order and certainty, there was nothing sacred about his sovereign government. Only the church—with its sublime architecture, divine paintings, and out-of-this world music—provided relief and hope in otherwise dismal conditions.

Authoritarian states have tried to suppress religious expression, with little success. There would seem to be a human need to find solace in decidedly non-human images and sounds.

Writing in Vanity Fair Christopher Hitchens, an avowed atheist, proclaims the King James version of the bible revolutionary: the king “saved God.” The words of scripture would no longer be the exclusive province of those facile in Latin and Greek. The new bible would be in English, the vernacular of British subjects.

But I wonder.

Bringing God to the people gave rise to proliferating preachers of myriad style and ability. Religion, like just about everything else, has become a commodity. And, I think, misses the point.

To serve as respite and hope for ordinary lives, God must be divine, insuperable, and ineffable. The further removed from human society the better. I doubt, then, that we crave a god of the muck.

Joan Osborne demurred:

What if God was one of us
Just a slob like one of us
Just a stranger on the bus
Trying to make his way home

I say, get your ass back to heaven.

Just to see what the other side is up to

April 7, 2011 by davald

My long-departed sister used to watch the televangelists. Since we were both lapsed Catholics and well-nigh atheists by then (this would have been in the late 70s or early 80s; she passed away in 1987), I wondered why. She told me that it was important “to see what the other side was up to.”

She was prescient, as the Christian right mushroomed into a potent political force. They’re still at it, of course.

I was reminded of this bit of biography on the announcement that Glenn Beck will be severing ties with Fox News. In one article, a person was quoted as saying that many people watch Beck “because they hate him,” or perhaps just to see what he’s up to.

I never saw or will see a Beck show. However, when I was sent a parody of him by Jon Stewart, I didn’t understand the allusion or why his audience was laughing so loud. I then caught a YouTube clip of one of Beck’s chalkboard performances. Funny, but not intentionally so. He’s deadly serious and evidently scared shitless of dark people, Democrats, and socialists.

While Beck’s show will end, he vows to continue with something. I’m sure that Mr. Stewart is relieved, as will be several million Beck followers.

Can’t say that this is a good thing. But being an American means, at least, that one is free to be loony.

Liberty is so beguiling.

Would you buy a used car from Mr. Ryan?

April 6, 2011 by davald

Washington is all a buzz over Rep. Paul Ryan’s “courageous” (David Brooks) budget presentation. His budget would make a magician blush. Get this. He’d lower taxes even more on the rich while slashing “entitlements,” which, abracadabra, yields historically low unemployment rates and dramatic GDP growth. Too much to believe?

Both Paul Krugman and Dean Baker, to name two, have thoroughly desiccated Mr. Ryan’s wet dream. Baker makes a special point to expose the nutty numbers regarding the representative’s plan for Medicare vouchers.

Representative Ryan Proposes Medicare Plan Under Which Seniors Would Pay Most of Their Income for Health Care

That’s the appropriate headline, Baker avers, for Ryan’s proposal. Baker cites the Congressional Budget Office analysis, which concludes that seniors would wind up paying almost all of their Social Security income on medical expenses.

Using the CBO assumption of 2.5 percent annual inflation, the voucher would have grown to $9,750 by 2030. This means that a Medicare type plan for someone age 65 would be $30,460 under Representative Ryan’s plan, leaving seniors with a bill of $20,700. (This does not count various out of pocket medical expenditures not covered by Medicare.)

Mr. Ryan is certainly counting on good old American gullibility to sell his lemon of a budget plan. Judging by last year’s election results, he has reason to be optimistic.

Danny Westneat wants it both ways

April 6, 2011 by davald

Doing his best Cotton Mather imitation, the Seattle Times‘s columnist Danny Westneat scolds readers for complaining about the University of Washington’s decision to limit in-state positions for its incoming freshmen classes. Not even a perfect 4.0 GPA entitles admission these days. Worse, for would-be UW parents, the university is increasing the number of seats for out-of-state applicants, for the simple reason that they pay nearly three times the tuition as state residents do.

Westneat titles his column “UW gives us what we asked for.” He writes:

A father of a girl with a 3.8 GPA wrote to me: “Reading about the UW’s preference for out-of-state students made me very angry. How can our university, funded with our taxes, simply go elsewhere to get its students?”

Because it is doing what we forced it to do? Which was to act more like a business?

I’m sorry to be flip. But for decades now we’ve heard the demand that government needs to be more like business. Can’t it be more self-sufficient, more attuned to the bottom line?

Well, yes it can. This is what it looks like.

He then chides the critics of the new UW policy for being hypocrites, in effect, objecting to taxes and fees to close the revenue gap while demanding more public services, including government subsidies for higher education.

People seem really hacked off about this. But it’s exactly what we told them to do. Make do with less.

Sheesh, we weren’t even willing to go along with a temporary, 2-cents-per-can tax on soda pop last year. Now we’re going to get all indignant when the UW looks to the other 49 states because we’re too cheap?

What Westneat omits from his broadside is that he, himself, devoted an entire column last year to the defeat of Initiative 1098, which would have established a state income tax on wealthy citizens. Initiative sponsors, including Bill Gates, Sr., said that the revenues raised from the new tax would directly fund education, including the public universities. Last October Westneat wrote:

This [initiative] does make me squirm. Even though, like Gates Sr., I think the uber-rich should pay more. But shouldn’t the rest of us also pay … something?

Mr. Westneat hasn’t been paying attention. It was none other than Warren Buffet, the second richest man in America, who also squirmed because his secretary was paying a higher tax on her income than Buffet was paying on his billions. He thought that unfair. It shouldn’t have been all that difficult for Mr. Westneat to Google ‘comparison regressive tax structures 50 states’ to find at the top of the searches this study (pdf). Where does Washington rank on the regressive tax list? Yep. We’re Number One.

Initiative 1098, by the way, made clear its purpose: “Seventy percent of the net revenues received by the trust must be deposited into the education legacy trust account and used exclusively for the purposes of that account.”

Also, Westneat also urged readers to vote no on the initiative to impose a 2-cent tax on soda.

So, why should I even bother to read one of the biggest hypocrites of them all?

More on the crumbling empire

April 4, 2011 by davald

E.J. Dionne takes on the GOP’s initiative to extirpate social service programs while transferring more wealth from most of us to the already privileged few. He hopes that Rep. Paul Ryan (R-Wis.) will be as transparent in presenting his budget-slashing proposals as they are draconian in scope. Once the Republicans have revealed to all the world what they are really about, the rest of us, it is further hoped, will finally awaken to the stakes at hand, which Dionne believes is the end of progressive government—although I seriously doubt that we ever had one.

I have this hypothesis: to the extent that the American people swallow this crap, the closer we move toward fascism. And I hasten to add that we’re pretty darn close already.

Legend has it that Emperor Nero fiddled while Rome burned. None of us know how to play the violin (or the piano, or the cello, or the flute, etc.), so we won’t be fiddling as the nation declines. Rather, we’ve abandoned artful pursuits for the trivial and banal.

Meanwhile, about the guy who said, “Yes, we can.”

And you wonder: Will President Obama welcome the responsibility of engaging the country in this big argument, or will he shrink from it? Will his political advisers remain robotically obsessed with poll results about the 2012 election, or will they embrace Obama’s historic obligation — and opportunity — to win the most important struggle over the role of government since the New Deal?

The audacity of modal logic

April 4, 2011 by davald

It was 1971. I was sitting in the back, which was my custom, observing Prof. McKinnon lecturing on some aspect of The Philosophy of Science. He had occasion to scribble some symbols on the board. At one point he paused, as if stumped. He looked at me across the rows of students for affirmation. Was this right?, he seemed to gesture. When I nodded, he expressed relief.

The professor had written a few elements of modal logic, with which he knew I was familiar. This subtle exchange was nevertheless perceived by my classmates. For that instant my head grew a bit larger than normal.

A far more clever person than I also had an interest in modal logic. His name was David Foster Wallace. It was the stuff of his senior thesis in philosophy, which received a university prize. Unlike me, Wallace actually had talent in the discipline. He combined it with intense passion, whereas I was merely biding time.

I commend this Boston Review article. Here you will gain some appreciation for that talent and intensity.

Note: I obtained a B.A. in History from Berkeley in 1969. I then moved on to California State University at Hayward to study philosophy.

Higher education: a huge disconnect

April 4, 2011 by davald

President Obama and his education henchman, Arne Duncan, tout the benefits of achieving a college degree. Moreover, they talk ad nauseam about their fervent belief that all children can—and should—attain a baccalaureate. That so many can’t or don’t is the fault of public education, which, they claim, must be reformed, beginning with jettisoning “bad” teachers and rewarding the “good.”

But a sobering article in the Seattle Times throws an ocean of cold water on Obama’s grand prescription for what ails the nation. Even a perfect high school GPA with high SAT test scores does not guarantee admission to the University of Washington. Worse, because of the state’s fiscal squeeze, UW administrators have opened up more seats to out-of-state applicants. Why? They pay three times the tuition as in-state students.

A series of worsening revenue forecasts and a $5 billion state budget shortfall have made it even more likely that the Legislature will again slash higher-education funding this year. So in February, top academic leaders at the UW made a painful decision to cut the number of Washington students the school will admit this fall to its main Seattle campus and increase the number of nonresident students, who pay nearly three times as much in tuition and fees. (Seattle Times)

The experience is hardly unique to Washington. Almost all state budgets are in distress. Lawmakers have shunned raising revenue through higher taxes. So they’re in a slashing frenzy, with education a principal target.

I graduated from the University of California at Berkeley way back in 1969. I received my degree in four years. Tuition, believe it or not, was zero. When I entered in the fall of 1965, I was charged $60 a semester for “incidental fees.” By the time I left, I was paying $60 $100 a quarter, or $180 $300 for the last year. Also, the state had an implicit contract with its young residents: achieve a 3.0 GPA or higher, and welcome to one of the UC campuses.

Since Ronald Reagan took over as governor, combined with the passage of Proposition 13 (which froze property taxes and thereby cut state and local revenues), California as a whole has been on a path to hell. It’s taking the once-vaunted university system, considered the best in the world when I attended, along with it.

Several years ago I noticed that the average incoming high school GPA for Berkeley freshmen was at least 4.0. Indeed, to gain admission, one had to ace Advanced Placement classes so as to boost GPA above perfect. One also needed increasingly higher SAT scores.

That experience has moved north, now affecting Washington’s prime university.

Among the students rejected: Brandon Stover, a valedictorian at Chief Sealth High School, who has a 4.0 grade-point average.

“It was just a shock,” Stover said. “I don’t know exactly what the UW is looking for.”

Stover, who wants to go to the UW’s Foster School of Business, took a few rigorous International Baccalaureate classes at Sealth, but focused his efforts on the school’s Academy of Finance because those classes most closely dovetailed with his goal to major in business administration.

Because the West Seattle resident needs to live at home and work to make ends meet, he did not apply to other colleges. He’s appealing his rejection, but also is considering going to South Seattle Community College or UW-Bothell next fall, and then trying to transfer to the UW. (Seattle Times)

Imagine that. Mr. Stover has done all that Messrs. Obama and Duncan have asked him to do. He’s obviously studied hard in fashioning a stellar high school record. But it’s not enough.

The ugly truth, of course, is that the rationale for pushing education reform is bogus in the extreme. Even those relatively few students who do all the right things may not gain admission to public universities. And should they pass muster, they’ll likely end their tertiary careers with burdensome debt, along with declining prospects for employment.

But if not college, what?

There are two trajectories at play. One steeply climbs, reserved only for a select few. Should there be one slip, the fall is far and painful. One lands on the second trajectory, which trends downward into the bowels of the poorly paid service economy, inhabited by burger-flippers, toilet-clearners, and bed-makers. The only spot of good news in the underworld is the lack of college debt.

Timing, they say, is everything. There is no way I would have gained admission to Cal given current circumstances. I was fortunate, because I could throw a baseball pretty well, earning a scholarship of sorts and an athletic exemption for admission. Yep. My high school academic record was mediocre. But once in, I survived. Today, that option would be closed to me. A victim of California’s fiscal mess is Cal’s baseball team, which is fielding its last squad after more than a century of operation.

The empire is crumbling. Pass it on.

The stimulus conundrum

April 2, 2011 by davald

President Obama sold his stimulus package, which Congress passed with the acronym ARRA, in the Keynesian manner. Only federal government spending, preferably on “shovel-ready” projects, could increase aggregate demand, given the uncertainty of the recessionary economy. Republicans, as is their wont, warned of further deficits and impending inflation, both ideological bugaboos of the GOP. To woo congressional conservatives, the final Obama proposal included substantial tax cuts, which is the Republican answer to any problem.

Now that the extra spending is about to run dry, the opposition party deems the Obama package “a failure.” Critics more to the left, including Paul Krugman, repeatedly argue that, of course, ARRA is a failure, because it wasn’t really a stimulus package at all—it was much too small to have appreciable effect.

In today’s New York Times, reporter Jackie Calmes tells us that Obama is touting the new employment numbers. The official unemployment rate has fallen to 8.8 percent, down from a whopping 10.1 percent in the fall of 2009. Why the decline? Well, it was his stimulus package. It’s not failing, it’s working. I say it’s premature, to say the least.

The dip in the unemployment rate may be temporary.

“While today’s jobs numbers are headed in the right direction, most Americans believe the economy is still pretty seriously off-track, making the jobs issue still a challenging one for the president,” said Neil Newhouse, a Republican pollster. “Looming on the nation’s economic horizon is the after-effects of the Japanese tsunami and the Middle East unrest, hardly the kind of stable economic environment needed for significant U.S. job growth.”

The big challenge in trying to divine economic cause and effect is to ferret out the signal from the noise. Given the very size and complexity of the economy, which is clearly global in scope, confounding factors should give economists pause. In particular, whether or not the stimulus package worked or is working or will eventually work may be the proper stuff of historians. But even then we’ve got a problem.

After all, economic historians are still arguing about the causes of the Great Depression. They’re even more divided on what caused the recovery. Remember, the GD began, our textbooks tell us, with the October ’29 Crash. Some eight decades later, a consensus explanation to both remains elusive.

One thing does remain clear: the fortunes of incumbent presidents and their challengers alike depend on perceived trends in economic performance. If the people believe that conditions are improving, even though in absolute numbers they suck, the current resident of the Oval Office will likely gain re-election. If the curves are flat or sloping downward, expect to see a new president.

That’s why Obama was quoted in the article thusly:

“You should know that keeping the economy going and making sure jobs are available is the first thing I think about when I wake up in the morning,” Mr. Obama said. “It’s the last thing I think about when I go to bed each night.”

Amateur economist that I am, I suspect that we’re in for a prolonged period of stagnation. Why? Because real wages for most Americans have been stagnant or diminished since the 1970s. Since 70 percent of GDP is you and me and millions of other consumers spending money, we either need the incomes to sustain this behavior or go into debt. And we know how the latter worked out.

Yet, I am not optimistic about wages. We no longer build things in America, and manufacturing is the foundation of a healthy economy (“family-wage” jobs). The multinational corporations easily pit American workers against cheaper counterparts across the globe. Attempts to boost wages here are rebuffed by outsourcing. The heads of these corporations are all about profits, which makes them indifferent to who’s doing the labor, provided it’s cheap.

We’ll see, of course. But I wouldn’t hold my breath expecting an American renaissance any time soon.
