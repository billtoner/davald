Crystal ball: 10 reasons that Apple will conquer

August 24, 2011 by davald

Now that HP is getting out of the PC business and has abandoned its tablet and WebOS coupled with Acer losing money on its own PC business and mediocre tablet initiatives, what happens to Apple? Could the world’s second most valuable company (first for a while) keep on truckin’? I say yes, and here’s why.

First, Apple is entering the Chinese market in a big way, opening up stores and signing agreements with telecoms there. China is now the world’s largest electronics market.

Second, Apple will be selling its new iPhone 5 via Sprint and T-Mobile. So the device will be available to the nation’s four biggest mobile networks. Recall that Apple started with AT&T on an exclusive basis.

Third, the new iPhone will be even better than the current iPhone 4 and likely be sold at the same retail price. And the iPhone 4 is the world’s most popular smartphone. (I’d add that it’s so far immune to malware, which is not the case with rival Android phones. This makes the Apple product more attractive to businesses.)

Fourth, the iPad 3, slated to ship early next year, will have a faster processor and a higher quality display (can you say retina?) than the iPad 2. Apple has cornered over 90 percent of the tablet market. With an even better product at the same price, and given the problems with would-be competitors’ devices, Apple will continue to sell multiple millions of iPads.

Fifth, no one has come close to replicating Apple’s app store, either in volume or in ease of use.

Sixth, Apple’s bricks-and-mortar stores are the envy of the entire retail world. It leads all retailers in dollars per square foot. And have you been in an Apple store lately? They’re packed and full of energy, and customers actually leave the stores with product in their hands. Apple continues to add new stores every week across the globe.

Seventh, Steve Jobs’s insistance on wedding hardware and software, long maligned in the industry, has proven to be a very winning strategy. As he says, “it just works.” There’s no “crapware.” There’s no fragmentation, as is the case with Android and Windows devices.

Eighth, with the release of iOS 5 next month, Apple devices will work even better and easier. I’m talking about iCloud and automatic, instant syncing of just about everything you do on the iMac, the iPhone, and the iPad. Take a picture, it’s pushed out to all your devices. Your music will be on iCloud and instantly available to all your devices. Write a letter or crunch some numbers on a spreadsheet—boom!—the file instantly appears everywhere, in all its versions.

Ninth, forget about a camera. The new iPhone will be the best you have because it’s always with you. Rumor has it that the new iPhone 5 will be equipped with an 8 megapixel lens, up from the current 5. Need to snap a photo or record a video? Use your iPhone. No need to carry around a camera.

Tenth, Apple has begun to make serious inroads into the enterprise market, and it’s not because Apple is actively selling to businesses. Rather, Apple products have become Trojan Horses; employees are asking if not demanding that their companies embrace the iPhone, the iPad, and even the iMac. Now that HP, the world’s largest PC seller, is getting out of that business, this widens Apple’s opening.

I’d add another factor. Let’s call it ‘cool.’ The marriage of design, materials, and engineering yields the best products. The new Mac Book Air, for example, as been called “the perfect computer.” The 13-inch model is already in short supply as demand skyrockets for the device. It’s fast, beautiful, and, of course, it just works.

UPDATE (Aug. 24, 2011):

OMG. Jobs just announced his resignation as CEO, effective immediately. He’s staying on as board chairman. Tim Cook will likely succeed him. My crystal ball tells me that Apple’s stock will fall sharply tomorrow. However, there’s too much good stuff going on in the company to detract from its progress. The stock price will rebound, for the reasons I mentioned above.

That said, I wish Steve Jobs all the best, although this is not a good sign, however much we expected the news.

Fallacy of composition

August 24, 2011 by davald

Let’s suppose that as a result of the Great Recession you find yourself in debt with little savings. This is not hard to imagine, as a general consequence of the housing bubble collapse people lost wealth in their homes, which they had relied upon to buy things rather than save. So, what do you do?

A now-frugal individual would probably try to do two things: pay off his debt then start saving money. Three cheers for him. But what happens if a whole lot of people behave in the same manner?

If one does either or both of these two activities, spending on goods and services—almost by definition—declines. Then what?

Should too many of us stop buying things, companies are forced to cut back on production. After all, why build more widgets if no one is going to buy them? If production falls, then the company needs fewer workers. The now unemployed workers have no wages, and they probably lack savings, as we mentioned above. They may receive unemployment benefits, but those are less than the wage they were earning at their jobs, and at some point the benefits run out. To be sure, the unemployed worker is probably spending all those benefits on necessities: rent, food, etc. But he’s surely not buying a new car or a new refrigerator or a vacation in Hawaii. It’s doubtful that he can save much, because he’s not bringing home as much as he was while working.

We can see that a whole lot of money is now being withdrawn from the economy in a massive, vicious cycle. Lower incomes lead to less buying, which reduces production, which increases unemployment—and so on.

The problem here is a prime example of the fallacy of composition. That is, what applies to or is suitable for the individual is not necessarily good for all individuals as a whole.

John Maynard Keynes invoked “the paradox of thrift.” In recessionary periods, especially, if everyone saves, no one saves. That’s because increased savings means reduced spending, which lowers income (the vicious cycle), which forces people to use their savings to pay bills. What’s good for one is bad for all.

On the international sphere we hear well-meaning political leaders calling for Spain, Italy, and just about every other Euro nation to act just like Germany. That won’t work, because of the fallacy of composition. Not every nation can have a positive account balance, where they’re exporting more than importing. Some nations have to import the exports.

In the United States the dominant theme is austerity: We all have to tighten our belts, and if the people have to do it than so should the government.  Reducing the nation’s debt now, while the people practice thrift, can only exacerbate our misery and prolong the tiny recovery.

Nearly all Republicans actively preach austerity and ignore the fallacy of composition. Should they prevail in next year’s elections…well, you can write your own narrative.

Libya: a good thing, then what?

August 24, 2011 by davald

I must confess that I haven’t paid that close attention to events in Libya. The rebels seem to have succeeded in gaining control, though Quaddafi’s whereabouts are unknown. I have no affection for dictators, especially brutal ones.

If the overthrow succeeds, what follows? Many of us, I suspect, will try to view events through the prism of our own history. We defeated the British, broke the yoke of King George, then wrote a constitution, which still controls. But the Libyans may have different notions about democracy and governance. So be it.

The American ruling class, of course, will be fixated on oil, and Libya has a lot of it. As long as the stuff keeps flowing our way, the form of Libyan government that emerges may be of no matter.

Stay tuned.

A few comments about pitching

August 23, 2011 by davald

In one of my previous lives I pitched baseballs. And when I wasn’t physically throwing a baseball I was practicing before a large mirror we had in the living room. I’d also watch as many baseball games as possible, paying particular attention to the style and delivery of the pitchers. For me, baseball was all about the contest between the batter and the pitcher, and I always rooted for the latter. Even now, when I’m watching games on TV, I study the pitcher.

I just got around to finishing Michael Lewis’s Moneyball, wherein he chronicles the application of statistics to the evaluation of baseball players. He focused on the Oakland Athletics, who played ball just a short drive from his Berkeley home. His fascinating book is all about the A’s’ general manager, Billy Bean, and his entourage of nerds. Their joint task: locate the diamonds in the rough; players that contributed more to the outcome of games than would be obvious to even seasoned veterans. It was all in the numbers.

On offense, the goal is to score the most runs. The defense, of course, is supposed to allow the fewest runs. So, what metrics play the most important roles in accomplishing both objectives? That’s what Billy and his small team of number-crunchers figured out. Unlike the huge markets of the Yankees and the Red Sox, who could afford to pay lavish salaries for the best conventionally evaluated talent, the A’s were a rather sorry bunch. Attendance was always mediocre, even during the string of three consecutive world championships. Bean et al. had to produce a winning ball club with a very limited payroll.

The players Bean assembled on the field would not impress. There were no obvious stars like a Barry Bonds or a Greg Maddux. They may not have been fast afoot, powerful at the plate, or possessed of a blazing fastball. But as a team, they were collective overachievers. Bean managed to win ball games, despite a lack of superstars and a New York bank account.

Since I’m most interested in pitching, I wondered what would be the key performance metrics by which to assess a pitcher’s ability. The usual stats are ERA (earned run average), wins-vs-losses, and, more recently, walks and hits per inning pitched (WHIP). But there are confounding variables that influence these measurements. Can the defense catch and throw the baseball? As we Mariner fans know, winning games depends a great deal on how well your offense can score runs. (To their credit, the folks responsible for selecting the Cy Young Award winner looked beyond this metric to give the prize last year to Felix Hernandez.)

It seems to me that there are three stats over which the pitcher has the most control. These are: walks, home runs, and strike outs. If a batted ball clears the fence, the defense could not have prevented it. Even factoring in the variations in umpires’ strike zones, over a season such preferences would tend to be neutralized. Thus, bases on balls and strike outs were mostly about the pitcher.

I came up with a simple formula. Opponents score runs only after rounding the bases then touching the plate. A walk puts a batter a fourth of the way to home. But a home run puts a number on the board. It’s worth four walks, then. If the pitcher strikes out a batter, that out is all about the pitcher; he’s prevented the hitter from reaching base while racking up one of the three outs necessary to complete an inning.

As it happens, Major League Baseball provides us with a trove of statistics on its website. I copied a page of stats to a spreadsheet, then added one more column to reflect the aforementioned formula.* I then sorted the pitchers by this last statistic. (Note: I did this a couple of weeks ago.)

We should not be surprised to see Roy Halladay at the top of the list. When I performed the calculation, he had tossed 175+ innings and given up only nine home runs while walking just 22 and striking out 163. I was surprised, however, to see Brandon Morrow in fifth place. His record has been spotty to date, with a high ERA. But as we look more closely at the key metrics, we see that he’d allowed just nine home runs while striking out 139 in just under 120 innings pitched.

There are some diamonds in this list, Morrow one of them, as it turns out. They do not command, yet, the wallets of Halladay, Lee, and Sabathia.

Billy Bean, at least in the Moneyball years, acquired these statistical anomalies and paid them low salaries. When their contracts were due to expire and others noticed how well they were doing, Bean would trade or sell them—rather than pay them their new market-based wages. That was by default, I guess, since he never had the money.

___________

*  WHRSOIP for [BB+ (4*HR)-SO]/IP

Retaliation?

August 23, 2011 by davald

Boeing has moved some of its Dreamliner work to South Carolina, whose governor has stated that she doesn’t like unions. The National Labor Relations Board, NLRB, filed a complaint against Boeing, charging the company with retaliating against its workers’ union. Fox News, predictably, bashed both the NLRB and the Obama administration for squelching jobs and punishing a U.S. manufacturer. (Boeing has actually added workers in both Puget Sound and South Carolina.)

Today, the New York Times Joe Nocera issued a broadside against Democrats. He writes:

Seriously, when has a government agency ever tried to dictate where a company makes its products? I can’t ever remember it happening. Neither can Boeing, which is fighting the complaint. J. Michael Luttig, Boeing’s general counsel, has described the action as “unprecedented.” He has also said that it was a disservice to a country that is “in desperate need of economic growth and the concomitant job creation.” He’s right.

Media Matters did a little research into the allegations. They write:

Labor Law Professor Secunda: Complaint Against Boeing Shows The NLRB Is “Carrying Out Its Congressionally Mandated Mission To Protect The Right Of Workers To Engage In Concerted Activity.” From a Seattle Times editorial by Marquette University Law School associate professor Paul Secunda:

The National Labor Relations Act gives workers the unequivocal right to engage in concerted activity — including the right to strike. Boeing stated publicly that it was moving production away from Washington because its workers there previously went on strike and could go on strike again in the future.

Such comments amount to an admission from the company that it was intentionally retaliating against employees and trying to limit their rights — a clear affront to the law that the NLRB is charged with enforcing.

At the end of the day, what we are seeing is the agency carrying out its congressionally mandated mission to protect the right of workers to engage in concerted activity for mutual aid and protection. The agency is simply enforcing the law, providing balance and fairness for workers and businesses alike. [The Seattle Times, 4/29/11]

Mr. Nocera’s publisher, the New York Times, carried this editorial on the NLRB complaint. They wrote:

The N.L.R.B.’s case rests on statements by Boeing officials that, it believes, prove retaliation. One Boeing executive told The Seattle Times that the main reason to put the new line in South Carolina was “that we cannot afford to have a work stoppage, you know, every three years.”

The paper’s editors, by the way, called the NLRB complaint “a welcome effort to defend workers’ right to collective bargaining.” I concur.

UPDATE (Aug. 23, 2011):

Economist Dean Baker takes on Nocera here. He writes:

The question in this case is whether the move to South Carolina is part of a threat against its unions. This involves an examination of the record of negotiations and discussions between management and Boeing’s unions. Without knowing this history, it is not possible to make an apriori assumption that the NLRB case has no merit, as Nocera does in his column.

The perfect crossover

August 22, 2011 by davald

I loved watching Tim Hardaway leave opponents sitting on their butts after he’d performed one of his superlative crossovers. None better, I’d say. Then Hardaway did the unpardonable. He told a radio audience that he “hated gays.” Whoa.

But look who’s evidently changed his mind? Dave Zirin writes about it here for Sports Illustrated. 

As for Hardaway, he told the media in El Paso that he was a different person. “My family and friends came to me and were like, ‘What are you doing?'” Hardaway said of his comments four years ago. “I talked to them and they made me understand that wasn’t right.”

“It’s not right to not let the gays and lesbians have equal rights here,” Hardaway told the crowd. “If I know El Paso, like they came together when the 1966 team won a championship and Don Haskins started those five [black] guys, I know the city will grow and understand that gays and lesbians need equal rights.”

Dave Zirin concludes: “This was truly Hardaway’s best crossover.”

Predictive theories

August 22, 2011 by davald

Milton Friedman, of all people, argued that the value of an economic theory lay in its ability to predict human behavior. That is, what would people, in the aggregate, likely do under particular circumstances. This is somewhat ironic to the extent that some of Friedman’s own theories fail to account for what the heck is going on today.

Today, of course, we’re in a mess, arguing over what, if anything, can and should be done about it. One intrepid economist, whom I often cite, appears to have been prescient about not only the diagnosis but also the cure.

Paul Krugman was recently asked by a few readers whether he had anticipated the protracted economic stagnation. In particular, did he express concerns over the efficacy of Obama’s stimulus package. He linked to three separate columns he penned for the New York Times.

The first prior assessment was made in early January 2009, a couple of weeks before Obama assumed office. He wrote:

But Mr. Obama’s prescription doesn’t live up to his diagnosis. The economic plan he’s offering isn’t as strong as his language about the economic threat. In fact, it falls well short of what’s needed.

Bear in mind just how big the U.S. economy is. Given sufficient demand for its output, America would produce more than $30 trillion worth of goods and services over the next two years. But with both consumer spending and business investment plunging, a huge gap is opening up between what the American economy can produce and what it’s able to sell.

Krugman believed that the stimulus package was only a third of what was needed to close the output gap. That is, instead of $800 billion (nearly half of which was in the form of tax cuts, which are not all that stimulative), Obama should have proposed and Congress should have adopted a measure of almost $2.5 trillion. Even then, it seems, despite Democrats controlling both houses, Obama was seeking a pragmatic, bipartisan solution. He and his political advisors thought that any package had to be less than a trillion dollars and much of it had to be tax cuts in order to woo Republicans.

His second and third previous columns are more of the same, although he ups the amount the stimulus should have been to almost $3 trillion.

But there was one point he made that caught my attention back then, in early 2009. Krugman wrote:

So here’s the picture that scares me: It’s September 2009, the unemployment rate has passed 9 percent, and despite the early round of stimulus spending it’s still headed up. Mr. Obama finally concedes that a bigger stimulus is needed.

But he can’t get his new plan through Congress because approval for his economic policies has plummeted, partly because his policies are seen to have failed, partly because job-creation policies are conflated in the public mind with deeply unpopular bank bailouts. And as a result, the recession rages on, unchecked.

O.K., that’s a warning, not a prediction. But economic policy is falling behind the curve, and there’s a real, growing danger that it will never catch up.

Well, that’s precisely what has happened, although Obama has never come forward to propose further stimulus, especially after the Republicans “shellacked” the Democrats in 2010. Worse, Obama sold the actual package on the promise that it would reduce unemployment and spur the economy.

He’s cooked his own goose with several different recipes.

In praise of cities

August 22, 2011 by davald

After spending a majority of our lives in the suburbs, my wife and I moved downtown. No, it’s not Paris or Manhattan. It’s Everett, barely a bump on the urban scale.

We had grown weary of having to drive cars everywhere and spending weekends pulling the damn weeds. Besides, who needs all that space?

Now we walk: I to work; both of us to restaurants, the library, to the occasional Aqua Sox game. No more weeds.

Scientific American devotes a special issue to the city—the good, the bad, a bit of the ugly. But more important, argues the magazine, cities could be so much more, if they were given a level playing field.

In matters of housing, education,transportation, the environment and social services, existing rules and spending priorities give cities a raw deal. Cheap gas, highway subsides, tax incentives for home ownership, complacency over urban education and the apportionment of legislators all give preferential treatment to suburbs and rural areas. Even national leaders who should be cheerleaders for an evenhanded urban policy have faltered. Barack Obama, the most urban president since Theodore Roosevelt, skewed the stimulus bill toward more dollars for rural America. The five least populated states got twice as much money per capita as the rest.

The magazine’s editors believe that cities must be our future, because sprawl is unsustainable, both economically and environmentally. They write:

The basic issue is fairness. Why should government policy favor owning over renting, driving over mass transit, or kids in one school district over another? The current incentives encourage people to settle in the outskirts when they might otherwise prefer to live downtown—a bias that makes little sense even when you leave out its environmental costs. And those costs are enormous. To keep our carbon emissions in check, we will need to edge closer to our neighbors. From the perspective both of simple fairness and of rational, science-based public policy, eliminating the incentives for citizens to spread out should be our goal.

Some more inconvenient facts

August 20, 2011 by davald

It has become the standard mantra of conservatives that the economy is in the doldrums because of excessive federal spending. Hewing to this line serves as a narrative glue binding the different shades of the GOP, twinned with a pledge not to raise taxes, even should the ratio of cuts to revenues exceed 10-to-1.

Now no one ever accused Republicans of letting facts get in their way. Indeed, they can yelp whoopers ’til the frogs cease to croak, then simply ignore or slough off the gaffes should a proximate sentient being mention the error. However, the Rest of Us should not shirk from our responsibility to record the blunders and, if possible, dissuade the weak from following the idiots down the dark holes of ignorance.

About that spending (chart from the St. Louis Fed):

 

Writing for ProPublica Michael Grabell does more service to our cause (how presumptuous) by dispelling several myths that are also part of the GOP’s narrative glue. He throws a few glancing blows at mainstream Democrats on the stimulus’ effects, for balance’s sake, I guess.

But I must take issue with his comment on the belief that the stimulus should have been bigger. He calls this “a red herring.” Well, no. There are many competent economists who argue that, given the size of the housing bubble collapse, among other factors, the stimulus really should have been much larger. I emphasize ‘should,’ which is different from ‘could.’ Yes, the politics were horrible, and it’s clear that Obama refused to accept the advice of his own economic advisors, Christine Romer being one, to push a larger stimulus package. So, it’s doubtful that a divided Congress would have acceded, as Grabell points out. That does not detract from the argument that the package should have been larger.

It’s become more obvious with each report on the dismal economy that Congress and Obama were wrong. I’d go further.

It was not only the puny size of the stimulus that failed to overcome the recessionary dynamics. It was also its limited duration.

Economic players, both consumers and producers, crave stability and predictability. Had Congress built in an indefinite stimulus, keyed to obvious metrics (e.g., the unemployment rate), the economy, I would argue, would be recovering much faster. Once growth returned, ease back on the stimulus. (Sweden does this.)

Yes, there is a time for austerity. Keynes said that it was appropriate during “boom” cycles, but hardly during recessionary periods, such as we’re in now. Austerity only makes matters worse.

In retrospect, at least, it was a humungous mistake to cut taxes, as Bush did. Launching two wars—without raising revenues to pay for them—added to the burden. The debt, which had been growing sharply since Reagan’s tax cuts, was being paid down during the Clinton administration and headed toward an actual surplus. (Graph from the Washington Post)

By the way, notice how little the stimulus (2009 Recovery Act) contributed to the problem. The tax cuts ($1.7 trillion) and the two wars ($1.3 trillion) are responsible for a huge chunk of the current deficits.

The solutions to our fiscal problems, as I’ve suggested previously, are relatively simple in concept, however difficult to achieve in this political cauldron:

  1. end the Bush-era tax cuts (better yet, get them back to the pre-Reagan days)
  2. end the wars
  3. invest in public infrastructure (e.g., fix dilapidated schools, non-automobile transportation sector)
  4. adopt Medicare for all
  5. eliminate sprawl-inducing subsidies (e.g., mortgage deductions, oil subsidies)

Piece of cake, I say.
