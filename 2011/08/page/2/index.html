The housing bubble

August 30, 2011 by davald

Economist Dean Baker, who blogs here, both identified the housing bubble and predicted its collapse, then estimating a drop in asset value of some $8 trillion. He was prescient, and now reminds just about anyone who would listen or read that we’re currently in such a mess because of the collapse.

The reasons are simple: with stagnating wages, people treated their homes like ATMs, allowing them to continue spending money on goods and services (representing 70 percent of GDP), while sharply dropping their savings. With home values plummeting, so has spending; the savings rate has risen, also. With reduced spending comes reduced industrial activity comes layoffs and wage freezes—and so on.

This recent housing bubble was the mother of all housing bubbles, as this graphic depicts. (The original chart prepared by the New York Times. It was updated—the red dotted line—by Steve Barry for The Big Picture.)

Some creative individuals have produced a clever animation. You’re on a roller coaster, following the curves of the Case-Shiller Index. Hold on tight.

Wishing for apples

August 30, 2011 by davald

Some time ago the Marysville School District jettisoned its Apple computers and switched to the dark side of cheap, largely unworkable, and frustratingly complicated Wintel machines. I predicted to a small body of citizens and staff that the district’s IT costs and personnel would mushroom immediately. As it happens, my prediction proved correct. Worse, now that the district is locked in to Redmond it is now decades behind in adopting newer, easier, and more robust technologies—the kind developed by Cupertino.

This past Sunday I accompanied my wife to her classroom in Marysville. Against the far wall were six Acer desktop machines all running Windows XP, a now-ancient operating system. Running from each computer were separate cables to connect the display, the mouse, and the keyboard. Another cord plugged into the electric socket. An Ethernet cable ran from the computer to an Ethernet hub, joined by the five other Ethernet cables. There was also a district-supplied laptop, designed to withstand an IED. It, too, had several cables connecting the laptop to a “doc-cam,” the Ethernet, and an electric socket. Did I say clutter?

Only two of the computers could connect to the Internet, including the laptop. I learned this after an eternity of waiting for the computers to come on. Did I say slow?

I didn’t bother to check the specs of the computers. Suffice it to say, they were vastly underpowered, no doubt possessing stingy quantities of RAM. With all computers on, the heat generated is almost unbearable. Did I say hot?

You may have read Froma Harrop’s recent column on Steve Jobs and Apple devices. She began by complaining about her previous PC experience—all bad; which drove her into the arms of Steve Jobs, figuratively speaking. She writes:

Apple has long worked under the solid assumption that consumers would pay more for sleek design, higher quality and superior service. Nothing it makes looks like it fell from a flying saucer.

Now let’s imagine my wife’s classroom equipped with all Apple products.

First off, there would be few cables. Instead, MacBook Airs and iPads would be connected wirelessly to the Internet, printers, district servers, and each other. You would find a large LCD television connected to an Apple TV. The iPads would be running iOS 5 to take full advantage of Apple’s Airplay feature.*

My wife would have an iPad, of course. Everything she did on her iPad, from slide shows, to math problems, to video would be instantly mirrored to the television via Apple TV.

And everything would “just work,” as Steve says.

Alas, my wife can only wish for such technology. School districts are in a world of financial hurt, with the state cutting education budgets each year. There is simply no money to make the switch. She and her colleagues are stuck with outmoded, misbehaving technology that is cumbersome, at a best, and nonfunctioning, for the most part.

By the way, I know that it would all “just work,” because I have all that Apple technology at home. It’s sweet.

*  Version 5 of the iOS will be released in a matter of weeks.

Into the vacuum…

August 29, 2011 by davald

Chris Hedges has a new, pessimistic book out, which he calls The Death of the Liberal Class. I’m making my way through it, a relatively easy task because Hedges has a way with words. These paragraphs near the beginning of the book jumped out at me:

In a traditional democracy, the liberal class functions as a safety valve. It makes piecemeal and incremental reform possible. It offers hope for change and proposes gradual steps toward greater equality. It endows the state and the mechanisms of power with virtue. It also serves as an attack dog that discredits radical social movements, making the liberal class a useful component within the power elite.

But the assault by the corporate state on the democratic state has claimed the liberal class as one of its victims. Corporate power forgot that the liberal class, when it functions, gives legitimacy to the power elite. And reducing the liberal class to courtiers or mandarins, who have nothing to offer but empty rhetoric, shuts off this safety valve and forces discontent to find other outlets that often end in violence.

The inability of the liberal class to acknowledge that corporations have wrested power from the hands of citizens, that the Constitution and its guarantees of personal liberty have become irrelevant, and that the phrase consent of the governed is meaningless, has left it speaking and acting in whats that no longer correspond to reality. It has lent its voice to hollow acts of political theater, and the pretense that democratic debate and choice continue to exist.

Whoa.

Hedges cites Fritz Stern, a refugee of the Nazi camps and a scholar on post-Weimar Germany and the rise of fascism.

Stern, who sees similarities between the deterioration of the U.S. political system and the fall of Weimar Germany, warned against “a historic process in which resentment against a disenchanted secular world found deliverance in the ecstatic escape of unreason.” Societies that do not reincorporate the unemployed and the poor into the economy, giving them jobs and relief from crippling debt, become subject to the hysterical mass quest for ecstatic deliverance in unreason.

Apropos of my previous post, into the void created by the “death of the liberal class” comes the whacko right, who have now been subsumed by and become the official face of the Republican Party. In turn, the new GOP, evidently dominated by the Tea Party-cum-Christian fundamentalists, serves as the only legitimate outlet for those who have been victimized by the collapsing economy and see Washington as the repository of evil.

Liberalism no longer has a constituency. The country has been divided into the obscenely rich and those seeking deliverance “in unreason.” There is no buffer, no class to ameliorate the excesses of greed and the misery of poverty through public institutions.

We are broke and broken.

Do facts matter?

August 29, 2011 by davald

I’ve often thought it a liberal conceit that one need only present facts to prevail in arguments. Joe believes x, but is told that the truth is y. Therefore, Joe abandons x and embraces y. Unfortunately, that’s not the way people decide issues. Like George Bush’s infamous quote, it’s more a matter of “the gut.”

In his column this morning Paul Krugman scares us. He merely recounts some sobering poll results that show Republicans believing the darndest things. Among the statistics:

According to Public Policy Polling, only 21 percent of Republican voters in Iowa believe in global warming (and only 35 percent believe in evolution).

Republicans, as a rule, also believe that Obama is responsible for most of what ails us. Now, I have my truck with the president, as I’ve revealed here on numerous occasions, but I know—don’t I?—that Bush spent more and took a federal surplus into the toilet.

The folks at Truthout.org are being helpful. They provided me, and you, with three charts that show my view of the world is more accurate than the Republicans’.

Whose administration spent more federal dollars?

What about the deficit?

And as for jobs?

Before I get carried away with these comparisons, I don’t think it’s appropriate to either credit or blame a president for all events that happen on his watch. However, it is appropriate to take issue with those who practice “the blame game,” especially if they’re obviously unfamiliar with the facts—or, more likely, choose to ignore them.

It would be comforting, nonetheless, to know that Americans, by and large, decide on whom to vote for by consulting the more obvious truths. For example, it would be silly to elect one of the Republican candidates because one thought that the GOP was better at creating jobs. The last chart, certainly, should disabuse one of that notion.

Also, if you believed that innovation in science and technology would help spur economic growth you really shouldn’t vote for Michelle Bachman, who said that hurricanes were signs from God. Nor should you select Rick Perry, who dismisses both evolution and global warming.

That said, Perry, the dimmer version of G.W., is now leading all other GOP candidates by double digits. Who are these people, the Republicans?

Krugman concludes his column thusly:

Now, we don’t know who will win next year’s presidential election. But the odds are that one of these years the world’s greatest nation will find itself ruled by a party that is aggressively anti-science, indeed anti-knowledge. And, in a time of severe challenges — environmental, economic, and more — that’s a terrifying prospect.

But, hey, Americans elected G.W. not once but twice. Well, that’s what the Supreme Court said, anyway, in the first go around.

In times of economic gloom, too many people lose their heads. Unfortunately, there’s always a sufficiently large pool of professional candidates who are willing to exploit the madding crowd. After all, they’re one of them.

Joe Hill and me

August 29, 2011 by davald

The New York Times featured an interesting piece on Joe Hill, the fabled union organizer tried, convicted and then shot by a firing squad in Salt Lake City. What had Hill done? He was accused of killing a grocer and his son on Jan. 10, 1914. Hill denied the charge. No matter. The jury found him guilty, despite flimsy evidence, which many believed had been manufactured. Before facing a hail of bullets, Hill’s supporters from around the world protested his execution, including President Woodrow Wilson, who wrote two letters to Utah’s governor on Hill’s behalf.

He was guilty, all right, but not for murder. No, he was guilty of fighting the bosses by organizing their workers, inciting strikes, and drawing attention to labor’s miserable working conditions.

Hill never testified in his own defense. There were two reasons, one conventional, the other only recently exposed.

Like many in the courtroom, Hill thought the prosecution had no basis for conviction, and he believed that he shouldn’t have to prove his own innocence; that was the prosecution’s job. The scant evidence was circumstantial, and no one could prove that Hill had fired the shots. Actually, Hill had been shot, but not by the grocer trying to defend himself. Which brings us to the second reason.

Evidently Hill was trying to protect the reputation of a his girl friend, Hilda Erickson. Hill was shot and wounded by a suitor of Erickson, whom she had jilted in favor of Hill.

Quite by accident, a letter of Erickson’s was uncovered in a stack of papers, discovered by Hill’s latest biographer, William M. Adler. The Times:

Mr. Adler uncovered a long-forgotten letter from Hill’s sweetheart that said that he had been shot by a rival for her affections, undermining the prosecution’s key assertion. The book, “The Man Who Never Died,” also offers extensive evidence suggesting that an early suspect in the case, a violent career criminal, was the murderer.

That person was Frank Z. Wilson. The Times:

The police arrested Mr. Wilson the night of the murders after they found him walking without an overcoat near the grocery. They also found a bloody handkerchief on him.

Mr. Adler said Mr. Wilson had lied repeatedly to the authorities after they arrested him, but they soon released him for reasons that remain unclear. Mr. Adler also discovered that Mr. Wilson had used at least 16 aliases during his many arrests and convictions, several for robbing trains. He was later involved in the St. Valentine’s Day Massacre in Chicago in 1929, with a getaway car registered under an alias he often used.

As for the person who shot Joe Hill, the Times:

In the letter found by Mr. Adler, Hill’s sweetheart, Hilda Erickson, wrote that Hill had told her he had been shot by her former fiancé, Otto Appelquist — someone she had broken off with a week earlier and who had asked her “if I liked Joe better than him.” In her letter, she added, “I heard Joe tease Otto once that he was going to take me away from him.”

When he wasn’t busy organizing, Hill was writing songs for the International Workers of the World, the Wobblies. How appropriate, then, that an Alfred Hayes would write a poem about Hill, which Earl Robinson put to music in 1936: “I Dreamed I Saw Joe Hill Last Night.”

So, what does Joe Hill have to do with me?

As it happens, my grandfather was a political radical in his own right, with whispered rumors of Wobbly connections, if not also the Communist Party. When I was a boy I’d thumb through and listen to his modest collection of records—this would have been in the 50s. One such album was a medley of songs sung by Paul Robeson, a fellow traveler, it seems.

I have in my music library a couple of Robeson CDs. The first is a live recording of Robeson in 1949 at the Tchaikovsky Hall in Moscow. Among the songs was “Joe Hill.” In my opinion, this is the best rendition of the Hayes/Robinson composition. Accompanied only by piano, Robeson gives real passion and eloquence in a voice unlike any other’s. He skips the verse beginning with “From San Diego…” to deliver a dramatic, yet softer end. The Russians cheered mightily at its conclusion. (Robeson, by the way, spoke Russian among a dozen or more other languages.)*

But that’s not the more significant connection I have with Joe Hill. For I, too, organized, or at least tried to.

In 1977-78 I attempted to form a union at Pemco Insurance Companies down in Seattle. I did not succeed. And, while I was not killed by “copper bosses,” I was set up for dismissal. Yep, I was framed—just like Joe Hill—on trumped up charges, later confirmed by former colleagues. Rather than be fired for my organizing activities, I simply quit.

Oh, and there was likely a third reason that Hill declined to defend himself. He reckoned that he was more valuable to Labor’s cause dead than alive. Before his execution he said that followers should not mourn. They should organize.

“Joe Hill ain’t dead,” he says to me,
“Joe Hill ain’t never died.
Where working men are out on strike
Joe Hill is at their side,
Joe Hill is at their side.”

Obama: now just “Political Man”

August 26, 2011 by davald

A litany of last straws continues apace—reasons not to vote for Obama next year. Briefly, the list includes:

  • perpetuation of war
  • woeful lack of leadership on pushing economic solutions
  • policy of “looking forward,” rather than investigate and, if warranted, prosecute the bad acts of the previous administration
  • failure to even propose, let alone champion, a climate change strategy
  • extracting money from Wall Street rather than curb its dangerous appetite
  • pervasive aloofness in the face of human suffering

We can add another: endorsement of a pipeline to deliver petroleum from the Canadian tar sands to the Midwest and beyond. The New York Times reports:

The Obama administration gave a crucial green light on Friday to a proposed 1,711-mile pipeline that would carry heavy oil from Canada across the Great Plains to terminals in Oklahoma and the Gulf Coast, saying the project would provide a secure source of energy without significant damage to the environment.

Despite strong opposition from the environmental community, Obama gives a thumbs up to polluters. Is this the guy you thought you voted for?

While the pipeline itself may not create excessive environmental risk, both the process of extracting the oil from tar sands and its ultimate combustion contribute huge quantities of greenhouse gas emissions, the major cause of global warming. Similarly, the transportation of Wyoming coal to China via western Washington ports may be relatively benign, but certainly not the burning of the carbon rock by Chinese energy companies.

Analysts and environmental advocates said these decisions had opened a wide and perhaps unbridgeable breach between the Democratic president and environmentally minded voters.

Do you think?

Canada, already the No. 1 source of imported oil to the United States, produced 1.5 million barrels a day of synthetic crude from oil sands in 2010 and hopes to expand that to 2.2 million barrels a day in 2015 and 3.7 million barrels a day by 2025. That level of expansion will require not only the Keystone project, but probably also pipelines to the west coast of Canada, where the crude could be exported to China and other Asian markets.

Keystone XL would increase Canada’s pipeline capacity by 700,000 barrels a day, roughly the amount of oil Malaysia produces. Oil sands alone already provide more imported oil to the United States than Saudi Arabia, Nigeria or Venezuela, countries that are potentially unstable or hostile.

Executives in the oil industry said they were satisfied that the administration recognized the importance of the pipeline project. “It’s more about jobs and energy self-sufficiency than anything else, but what’s wrong with that?” said Chip Johnson, chief executive of Carrizo Oil and Gas.

Look for huge contributions from the energy industry to help fund Obama’s re-election campaign. It’s all about politics now.

More on cities

August 26, 2011 by davald

James McCusker, who writes a weekly column for the Everett Herald, devoted today’s entry to “urbanization costs have taken a heavy toll,” the heading for his piece. Therein, McCusker, an economist living in Bothell, writes:

Ordinary people recognized that simply adding population didn’t translate into economic growth. Instead, most often it would translate into higher taxes.

The underlying cause of higher taxes also relates to something that the American people understood a lot better than their politicians: It costs more to live in the city.

This begs the question: Do increasingly denser urban centers yield lower or higher economic growth? I’d pose a related question: Are cities more or less efficient than either suburban or rural environs?

The September special issue of Scientific American is all about cities, most of it positive and optimistic. Physicists Luis Bettencourt and Geoffrey West authored one of the articles, entitled “Bigger Cities Do More with Less.” They write:

The numbers show that urban dwellers produce more inventions and create more opportunities for economic growth. Often large cities are also the greenest places on the planet because people living in denser habitats typically have smaller energy footprints, require less infrastructure and consume less of the world’s resources per capita. Compared with suburban or rural areas, cities do more with less. And the bigger cities get, the more productive and efficient they tend to become.

The authors concede that housing prices are higher in cities than elsewhere. But the overall benefits exceed aggregate costs.

In a city with high rents, only activities that add substantial value can be prof- itable. These economic pressures push urbanites to come up with new forms of organizations, products and services that carry more value added. In turn, higher profitability, excellence and choice tend to attract more talent to the city, pushing rents higher still, fueling the need to find yet more productive activities. This feedback mechanism, in a nutshell, is the principal reason cities accelerate innovation, while diversifying and intensifying social and economic activity.

Data suggest that a doubling of a city’s population increases economic output by 15 percent than if the same population were split in two. Size does matter.

Likewise, the larger and denser a city the lower the per capita environmental footprint. The reasons are fairly straightforward. Owning and driving a car in New York City is a burden rather than a necessity, as it is elsewhere. People walk or use public transportation or get purchased items delivered; and living in apartments requires less energy to heat and cool vis-a-vis a suburban house, where all walls are exposed to the elements.

Also, as a city’s population increases so do wages. However, we should not draw the conclusion that merely living in a city translates into a bigger paycheck. Rather, cities are more productive and innovative, thereby attracting more capable citizens, those with more education and, therefore, greater earning ability. (On a personal note, my wife and I could not afford to buy a house on Seattle’s Capitol Hill, but our lawyer daughter and her lawyer husband can and did.)

I wish to address a few gratuitous comments of McCusker. He writes:

The higher costs of urban societies are aggravated by the fact that most of these costs are the domain of government, a traditionally inefficient organization.

I don’t pretend to understand what McCusker means by this. Are rents and housing prices, the biggest component of urban living’s costs, the “domain of government.” Wouldn’t real estate value be the product of supply and demand rather than municipal government? The obvious attractiveness of Seattle combined with geographical constraints lead to higher rents and housing prices than in, say, Marysville, which has few amenities, little commercial activity, and automobile-dependent densities.

McCusker also writes:

Costs and productivity limitations are the reasons why we can’t buy way out of the fix we are in. To get out we must shift resources away from federal, state and local government and into manufacturing, mining, agriculture and petroleum industries so that we can regain some productivity growth.

The recessionary economy is doing its part to reduce the size of governments across the board, along with a ridiculous aversion to taxes. State, county, city, and school district governments have been slashing budgets right and left. Why? They’re losing revenues.

And how might “we” transfer resources to the private sector? Americans are among the least taxed citizens in the industrial world. If the money isn’t going to governments, where is it being spent? Well, McCusker has apparently failed to notice that nobody is spending money, which is the source of the economic problem. Consumers aren’t consuming because they’re either out of work or their wages have stagnated or they’ve lost the value in their homes, which had enabled continued buying during the housing bubble’s expansion. Firms aren’t spending money to expand their business because they have no confidence that people can or will buy their goods.

McCusker also fails to appreciate that labor productivity is up in almost every sector. Corporations are getting more and more output per worker, which helps explain why they’re not hiring. That said, it’s true that growth is mediocre and far below capacity in each of the industries he mentions. Again, that can be explained by a multitude of reasons, including a concerted effort to shift manufacturing off shore.

And what shall we mine? I suspect that McCusker is thinking of oil, coal, and natural gas. He evidently hasn’t noticed that the U.S. reached its peak oil production decades ago (it costs too much to extract relative to retail price). Nor has he noticed that we’re in the midst of a natural gas glut, with prices steadily falling. Further, McCusker appears to dismiss global warming either as science or threat. There’s one thing the planet needs less of and that’s more carbon-rich resources.

Finally, McCusker tosses out a non sequitur. He writes:

We will also need a new economic model—not a Keynesian hand-me-down…

What does Keynes have to do with anything he’s been discussing? Moreover, how could he with a straight face assert that “we” are living under a “Keynesian hand-me-down” economic model? No one in the White House, no Republican, and only a handful of Democrats espouse Keynesian doctrine. That’s too bad, since Keynes’s model explains our current economic problems, one defined by constrained aggregate demand and pervasive uncertainty (see Keynes’s chapter 12).

I infer from McCusker’s “hand-me-down” expression that he believes that all of Keynes can be summed up in government giveaways. My guess is that McCusker criticized the stimulus package, because it was both Keynesian and “hand-me-down.” It was neither. Real Keynsians understood that the economy needed a huge infusion of dollars, at least three times as much as the Recovery Act, which was inadequate in size and form—too much of it was tax cuts and not enough in public infrastructure.

So, does McCusker think that the economy will spontaneously recover? If so, what will be the trigger? What event or events will restore confidence and induce spending and hiring?

I’m listening, Mr. McCusker.

Want to save money? Raise the Medicare eligibility age.

August 25, 2011 by davald

Not.

The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities (CBPP) published a report on this very proposal, which we hear from the mouths of Republicans and Democrats alike. Why, we’re living longer, right? So we’ll simply push access to Medicare a few birthdays later. We’ll start at, say, two, then gradually up it to…what? A slippery slope, I suggest.

To be sure, the federal government could save a nickel or two, although that’s dubious, as I’ll discuss briefly below. But costs will surely shift to vulnerable citizens, those who are in poor health and without private medical coverage. They’ll have to wait a few more years to receive Medicare benefits. The CBPP writes:

The principal study of the effects of raising the Medicare eligibility age, by the Kaiser Family Foundation, estimates that its increased state and private-sector costs would be twice as large as the net federal savings.  If the proposal were fully in effect in 2014, Kaiser estimates, it would generate $5.7 billion in net federal savings but $11.4 billion in higher health care costs to individuals, employers, and states.

Let’s think a bit more on this.

Suppose you’ve been working at a blue-collar job, one that has required lots of physical labor. You finally retire at age 65. However, the thirty years on the job have taken their toll on your body. You’ve got a bad hip that requires replacement. Perhaps you’ve developed a heart condition. Whatever, you don’t get Medicare. You’ll have to wait until you’re 67 or maybe even older. Well, you don’t have any money for the necessary medical treatments. So you put off getting them. The months and years pass and you’re only getting worse. Now your condition has deteriorated to the extent that corrective procedures will be far more involved and much more costly than they would have been when you retired. Now you’re eligible for Medicare. You’ve become, because of the longer wait period, a greater financial liability. Your claims against Medicare are much higher because of the delays in getting treatment.

One of the compelling arguments for universal health care, or even “Medicare for all,” is that people can and will seek medical attention after initial symptoms. No need to put off seeing a doctor, because you don’t avoid out-of-pocket expenses. You’re covered. Period. Well, early prevention and treatment are less expensive in the main than letting conditions go untreated.

Consider a diabetic. If the person can’t afford regular physical examinations, his problem gets worse. He may have been able to effectively counter the diabetes through diet and exercise, provided he had an early diagnosis. Now that he’s forced to wait, because he lacks health insurance or cash, the diabetes requires insulin or, god forbid, amputation of an extremity. He’s much worse off, an invalid, and taxpayers get a much higher bill.

The rational approach to medical care is to cover everyone on an equal basis, from pre-birth to death. Receiving medical care should be a right rather than a privilege. It would certainly be cheaper for all of us.

Greenwald on Cheney’s crimes

August 25, 2011 by davald

It’s been a while since I visited Glenn Greenwald’s website, which I used to consult every day. Can’t tell you why, save for a personal need for relief from his passionate critiques of government misdeeds. But I happened to be browsing just a moment ago. Hey, what’s Glenn up to?

Former Bush VP Dick Cheney has written a memoir. You’ve seen the headlines wherein he not only declines to express regrets for water boarding, torture, mass imprisonments without charges, domestic surveillance—he seems to be rather proud of his behavior.

Greenwald reminds us of just what a bastard Cheney was and how he’s now able to amass a small fortune with the book and associated tour, he’s being resurrected as a very serious person people should listen to. Greenwald:

Less than three years ago, Dick Cheney was presiding over policies that left hundreds of thousands of innocent people dead from a war of aggression, constructed a worldwide torture regime, and spied on thousands of Americans without the warrants required by law, all of which resulted in his leaving office as one of the most reviled political figures in decades. But thanks to the decision to block all legal investigations into his chronic criminality, those matters have been relegated to mere pedestrian partisan disputes, and Cheney is thus now preparing to be feted — and further enriched — as a Wise and Serious Statesman with the release of his memoirs this week: one in which he proudly boasts (yet again) of the very crimes for which he was immunized.

While the Obama administration has refused to revisit prior bad acts, saying that it and the nation must “move forward,” Cheney is now free to spout and prance. Not so for a victim of Cheney’s crimes, who has been barred from receiving money for a book he wrote chronicling his mistreatment at Guantanamo. He committed no crime and was not charged. But what the heck, off to prison anyway. Greenwald:

The U.S. Government loves to demand that other countries hold their political leaders accountable for serious crimes, dispensing lectures on the imperatives of the rule of law. Numerous states bar ordinary convicts from profiting from their crimes with books. David Hicks, an Australian citizen imprisoned without charges for six years at Cheney’s Guantanamo, just had $10,000 seized by the Australian government in revenue from his book about his time in that prison camp on the ground that he is barred from profiting from his uncharged, unproven crimes.

Makes you want to stand tall and salute, doesn’t it?

Governor Good Hair

August 25, 2011 by davald

Molly Ivins, may she rest in peace, relished Texas politics for its entertainment value. She was also quick with the quip, as in calling Rick Perry “Governor Good Hair.” You can read more of her Perry quotes here.

This morning the editors of the New York Times weighed in on Perry’s hypocritical views on government. The latest entrant in the presidential race wants a flat tax to raise enough revenues to fund a “constitutional” government. With the exception of defense, Perry considers nearly the whole of the federal government “unconstitutional.” But the editors point out that Perry adeptly exploited Texas government—a huge labyrinth of commissions, boards, and agencies—to extract millions of dollars for his political campaigns. They write:

Since 2001, more than a fifth of the $83 million that Mr. Perry has raised for his gubernatorial campaigns has come from people he appointed to state boards and commissions, according to a study by Texans for Public Justice, a watchdog group. Just 150 individuals and couples gave him $37 million of that total, and nearly half received substantial tax breaks, business contracts or appointments from Mr. Perry, The Los Angeles Times reported.

If, God forbid, he gains the Oval Office in next year’s election and his party wrests control of the Senate, will he use Washington, D.C., as he as the whole of Texas government? The editors:

On a national level, Mr. Perry says he wants to make government “inconsequential.” In Texas, he has made government very consequential — for his political career. The people of Texas have not fared nearly as well.

Oremus.
