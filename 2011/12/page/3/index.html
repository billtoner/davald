More on Hungary

December 20, 2011 by davald

Paul Krugman’s colleague followed up on her guest post. She answers the question I could only guess at. Why did Hungarians go to the right?

Hungary was saddled with communist-era debt that was the largest per capita of any of the former Soviet satellite states. It has not been able to get its books in order since. The prior Socialist government, elected in 2002 and reelected in 2006, was mired in scandal and became hugely unpopular. But then economics made things worse.

Hungary had a housing bubble, fueled by mortgages that Hungarians took out in euros and Swiss francs. The world economic crisis, combined with economic mismanagement of the prior government, resulted in a sharp fall in the forint (Hungary’s national currency). As a result, Hungarians’ mortgage payments suddenly doubled and even tripled.

Fortunately, the U.S. has its own currency. So, despite even worse numbers, we Americans haven’t yet seen the need to install fascists.

Hungary’s “authoritarian slide”

December 19, 2011 by davald

The economic situation in Europe is both tenuous and disturbing. Even Germany, considered the solid core of the Eurozone, discovered that it couldn’t sell all its bonds a few weeks ago. Nearly every country in the Euro lacks investor confidence.

Europeans old enough to remember should fear a return to the right. Hitler rose to power on the back of a severe economic depression exacerbated by war reparations imposed by Britain, France, et al. Are conditions deteriorating to the point that authoritarian regimes emerge?

Paul Krugman thinks so. He asked his Princeton colleague Kim Lane Scheppele to pen a sort of guest column for his blog. What’s happening in Hungary, the subject of the post, is frightening, certainly for Hungarians but also for neighboring countries. It remains to be seen if the European Union intervenes, since Hungary is a constituent member.

I am old enough to remember, albeit vaguely, images of tanks rumbling down the streets of Budapest in 1956. The Russians invaded so as to bring the wayward nation back in line and firmly behind the Iron Curtain. One would think that Hungary’s citizens would not wish a return to autocratic governance.

I have no idea why Hungarians put the center-right Fidesz party into power. Our presidential elections are strongly determined by the state of the economy in the few months before voting. So, let’s look at some financial numbers (OECD data).

GDP rose from 2003 to 2008, until the recession—similar to other countries’ experience. It then fell, but had stabilized before the elections that placed Fidesz in charge of the government.

Taxes on incomes have been high—above 50 percent of average labor costs. U.S. taxes have been just below 30 percent. Hungary’s government spent about 22 percent of GDP on public services. Finland spends about 25 percent of GDP; the U.S. a miserly 16 percent.

When the Nazi’s were consolidating power in the 1930s, the German inflation rate rose exponentially in the Weimar Republic. This could not stand, making Germans susceptible to dramatic political change. So, what about Hungary’s inflation?

As in other recession-hit countries, Hungary’s core inflation rate fell prior to the recent elections. Higher than Finland’s, to be sure, and higher than most of Europe. But these numbers could never be construed as hyper-inflationary.

Following this very cursory review of Hungary’s financial indicators, I am at a lost to discern an extraordinary pattern that could help explain the electorate’s decision to install Fidesz. Wikipedia tells us a bit about the party, about how Hungarian youth persecuted by the then-ruling communist party formed Fidesz, which was originally liberal. It eventually switched to conservative. (Tough time wrapping my brain around that one. On second thought, our Democratic Party is hardly as liberal now as it was under FDR or even LBJ, if we leave out the Vietnam War.) After several failed elections, it finally won an outright majority in 2010.

Fidesz’s wholesale reformation of government makes it extremely difficult for opposition parties, especially the once-ruling Socialists, to gain a foothold, let alone pose a serious threat. Hungary’s experiment with democracy may be over, if Kim Lane Scheppele is correct.

Hitch arraigns

December 19, 2011 by davald

Christopher Hitchens, commissioned to cover our national party conventions for the London Review of Books, found much to be desired. He took little comfort in this, because he declared America his new home, officially so, and thought we deserved better. Here he uses his journalistic assignments to inveigh against our electoral system and its evident follies. There is no candidate left unscathed. As always, the writing is preciously provocative.

He regrets what has happened to presidential elections, particularly the respective parties’ anointment of their chosen ones long before the November balloting.

A Republican National Convention had been arranged, as a sort of sound-stage or a mixed-media event, entirely for the convenience of the press and TV. The delegates were mere extras on the set, the coronation of the two nominees was a sure thing, the feral extremists and fundamentalists had been tidied out of sight, the corporate-sponsorship logos were beautifully placed, the camera angles and background briefings were the chief preoccupation of the Party managers and – there was exactly nothing to cover, nothing to transmit, and nothing to write about.

While he would eventually champion the invasion of Iraq, he found nothing to like in George W. Bush, whom he excoriates for his gubernatorial  murders and the glee with which he approached this dreadful practice.

I say ‘twisted’ about the dislikably boyish Governor of Texas, because he has signed more than a hundred death warrants during his entirely undistinguished tenure, and because he was only criticised (and that faintly) for two of these capricious crowd-pleasing atrocities. In one case, a Canadian citizen was condemned to death without any notification to the Canadian Embassy, as is reciprocally mandated by both US and Canadian law. In another, a woman was executed despite good evidence that she had undergone a transforming redemption while on Death Row. Bush met the Canadian objections by saying that the man ought not to have committed a crime while in Texas (which would mean that as President he would not care about consular representation for Americans arraigned overseas). He countered the Christian conservative petition on behalf of Karla Faye Tucker by mimicking her last-minute entreaties (‘Oh, please don’t kill me’) to a reporter for Rupert Murdoch’s Weekly Standard. That does it for me, and I don’t even believe – as Bush is supposed to – in Biblical repentance.

It’s worth going through the list, providing Hitchens’s unique perspective.

John McCain – nobody’s idea of an intellectual but likes to talk dirty and got himself shot down while scattering high explosives over someone else’s country. (People keep describing him as a POW but I think that there must be some mistake – no war on Vietnam was ever declared.)

Donald Trump – a ludicrous figure, but at least he’s lived it up a bit in the real world and at least he’s worked out how to cover 90 per cent of his skull with 30 per cent of his hair.

Bill Bradley – boring and pompous and tenth-rate but used to play basketball and take showers with African Americans.

The weird one is [Al] Gore. When he isn’t robotically normal he is decidedly odd. Who would claim, as he did, to have been (with his wife) the model for Erich Segal’s treacly novel Love Story? Who would opt, as he did, for paid advice from a simpering Clintonoid feminist about how to become an ‘alpha male’? Who would make as little as he does of having had the ravaged and husky Tommy Lee Jones for a college room-mate? Who would get his wife to proffer an on-the-record interview, asserting that he was a secret Tamburlaine in the sack? He’s not a psychopath like his boss, and he inspires piercing pity for his writhing attempts to break free, but still.

Alas, we won’t see this kind of writing again.

Charitable dollars doing little charity

December 19, 2011 by davald

The Oregon attorney general’s office has released its list (pdf) of 20 worst charities in 2011. Their metric comes from the Better Business Bureau guidelines: at least 65 percent of charities’ expenditures should go to charity. Fancy that.

At the top of the list is Shiloh International Ministries. It spent nearly $850,000, but only 3.2 percent went to their stated cause.

An Everett charity made the list: Operational National Lookout for Missing Youth. Of it’s $1.5 million expenditures just 11.5 percent was spent on, well, looking for missing youth.

Political incest

December 19, 2011 by davald

For Obama and Democratic leaders who are trying to set an election agenda focused on income inequality, wage stagnation, and downward mobility for the middle and lower class, the prominence of Democratic lobbyists has become problematic.

Writing for the New York Times, professor Thomas Edsall treats us to some more disturbing news. Former Democratic members of Congress, who may have championed the needs and aspirations of the Rest of Us, let greed do their walking—straight to K Street. They now peddle on behalf of corporate interests as they put the screws to the narrowing middle class.

First on the list of malevolent turnarounds is Dick Gephardt, who used to defend labor and unions. Edsall:

Upon leaving Congress in 2005, Gephardt joined the Washington offices of DLA Piper as senior counsel. Two years later, he founded his own lobbying firm, the Gephardt Government Affairs Group.

By 2010, annual firm billings had shot up from $625,000 in 2007 to $6.59 million. Gephardt’s client list was blue chip, Goldman Sachs (paid Gephardt $200,000 in 2010); Boeing Co. ($440,000); Visa Inc. ($200,000); Ameren Corp, the energy holding company ($200,000); and Waste Management Inc., the leading provider of trash and garbage removal ($320,000).

A normally voluble political strategist and committed liberal who has worked with Gephardt in past elections said about the former Congressman’s lobbying clientele, “I don’t want to talk on the record, it sucks. It’s a sad situation for us.”

Union leaders like Richard Trumka (president of A.F.L-C.I.O.) spoke to this issue in a recent speech, quoted in the article:

Our role is not to build the power of a political party or a candidate. It is to improve the lives of working families and strengthen our country. It doesn’t matter if candidates and parties are controlling the wrecking ball or simply standing aside — the outcome is the same either way.

Money corrupts, and never more so than in Washington, D.C. The Supreme Court, in a series of rulings, has catapulted the role of money into the stratosphere. Obama is expected to raise and spend a billion dollars in his campaign for reelection.

And speaking of campaigns, they are obviously far too long, which makes them even more expensive. Parliamentary democracies limit the blabbering season as they also limit campaign spending.

More insidious, it seems, is that the corrupting power of money has left the Rest of Us devoid of effective advocates. Why represent workers for a pittance when you can siphon off millions in defense of the strong and mighty?

I’ve never been to Washington, D.C., despite many opportunities to do so on the public’s dime. I suspect that were I to travel there, the stench would overwhelm from miles away, through the plane’s ventilation system.

The Brandeis ratio

December 19, 2011 by davald

Neither had I—heard of it. It’s featured in a New York Times op-ed this morning.

Quoting Supreme Court justice Louis Brandeis, the authors:

“We may have democracy, or we may have wealth concentrated in the hands of a few, but we cannot have both.”

The ratio, per the authors, is the average income of the One-percenters divided by the average middle-class income. Before the Great Divergence, roughly associated with Ronald Reagan’s terms, the ratio was 12.5. As of 2006, the last year of data, the ratio had skyrocketed to 36.

The authors, both university professors, Yale and Berkeley, propose a one-time law by Congress. (Already I’m squirming.) Each year the IRS would calculate the Brandeis ratio of the previous 12 months. If the number exceeded 36, the One-percenters would be charged a marginal tax on incomes in excess of the “poorest” in the fractile, that is, incomes above $330,000 a year.

There’s a Rawlsian* element in here, too. The law would allow for income increases of the middle class, and as long as the ratio remained at or below 36, the marginal tax would not kick in.

The authors:

Part of our goal is to change the way politicians speak about income equality. Framing the income of the wealthy in relation to the median income will help us all keep in mind the relative success of the middle class. Our grandparents would be shocked to learn that the average income of the 1-percent club has skyrocketed to more than 30 times the median income — just as we will be shocked if 20 years from now 1-percenters make 80 times the median, which is where we will be if inequality continues to grow at the current rate unabated.

 

As much as I might applaud this idea, I’m sure that you would agree with me that our current caustic politics precludes any meaningful considerations of our tax structure. The Republican-controlled House prefers that the Great Divergence become even greater. The GOP in both houses wants to lower taxes on the very wealthy, arguing—against reason and facts—that putting more money in the hands of the extremely privileged will create jobs.

In a previous post I drew attention to the conclusion of the International Monetary Fund, via New York Times columnist Charles Blow, that countries marked by significant income inequality were less able to grow their economies. If so, then further enriching the One-percenters will impede, not promote, economic growth.

____________

* John Rawls, who wrote A Theory of Justice.

Brown wants green, and so should we all

December 18, 2011 by davald

California governor Jerry Brown blasted global warming deniers in a recent all-day conference in San Francisco, called “Extreme risks and California’s future.”

“We have an entire political party who has a doctrine of absolute denial,” said Brown, who was wearing a green tie. “All the denial in the world doesn’t change the facts that greenhouse gases are building up. The main thing we have to deal with in climate change is the skepticism, the denial and the cultlike behavior of the political lemmings that would take us over the cliff.”

Golden State has 840 miles of coastline. Increased warming of the planet will melt glaciers and expand ocean volumes, threatening shoreline developments.

Max Auffhammer, an environmental economist at UC Berkeley, said if there is a 4-foot rise in sea levels by the end of the century, and then a storm surge, both the San Francisco and Oakland airports would be under water. So would parts of Silicon Valley, he said.

Then-governor Arnold Schwarzenegger signed into law the nation’s most ambitious program to curb greenhouse gas emissions. Other states have adopted energy resource portfolio requirements, including Washington with Initiative 937, which governs electric utilities; however, it doesn’t touch transportation, Washington’s biggest source of carbon dioxide emissions.

Despite such efforts, last year global CO2 emissions jumped by the highest amount on record, after a decline in 2009 owing to the recession. Because modern economies run on fossil fuels, economic growth yields higher emissions.

The challenge, which receives scant attention from our political leaders, is to grow the economy without adding more carbon dioxide to the atmosphere. Since coal is the most carbon-intensive fuel, any serious strategy must effect the closure of coal-fired power plants.

Washington has but one coal plant, in Centralia. It will closed by 2025, as directed by Olympia. But many states have dozens of coal plants, which supply almost all the electricity consumed by their citizens. Nationwide, coal plants represent nearly half of all electricity generation.

Sourcewatch.org has an interactive map with an inventory of each state’s coal plants. I clicked on Ohio.

Unlike Washington, states like Ohio lack significant hydropower resources. If not coal, what?

Natural gas-fired plants emit about half the CO2 of coal plants. If every coal plant could be and were converted to natural gas, carbon dioxide emissions would fall significantly. The question then becomes natural gas supply, the extraction of which also emits greenhouse gases.

Here’s a table comparing CO2 emissions by type of generation (source).

The Snohomish County PUD, of which I’m a board member, derives almost all of its electricity from hydro facilities, in particular those along the Columbia and Snake rivers. We have no carbon in our supply portfolio, save for what we purchase from the market pool, which is a small fraction of the total.

But this has much to do with geography, aided and abetted by the massive New Deal projects like Grand Coulee dam. Washington’s utilities will only get greener over time, because of the initiative.

But that’s not the case in Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Kentucky, Alabama, Texas, and the list goes on. If their coal plants were shut down, economies would collapse and the people would wilt in the summers and freeze in the winters.

The problem with carbon dioxide is that it doesn’t matter whence it’s emitted. Ohio carbons mix with all of the other emitted carbons in the atmosphere where they remain for more than a hundred years. So, we can be green in Washington and feel all the better for it. But we’ll suffer the consequences of climate change if others can’t find a viable path to green. Yet, one has to start somewhere, and since our national governments can’t commit to meaningful solutions it may be up to individual regions, states, counties, and cities—hoping, I guess, that a sufficient number of entities adopt effective programs to actually reduce carbon emissions.

It certainly doesn’t help that the two most powerful Republicans in Congress come from states heavily dependent on coal. Even if they were convinced that climate change is serious and mostly human-caused, neither could advocate the closing of their dirty plants should they wish to remain in office.

As you can tell, I am downright pessimistic on all this. There’s no political will. Besides, it’s probably too late to stabilize, let alone reverse, global warming.

Our descendants will somehow have to adapt to much harsher weather and flooded coastlines. There will be prolonged droughts in already dry regions and likely more precipitation in wet ones.

I foresee the day when the entire Southwest begins a mass exodus, after the wells run dry. To where will they run? The Pacific Northwest, of course.

Catholic collisions

December 17, 2011 by davald

Pope Benedict, nee Joseph Ratzinger, issued a statement yesterday calling for “adequate mechanisms for the redistribution of wealth.” On October 24 the Vatican-based Pontifical Council for Justice and Peace released a lengthy document on economics. The Council said:

What has driven the world in such a problematic direction for its economy and also for peace?

First and foremost, an economic liberalism that spurns rules and controls. Economic liberalism is a theoretical system of thought, a form of “economic apriorism” that purports to derive laws for how markets function from theory, these being laws of capitalistic development, while exaggerating certain aspects of markets. An economic system of thought that sets down a priori the laws of market functioning and economic development, without measuring them against reality, runs the risk of becoming an instrument subordinated to the interests of the countries that effectively enjoy a position of economic and financial advantage.

Regulations and controls, imperfect though they may be, already often exist at the national and regional levels; whereas on the international level, it is hard to apply and consolidate such controls and rules.

The inequalities and distortions of capitalist development are often an expression not only of economic liberalism but also of utilitarian thinking: that is, theoretical and practical approaches according to which what is useful for the individual leads to the good of the community. This saying has a core of truth, but it cannot be ignored that individual utility – even where it is legitimate – does not always favour the common good. In many cases a spirit of solidarity is called for that transcends personal utility for the good of the community.

Today’s New York Times reports on Newt Gingrich’s conversion to Roman Catholicism. He relishes the Church’s opposition to abortion and same-sex marriage. My guess is that he ignores the rest of Catholic doctrine on matters pertaining to justice and peace. Gingrich favors capital punishment, which the Church condemns. He’s also quite comfortable with the kind of capitalism practiced in the U.S., with its emphasis on the individual making free economic choices to enhance his utility. Gingrich opposes financial regulation; the Church favors it.

As a one-time Catholic who could never quite acquire “the gift of faith,” I’m quite familiar with Church teachings, which do not, as a rule, gain the attention of the mainstream media, which seems fixated on pernicious clerical sex. Most people, I dare suggest, are unaware of the Church’s strong attitudes of peace and justice. Should you by chance read one or more of the encyclicals, you may be startled by the words. For example:

Without truth, without trust and love for what is true, there is no social conscience and responsibility, and social action ends up serving private interests and the logic of power, resulting in social fragmentation, especially in a globalized society at difficult times like the present. [Caritas in veritas]

Or this, from the same encyclical:

The world’s wealth is growing in absolute terms, but inequalities are on the increase. In rich countries, new sectors of society are succumbing to poverty and new forms of poverty are emerging. In poorer areas some groups enjoy a sort of “superdevelopment” of a wasteful and consumerist kind which forms an unacceptable contrast with the ongoing situations of dehumanizing deprivation. “The scandal of glaring inequalities” continues. Corruption and illegality are unfortunately evident in the conduct of the economic and political class in rich countries, both old and new, as well as in poor ones. Among those who sometimes fail to respect the human rights of workers are large multinational companies as well as local producers. International aid has often been diverted from its proper ends, through irresponsible actions both within the chain of donors and within that of the beneficiaries. Similarly, in the context of immaterial or cultural causes of development and underdevelopment, we find these same patterns of responsibility reproduced. On the part of rich countries there is excessive zeal for protecting knowledge through an unduly rigid assertion of the right to intellectual property, especially in the field of health care. At the same time, in some poor countries, cultural models and social norms of behaviour persist which hinder the process of development.

Pope John Paul II’s Laborem exercens focused on labor. The Pope emphasized throughout the Church’s unequivocal position: labor is prior to capital. However, he emphasized that neither should be opposed to or in conflict with the other. Yet, that’s what has happened. The Pope:

The break occurred in such a way that labour was separated from capital and set in opposition to it, and capital was set in opposition to labour, as though they were two impersonal forces, two production factors juxtaposed in the same “economistic” perspective. This way of stating the issue contained a fundamental error, what we can call the error of economism, that of considering human labour solely according to its economic purpose. This fundamental error of thought can and must be called an error of materialism, in that economism directly or indirectly includes a conviction of the primacy and superiority of the material, and directly or indirectly places the spiritual and the personal (man’s activity, moral values and such matters) in a position of subordination to material reality.

The Church has always rejected the “cafeteria Catholic,” those who pick and choose which edicts or recommendations to follow. Gingrich is surely one of those, as was William F. Buckley. The list is quite long, actually.

Conservative Catholics may side with Republican Gingrich because he is one of them. Progressive Catholics have always been Democratic, or better, so most would support the non-Catholic Barack Obama over the nominally Catholic Newt Gingrich or Rick Santorum.

If one attempts to weave together the various pronouncements and insights of the encyclicals, a clear answer emerges to the question: With whom would Jesus pitch his tent, Wall Street or Main Street? It’s not the former.
