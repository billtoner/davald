A proper perspective on the recent unemployment data

December 3, 2011 by davald

The New York Times editors:

Most of the decline in November’s unemployment rate was not because jobless people found new work. Rather, it is because 315,000 people dropped out of the work force, a reflection of extraordinarily weak demand by employers for new workers. It is also a sign of socioeconomic decline, of wasted resources and untapped potential, the human equivalent of boarded-up Main Streets and shuttered factories.

Overcoming the intuitive

December 2, 2011 by davald

We are mired in debt. That’s certainly the headline, and it’s true. We’re not alone; nearly all of Europe is over-leveraged. Debt, of course, is not the only problem. Despite some relatively good news today on the unemployment rate, millions of Americans have lost their jobs, their homes, their small savings, and are now wondering what to do, having exhausted their government benefits. Adding to the misery, the New York Times reported yesterday that only seven percent of the unemployed who eventually found work landed jobs that paid the same or better than their previous jobs.

What to do?

I don’t think that I’m venturing too far out on a limb to suggest a common expectation: that those in a position to effect change are observing the same universe. That doesn’t seem to be the case.

In the Eurozone the principal players, notably Germany’s Ms. Merkel, have demanded austerity from the peripheral states. Reduce government spending while paying down debt. England’s Mr. Cameron has imposed the same austere measures on Great Britain.

Here at home, Republicans have rallied round the same austerity flag. They want to reduce America’s debt through government cuts alone; they refuse to budge on their no-new-taxes pledge.

The proffered reason for this tighten-your-belt strategy is two-fold: (1) raising taxes, while never a good idea in the Republican mind, would be disastrous for this weak economy; and (2) spending cuts will keep inflation low and restore “confidence” for  Wall Street and CEOs. As a result of the latter, businesses will expand, hire more workers, and banks will start lending them money.

But suppose that this recipe would actually make matters worse, much worse. Suppose that the solution to our problems lies in part on taking on even more public debt.

That’s the argument submitted by a number of prominent economists, including Mr. Krugman, Mr. Stiglitz, Mr. DeLong, and Ms. Romer. In his column this morning Mr. Krugman writes:

How did things go so wrong? The answer you hear all the time is that the euro crisis was caused by fiscal irresponsibility. Turn on your TV and you’re very likely to find some pundit declaring that if America doesn’t slash spending we’ll end up like Greece. Greeeeeece!

But the truth is nearly the opposite. Although Europe’s leaders continue to insist that the problem is too much spending in debtor nations, the real problem is too little spending in Europe as a whole. And their efforts to fix matters by demanding ever harsher austerity have played a major role in making the situation worse.

With much evidence, the underlying assumption of Krugman et al. is that economies are suffering from too little demand, a point I’ve made on these pages. Since neither consumers (who are income-constrained) nor businesses (who won’t expand their enterprises if too few people will buy their output) can boost demand on their own, the government becomes the last resort, as J.M. Keynes argued.

How can the government accomplish this? In two major ways: it can print more money and it can spend more money, thus increasing public debt. These are anathema to the Republicans; and since they control the House, there’s virtually no chance that expansionary policies will be effected.

It does sound counterintuitive that the solution to our economic woes lies on the path of increasing debt. But that’s what the doctor has ordered.

However, this is judged a temporary fix. In the long run, we should reduce debt, not by cutting government budgets but by raising revenues via higher taxes. After all, we’ve had much, much higher tax rates in the past. Indeed, the US lived in prosperous times, with ever increasing GDP, when the marginal tax rate was around 90 percent. Today it’s at 35 percent.

Without a significant change in the ideological complexion of Congress, the prescription will remain unfilled.

A voice is needed

December 1, 2011 by davald

Thus saith Bob Simmons, who covered the Berkeley student protest movements in the 60s and is suggesting that the current Occupy movement find its own Mario Savio, whom Simmons describes as “a brilliant young person with an unyielding passion for fairness and a superb command of the language, to incite and inspire non-violent change.”

I arrived on the Berkeley campus too late for Savio but was there for the People’s Park demonstrations. Never has one small piece of dirt created so much interest. While students occupied the park*, a misnomer at best, the rest of Berkeley was occupied by state police and the National Guard. Military jeeps filled with uniformed soldiers buzzed through the streets, guns drawn and aimed somewhere. I recall the bystander atop the roof being struck and killed by a stray bullet fired by a member of the security forces. I also remember pitching at Evans Field with military helicopters flying overhead. The odor of tear gas was everywhere. Eyes burned. Surreal.

I think Simmons is correct in reminding us that the authorities in Berkeley and Sacramento  committed major mistakes in their efforts to repel the protests. Their actions, including, of course, the killing and the beatings, only inflamed passions, drawing sympathy for the movements.

It seems inevitable that governments pump energy into such causes by trying forcibly to repress them. The more the authorities crack down, the more the movement gains energy and traction. In 1969, a few years after Savio’s speech, it was Gov. Ronald Reagan tear-gassing the University campus and adjacent Berkeley neighborhoods, including a hospital where the gas entered the air-conditioning system. This year it’s Seattle police pepper spraying an 84-year-old woman in the face; officers at the University of California-Davis spraying seated demonstrators; New York City police forcibly preventing journalists from photographing police actions at Zucotti Park.

I don’t know what’s to become of the Occupy movement. I doubt that Wall Street pays attention. Nor, it seems, do political leaders, although Republicans and Fox News can’t help but demonize the protesters.

My guess, though certainly not my hope, is that the movement will wither, especially as the winter cold approaches. The antipathies will surely remain, however. Somethings going to break; such inequity can’t long endure.

________

*  People’s Park really was a patch of dirt. UC Berkeley officials had purchased the land to build a parking garage, which they never got around to building. Local street people, mostly those who lived along Telegraph Ave., decided they would plant some flora and transform the place into a makeshift park. This situation, as I recall, persisted for weeks if not months, until officials evidently had had enough. One night police forcibly evicted the park occupants then hastily erected a chain-link fence around the perimeter before posting guards. Dumb. Student radicals, we’ll call them, quickly got wind of this. They organized a group of several hundred in front of Sproul Hall, where Savio had given his famous “rage against the machine” speech five years before. They then marched a couple of blocks to the park. That’s when the fun started, resulting in arrests, shattered storefront windows, including Bank of America’s, and protesters’  attempts to reclaim the park.

Speaking of the environment…

December 1, 2011 by davald

As we busy ourselves saving Columbia River salmon, our neighbors to the north, specifically in the Athabasca oil sands of Alberta, are wreaking substantial environmental destruction on an almost unimaginable scale. And were it not for mounting protests, Pres. Obama would have approved the Keystone XL pipeline to deliver the Canadian oil to refineries and terminals in the U.S. He’s postponed the decision until after next year’s general elections.

You can locate the oil sands on Google Earth. You’ll find it in the upper east quadrant of Alberta. Here’s a shot from NASA via Wikipedia:

The area under destruction is much larger than this clip. But by all means, go drive your car.

I’ve just started reading John Bellamy Foster’s essay in the December 2011 issue of Monthly Review. (Disclosure: It’s Marxist.) He quotes historian William McNeill:

…catastrophe is the underside of the human condition—a price we pay for being able to alter natural balances and to transform the face of the earth through collective effort and the use of tools.

The value of the oil accumulated in the Alberta tar sands is estimated to be an astonishing $14 trillion. The Rest of Us and our descendants are not in line for any of that money. Rather, we’ll pay the price for its extraction, transportation, refinement, and consumption. With so much at stake, it will be extremely difficult to keep the stuff in the ground. The “market” and ordinary greed argue for pollution on an unprecedented scale.

An appropriate nod back to MI-5, the BBC television series I’m watching. In the last episode some members of an environmental group called, I think, Divine Nature, set out to hold London hostage to an impending flood, occasioned, the show says, by global warming. The Brits have built a sophisticated barrier in the Thames that will prevent the flooding, which is the target of the group.

The group is demanding the release of a top secret report called “Aftermath.” Circumstances have conspired to compel a member of the British government to join ranks with Divine Nature, details of which I’ll forego here. Over the course of the episode it’s revealed that “Aftermath” is a joint study by the UK and the US. The essential points are as follows:

  • global warming is real
  • it will cause the deaths of millions
  • there will be strife and violence on a planetary scale
  • the countries are resigned to this, since there is insufficient political will to curb our addiction to fossil fuels
  • therefore, steps must be taken to control the oil and coal for the survivors

Scary stuff, but it rings true. I can certainly imagine that the Pentagon, the CIA, MI-6, and a host of other government agencies on both sides of the Atlantic have developed and continue to develop worst case scenarios related to climate change and its impacts. It occurs to me that there is a “doomsday” syndrome afoot not unlike General Buck Turgidson’s monologue in Kubrick’s Dr. Strangelove. From Wikidpedia:

Turgidson [played by George C. Scott] tries to persuade [President] Muffley [Peter Sellars] to seize the moment and eliminate the Soviet Union by launching a full-scale attack on the Soviet Union. The General believes the United States is in a superior strategic position and a first strike would destroy the majority of the Soviets’ missiles before they could retaliate.

Then-Secretary of State Madeleine Albright told then-Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff Colin Powell: “What’s the point of you saving this superb military for, Colin, if we can’t use it?” Well, we’ve still got this superb military, which really has less to do with fighting wars than acquiring, preserving, and protecting the earth’s natural resources, oil being chief among them.
