When ideology trumps policy

October 11, 2011 by davald

A familiar theme, to be sure, that policymakers’ belief systems trample reason and fact. We are in the midst of what could be a prolonged period of economic stagnation, marked by stubbornly high unemployment, declining incomes, and staggering wealth inequality. Yet, politicians exploit the pain and misery to further their own electoral ends.

A simple reason as to why the Rest of Us are both ignored and berated: the ideological conservative, whether inside or outside the Republican Party, cares not a whit for those different from them, which is generally white, rich, and cloistered. Should they also be religious, their privileged existence was preordained by god. So, tough luck, Charlie.

These thoughts recurred as I first read Joe Nocera’s column then skimmed the referenced report. The authors offer diagnosis, cures, and prognoses should their proposals be either followed or dismissed—the latter more likely, of course. In that predicted case, the U.S. and perhaps the entire planet will become Japan writ large. The report begins thusly:

Notwithstanding repeated attempts at monetary and fiscal stimulus since 2009, the United States remains mired in what is by far its worst economic slump since that of the 1930s. More than 25 million working-age Americans remain unemployed or underemployed, the employment-to- population ratio lingers at a near-historic low of 58.3 percent, business investment continues at historically weak levels, and consumption expenditure remains weighed down by massive private sector debt overhang left by the bursting of the housing and credit bubble a bit over three years ago. Recovery from what already has been dubbed the “Great Recession” has been so weak thus far that real GDP has yet to surpass its previous peak. And yet, already there are signs of a possible renewed recession.

The authors lay out a three-pillared recovery plan. Briefly,

  • pillar one: a multi-year program of public investment in infrastructure, including schools and transportation (both labor and material costs are at historic lows); they estimate that such a program would add 5.2 million annual jobs;
  • pillar two: debt restructuring, including mortgage relief for the victims of the housing bubble collapse;
  • pillar three: global reforms that address accumulated debt, currency exchanges, and current account balances; they even call for a global capital reserve to aid troubled economies.

While I have never been abroad (and those who know me can attest to my travel allergy), I imagine or recollect from reading that Europe’s parliaments tend to facilitate idea-based parties rather than the politics of dirt so prevalent in the U.S. republican system of winner-take-all. A paper of the kind mentioned above would be treated more seriously across the pond than by our Congress, and the proffered solutions would stand a better chance of being enacted.

Whether or not true, it’s obvious that our reputed policymakers seem inured to, well, policy. They are also loath to debate, discuss, argue, ponder, negotiate, or just plain think. Rather, members of Congress, or state legislatures, prefer soundbites. Thus we are treated to addled phrases and expressions, including: “class warfare”; “wealth envy”; and “punishing the job creators.”

I suspect that they get away with their puerile palaver because too many of the Rest of US prefer the simple and profane to the complex and sublime. After all, we put G.W. in the White House for eight long years because we chose resolve over rectitude. He may have been wrong, but at least we knew where he stood. Or something like that.

Liquidity traps

October 10, 2011 by davald

I must admit to some confusion regarding diagnoses of and prescriptions for economic conditions. I am hardly alone, of course, since the economics profession as a whole is so sharply disagreeable.

If you read Paul Krugman’s blog, for example, you will be exposed to much talk of liquidity traps. He argues that we’re in one. If we are, then monetary policies won’t work. He writes:

By contrast, the model of an economy in a liquidity trap, in which big increases in the monetary base don’t matter, comes through just fine.

He includes this chart in a recent post:

But tripling the money supply has had no effect on GDP.

If you do a search of ‘liquidity trap’ you will see multiple references to John Maynard Keynes as the originator of the concept, although he did not use the term in his General Theory. Keynes focused on uncertainty in Chapter 12 of his opus. Investors have little if any confidence that people will buy products and services, so they hoard their money. Consumers may have even less confidence, since so many of them are out of work and those who are working have seen their incomes decline. Moreover, they are using what little discretionary dollars they have to pay down their considerable debts.

If we are indeed in a liquidity trap right now, then expanding the money supply will not help grow the economy. Indeed, that is the very definition of ‘liquidity trap.’

Yet there is considerable confusion, as I said above, about conditions and their cure. Much of the confusion involves the notion of liquidity trap. In particular, are there lessons to be learned from the Great Depression that can be applied to present circumstances?

Christine Romer wrote a paper in 1992 entitled “What ended the Great Depression?” She concluded:

Plausible estimates of the effects of fiscal and monetary changes indicate that nearly all the observed recovery of the U.S. economy prior to 1942 was due to monetary expansion. A huge gold inflow in the mid- and late 1930s swelled the money stock and stimulated the economy by lowering real interest rates and encouraging investment spending and purchases of durable goods.

If she is correct, then there was no liquidity trap in the 1930s, since a growing money supply spurred economic growth, albeit slowly and modestly. But insofar as economists and policy makers insist that there was a liquidity trap during the Great Depression then they will be loath to advocate monetary expansion, since, be definition, it wouldn’t do any good.

But it seems that the Federal Reserve does not believe that we’re in a trap, since they have tripled the money supply, no doubt expecting GDP growth.

Well, if we really are in a trap, as Krugman et al aver, then alternatives to monetary expansion will have to be investigated and applied. The Keynesians, and Krugman is a proud one, push for a massive fiscal stimulus—upwards of two trillion or more. Such government spending won’t crowd out private investments, the usual rejoinder to fiscal strategies, since it’s clear that the rich are simply hoarding their dollars. They do not lack for money; they’re just unwilling to invest it in productive enterprise.

Meanwhile, the Rest of Us struggle as we wait for the economists and policymakers to fix the mess. Speaking of confidence…

A meaningful, spot-on protest

October 7, 2011 by davald

Finally people are getting agitated—and against the right targets: Roosevelt’s “malefactors of wealth.” And the protests are spreading, perhaps coming to a town near you.

Paul Krugman has a splendid column in this morning’s New York Times. He summarizes what has happened over the last three years:

In the first act, bankers took advantage of deregulation to run wild (and pay themselves princely sums), inflating huge bubbles through reckless lending. In the second act, the bubbles burst — but bankers were bailed out by taxpayers, with remarkably few strings attached, even as ordinary workers continued to suffer the consequences of the bankers’ sins. And, in the third act, bankers showed their gratitude by turning on the people who had saved them, throwing their support — and the wealth they still possessed thanks to the bailouts — behind politicians who promised to keep their taxes low and dismantle the mild regulations erected in the aftermath of the crisis.

I’ve posted ad nauseam on the Great Divergence in which the very, very wealthy have siphoned off the lion’s share of the country’s wealth while the Rest of Us have seen our incomes stagnate or decline. While nearly 10 percent can’t find a job and perhaps another similar amount are working far below their skills or previous wage levels, the fat cats of Wall Street go on about their business as if they were “doing God’s work.”

Say you were a millionaire

October 7, 2011 by davald

Tax rates have varied significantly since the beginning of the income tax in 1913. The marginal tax rate on the top incomes has gone from just seven percent in the first three years of the tax to a high of 94 percent in 1944, just before the end of WWII. But the threshold amounts to which the top rate applied have also varied considerably.

So, I asked myself: Suppose I were a millionaire. How much would Uncle Sam collect from me? I prepared this chart based on data found here.

The line represents inflation-adjusted dollars. That is, the millionaire in 1930 had as much purchasing power as the millionaire in 2011. But we can see that the millionaire living just after the income tax was established gave the government only $30,000. In the immediate post-war years the millionaire had to fork over about nine out of every ten dollars to the IRS. Today, the millionaire pays $350,000 in taxes. (I’m assuming that the million dollars is the amount subject to the tax, after deductions, etc.)

The US has a lot of millionaires. Of the world’s total (37,978,000) we have about a third, although estimates vary, with some pegging the number a bit higher, some much lower.)

I hasten to add that the above numbers refer to net worth, whereas the tax rate, of course, applies to annual income after deductions and exemptions. The number of individuals who make a million dollars or more a year was 235,649 in 2009, the last year for which IRS data are available. The next chart shows the distribution of tax returns by income level. (Source)

We’ll note that a plurality of returns came from people making between $50,000 and $75,000 a year, about 18.7 million of us. Nearly 110,000 individuals or families (if filed jointly) brought in between $1 million to $1.5 million. Over 8,000 tax returns came from those making $10 million or more. That group would include a fair number of professional athletes, as well as top-tiered movie stars and CEOs. At the other end of the scale we see that roughly 2.5 million people had no taxable income at all.


I will stick my neck out to say that those earning a million dollars or more a year have an easier if not also much better life than those earning below the poverty line. And if money equals utility (which I believe, for the most part, is true), then the rich have fared even better since the 1950s. Since the 1970s not so much for the Rest of Us.

Inevitable, yes, but…

October 6, 2011 by davald

…still a shock. Steve Jobs is dead.

I was sitting in front of the television watching the Phillies-Cardinals game with my iPad in my lap. A notice from MacWorld flashed on the display: “Steve Jobs has died.” I immediately went to the NY Times website. There it was under “breaking news.” Then to Apple’s website. Simple. Elegant. Final.

I had intended this morning to write about my memories of baseball. I’ll leave that to another day. What better occasion, now that Steve has succumbed to “Life’s best invention,” to share my Apple experiences, of which there are plenty.

It was in Bellevue that I first encountered an Apple product—the IIe. Clunky by today’s standards, but more polished than anything else in the storeroom. I was the owner of a dual-floppy (and I do mean floppy) CP/M machine, complete with a keyboard and a black-and-white monitor. Put in the system floppy. Voila, the ‘A’ prompt. Henceforth, nothing but keystrokes. Yet I did not purchase the IIe; a bit too expensive for me back then.

Fast forward to 1984. I’m in the Everett Mall, which then had a computer store. There it was: the Macintosh. You all remember the commercial directed by Ridley Scott. The 1984 Super Bowl brought the computer for the rest of us. I could not wait to get up close and personal. There on that tiny 9-inch screen was Microsoft Word. That’s right. Gates et al wrote for Apple before the PC. And it had a mouse! Type a few lines. Italicize this. Put these letters in bold. Copy, drag, and paste. Wow!

I finally bought my first Apple product two years later—the Macintosh Plus. I added a LaserWriter printer. Lots of money. But, hey, I was going into desktop publishing. Besides, I could write off the products as a business expense, which I did—legitimately. However, it was frustratingly difficult to draw accident scenes on it. (I consulted forensically on traffic accident reconstruction.) Slow with that tiny screen. But the printed product, after much painstaking work, looked neat, clean, and more professional than drafting old school, even if I did have to tape several letter-sized pages together. Also, I sold my IBM Selectric, since I could now do my reports and invoices on the Mac and LaserWriter. Heaven.

I have not stopped buying Apple products since that first Macintosh purchase. Here’s a list, which may not be exhaustive:

  • Macintosh II
  • PowerBook
  • Macintosh IIci
  • iMac (turquoise)
  • Newton
  • G4 desktop (PowerPC)
  • G5 desktop (PowerPC)
  • G4 laptop
  • MacPro (current)
  • MacBook Pro (passed on to my son)
  • iPad (first edition)
  • iPad 2
  • MacBook (for teacher-wife)
  • MacBook Air (for teacher-wife)
  • Time Capsule
  • Apple TV (1st and second editions)
  • iPod (80 GB)
  • iPod nano (for teacher-wife)
  • iPhone 3
  • iPhone 3s (for teacher-wife)
  • iPhone 4

(Note: If any of these get stolen, proof of my inventory is now in the clouds.)

During Apple’s dark days, between Steve Jobs’s tenures, the Marysville School District, where my wife teaches, decided to abandon Macs in favor of Wintel machines. I spoke against it at a public meeting, no doubt ineffectively, since passion ruled my mind. I warned the district that it would soon see a bloated IT staff and budget with technical problems that would overwhelm support personnel. Besides, I said, the cheap PCs would likely crash, break down, or just stop working altogether. I was right. To this day, unfortunately, the district has stuck with its unworkable and unmanageable PC-cum-Windows (still using XP) system, and teachers have to wait months for fixes. How much better for students and teachers alike if they could use MacBook Airs, iPads, Apple TVs connected to widescreen televisions—with everything connected wirelessly.

The PUD also jettisoned its mostly-Mac installed base a year or so before the school district. That decision—which probably made sense at the time given Apple’s downward trajectory—also led to much higher IT costs and a larger support staff. The utility is still using Windows XP. It’s familiar and most of the bugs have been worked out. There are a few isolated Macs in the communications department, which employs graphic designers.

Jobs never pushed his products to the enterprise. Instead, they are serving as Trojan Horses. Employees buy Apple products for their own use, then bring them to work. If they’re high enough in the food chain, they insist on IT’s accommodating their iPhones and iPads. Gradually, corporations are becoming more Apple friendly.

This includes the PUD, which is set to adopt iPhones rather than Android devices; iOS is more secure. This past week the board witnessed a demonstration of iPads, which will eventually be used by commissioners and upper management in lieu of printed binders. Of course, I was all over this, although my colleagues are a bit wary. The Trojan Horse has entered.

Apple products just work, an expression I’m sure that you’ve heard more than once. All of my old devices have either been sold or handed down to family and friends. They have not been tossed, because they still function—although I don’t remember what happened to the Newton.

While I am terribly saddened by the news of Jobs’s demise, I think Apple is just starting. While it’s the most valuable company on the planet, it will continue to be insanely great. Just look at all the patents the company has either registered or secured.

We ain’t seen nothing yet.

Whither Greece?

October 5, 2011 by davald

Europe is in a mess, mostly of its own choosing. The idea for a Eurozone and a common currency may have seemed attractive, certainly as a way to aggregate productive capacity and compete against North America (NAFTA) and the BRIC economies. And it worked—for a while. In the end, however, it was a terribly flawed concept, because Euro members, while sharing a common currency, were nevertheless viewed by themselves and others as sovereign, in politics and in economics.

Today, all eyes are on Greece and the Eurozone’s core nations, principally Germany and France, the continent’s two largest economies, in that order. They’ve already lent money to Greece, but only on the condition that Greeks tighten their belts more and more. The citizens there understandably resist. Why should we have to be punished for your sins? Why, indeed?

Greece has another option, one that does not bode well for the future of the Euro or its zone. (Yet neither does the present strategy.) It could default then withdraw from the Eurozone and reestablish its own currency, the drachma.

That’s the advice of economist Nouriel Roubini. He suggests that the current deal worked out between the core nations and Greece was a “rip-off.” It provided “zero” debt relief. He adds that the only way Greece (or the U.S., for that matter) can cure its debts is through economic growth. But austerity measures, those imposed by Germany et al, effectively prevent growth. Greece, like the U.S., should sharply devalue its currency with respect to others, which implies that it must return to the drachma.

So, what might this look like, Greece defaulting? We can get a clue from the Argentinian experience.

Argentina had fixed its currency to the peso and dollar, much as Greece’s is effectively fixed to the Euro. When its public debt soared an economic crisis followed, much as we’re seeing with Greece-cum-Euro. Argentina balked at austerity measures. It defaulted instead. As expected, capital quickly flowed out of Argentina to safer venues. Eventually Argentina’s leaders persuaded its creditors to accept reduced repayments, on the belief that something is better than nothing.

Argentina officially defaulted in 2002. It was only last year that it was able to reopen its debt exchange to bondholders.

Default, like personal bankruptcy, leaves a bad taste. It also punctures confidence. Why should I lend you money (buy your bonds) if it’s not clear I’ll be repaid? Once spurned, creditors need some time to lick their wounds then return.

If Greece takes Roubini’s advice (he’s not alone, of course), it will be in for a long period of economic yuck. But it, and the international financial system, could be better off in the long run. The alternative may be a series of falling dominoes knocking over the Rest of Us.

Fossil-fuel subsidies

October 5, 2011 by davald

All told, economic research seems to suggest that massive fossil-fuel subsidies actuallyhinder economic growth in the long run. The Global Subsidies Initiative conducted a review of six studies and found that “fossil-fuel subsidy reform would result in aggregate increases in gross domestic product (GDP) in both OECD and non-OECD countries. The expected increases among the studies ranged from 0.1 per cent in total by 2010 to 0.7 per cent per year to 2050.” Not too shabby.

Brad Plumer, writing for a Washington Post blog (Ezra Klein’s), argues that government subsidies to offset rising fuel costs are not only bad for the environment (remember global warming?) but also bad for the economy. Subsidies actually retard economic growth.

The linked study also found that “reforming” subsidies would cut greenhouse gas emissions by six percent. Not too shabby.

But here’s the kicker. Removing those subsidies during recessionary periods (we’re in one of those now) could exacerbate the aggregate demand problem. That’s because, in part, so much of our economy and so much of our transportation, both private and public, rely on fossil fuels. Moreover, coal-fired generation still accounts for over half of the nation’s electricity output.

Can you imagine a politician getting elected or staying in office championing higher fuel prices? It would clearly be the right thing to do, certainly for the environment but now, it seems, also for the long-run economy. However, electoral politics is all about the here and now.

As a people we’re quite content to kick all our problems down the road. Yes, we should protect the environment. But not now. Yes, we want long-term, sustainable economic growth. But not now. Yes, we should pour more money into our schools. But not now. Name your urgent concern. But not now.

I feel a sense of dread for my children and, should they arrive, grandchildren. They will be buried under so many cans.

What’s in a number?

October 5, 2011 by davald

The number 5, to be precise. Apple, despite rampant speculation, did not release a fifth generation iPhone, at least not one ending in the suffix ‘5.’ This prompted pervasive “disappointment.” Why? Because pundits and fanboys had been led to believe—based only on speculation and rumor, mind you—that new CEO Tim Cook would unveil the iPhone 5.

Also, watchers were “underwhelmed” by Mr. Cook’s “performance.” He’s no Steve Jobs. Fancy that.

But what did Apple present yesterday in Cupertino? Quite a bit, actually.

No, it’s not an iPhone 5; it’s an iPhone 4S. Get it: the ‘s’ substituted for ‘5.’ Apple did not change the “form factor,” an evidently favorite expression of geeks and technologists. The iPhone 4S looks just like the iPhone 4: same dimensions, although the S weighs three grams more.

It’s what’s under the hood that many pundits have overlooked. Here’s a table from Apple Insider comparing the specs of the “old” and the “new” iPhones.

What should matter most to users is both usability and capability. Let’s peruse the key features of the S:

  • a new A5 processor (same as in iPad 2) that will double CPU performance;
  • that same processor will boost graphics capabilities seven-fold;
  • for a hefty sum, you can purchase the S with 64 GB of RAM, equivalent to the high-end iPad;
  • extended battery life;
  • a “world phone” that can run on either GSM (AT&T and Sprint and Europe) or CDMA (Verizon)—I suspect that Apple will save some production costs here;
  • a new and greatly improved camera, with more megapixels and much better lenses; it’s also faster and much easier to use than the current iPhone, which already takes excellent pictures;
  • full 1080 HD video;
  • the new iOS software, version 5 (see, you do get a ‘5’ after all);
  • iCloud;
  • iTunes Match (all your music in the cloud—i.e., on Apple servers—making songs available on all Mac devices);
  • streaming of music, photos, and video—from the cloud and to your Apple TV-connected devices;
  • automatic backup;
  • wireless set up and transfer (no need to plug the iOS device into a computer); and
  • Siri (“your humble personal assistant”).

This new iPhone, though not a ‘5,’ is vastly superior to anything on the market.

As for acknowledging the surge in Android-based phones (cheaper and inferior), Apple will allow you to buy the existing iPhone 4 for under a $100 and the 3GS (which was twice as fast as the 3) can be had for nothing. Yep. Free. It will run iOS 5.

Don’t weep for Apple. It’s still just getting started.

The monster

October 4, 2011 by davald

From Hullabaloo:

Republicans have managed to muscle their way into power in this country by carefully nurturing a poisonous, nonsensical and unrealistic view of the world among somewhere between 30% and 40% of the American public. Like a foolish comic book or B-movie villain, they thought they could keep that monster in check to use for their own ends. In reality, that monster now controls their destiny.

A big part of me wants to know how that many Americans developed this “poisonous, nonsensical and unrealistic view of the world.” Why so many willfully ignorant people in this country?

Huge swaths of the American public are vulnerable to the most dastard and utterly false propaganda, and it’s been this way for quite some time. Consider what happened before the U.S. entered World War I. Most Americans favored neutrality. Indeed, Woodrow Wilson won re-election on the promise to “keep us out of war.” But he changed his mind, if he was ever sincere in the first place.

He appeared before Congress, had only said a few words, and the assembled body manifested instant bloodlust. You want war, Woodrow? By all means, yes, yes, yes!

Still, Americans appeared to have better things to do than volunteer for the army or scream “Kill the Huns.” As Chris Hedges tells us in his book The Death of the Liberal Class, Wilson established what became known as the Creel Commission. It very quickly turned around public opinion, from disinterest about the European battles to full throated enthusiasm for vanquishing the German military.

The propaganda effort could not have succeeded if more Americans possessed what Noam Chomsky calls “intellectual self-defense.” That is, it’s important to develop a b.s. meter. But too many of us must love this b.s., because we can’t seem to swallow enough of it.

By the way, one of the people behind the Creel Commission (known officially as the Committee for Public Information) was none other than Edward Bernays, the nephew of Sigmund Freud and the father of both advertising and propaganda. Indeed, George Creel wrote a memoir after the war was long behind us. It’s title was How We Advertised America.

So, why exactly are we in Afghanistan?

October 4, 2011 by davald

Most, if not all, electronics, from iPhones to LED lightbulbs to X-ray machines use rare-earth elements. As the name suggests, the elements are not found in abundance. The US is 100% dependent on imported rare-earth elements for the manufacture of its devices. China happens to have 92% of the known world total. But guess where we’ve found a bunch more?

Yep. Afghanistan.

The October 2011 issue of Scientific American tells us:

Geologists say newfound deposits in the embattled country could fulfill the world’s desire for rare-earth and critical minerals and end opium’s local stranglehold in the process.

Gosh. So that’s why all the hardware and killing.

Oh, and those costs. Past $461 billion and counting.
