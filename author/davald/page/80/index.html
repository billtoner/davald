Meddling Gates

June 19, 2013 by davald

These days whenever you read or hear about education reform you’ll likely notice that the Gates Foundation is busy behind the scenes. So I was not surprised to learn that the report card on teacher-education programs, pretty dismal in itself, received funding from the foundation, as the Seattle Times reports.

Readers of this blog are familiar with my opinions on the state of teacher training in America. It sucks, as compared with Finland’s, for example. The differences are stark, beginning with selectivity. Finland’s political and educational leaders determined a couple of decades ago that they would reverse the country’s then-mediocre schools by choosing only the best and the brightest to enter what became a very rigorous training curriculum.

The report by the National Council on Teacher Quality (NCTQ) reached an obvious conclusion, that most of our teacher-ed schools set a low bar for acceptance, then poorly prepare would-be teachers for the challenges they will face in the classroom. Well, duh.

Yet critics of the report were quick to pounce. From the Times:

Washington State University’s program was one of only 104 in the nation that made NCTQ’s honor roll for quality — earning three stars out of four.

While he was pleased by the ranking, WSU education dean Mike Trevisan said the evidence is thin that the programs cited as best or worst belong in those categories.

“This is a group that has a political agenda,” he cautioned, describing NCTQ as wanting “market forces to weed out weaker programs … and to increase the strength of programs that meet their expectations.”

In America money infects all. Let’s consider the recent high school graduate who must choose an academic program. Even tuition at public universities has quickly outpaced inflation, so that the typical college student departs with a huge debt, on average $25,000. Since teaching draws smaller salaries than other professions, it can take decades to retire the loan. Becoming a lawyer, doctor, or engineer promises higher income and a shorter repayment schedule. Moreover, teaching is hardly the noble calling it may have been, since, as the Everett Herald points out in its coverage of the report, most teacher-ed programs are:

…”churning out first-year teachers with classroom management skills and content knowledge inadequate to thrive in classrooms” with an ever-increasing diversity of ethnic and socioeconomic students, the report’s authors wrote.

In Finland, education is free to students, up to and including graduate school. Though the Nordic country spends less money on its public system, it graduates highly capable teachers who become part of a profession that enjoys as much, if not more, status than those of doctors and lawyers.

Of greater importance, perhaps, is that Finland eschews high-stakes testing like Washington state’s Measurement of Student Performance, formerly called ‘the WASL.’ Nor does it set schools up in a grand competition. The Gates Foundation, on the other hand, is about Arne Duncan’s Race to the Top and the Common Core Standards. All students will be expected to learn the same things, deemed important by others, including the Gates Foundation. U.S. teacher-education programs, then, receive higher marks if they focus on the Standards in training teachers. 

To conclude, I agree with the report’s obvious assessment on teacher-ed mediocrity. Where I differ is that Gates et al. aren’t really all that serious about creating genuine professionals and what that entails, from high reputation and generous financial support for education to teacher-based curricula and student evaluations—conspicuously lacking in the one-size-fits-all approach of Common Core and Race to the Top.

By the way, save for his commendable efforts to eradicate diseases, any initiative that receives financial support from the Gates Foundation should be viewed with suspicion. Somebody there is up to no good.

Expectation conundrum

June 19, 2013 by davald

When Federal Reserve chairman Ben Bernanke was a professor at Princeton, a post to which he will shortly return, he urged Japan’s central bank to target a higher expectation of inflation, and he did so in this paper (pdf). That was in 1999, a period of lingering economic stagnation. Faced with economic stagnation of our own, Bernanke tepidly sought to follow a bit of his own advice by committing the Fed to monetary stimulus, including reduced interest rates and money expansion. However, he stopped far short of targeting a higher inflation rate.

Today the New York Times reports that the U.S. central bank will curtail its bond purchases (aka “quantitative easing”) with the end goal of “winding down stimulus.”

The Federal Reserve chairman, Ben S. Bernanke, said on Wednesday that the central bank intended to reduce its monetary stimulus later this year — and end the bond purchases entirely by the middle of next year — if unemployment continued to decline at the pace that the Fed expected.

As we know market players tend to be skittish and generally overreact to the slightest news, especially signals of changed policies. Ironically, I think, Bernanke’s statement may frustrate the downward pace of the unemployment rate. In response to today’s announcement, stock prices fell sharply—both the Dow and S&P declined by 1.4 percent. The Times:

The program [bond purchases], in other words, is an effort to instill confidence in investors. And any reduction in the pace of purchases tends to undermine that message.

WTF? Oh, and in case you harbor notions that the Fed knows what it’s doing, consult this. The embedded chart speaks volumes.

A’coaling we will go

June 14, 2013 by davald

This New York Times article lays out the economics of coal exports. Simply put, there is still lots of coal in the U.S., but domestic consumption has declined, leaving exports as the attractive alternative. The problem: how to get more coal to Asia, the prime target? The solution: build more export terminals in the Pacific Northwest.

The Powder River basin produces more than 500 million tons of coal a year, nearly half the country’s total, but only about 15 million tons are exported every year, mostly because of the lack of export outlets. Some of the basin’s coal can be shipped down the Mississippi River to ports on the Gulf of Mexico, but that mostly goes to the crowded European market.

Coal experts say the basin could export 10 times what it does today if the Pacific Northwest terminals were approved, producing thousands of jobs and bolstering the economies of several states.

Whoa. If you don’t like coal trains going through your neighborhood now, you’ll absolutely hate 10 times as many, should the ports be built.

And what about global warming? What’s that? Oh, right, I seem to remember people talking about it a few years ago. It’s a big hoax, isn’t it?

Dr. Ben

June 14, 2013 by davald

Uncertainty. That seems to describe the state of the economy and its principal actors. How else to explain the jitteriness, the volatility? A case in point:

Amid the current turmoil in global markets, one question is being obsessively debated on Wall Street: Just what was Ben S. Bernanke thinking three weeks ago when he said that the Federal Reserve might soon cut back its stimulus efforts?

The reaction of market indexes?

Since May 22, when Mr. Bernanke made those remarks, global stock markets have lost $3 trillion in value, according to Bank of AmericaMerrill Lynch.

How fragile things are that a mere suggestion, a hint, perhaps, can torpedo stock prices? The finance-dominated economy sits on a house of cards. A whisper, a turn of phrase, a raised eyebrow sends shockwaves.

The Times‘s Peter Eavis tries to divine Bernanke’s intentions. He discusses several possibilities, and I quote:

  • a bone for the hawks
  • bursting bubbles
  • dress rehearsal
  • a tap on the brakes

The hawks, in this case, are those who disagree with the Fed’s quantitative easing programs and the expansion of the monetary base. They have consistently warned that such moves will create hyperinflation, for example, though their predictions have been wrong, repeatedly. Keep in mind that the Federal Reserve has not yet changed policies; it’s still officially committed to fostering higher employment, as Bernanke said, whether or not the efforts prove successful, and so far they have not made much of a dent in the unemployment rate. But things could be worse, I suppose, without the Fed’s actions.

One criticism of easy money is that it enables the bubbling of assets, including, of course, real estate. Money is so cheap these days, but where to spend it in a period of uncertainty? Eavis:

There is no gigantic, overarching bubble right now that could harm the wider economy. But over the last two years, as the Fed has pumped money into the financial system, large markets have been driven higher by significant amounts of speculation.

A Fed governor, Jeremy C. Stein, has highlighted the risks in some of them. On Wall Street, with interest rates this low for so long, it has become easy to make bets with borrowed money. But such investments can unwind violently with even the slightest tightening of credit. Mr. Bernanke may have wanted to throw a little bit of sand into this giant leverage machine.

It could be that Bernanke is setting the stage for the “paring of purchases” in the not-too-distant future. After all, at some point the central bank will have to change course. Right? But when and under what conditions? The unemployment rate remains high and there are still no signs of inflation. And low inflation may be a problem, as Professor Bernanke suggested some years ago in writing (pdf) on Japan’s stagnation. In deference to his hawks, perhaps, he’s so far stayed clear of his previous prescription of having the government commit to higher inflation.

Or Bernanke may have indeed intended to send a signal that he will indeed curtail, if not altogether cease, bond purchases. Again, I don’t see that conditions warrant departure. But I’m not the Fed chairman with access to all sorts of data.

In the main I find it disturbing that we once had an economy that was boringly productive for the Rest of Us but that we now have an economy dominated by financial interests whose sole obsession is creating money, lots of it. And they don’t do this the old-fashioned way by making products and services that the Rest of Us consume. Rather, they appear to fashion things out of thin air that they somehow render valuable through sheer speculation. In this environment there is no unworthy derivative, to hell with underlying assets.

Get an education

June 14, 2013 by davald

Or, perhaps not.

A couple of op-ed columns in this morning’s New York Times, one by David Brooks the other by Paul Krugman, address the plight of workers, albeit from completely different perspectives. Brooks bemoans the changing mores of the wealthy, suggesting that “seemliness” has given way to more conspicuous, in-your-face consumption. Krugman invokes the Luddites, suggesting that most of us are similarly situated in the modern economy.

Brooks begins with a story of Walter Judd, who would serve 20 years in Congress after becoming a doctor. Denied financial assistance by his father, Judd was able to work his way through college cleaning dishes for the local Y.M.C.A. That, of course, would be impossible to pull off today, since tuition costs greatly exceed the wages of washing. (Note: I was able to pay for a university education by cleaning the slopes of the football stadium and washing pots and pans at a fraternity. But that was a long time ago.) After acknowledging the remarkableness of Judd’s story, Brooks then writes, curiously:

More important, that people then were more likely to assume that jobs at the bottom of the status ladder were ennobling and that jobs at the top were morally perilous. That is to say, the moral status system was likely to be the inverse of the worldly status system. The working classes were self-controlled, while the rich and the professionals could get away with things.

I think that Brooks is too quick to generalize from an inference about Judd and washing dishes. First off, it seems that Judd had little choice but to find work if he was to complete college. Second, it’s clear that Judd was not content to stay in the kitchen; he aspired to become something much more, including a national political figure. That Judd parlayed his physician’s degree into missionary work suggests the importance of religion in his life, which is one of Brooks’s principal themes. The presumed moral precepts that may have governed or guided Judd’s career, from college to Congress, had their roots in the Bible, Brooks avers. Maybe. From this, Brooks laments:

Over the years, religion has played a less dominant role in public culture.

It’s quite possible that the rich don’t bother going to church any more, if they ever did, but it’s quite a stretch, I think, to say that “religion has played a less dominant role in public culture.” Has he forgotten about the rabid Republicans of the Christian right, who appear to have gained a foothold in the party, essentially determining the outcomes of primary elections?

One thing is clear, however. There can be no more Walter Judds, or yours truly, for that matter. Religion or no, part-time menial labor cannot purchase a college degree. Which brings me to Krugman.

The Princeton professor wonders if we have been forced into neo-Luddite situations. He quotes from the originals:

“How are those men, thus thrown out of employ to provide for their families?” asked the petitioners. “And what are they to put their children apprentice to?”

Today’s youth are urged to go to college to gain new or different abilities. But at what cost? As Brooks points out, the path of Walter Judd is no longer available. Instead, as Krugman reminds us:

…we go deep into debt to acquire the skills we’re told we need, only to learn that the economy no longer wants those skills…

Krugman’s prescription will surely be denounced by the religion-soaked conservatives.

Education, then, is no longer the answer to rising inequality, if it ever was (which I doubt).

So what is the answer? If the picture I’ve drawn is at all right, the only way we could have anything resembling a middle-class society — a society in which ordinary citizens have a reasonable assurance of maintaining a decent life as long as they work hard and play by the rules — would be by having a strong social safety net, one that guarantees not just health care but a minimum income, too. And with an ever-rising share of income going to capital rather than labor, that safety net would have to be paid for to an important extent via taxes on profits and/or investment income.

Not going to happen.

Are taxes a form of austerity?

June 13, 2013 by davald

Matthew Yglesias writes:

In fact the general trend over the past several years among people on the left to start using “austerity” as a meaningless signifier for “policies I don’t like” has had an unfortunate negative impact on the quality of discourse. When state governments cut back social services in the face of falling tax revenue and rising employee health care and pension costs, that is a kind of austerity. But the available alternative of raising taxes is also a form of austerity.

I have been using ‘austerity’ to refer to ‘cutbacks in government spending,’ especially acute among the states, which typically have to balance their budgets. In the face of recession-triggered reductions in revenues, they have chosen to reduce expenditures on public services rather than raise taxes, a politically difficult choice.

But I have to wonder how raising taxes is necessarily a form of austerity. Yglesias seems to be suggesting that austerity can imply economic contraction. If so, then the answer depends.

If governments raise taxes on the wealthy, say the top one percent, would that in itself retard economic growth? It could if the dollars the rich pay in taxes were otherwise spent on productive activities. Yet the rich spend a disproportionately lower percentage of their income than the Rest of Us, who can use every penny we make. Here’s Joseph Stiglitz, writing for Vanity Fair:

The relationship is straightforward and ironclad: as more money becomes concentrated at the top, aggregate demand goes into a decline. Unless something else happens by way of intervention, total demand in the economy will be less than what the economy is capable of supplying—and that means that there will be growing unemployment, which will dampen demand even further. In the 1990s that “something else” was the tech bubble. In the first dec­ade of the 21st century, it was the housing bubble. Today, the only recourse, amid deep recession, is government spending—which is exactly what those at the top are now hoping to curb.

Also, we know that during the 50s and 60s the marginal tax rate at the federal level was much, much higher than it is today. Nevertheless, the economy grew at a far faster clip than it does currently when tax rates are low.

So, I would argue that increasing taxes on the wealthy, either by raising the marginal rate or by instituting Robert Frank’s consumption tax, could actually spur economic growth by stopping the flow of dollars from the spending bottom to the hoarding top. Here’a s chart I’ve used before from the Federal Reserve showing the Gini index and the velocity of money, the latter decreasing over time with increased wealth concentration.

 

During the relative halcyon days of the post-war era, the federal government balanced its budgets, collecting sufficient revenues to meet expenses, which continued to grow. With the fall in tax rates on both personal and corporate income, revenues have declined but federal spending has not, leaving us with a series of deficits and higher debt.
