The dink over the bam

April 23, 2013 by davald

The Mariners defied history and predictions by leading the Major Leagues in bashing home runs and extra bases during spring training. This anemic lineup hit more balls over fences than any other club. But that was then. Now that things have started in earnest, the M’s are retreating to form, whiffing rather than contacting.

And hitting the baseball is all about making contact, a simple fact oblivious to Seattle’s offense. You have a bat in your hand, why not use it to hit the damn ball?

When I played the game a very long time ago, we’d begin our individual batting practice regimen by bunting the ball from left to right. The idea was to get better at bunting, of course, but also to gain some feel for the bat making contact with the ball. The hope was that feel would carry over into a full swing.

As I watch the Mariners at the plate I wonder if they have any sense whatsoever as to what it’s like to place lumber to leather, round to round? Let’s take Justin Smoak, for example.

In the parlance of M’s announcers Mike Blowers and Dave Sims, he is a “strong man.” And he takes a mighty swing. If only Smoak would actually hit the ball we know that it would travel high and far. But he rarely brings the two materials together, wood and leather.

Like many Seattle batters, Smoak takes the first pitch. Opposing managers and coaches aren’t dumb. They pay attention to such things. So they instruct their pitchers to throw that first pitch over the plate, assuming with very good reason that the M’s hitters will just listen for the umpire’s “strike one.” Already behind in the count, the Mariner hitters are quickly at the mercy of the pitcher, who now has more options on speed, curvature, and location. It must be fun.

The other night I accurately predicted a Smoak at-bat. The first pitch would be right down the middle and Smoak would watch it go by. It happened. I then predicted that the next pitch would be a breaking ball thrown at knee level. Smoak would swing and miss. Yep. Then the pitcher, feeling no need to waste a pitch, would throw a fastball above the zone, inducing Smoak to take another of his mighty but empty swings. Sure enough.

For whatever reason Seattle casts a spell on Mariner hitters. They lose all sense of aggressiveness. They prefer to watch and imagine, as if they were playing whiffle ball in a neighbor’s backyard while sipping a latte. Why should things be any different in The Bigs?

What do I mean by aggressiveness? Simple. Each Mariner must be both willing and able to swing at the first good pitch. Since the odds are that the best pitch will be the first, Mariners should step into the box prepared to hit rather than take that initial offering.

I would suggest one more thing. There is in baseball the expression of “opposite field.” The M’s batters haven’t heard of it. There is only half,  never a full path to success. They approach the plate with the intention of pulling the ball, exclusively.

Take shortstop Brendan Ryan, for example. Here’s a guy who never got off the interstate as a Mariner.* He doesn’t hit his weight. Yet, there he is batting ninth swinging as if he were Michael Morse, another “strong man” in the Mariner’s lineup. He swings hard and up, regardless of where the pitch is located. That he makes any contact at all astounds. What should he do?

There’s a guy who bats second for the San Francisco Giants. His name is Marco Scutaro. He is so confident that he can actually put the bat to the ball, that he can afford to get deep into the count. Two strikes. No problem. Why? Because Scutaro has only one thing on his mind: hit the ball. He’s not going for home runs. More often than not he wants to punch the ball to the opposite field. He keeps his swing nearly level with the ground, with a slight upward trajectory, so that the bat is on the same plane as the pitched ball.

Ryan, on the other hand, swings as if he’s in a water well and the only space is up. WTF? Also, he pulls away from the plate from a decidedly open stance to begin with. There is just no way that he can force the ball to the right between first and second. Pathetic.

If I were management, I’d have Ryan bunting the hell out of the ball, close his stance, and force him to think horizontal rather than vertical. And punch, man, punch. Beyond that the coaches should take the pressure off Ryan as a batter to let him focus on what he does best, fielding the ball on defense. He’s the top glove at his position, so he brings value to the team even when he swings aimlessly at the plate.

Dustin Ackley, whom I really want to succeed, shares many of the defects exhibited by his colleagues. He loves to watch pitches, and he’s rarely prepared to attack the first good pitch that he sees. As a result, he often winds up swinging foolishly at a breaking ball in the dirt or a fastball over his head. His stance, like Ryan’s, is also open. He needs to put his front foot closer to the plate and think opposite field. Home runs are nice. But, Dustin, you’ll never be a Ken Griffey, Jr. The Mariners need you on base, not walking disgustedly back to the dugout.

There have been some bright spots in the batting order. Kyle Seager gets the aggressive part. You know that he can’t wait to hit that ball hard, no matter where it’s pitched. I’ve always liked Kelly Shoppach behind the plate, and he’s now leading the club in hitting, though with few at bats. Besides he provides a veteran influence to a young pitching staff. Michael Saunders has continued to improve at the plate. I look forward to his return to the lineup, after his shoulder paid a visit to a right field wall. Pitching, as expected, is solid. Felix has been “the King.” Hisashi Iwakuma has been nothing short of amazing. There are some really good arms in the pen.

But until the Mariners figure out how to hit the damn ball with consistency, they and their dwindling fan base are in for a very long, dismal season. Even if the owners continue to rake in the dough with their lucrative television contract.

* I’ve been hearing this term more and more lately. A batter who gets off the interstate is hitting above .200. Those below that threshold stay on the interstate. That’s the extent of my surmise.

Boston vs. West

April 23, 2013 by davald

I wrote a bit on the differences between the events of Boston and West, Texas. The former, of course, involved deadly bombs detonated near the finish line of the fabled marathon. The latter, which took more lives, was deemed an industrial accident—a fertilizer plant explosion.

Writing for the New York Times, Bruce Machart expands on a similar theme.

We tend to discount that which is accidental as somehow less tragic, less interesting, less newsworthy than the mayhem of agency. Lives have been “lost” in Texas, but in Boston, by God — lives have been “taken.”

But this distinction means nothing to the victims or, I imagine, to their families. In Boston, in West, whether by sinister design or by accident, whether on a television-ready stage or hidden away in a rural factory, when people are hurt, when lives are lost, the essential human cost shouldn’t be lost on the living.

Indeed.

Krugman’s mouthful of spit

April 23, 2013 by davald

It’s a big one, and that’s good. For the hell he charges is large and formidable.

I never cease to wonder about where the man gets his energy. He’s both indefatigible and ubiquitous, traveling more in a single week than I’ve traveled in a lifetime. In between his myriad appearances he manages to teach a class or two at Princeton and write two columns a week for the New York Times and numerous blog posts. He’s also squeezed in a book or two.

But it’s that volume of intellectual and rhetorical spit that amazes. He has almost singlehandedly taken on those he calls ‘the Very Serious People,’ a term used to describe policymakers and pundits, and he may eventually prevail in his quest to crumble their Edifice of Wrong.

In particular, Professor Paul Krugman has unabashedly shamed those who have preached austerity in the face of the world’s second worst depression. The policy of “fiscal consolidation,” otherwise known as the slashing of central government spending, has proved disastrous, as tens of millions of people who want and need to work go begging for nonexistent jobs and public services get chopped and diced by fact-resistant ideologues.

Consider his recent chart on Europe’s situation posted here:

 

The Very Serious People evidently relied in part on the work of professors Carmen Reinhart and Ken Rogoff. The two Harvard economists published a paper in a noted journal in which they claimed that once government debt exceeded 90 percent of a respective country’s GDP, economic growth faltered. The economists reached their conclusion after studying historical data. If their opinion is true, then the following chart should give us pause:

 

U.S. federal debt is now more than 100 percent of GDP. We must be in trouble.

As it happens, we are. But, as Krugman and others have suggested, the reason that debt is so high is because the economy continues to falter. That is, Reinhart and Rogoff have got the causal relationship backwards. A badly underperforming economy has starved governments at all levels of tax revenues that they would otherwise be receiving in normal times.

Yet, even if we suppose that R-R had the causality right, as it happens their analysis was deeply flawed, embarrassingly so. Economist Nancy Folbre describes the academic mess for the New York Times here.

Those of us who use Excel can appreciate coding errors, like forgetting a few rows in a summation equation. It appears that R-R committed such a mistake. Moreover, they cherry picked data while ignoring the possibility that cause and effect worked in the opposite direction.

The Edifice of Wrong appears to be crumbling, due in no small part to the efforts of Krugman et al. When, we may ask, will the policymakers concede defeat and change their tune? Or must we hope that Krugman has an unending supply of spit?

No tears for the bastard

April 23, 2013 by davald

After putting the screws to the Rest of Us for decades, Max Baucus decides that he will not run for re-election. But he will not go quietly into the retirement night; he’s more recently engaged in scuttling the proposed Internet tax, a measure that would bring much-needed revenue to fiscally starved states.

Now I would not let Baucus’s provenance guide your answer to this question: What will Max do after he leaves the Senate? If you think he’ll repair to a ranch to demonstrate his affection for cows and horses, you haven’t been paying attention. The man who did his utmost best to line the pockets of monied interests no doubt envisages a sure path to untold personal riches. He did the bidding of the wealthy while in office. He fully expects to be handsomely rewarded once he leaves. Indeed, I’m guessing that he’s already received a substantial downpayment on his future.

Writing for the New York Times, Jonathan Weisman reports:

As recently as Friday, aides to Mr. Baucus indicated that he had no intention of retiring. He had amassed a $5 million campaign treasury and was in New York last week meeting with donors. His votes against gun legislation promoted by President Obama and against the Democratic leadership’s budget plan seemed calculated to appeal to conservative voters in his state.

But those votes isolated Mr. Baucus from other members of the Senate Democratic Caucus in ways reminiscent of 2001, when he infuriated Democrats by working with President George W. Bush to pass a 10-year, $1.35 trillion tax cut.

This week, over Mr. Baucus’s fierce opposition and without the Finance Committee’s imprimatur, Democratic leaders forced Internet sales tax legislation to the Senate floor. Montana is one of four states without a sales tax, and Mr. Baucus has tried to rally opposition to the Internet bill, which is championed by Senator Richard J. Durbin of Illinois, the Senate’s No. 2 Democrat.

“Baucus versus the Caucus” has become a catchphrase in the halls of the Capitol.

Good riddance to bad rubbish. Baucus exemplifies all that is wrong with Congress.

The nature of death

April 22, 2013 by davald

There were two major events that once again shattered everyday lives. First there was Boston. Then there was Texas. In each event, people died: three from the bombs set off near the marathon’s finish line; perhaps dozens killed by a massive explosion at a fertilizer plant. Dying defined their commonality. All else was different.

Our reactions differed, certainly. We immediately spoke of “tragedy” and “senseless violence” when describing the Boston bombs. Who did this? Who could have done this, and why? The media quickly filled the airwaves; there was much to be said. The ensuing dragnet focused on two suspects. One was killed by police. His younger brother was eventually apprehended. We were interested—big time.

In West, Texas, a fertilizer plant exploded. There was nothing much left, and hundreds are reported missing, with more than a dozen confirmed dead. It was described as “an accident,” as if no one is responsible for what occurred. Boston had perpetrators. Exploding fertilizer plants don’t. But I would ask: Who did this? Who could have done this, and why?

There will be a post-accident investigation, of course. People will endeavor to identify the “proximate cause” of the explosion and elements of the causal chain. This will no doubt take a very long time. In the end, there will likely be litigation. Fines may be assessed. By then we may have forgotten about “the accident.” After all, accidents happen all the time and we no longer remember them.

We will remember Boston and derive satisfaction from knowing that the evildoers faced justice. Exploding bombs in crowds is not the stuff of accidents. Exploding fertilizer plants, or petroleum refineries, or coal mines, or nuclear power facilities are. And that makes all the difference, save for the victims and their families.

Why Congress frustrates the people’s will

April 20, 2013 by davald

The measures recently rejected in the Capitol had the support of the people. While I’m not about to post a treatise on the reasons why Congress chose to deny the people’s will, I can point to an obvious factor. It has everything to do with the conflict between demographics and electoral districts, and that conflict is most extreme in the U.S. Senate. It is less so in the House, but even there the will of the people is denied in large part because of recent gerrymandering.

Consider this map from Wikipedia.

 

The darker the color the greater the density. Yellow depicts very low population density. If our nation’s representatives were apportioned in accordance with this map, legislative outcomes would surely be different, as in far more liberal and progressive.

Those yellow areas are vast swatches of thinly populated rural territory. Yet, in the Senate especially, the interests of the few (yellow) prevent the interests of the many (purple) from becoming law.

Those of us who live in urban centers, and 82 percent of Americans do, tend to be Democratic, liberal, and progressive. We’re more educated than our rural counterparts and make more money. We urbanites prefer: restrictions on guns, higher taxes on wealth, universal health care, a woman’s right to choose, racial and sexual tolerance, and a cleaner environment. People who live in Kansas, say, prefer the opposite, or nearly so.

As things now stand, the minority holds sway in Congress. Not very democratic, if you ask me.

Political dysfunction

April 19, 2013 by davald

The U.S. Senate may be the least democratic institution on the planet, giving the folks in Wyoming as much power as those in California, despite the latter outnumbering the former 66 to 1. So much for one person one vote and the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th and 5th Amendments. Moreover, as the world’s “most deliberative body,” a fine euphemism for “nothing gets done around here,” the Senate, with its skewed representation and byzantine rules and procedures, guarantees that little of helpful consequence passes out of its chamber.

For those who relish, if not worship, the idea of limited government, the Senate is made to order. If you believe that government is best when it governs least, then fall on your knees in praise of senators and their institution.

This would beg an empirical question: Are we better off, for the most part, when Congress does nothing? This, in turn, suggests another: Could we abide the absence of positive legislation if we could avoid the most pernicious?

I, for one, suspect that Congress is very good at making bad laws that harm the Rest of Us as it fails to do much that benefits. It okays unnecessary wars. It gives tax breaks to the rich. It denies more generous public services enjoyed by counterparts throughout the western world. It actually encourages environmental destruction through gross subsidies to injurious corporations, while refusing to take climate change seriously.

So, here’s my take. As long as our D.C. officials continue to damage the Rest of Us, we cannot simply sit by, believing that its dysfunction is a blessing in disguise.

For me, the elder, I will continue to throw epithets at the elected evildoers, noting that Bastard Baucus is just one of far too many. My street protest days are history. I’ll let Shakespeare console, though I no longer use a pen and have never wielded a sword.

Inflation vs. growth

April 19, 2013 by davald

Despite dire warnings about impending inflation, the annual change in the consumer price index remains low by historical standards. The post-War average is 3.7 percent, year to year. It has been below that level since 2008, when it was 3.8, and the last time before that was in 1991, when it reached 4.2 percent.

Those who are owed money or sit on piles of it prefer low inflation rates to ensure that their money retains value. Those who owe money would prefer higher inflation rates, for opposite reasons. Since the beginning of the Great Divergence, about the time of Reagan’s reign, inflation has fallen. Let’s look at this chart from the St. Louis Fed (FRED).

 

One could believe that the dramatic change in the C.P.I. under Reagan was simple accident or coincidence, but I wouldn’t. Rather, it occurs to me that the monied interests didn’t like seeing the real value of their assets diminish under higher-than-normal inflationary periods, and, as we see from the above chart, inflation was steadily climbing from the 60s to Reagan’s first year in office. With only two exceptions, mentioned above, the inflation rate since Reagan has stayed below the post-War average.

So, the rich must be pleased. As for the Rest of Us, the real value of our wages has either stagnated or declined. Many of us were able to sustain our lifestyles only by borrowing, mostly via home equity. When the housing bubble collapsed, so did that option.

We hear a lot about economic growth and how important it is to boost employment and, perhaps, increase wages. But here’s an interesting comparison. When the economy is growing so is inflation. Look at this chart showing the change in C.P.I. and the change in GDP from 1948 to 2012.

 

Is it possible that the talk of growth is disingenuous at best? We know that the percentage of GDP devoted to wages has been steadily falling since the Great Divergence while capital’s share has grown. Although the economic pie could be larger, as it would be with higher GDP growth rates, asset holders appear to be comfortable with stagnation. They are extracting more and more money from the economy and what they own retains its value, given low inflation rates.

The Rest of Us, however, would benefit from higher growth rates. However, if there is a strong correlation between growth and inflation, and that one can’t happen without the other, then our rulers will make certain that low inflation wins at the expense of a more robust economy.

And one way to stymie growth is to contract government spending, as Keynes understood well. So we have austerity, the Rest of Us be damned.
