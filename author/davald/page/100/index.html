Speaking of grumps

February 13, 2013 by davald

I’m a lapsed Catholic, though that term seems inappropriate, since I have no intention of returning to the church, the church of my youth and indoctrination. I miss some elements of that experience, as even an abused child might strangely cherish the familiar, since that is all that he or she knows. But I said goodbye decades ago and now wonder why I stayed so long.

As I enter the first year of my Social Security checks I’m old enough to remember mass in Latin. I can still recite the Lord’s Prayer in the “language of the dead.” During my lifetime the liturgy changed—for the better, I think. I understand English. The priest faces the people, and the people don’t have to kneel as much. One can now chew the wafer, or should I say “the body of Christ.” (Robert Heinlein must have had the transubstantiation-cum-communion in mind when he wrote Stranger in a Strange Land. Grokking, anyone?) The nuns are gone, or at least their habits and clanging rosaries. The music is more contemporary and far less hoary and mysterious, though I am still fond of some ancient hymns.

My reminiscences give way to a pervasive sense of tragedy. The church could have been a contender. That it failed to be one I blame the reactionary minds of a series of popes and cardinals who collectively told the modern world to shut the fuck up. They proclaimed celibacy forever, confined women to subordinate roles, and targeted all things sexual, save for the perversities of their own. Squeezing the bounds of acceptable belief and discourse, the increasingly rigid hierarchy demanded fealty to a doctrine straight from the Dark Ages, Enlightenment be damned.

John Cassidy also grew up Catholic. Writing for the New Yorker he, too, rues the rightward turn of John Paul II and Benedict, née “The Rat,” recalling with some hint of regret his youthful tenure with the clerics, the “sisters,” and the accoutrements of religious ritual. With similar nostalgia he sensed potential in the reforming breezes of Vatican II and John XXIII. Alas, the doors were quickly shuttered with the accession of a Polish cardinal, who, as Cassidy reminds us, heightened the conflict between his more liberal political sentiments and strict adherence to rigid orthodoxy.

In theological and practical terms, though, he was a dreadful throwback. With the Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger, the future Benedict XVI, at his side, as the Vatican’s chief theologian, he set about unmaking much of the modernization project of the previous twenty years. He issued lengthy and emphatic rulings condemning abortion, birth control, and homosexuality. He dismissed calls for the relaxation of the celibacy rules for priests, and for the ordination of women. He criticized liberation theology and surrounded himself with dyed-in-the-wool conservatives like Ratzinger. Within the hierarchy of the Church, questioning traditional teachings, even gently, became a potential career-ender.

And so Benedict, infirm and decrepit, decides to step down “for the good of the church.” Yet, there will be no chance in hell that his successor will deviate an iota from the church’s inexorable march to irrelevancy. The last two popes, by selecting only the narrow-minded as cardinals, ensured that the Vatican will defiantly resist any and all efforts to join the modern world.

The robots did it

February 11, 2013 by davald

Why is American society so unequal? Some blame technology: robots and other electronic devices have reduced the need for human labor, which means an expanding surplus of people with nothing to do.

In a recent op-ed for the New York Times Harvard economist Greg Mankiw writes:

Over the last several decades, for the most part, the wages of workers without any higher education have stagnated, while the wages of those with advanced degrees have risen. The main forces driving these trends are technological change, which tends to increase the demand for skilled workers relative to unskilled workers, and, to a lesser extent, international trade.

But economist Dean Baker begs to differ:

Mankiw asserts as a fact that technology is responsible for the upward redistribution of income over the last three decades, but it is not clear that the evidence supports his story. After all technology had a much larger impact in increasing productivity in the decades from 1947 to 1973 yet workers shared in these gains more or less equally.

A chart may suffice.

What about productivity?

Hmmm. When worker productivity was at its highest, in the post-War decades, workers took a larger share of income.

As I recall, there was a lot of new technology in those days, especially at the consumer level, from wide-scale adoption of the telephone and television to cars and freeways and airplanes. (As a three-month-old infant I accompanied my mother on a very expensive voyage from New York to San Francisco. I’m told that the plane was a propeller-driven DC 9. That would have been in 1947. Fast forward to 1969, the year I graduated from Berkeley. I traveled by jet, I tell you, from San Francisco to Philadelphia. In that period our family switched from a rotary telephone on a party line to a touch-tone, single-line device. We purchased a color television, and transistor radios became ubiquitous. My first official job was working with business-class mainframe computers in New Jersey; there were none until the 60s. I could go on. Oh, and they put a man on the moon.)

Dean Baker offers some alternative explanations for rising inequality:

The villains that some of us would point to are anti-union measures by government and businesses that have weakened workers’ bargaining power, trade policy that was designed to put less-educated workers in competition with people in the developing world while largely protecting the most highly educated workers, patent and copyright policy that increased the rents pulled out of the economy for these monopolies, and macroeconomic policy that has led to more unemployment in the last three decades than in the early post-war period. High unemployment tends to disproportionately hit less educated workers, both by having more impact on their probability of being unemployed and reducing their wages.

Wrong medicine

February 8, 2013 by davald

Republican political doctors prescribe austerity—always. The government, we are to believe, spends too much money, which adds to the fiscal debt. So, the remedy is to curb spending now and forever.

Paul Krugman rebukes John Boehner in his column this morning. Boehner said that Congress had to “deal with its spending problem,” and that he’s tired of kicking the can down the road. Krugman argues that given current recessionary conditions, marked by high unemployment and sputtering GDP, we should continue kicking the can down the road. Now is no time to tighten our fiscal belts. Besides, where was Boehner when his man in the White House, G.W.Bush, squandered the Clinton surplus by cutting taxes and launching two expensive wars?

In the above chart we’ll notice that federal spending as a percentage of GDP remains fairly flat since 1981, between roughly 20 and 25 percent of GDP. The ratio fell under Clinton, then began to rise sharply under Bush II. We’ll also note that GDP (black line) dropped at the end of 2007, the beginning of the Great Recession. That is, the denominator fell, so that even if spending levels stayed the same, the ratio would increase.

Since 2010, however, federal spending has begun to fall as a percentage of GDP. In part that’s because GDP has started inching upwards. But spending itself has also stabilized.

Would Boehner want the line to fall? Has that ever happened?

 

We can see that only twice since 1947 has federal spending dropped against the previous year. On both occasions, 1950 and 1954, the country was in a mild recession. Also, military spending fell in the latter year with the end of the Korean War.

 

Krugman:

Start with a basic point: Slashing government spending destroys jobs and causes the economy to shrink.

He cites the experiences of Ireland and the U.K., whose governments both cut federal spending only to see their countries experience dramatic contractions in GDP. Since Washington has also cut spending, with defense down by 22 percent in the last fiscal quarter, our economy continues to sputter.

Yet, despite the problems associated with austerity, if we are to believe Krugman, the European Union leaders just capitulated to demands of U.K. prime minister David Cameron; the EU nations will cut spending. Why would the EU take advice from Cameron, whose economy is doing much worse than ours?

It’s this kind of major disconnect between policy makers and at least some economists that I find disturbing. Shouldn’t there be some sort of consensus on the prescriptions? At what point do Cameron, Boehner, et al. reconsider their positions? Do they know something that Krugman, Stiglitz, DeLong, and Bernanke don’t know? What could it be?

Carbon tax, continued

February 7, 2013 by davald

Should we tax the things we don’t want or desire less of? The economist Arthur Pigou believed that market transactions often create negative externalities. A good example would be our buying then consuming gasoline. Burning gas yields carbon dioxide, which in sufficient amounts will surely doom the planet. This negative externality is not (yet) incorporated into the price of the commodity; Pigou suggested a tax to rectify the “market failure.” A carbon tax is therefore referred to as a Pigovian Tax.

In a recent post I linked to a Monthly Review cover article on James Hansen’s proposed “fee-and-dividend” approach, a concept introduced by Maria Cantwell and others in the U.S. Senate a few years ago. It went nowhere.

One or more groups in Washington state are pushing a ballot initiative (pdf) that has several of the ingredients of Hansen’s proposal. It begins:

The intent of this initiative measure is to promote sustainable economic development in Washington state by funding transportation investments and tax reductions and by imposing a carbon tax. This measure proposes that fifty percent of the revenue from the carbon tax must be devoted to tax rebates and reductions, including the working families tax exemption, business and occupation tax relief for manufacturers, the high tech research and development tax credit , and property tax relief; and that the remaining fifty percent of the revenue must be devoted to public investments in transit, road maintenance, and freight mobility.

If enacted by the voters, the initiative would impose a tax of $30 per metric ton (2,200 pounds) on all fossil fuels, including coal, gasoline, natural gas, and diesel (carbon dioxide equivalent). The amount of the tax would rise with inflation.

Let’s look at the fuels. Here’s a chart showing carbon dioxide emissions in pounds per gallon burned.

If my arithmetic is correct, a $30/tonne tax on gasoline would add about 27 cents to a gallon (19.6 ÷ 2,200 = 0.009; $30 x 0.009 = $0.267). Assuming a $3.50/gallon price at the pump, the carbon tax would raise the amount to $3.767, a 7.6% bump.

We can imagine the horrified reaction to such an increase. Yet, it wasn’t that long ago that we were all paying over $4/gal. for gasoline. Using Wikipedia data, here is a comparison of retail gasoline prices of different countries.

Of course, the comparison won’t stop the outcry. I guess it would have something to do with our sovereign right to cheap gas, or whatever. But that low price we pay (in the Puget Sound area it’s down to below $3.40/gal. as I write) does not include the negative externalities. There are several: military spending on securing U.S. access to foreign oil; the pernicious effects of sprawl; toxic chemicals; carbon dioxide.

If we paid, say, $8/gallon of gasoline, that would be equivalent to a carbon tax of $500/tonne (again, if my math is correct). The tax would be $4.50/gal., which alone is more than the current U.S. average retail price of about $3.50/gal.

Yet, what the ballot measure proposes is to offset some existing taxes and fund several initiatives, including renewable research and development, manufacturing, and public transportation. Instead of cheap gas, we would get “relief” from property taxes, for example, better roads, and perhaps trains to replace private cars. Also, and most important, is that the Pigovian tax would reduce the consumption of carbons, an altogether good thing.

However, even the modest rate of the initiative may scare too many voters, in which case further actions to reduce carbons may be jeopardized. The very small optimist part of me believes that a comprehensive educational campaign that highlights the benefits of such a tax could enhance citizen support. After all, California now has a cap-and-trade scheme in place. Boulder, Colorado, has a municipal carbon tax as does Montgomery County, Maryland. Why not Washington?

A provocative piece

February 6, 2013 by davald

Apropos of a recent post yesterday’s mailbox contained the latest issue of Monthly Review. The cover article is entitled James Hansen and the Climate-Change Exit Strategy. The author, John Bellamy Foster, is both the editor of the publication and a University of Oregon professor. He takes Hansen’s analysis and comments seriously, arguing that we face an ecological disaster beyond our imagination.

But Foster believes that Hansen’s proffered “fee-and-dividend” proposal to curb carbon consumption will be undermined by corporate profit-takers, led by the fossil-fuel industry. Foster asserts that the underlying cause of climate change, in particular, and pervasive human misery, in general, is capitalism. That’s a very big target, and good luck with that.

At any rate, I encourage you to read the article, then offer your comments here—or anywhere else, for that matter. And one more thing…

It occurs to me that if carbon were to become more expensive, fossil fuels would be less desirous, obviating the presumed need for America’s global military reach. Just a thought.

UPDATE (Feb. 6, 2013):

While Hansen prefers a tax to a cap-and-trade regime, Paul Krugman takes exception to Hansen’s criticism of the latter here. Krugman:

Unfortunately, while I defer to him on all matters climate, today’s op-ed article suggests that he really hasn’t made any effort to understand the economics of emissions control. And that’s not a small matter, because he’s now engaged in a misguided crusade against cap and trade, which is — let’s face it — the only form of action against greenhouse gas emissions we have any chance of taking before catastrophe becomes inevitable.

First and goal…

February 4, 2013 by davald

…from the seven-yard line, no less. A little run, then three errant passes to the same receiver. Nada. The game essentially over.

The problem with really close games, and yesterday’s Super Bowl was one of them, is that they invite lots of second-guessing and finger-pointing. Both activities fall under the counterfactual category. If (only) this had happened, then that would have occurred. If only Akers had kicked the ball higher, then Jones doesn’t tie the record for return touchdowns. If only the refs had called holding, then the Niners would have gotten another four downs, though not more precious time. If only Kaepernick had more games under his belt, then he would have made better decisions. We could go on, of course.

San Francisco was participating in its sixth Super Bowl, having won the five previous. One interesting stat: Kaepernick’s interception was the first by a 49er quarterback in the series. Guess which quarterback this year had the highest rating? Would you believe Alex Smith? If the Jets have any front-office talent left, they’ll snag him as fast as a Tebow arm flash.

My beloved Niners lost. I’m sure that they’ll be back, just as they overcame last year’s crushing defeat in the NFC championship game against the eventual Super Bowl winners. However, my allegiance has shifted north to the Seahawks. After all, I’ve lived in Puget Sound a lot longer than I lived in the Bay Area.

But, damn it. I hate to watch Super Bowls. The plays themselves can be measured in seconds. From kickoff to finale, the clock ticked off the better part of four hours. Man was my butt sore. My eyeballs didn’t fare much better. And can any company produce an interesting commercial. Why pay millions for lame? Several even grossed me out. Yuck.

Legislative herding

February 4, 2013 by davald

Legislators sponsor bills that prescribe certain behaviors. For example, when dealing with the environment or global warming, representatives propose legislation designed to induce the acquisition of renewable and efficiency resources over the bad stuff—anything with carbon or soot or toxins. I will assume that such bills result from the best of intentions. However, there is a simpler, more effective approach.

Let’s focus on climate change. If one is serious about preventing or mitigating global warming, then, by all means, dramatically reduce, if not eliminate, the combustion of carbon. A little bit of atmospheric carbon dioxide is a good thing; too much will certainly destroy the planet, one species at a time.

Economists have known for some time now, probably for centuries and before ‘economist’ joined the dictionary, that raising the price of a good or service will reduce demand for the item. If we want less carbon, we need to make it more expensive.

There are many ways to do this. But surely the simplest and most effective is by taxing it. 

But legislators in Olympia and D.C. can hardly even say the word ‘tax,’ let alone establish one. Instead, they attempt to herd us all into metaphorical pastures.

The Snohomish County PUD, of which I am a board member, tracks legislation introduced in both Washington state and Washington, D.C.. Typically, we commissioners receive updates, which are ranked by topic and priority. The latest Olympia update was sent at the end of last week. I’ll mention a few legislative titles:

  • Modifying the renewable energy and cost recovery program
  • Regarding net metering of electricity
  • Regarding the use of an energy storage facility to meet annual targets under RCW…
  • Requiring integrated resource plans developed by electric utilities include an assessment of energy storage systems

Implicit in these bills is an underlying concern for climate change. But they are all prescriptive or proscriptive, another way of “herding” utilities, in this case. So, we at the PUD pay attention on behalf of our customers. If we believe that a bill’s passage will harm ratepayers, we speak against and otherwise try to modify or even “kill” it. On the other hand, some legislation we deem beneficial, in which case we actively support. In most instances the PUD “monitors” or perhaps expresses “concern.” Since legislators are in session for months at a time, the process is long and occasionally tedious. At the end, dozens, if not hundreds, of bills “die,” with only a handful landing on the governor’s desk for signature.

Suppose the legislators imposed a carbon tax. Oregon has one. California established a cap-and-trade regime (a form of taxation). Ireland recently instituted a stiff carbon tax. Wherever taxes or caps have been imposed, consumption of carbon has fallen. What would happen if Olympia did?

Rather quickly, if international examples attest, those subject to a tax would take steps to avoid them. Electric utilities would pursue non-carbon strategies. These would include more conservation and renewable resources. If the tax were high enough, more utilities would have an incentive to pursue such initiatives as energy storage, which has proven costly, given its infancy. (The noble Snohomish County PUD does all this good stuff in the absence of a tax. We board members recognize that carbon will become more expensive; it’s just a matter of time. As always, the PUD is ahead of the curve.)

However, utilities do not hold a monopoly on innovation. A carbon tax would encourage utilities to serve as a conduit between third parties (the entrepreneurs and innovators) and their customers, who, after all, would ultimately pay the tax in the form of higher electricity rates. The higher the tax, the wider the conduit and the greater the opportunity.

Another thing would happen. Legislators could busy themselves with other pressing matters, like education and transportation, confident that the tax would do the herding, like Adam Smith’s “invisible hand.” 

If any legislators are reading this, why not start the conversation among your colleagues? Hey. We’re only trying to save the earth.

Gambling ironies

February 4, 2013 by davald

James Surowiecki, writing for the New Yorker about states’ efforts to legalize sports betting:

The leagues insist that legalized betting will make people suspect that games are fixed, thus harming their brands. Yet in Vegas billions are wagered legally on sports every year, apparently without ill effect, and legal sports betting in Great Britain doesn’t seem to dim anyone’s passion for Premier League soccer.

While passions may not have dimmed, today’s headlines cast a very dark shadow:

Criminal organizations have infiltrated the highest levels of European and international soccer, threatening the integrity of the sport, global law enforcement officials said Monday as they disclosed the results of a 19-month investigation that indicated that hundreds of people had been involved in match-fixing.
