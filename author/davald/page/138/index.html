Greece: a straightforward analysis

June 18, 2012 by davald

Despite being a Nobel Prize winner for work too impenetrable for most of us, Princeton’s Paul Krugman manages to write coherently and succinctly, even in a somewhat folksy manner. For sure, he need not take on airs; he’s unarguably one of the top economists in the world. The prize says as much.

Onto Greece, the scene of a vast morality play conducted from afar by Germans, especially. Krugman tells us that all this blabber about Greek misbehavior is beside the point. First, it detracts from the necessary solution. Second, according to Krugman, it will be the Germans who must provide the solution to Greece’s woes. Spain’s, Italy’s, and Portugal’s are not far behind. Krugman:

So how did Greece get into so much trouble? Blame the euro.

Fifteen years ago Greece was no paradise, but it wasn’t in crisis either. Unemployment was high but not catastrophic, and the nation more or less paid its way on world markets, earning enough from exports, tourism, shipping and other sources to more or less pay for its imports.

Then Greece joined the euro, and a terrible thing happened: people started believing that it was a safe place to invest. Foreign money poured into Greece, some but not all of it financing government deficits; the economy boomed; inflation rose; and Greece became increasingly uncompetitive. To be sure, the Greeks squandered much if not most of the money that came flooding in, but then so did everyone else who got caught up in the euro bubble.

And then the bubble burst, at which point the fundamental flaws in the whole euro system became all too apparent.

Krugman compares Greece to, say, Texas and Florida. The latter states got into financial difficulty with no means to solve their problems. To the rescue come the other states. Greece, however, must depend on the rest of the Eurozone to shore up its finances. But Germany, in particular, and its banks even more so refuse to behave like our federal government does toward Texas, Florida, et al. Angela Merkel is telling the Greek political leaders, whomever they may be at the moment, to buck it up, tighten your belt, and, in effect, visit hardship and misery upon your people.

It’s “structural”

June 17, 2012 by davald

Assertion: The higher-than-average unemployment rate is the result of too many jobs looking for too few skills. That is, the problem is structural.

Let’s run with this for a moment. If a broad swath of Americans lack the requisite skills, then the situation must be fairly long-standing, since people don’t suddenly lose or gain the qualities that previously kept them employed.

Yet, when we look at the employment data we see an abrupt increase in unemployment across all sectors beginning with the Great Recession. The rate of change belies a sudden loss of skills.

Here’s the rate for the civilian population over time (data from the St. Louis Fed).

 

What about the rate of change, year to year?

Again, how could Americans, or any country’s citizens for that matter, gain or lose skills so quickly? Nor does it seem plausible that the private sector is innovating or changing foci so often and to such a great extent as to abruptly have either a surfeit or a paucity of the right workers with the right skill sets.

Not convinced? Let’s break down the data into different categories. How have those with bachelor’s degrees fared?

How did professionals do?

Not everyone has a college degree. Here’s a graph showing unemployment of the population with only a high-school diploma.

We could go on. The point is that the Great Recession and the anemic recovery has hit all sectors hard, sparing no one, save, I suppose, currently elected officials, professional athletes, and the upper tiers of Wall Street.

There must be something else going on that explains the volatility in the unemployment rate over time. We’ll look at the annual change in nominal GDP.

Some economists tout the real business cycle theory. According to this theory, the fluctuations evident in the above charts are due to external shocks and that the ups and downs represent efficient market responses to those shocks. Examples of such shocks would be a change in oil prices, or droughts in the Midwest that adversely impact agricultural output, or a new technology (e.g., iPhone) that alters the way we do business. Proponents of the theory (largely associated with the Chicago School of Economics) argue that federal government attempts to smooth out the curves (either through monetary or fiscal policies) interfere with the market’s self-correcting mechanisms. Adherents would also suggest that the changes in employment, either overall or by sector, are also the product of external shocks, forcing workers into involuntary transitions: they must adapt or go without work. As a rule, RBC theorists reject Keynesianism.

Does the RBC explain the Great Recession? What was the external shock or group of shocks that sharply reduced economic output? Did oil prices suddenly jump?

One could argue that prices fell in response to the recession. The effect rather than the cause.

Well, as it turns out, we do have a pretty good idea as to what precipitated the Great Recession.

It’s called the housing bubble. And it popped. Is the bubble collapse an external shock?

I’d suggest that the steady climb in housing prices, both real and nominal, was endogenous, internal to the market and its participants. It was a matter of “irrational exuberance,” when, like finding gold in them ‘thar hills, investors found the financial equivalent in housing, or more specifically, the associated mortgages, which were rolled into other financial instruments, including derivatives and swaps.

It all collapsed, in what Paul Krugman calls the Wile E. Coyote moment. Those holding the packaged securities suddenly realized that there would be a free fall, but only after they’d left the cliff.

This precipitated another phenomenon, a Minsky moment. Straddled with so much debt and rapidly declining value of the underlying assets (i.e, house prices), everyone tried to deleverage at once, sending the economy into a deep recession, the worst since the Great Depression. This brings us to a related situation: the paradox of deleveraging. That is, while it makes sense for individuals to reduce their debt; when too many do it at the same time, aggregate demand plummets. And remember, the modern capitalist economy depends on a large portion of us spending money. Indeed, seventy percent of GDP is in the form of consumer purchases.

John M. Keynes appreciated what was going on during the Great Depression. He realized that too many people had too few dollars to buy the goods and services of firms. So, why should companies produce if no one could purchase their output? If they weren’t producing, they needed fewer workers. A vicious cycle. Who can stop it?

Keynes argued that only the federal government was in a position to intervene in any significant way. FDR boosted government spending in his first term, then wrongly listened to the austerians as he assumed his second term. GDP fell. Correcting his error, he resumed federal spending under the New Deal, putting hundreds of thousands to work building things like the Grand Coulee Dam. Yet, even these measures were insufficient to return the economy to its pre-Depression trend. Only the massive spending on World War II put the country into full recovery.

When Obama took office in 2009, the unemployment rate had climbed from four to ten percent. The Federal Reserve eventually tripled the money supply and kept the federal funds rate at or near zero. Monetary expansion did not help much. Why not? We’re in a liquidity trap, where those with money prefer to invest in safe havens. Right now, that would be Treasury securities. Nor has the increased money supply crowded out private spending, since the economy suffers from inadequate aggregate demand.

Obama managed to persuade Congress to pass a stimulus package that Keynesians said was far too little to have significant effect, leaning on the lessons of the New Deal. The unemployment rate fell at first, but has stagnated over the last couple of years, about double the pre-Recession levels. Meanwhile, GDP is growing too little to put people to work. We’re stuck.

Japan has been stuck for a couple of decades now. The United States and Europe show no signs of meaningful economic recovery. How long could the stagnation last?

Keynes believed that a market economy could reach an equilibrium state of low output and high unemployment—and stay that way indefinitely. But such a condition means that millions will suffer, and he thought that unnecessary and ultimately disastrous for the economy’s future. The only entity that could jump start the economy was the federal government through massive stimulus, and, Keynes suggested, it really didn’t matter how the money was spent. Just get it out there into the hands of people so they could spend and produce.

Today’s political climate offers zero hope for the proper Keynesian remedy. If Keynes, Krugman, et al. are right, then what we see today around us will become the new norm. And it has nothing to do with too few people possessing too few skills.

God’s traders

June 14, 2012 by davald

Economists will tell you that mutual exchanges between willing agents increases wealth. The inherent logic, they say, is both simple and obvious. Agent A has something that B wants, and vice versa. So A trades with B, making each better off. Writ large, that’s the market economy.

Let’s stick with the simple for a moment. We might imagine that I have skills that an employer needs. So the employer hires me. I get money while the employer benefits from my skills, or whatever. I’m better off and so is the employer.

I go to the grocery store. I need provisions. I pay the grocer for the items I’ve stuffed into my cart. The grocer is happy that I parted with some of my money, and I get a week’s worth of food and beverage. Our aggregate wealth has increased.

Firm A is good at making x and not so good at making y, while Firm B is good at making y but not so good at making x. One has a comparative advantage over the other. If each focuses on doing what it does better, then the collective wealth improves.

So far, so simple. But what about the financial sector, or Wall Street for short?

Over the last few decades the financial sector has seen its profits rise while the manufacturing sector’s profits have fallen.

What, exactly, does the financial sector do, other than deliver enormous paychecks to a relative few? Keeping with the simple, how do we explain the exchanges mentioned in the first couple of examples above in a Wall Street context? What or where do we find the comparative advantages?

Of course, Wall Street is anything but simple, as we’ve come to learn. The financial instruments developed by PhDs in mathematics and physics have become so complex that few understand them. But I should think that they have one thing in common: they are conditional algorithms. If this happens, do that. For instance, a change in temperature in the Midwest could trigger a buy or sell or a put or a call on agriculture futures. I suppose, really, that an action can be tied to any number of possible circumstances. Inherent in the conditionals is a presumed correlation between one or more events.

Recently JP Morgan lost billions of dollars in a complicated scheme (well, they’re all complicated). It appears that someone outside the firm was closely tracking a particular trade or set of trades by some JP Morgan employees. When JP Morgan turned left, others turned right. As it happens, turning left proved to be the wrong direction.

What’s really going on here and defines what Wall Street and other financial trading hubs is betting, plain and, I dare say, simple. I imagine that I’m a trader (a bettor) on Wall Street. I notice that a particular stock is lower than I think it should be. So I place a bet on the stock, believing that its price will rise. What if I’m wrong and the stock goes even lower? Well, I could hedge against this happening. I persuade another trader to provide me with insurance against a potentially bad bet. The other trader exacts a premium for the insurance. That other trader may himself seek a hedge. And so on? There’s nothing to prohibit a long series of hedges based on the initial trader’s bet. Regardless of which direction the stock price goes, there will be winners and losers, though the wins and losses will be mitigated by virtue of hedging.

But notice that there is no mention of an underlying asset of the particular stock, call it Stock A. We don’t know what the company associated with Stock A makes or provides. For the trader it doesn’t matter.

Here’s an example to illustrate the point: the price of housing. It was assumed as an article of faith by far too many that the price of houses only rises; it does not fall. The implicit assumption is that the population grows, people need a place to live, they buy houses. Home buyers also believe that they are making an investment, since the value of their real estate will increase over time. Since the demand for housing increases with population growth, or so it is assumed, then the price of houses will keep rising.

We can then say that the underlying asset in mortgages and the financial derivatives upon which they are, or were, based is the value of the home. However, as we have witnessed, housing prices do fall. The expression ‘house of cards’ seems particularly apt, since a significant chunk of the edifice of financial instruments was in the form of house prices, always expected to rise.

The late economist Hyman Minsky wrote about “speculative euphoria.” When things are going good, everyone wants to get into the act. Housing and its financial edifice attracted more and more traders, who bundled up the mortgages in increasingly complex instruments then sold them to other traders, who then bundled and sold to another set of traders. And so on, creating a burgeoning mushroom of presumed financial value. As more and more individuals put their money into these instruments, their prices, and therefore their value, rose, attracting even more investors.

Here’s the peculiar thing about these financial mushrooms. Their collective value is many times greater than the value of the underlying asset. How can this be? I suspect that the differential has everything to do with the notion of value.

What is the value of x? It is the price that one or more individuals is willing to pay for it. An ancient painting fetches several millions of dollars at auction. The value of something bears little resemblance to its costs, which is certainly true of rare art, say.

Assume that you are a banker. You’ve just lent me $100,000 to buy a house. You hold my mortgage—for a moment, anyway. You, the banker, are in business to make money. So you sell the mortgage to an investor, or one who represents a group of investors. The price the investor pays is greater than $100,000—or why else sell it? Now you’ve cleared your books of the accounts receivable, the mortgage, and pocketed some extra money, along with divesting risk.

In turn, the investor bundles the mortgage he’s just purchased with several other financial instruments. Each of these, including the mortgage, is assumed to have an underlying asset, though to the investors, it really doesn’t matter. The objective is to turn these instruments over, holding them only for a short while, then selling them to others for a profit. Each transaction drives up the value of the traded instruments, since each player in this series of transactions is charging the buyer more than the player paid for the instrument. When the notional value of each transaction is summed the result can be three to four times the total of financial assets.

We can begin to appreciate this mushrooming effect as a combination of “speculative euphoria” and the corresponding rising value (i.e., the price that individuals are willing to pay). That original house upon which I took out a mortgage for $100,000 is still the same house. But that mortgage generated many times the initial value of the mortgage.

Back to Wall Street traders. While it can be said that they are exchanging one thing for another (e.g., the notional value of a financial instrument for payment to the holder of the instrument), which could be construed as trading, Wall Street, as John Maynard Keynes described it, is one big casino where trillions of dollars are at stake at any given time.

For the Rest of Us, this means that we are at the mercy of betting. If enough “traders” guess right, we can breathe a sigh of relief. If too many guess wrong, shit happens—as in a Great Recession.

It hasn’t always been like this. The increasing volatility occasioned by financialization of the economy has placed all of us at risk.

But for all their shenanigans, Wall Street’s mucky-mucks believe that they’re doing “God’s work,” and that we simply don’t understand their enormous contribution to the Rest of Us. Appearing before the Senate Banking Committee yesterday JP Morgan’s Jaimie Diamond admitted that there were “negatives,” including, “you know, greed, arrogance, hubris, lack of attention to detail.” Yet, he keeps insisting that he and his brethren should not be constrained. The loss of billions should be treated as bygones, an aberration along an otherwise glorious path of wealth creation.

The editors of the New York Times chastised members of Congress for their abject failure to impose the regulations contained in the Dodd-Frank bill, in particular the Volcker Rule, which would restore a bit of Glass-Steagall. Don’t the editors know that when Diamond et al. address Congress they’re appearing before chattel? After all, this is the best Congress money can buy, and Wall Street is where the money is.

Sunset Falls [u][u2][u3]

June 13, 2012 by davald

[u3] I was trying to take pictures while listening to all the facts and figures. It seems that I got a few items wrong, as AGM Kim Moore discovered. In the interest of accuracy, here goes:

  • on fish handling 10,719 was the record one day haul of pink salmon (9/14/2009)
  • entrance to ladder rather than intake to ladder
  • transform Sunset Falls AREA into a better place than it is now [davald: the falls themselves will not be altered]
  • the trap and haul was built in 1958 by Department of Fisheries and not Puget Power and Light; the land was previous owned by Puget Power and Light and intended as a hydroelectric project
  • more than one home can see the intake [davald: a few will be able to glimpse a side view of the intake]

[u2]Video at the end.

[u]Correction below.

The Snohomish County PUD has a rather simple resource strategy: capture all cost-effective conservation and, if necessary to meet increasing load, develop renewable resources in our own backyard. This strategy allows us to comply with Initiative 937, reduce financial risk, add resources in small increments to match load growth, impose a smaller ecological footprint, and keep costs, and therefore rates, stable.

Over the last five years the PUD has broken its own historical records for annual conservation savings. Last year the utility saved 10.1 average megawatts.

On the resource front we’ve been exploring tidal power in Puget Sound, installing solar voltaic panels on rooftops, constructing new low-impact hydro projects (we’ve got mountains and rain in Snohomish County: a good combination for letting water and gravity generate electricity), and engaging in serious efforts to add a geothermal project to our portfolio.

As always, the bulk of our power—over 80 percent—comes from the Bonneville Power Administration. With the exception of the output from the state’s lone nuclear power plant (Columbia Generating Station), all of that electricity is generated by a series of hydroelectric projects along the Columbia and Snake rivers, including Grand Coulee.

We’ve begun exploring another hydro project, this one at Sunset Falls. It appears to be an ideal site. But by merely announcing our intentions to investigate we’ve encountered opposition from some of the local residents and various non-governmental groups. Unfortunately, much of their rhetoric is inaccurate, often wildly so.

This morning I joined my commission colleagues, the general manager, the clerk of the board, and the PUD’s assistant general manager in charge of generation in a visit of Sunset Falls and surrounding environs. We commissioners wanted to see for ourselves what the fuss might be about.

The falls are certainly spectacular. Here’s one shot I took from the shoreline opposite the falls.

Near the center of the photo is an existing intake structure and fish ladder. Puget Sound Power & Light The state constructed both about 50 years ago. The investor-owned utility, now known as Puget Sound Energy, did not build a hydroelectric facility. The Department of Fisheries uses the structure to capture adult salmon swimming upstream. The fish cannot negotiate the falls; they must be trucked up stream, where they spawn. The juveniles then make their way downstream and over the falls then eventually out to the Pacific Ocean.

Here is a closeup of the capture tank. Fisheries workers use nets to pull the fish out of the tank then place them onto a single, rather ancient truck. I was told that as many as a thousand adult salmon a day may be transported. Should we proceed with the project, we’ll greatly improve this structure to enhance both the aesthetics of the site and the capturing and transporting of the salmon.

Some of the opponents of the project, whose details are far from being finalized, imagine that the utility will construct a giant concrete dam to impound the water for hydrogenation. Hardly. The plans thus far involve constructing an inflatable weir across the stream, reducing flows by approximately 2,500 cubic feet per second. (Flows can exceed 80,000 during extreme flooding. This morning, I was told, the river was flowing at 6,500 cfs.)

Here’s a slide from an October 2011 presentation to the board. It’s a plan view of preliminary designs for the intake. The inflatable weir is the balloon-like object near the middle; the intake is at the bottom left.

I’ve been informed that only one local resident would be able to see the intake structure and the weir, which would be inflated during low-water periods. During the winter when the PUD would need the electricity, the weir would likely be deflated, since the water flows will be at their highest. (The weir would inflate and deflate automatically according to river flows. It will be monitored remotely in real time and can be operated via computer if necessary.)

Here’s a preliminary layout of the proposed project.

You’ll notice that the water flows around a peninsula. At the left-most point of that peninsula the PUD would construct the inflatable weir that would connect with the side opposite, where the intake structure would be located. The diverted water would flow downhill through a constructed tunnel under ground to the site of the current fish trap structure. There the PUD would build a powerhouse that contains the turbines for generating electricity.

Here’s a picture of the intake area from a location just to the right and near the same shoreline.

To the left of the picture is the tip of the peninsula. The water flows around the peninsula from the picture’s left to right, then toward the camera. It continues to Canyon Falls, which is above Sunset Falls.

As I said at the outset, the falls are magnificent. The PUD’s proposed project will have no effect on the appearance or operation of the falls, since the utility would divert only a fraction of the river flow above. No resident, with the possible exception of one, would see any difference after the project’s completion. That one resident, as I mentioned above, would have a view of the intake structure.

Construction, itself, is a different story, of course. Building things makes noise. There will be trucks and crews on site for a number of months.

But once the project is finished, the entire site will be visually improved. The power house, for example, will be built so as to resemble the existing rock formation the power house will replace. The Tulalip Tribes and government fishery agencies support the PUD’s plan to greatly improve the trap-and-haul component of the proposed project.

Nevertheless, the PUD, as always, will be especially sensitive to the concerns of those who now oppose the project. We’re still in the preliminary phases, gathering and studying data, meeting with local residents and other stakeholders. We have a well-deserved reputation for negotiating in good faith, accommodating the wishes of others. I can make this guarantee: should the project proceed, the PUD will transform Sunset Falls into a better place than it is now.
