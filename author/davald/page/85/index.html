The things we don’t need

May 17, 2013 by davald

First on my list is ammunition for the looney right. But that’s just what the Obama administration has done, and may still be doing, with the revelations of I.R.S. targeting of conservative groups.

To be sure, the tax code has been exploited, if not circumvented, by a slew of organizations whose leaders assert with straight face that they’re all about promoting social welfare. In reality they’re avoiding taxes as they work to influence political outcomes.

And it’s politics that ultimately bites you in the butt. A more clear-eyed president and his minions would know better than to allow Republicans, especially this current brand, to control the narrative, which is precisely what they’re doing. Once they seize on an alleged scandal, they will not relent. They are like sharks in bloody water, intent on devouring their prey.

Obama, stymied at every turn, needs to be on the offensive—big time. Yet, with each new disclosure, he’s put on the defensive.

The audacity of hope has morphed into a state of perpetual backpedaling. This is not pretty.

On the other hand…

May 16, 2013 by davald

I don’t like the idea of sports franchises dancing with cities. Sacramento has the Kings, and should always have them, notwithstanding a shoddy bunch of Maloofs. The Sonics, born and raised in Seattle, should never have left.

The Board of Governors should approve a new franchise in Seattle. Hansen et al. can then draft any foreign player to populate the reborn Sonics, without having to steal from existing NBA rosters.

Better yet, Congress, which really needs to prove that it can accomplish something, could outlaw professional sports cartels and subject the owners to anti-trust laws. If a bunch of rich guys want to own a franchise in Seattle or anywhere else and pay for a new arena to house the players, so be it.

Seattle denied

May 16, 2013 by davald

When the NBA board of governors, otherwise known as “the owners,” approved the sale and relocation of the Sonics to Oklahoma City, where they would become the Thunder, the reason was made clear: Seattle voters said no to a new arena, the kind worthy of billionaires with toys. Yesterday, essentially the same owners turned thumbs down on a lavish proposal to relocate the Sacramento Kings to Seattle, despite a bona fide plan to construct a state-of-the-art facility next to Safeco Field.

So, what’s going on here?

I think Matthew Yglesias got it right. He suggested that the owners loathe private financing of basketball arenas, as Seattle’s proposal included. They want the public to pay the lion’s share of facility costs. If they had approved the record-setting deal of Messrs. Hansen, Balmer, and Nordstrom, they would have established a precedent. Municipal citizens could demand that rich people pay for their own toys.

Or, it could just be that David Stern is a jerk.

Simple, one would think

May 14, 2013 by davald

Memories fade, which is a shame. It wasn’t that long ago that Enron was up to no good manipulating energy markets, with the blessing of the ratings agencies. People went to jail following the exposure of criminal activities. But the rating agencies continued. More recently, the agencies dubbed mortgage-backed securities triple-A, their highest. The collapse of the housing bubble (prices do fall) nearly destroyed the economy, and we’ve yet to recover.

So, in the aftermath of the latest misadventures Congress proposed to eliminate a glaring conflict of interest, which had bond-issuers soliciting ratings from the agencies, who were paid by the issuers. You can see the incentive here for the agencies to stamp their highest approval; they want repeat business. But that would be simple, too simple it seems.

Economist Dean Baker, who identified the rising housing bubble and warned of its collapse, tells us:

Senator Al Franken proposed a simple way to eliminate this obvious conflict of interest. He proposed having the issuer use the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) as an intermediary in the hiring process. Essentially, this means that the issuer would have to call the SEC when they wanted to have an issue rated and the SEC would then pick the rating agency. This would eliminate the incentive for the rating agency to issue an investment grade rating to get more business. The Franken Amendment passed the senate by a huge 65-34 majority, winning bi-partisan support. (Disclosure: I had written about this sort of reform and discussed it with Franken’s staff.)

While this might have seemed like a victory for simple common sense, the amendment was largely eviscerated in a conference committee, apparently at the urging of then House Finance Committee Chair Barney Frank. Instead of implementing the amendment, the conference bill called for the SEC to study the issue and make a decision by the end of July, 2012.

Oh, and there’s been no decision yet. Don’t hold your breath.

A media future

May 14, 2013 by davald

I’ll go out on a short limb here. My guess is that in a more perfect media world, all content, and I mean all, would be instantly available at any time on any device of our choosing. That world does not exist, but I think it inevitable.

What prevents the ideal from becoming reality sooner rather than later is money, of course. It’s always about money. Right now the major factions divide between content and providers. The former include creators and owners. Cable and satellite dish companies compose the latter. The networks occupy both, more or less. Against the tide of consumer demand, all are jockeying to either hold on to what they have or exploit the inevitable, albeit grudgingly.

As things now stand, I can watch some sporting events live on my iPad. I have a subscription with MLB.com that allows me to view major league baseball games, including my beloved Giants, on my iPhone, iPad, computer, and Apple TV-cum-television. The streaming quality is still inconsistent, but getting better. ESPN also streams live to my portable devices, except the Apple TV.

I pay an exorbitant amount of money to Comcast each month. Channels, in the hundreds, are “bundled” with each other and with a land line and Internet. I can also watch some programs on my Apple devices, with emphasis on some. There’s also “on demand.” But that’s a hit-and-miss proposition. Comcast doesn’t provide all episodes of all seasons of all series.

Frustration rules. Why can’t I just watch this or that when I want to?

First, baby steps. ABC recently said that it would begin streaming live content via cable providers to phones and tablets. Will that move spur others to follow? Probably.

There’s another problem with my ideal provisioning scenario: the lack of a central hub or portal through which content is acquired then streamed. I’m sure that Apple would like to play that role, and it’s already captured the lion’s share of digital music. Many of us buy television programs and movies from the iTunes store. But there’s nothing “live” in the online library.

Amazon, I suspect, stands the better chance of becoming that digital hub, since Jeff Bezos seems intent on dominating the retail world. He began with books several years ago. Now I can have just about anything delivered to my front door. As a “prime” member I can also stream media content for no extra charge. Still, nothing live.

The Nation magazine used to print a double-paged spread illustrating in graphic form the concentration of the media companies. I haven’t seen one for a while, but I recall there being only a handful of corporations pretty much owning everything. They have lots of money, which means that they can pay for bushels of lawyers and lobbyists, not to mention purchase their requisite lot of senators and representatives. They are unlikely to let the future catch them with their pants down. Rather, they will make sure that whichever way the consumer winds blow, they’ll steer things in their direction, with dollar bills attached.

But it will happen, ladies and gentlemen. We will have everything everywhere anytime. It just won’t be cheap.

Exploit the crisis

May 14, 2013 by davald

Noah Smith conjectures:

I conjecture that “austerians” are concerned that anti-recessionary macro policy will allow a country to “muddle through” a crisis without improving its institutions. In other words, they fear that a successful stimulus would be wasting a good crisis.

In principle, of course, what man has wrought he can put asunder. Some putatively wise men created a unique system of governance. Yet, despite its massive shortcomings, the government structure persists, and no one or group has mounted a serious effort to replace it with something more workable, notwithstanding Jefferson’s wishful thought that men should not rule from their graves.

I have to wonder, then, if Prof. Smith’s conjecture makes sense, given our history of perpetuating the status quo through all manner of crises, including the Great Depression, wars, and, more recently, an endless series of “cliffs” and “ceilings.” That persistence suggests that there are particular interests that benefit from business as usual. No, it’s not the Rest of Us.

I, for one, believe that ideology and political bias determine economic opinions. It’s so rare, after all, to have an economist   reverse course in the face of contrary evidence. Witness the several mentioned in Prof. Smith’s post.

Those who came into the latest crisis with the attitude that all fault lies with the government, regulations, and taxes are likely to stick to their guns. Should their predictions fail to materialize, no matter. What counts are conviction and consistency. The truth be damned.

Determining b.s.

May 13, 2013 by davald

On occasion I cross breaths with climate change skeptics, although I strongly suspect that in my absence they descend into group-thinking denial, following the lead of that noted eminent scientist Jim Inhofe, who has repeatedly called global warming “a hoax” and likely something stronger when he’s surrounded by fellow imbeciles. A preponderance of facts should never get in the way of a good story, as Ronald Reagan opined—he of “trees pollute” and “ketchup is a vegetable” fame. The stories range from carbon dioxide is as natural as flower petals to god will decide humanity’s fate, so might was well not worry about it. Oh, and it seems that those who hold one conspiracy embrace dozens, all whackier than mimes on meth.

The combination of social media and the Internet breeds incoherent screeds that instantly infect idiots. Have you heard the latest on the Clintons’ murders? Did you know that the moon landing was fake? Vaccinations will kill you, of course.

Suppose that you have the good fortune of having landed a position in a university, and it really doesn’t matter which, save for those founded and run by aforementioned idiots. Let’s further assume that you teach political science or sociology, fields that don’t easily lend themselves to formulas and laboratory observations as physics or chemistry do. Now you pass out your first assignment, asking students to explore a topic, reach a conclusion, and base findings on research. What should you expect from your students?

A midwestern professor of political science, who goes by “Ed,” has a blog that I’ve referenced several times, ginandtacos.com. He prefers to remain somewhat anonymous, and who can blame him? His prose is colorful. His tone irreverent. Today he mourns the state of student stupidity.

He mentions an actual paper submitted by an undergrad, replete with footnotes!

Many years ago a student submitted a research paper that I continue to use (without personal information, obviously) in classes as an example today. Unfortunately it’s not a positive example. The paper is a ten-page treatise on how the American presidents who died in office were all secretly murdered by the Freemasons.

The student was quite unhappy with the (failing) grade this mess of internet conspiracy theories received. “I did research and cited all of the sources”, s/he stated. It was true; the paper was exhaustively cited and clearly represented a good deal of research. The problem – and we/I did cover this in class repeatedly throughout the semester – is that not all “sources” are made equal. Some are legitimate, some are questionable, and some are flat-out nonsense. And back when I was still learning how to teach, it surprised the hell out of me to find that a non-trivial minority of students cannot tell the difference.

“Ed” goes on to discuss how we can distinguish between obvious bullshit and trustworthy sources. As he puts it:

My best advice, I think, is to err on the side of caution when the objective is to cite supporting research. Sure, government or major media outlet sources will not always be correct and may be flawed. But if the choice is between the Benghazi story on CNN or a post on something called WheresTheBirthCertificate.com, it is in students’ interest to play it safe.

Indeed.

Spanish wine

May 13, 2013 by davald

I am hardly an enophile, although I generally enjoy the product of fermented grapes. I usually stick with known vintners and varieties. However, I recently took the bait from the local wine shop. It featured a Spanish wine, marked down until supplies run out. I tried one. Then another, and so on. Good stuff, and happy to oblige.

Spain—along with Greece, Portugal and other European peripheral nations—is suffering mightily under imposed austerity. Before the Great Recession, business was booming. Too much, it seems. Capital flowed into Spain from the core nations, funding major asset expansion in the private sector. Spaniards earned rising incomes and spent freely. Then the spigot suddenly closed. Although the government’s debt was minimal before the crisis, it soon rose once tax receipts waned as a consequence of diminished economic activity.

Another consequence is very high unemployment. Among working-age youth the rate is 50 percent. But the government can’t provide much assistance to those out of work, since the heads of the Eurozone insist on “fiscal consolidation” to reduce deficits.

If you’re not working and you get little, if anything, from your government to tide you over, you can’t buy things, including your country’s own wine. Spanish vintners must try to sell their output to foreigners like me.

This piece by Gavyn Davies in the Financial Times addresses the improving Eurozone trade imbalances, which mask underlying problems. He writes:

…the reason for the trade improvement is not wage disinflation or structural reform, but the impact of the crisis and austerity programmes on domestic demand in the south [the southern peripheral nations of Spain, Greece, Italy, and Portugal].

People long out of work become desperate. Desperation leads to disruption both in parliaments and the streets. Nothing left to lose, it would appear. And I’m sure that they could use some of their own wine right now.
