iPhone 5: initial thoughts

November 16, 2012 by davald

When I bought my iPhone 4 it was considered the world’s best mobile phone, with the best camera, the best display, and best in several other categories. But neither Apple nor its competition would let things be.

Cupertino, to the disappointment of many, followed up with the iPhone 4 S, not a full-fledged 5. Putting the 4 and 4S side by side revealed no discernible differences in appearance. The weight was the same as were the dimensions. The changes were under the hood, as they say. My wife has a 4S, so I can attest to its improvements over my now-antiquated device. Customers didn’t seem to mind where pundits did; they snapped up the phones in record numbers.

Meanwhile the competition soldiered on. And there is a lot of it these days. Samsung now outsells Apple in number of units. It’s even making profits, though not nearly as much as Apple. Then there’s everyone else, including a struggling HTC, Nokia, and RIM, all gasping for their last breaths. A few weeks ago Microsoft jumped in, after initially mocking Apple’s first iPhone. Each company strives to differentiate itself from the pack, whether it be in number of features, phone size, and nerd-like specs.

The other day while walking I noticed a guy with a large white brick held up to his ear. I surmised that it was a phone, because I saw his lips moving. I immediately thought of the movie Wall Street and the humungous “portable” device used by Gordon Gekko that was the envy of those who couldn’t afford one. It was huge and conspicuous, because that was the point. I don’t know what make and model the white brick was, but it was so wide that it demanded extraordinarily large hands and fingers to hold it. That’s progress?

So, my old unit was finally eligible for an upgrade. I confirmed, to my surprise, that my carrier, AT&T, had extended its LTE network north from Seattle to include Everett. Splendid, since the iPhone 5 now works with the faster network. I then checked the stocks of nearby stores. All sold out. However, the AT&T website told me that I could get the new unit in three to five days. I pulled the trigger, ordering the largest capacity model, 64 GB.

I should pause to think about that last number for a moment. My computer days stretch way back to the IBM 360 mainframe and then the CP/M personal computer with two, not just one, floppy disk drives. Storage was virtually nonexistent, confined to those silly little floppies, one of which contained the operating system. We’re talking just a few kilobytes. Yet, I can now have a full 64 gigabytes at my disposal, all within a handheld device—the new iPhone 5.

I’ve commented on the iPhone’s speed before, including this chart showing one benchmark.

I can confirm that the specs don’t lie. This unit is fast, and as you can see, much faster than my wife’s 4S. No doubt the new A6 chip has much to do with the increased speed. What I readily and quickly appreciate is the seamlessness in navigating within and between apps on the iPhone 5. There is no stutter or delay. Click, swipe, pinch—and images instantly appear or transform. Web pages load much, much faster than before. Videos play perfectly. (I don’t play games, so I can’t comment on their performance.)

The new iPhone is light. Some have suggested that it’s too light. Certainly in comparison to my iPhone 4, the unit feels almost weightless, as if it floats in your hand—bad hyperbole, I know. The 5 is nearly 18 percent lighter than previous models. (You can view technical comparisons here.)

I, for one, like Apple’s keeping the width the same as the 4, or 2.31 inches. It’s easier to navigate with one hand and thumb. I’m less concerned about the length, or height, which is just eight percent longer than the 4 and 4S. The display, however, is about 14 percent longer; Apple reduced the size of the borders to accommodate the greater viewing area.

What I haven’t yet done, but am anxious to do, is put the iSight camera to the test. I’m hardly a photo bug, so I don’t take a lot of pictures. But I have a little project in mind. Weather permitting, I’ll take the phone out for a photographic spin. The newest iPhone sports an 8 megapixel camera that puts my little Nikon CoolPix to shame.

Apple’s new earphones, which the company calls ‘earbuds,’ are superior to previous iterations. I hear bass for the first time, and the music is accurate. That said, I use them only when I’m out walking and would not substitute them for my Sennheiser 580 or 600 headphones for serious listening.

Those of us who have used mobile devices for the last few years admit that they are no longer just phones. If all one wanted to do was talk to another wirelessly, one need not have an iPhone or a Samsung Galaxy S III. We are talking about smartphones, after all, with far more emphasis on smart than on phone. We text, we email, we take pictures, we surf the Net, we listen to music, we watch videos. Indeed, we really don’t use the phones as a phone. Fancy that. I’ve had the new iPhone 5 since Monday, and I’ve yet to receive a phone call or place one. I understand the voice quality is much better on this than on the 4, but I’ll have to wait and see.

Why did I upgrade? Why do people smoke, drink, or take drugs? I think it’s called ‘an addiction.’ I hasten to add that this new, wonderful device won’t kill me, though. I’m just a bit poorer.

Gaza

November 14, 2012 by davald

Noam Chomsky recently visited Gaza. He writes:

And it hardly takes more than a day in Gaza to appreciate what it must be like to try to survive in the world’s largest open-air prison, where some 1.5 million people on a roughly 140-square-mile strip of land are subject to random terror and arbitrary punishment, with no purpose other than to humiliate and degrade.

Shamelessly and with no evident regard for what others might think, Israel just launched an airstrike on a car traveling the streets of Gaza. The missile killed Hamas’s top military commander, according to the New York Times. The paper included this photo:

Not much left, including the occupants. Justified, says Israel. They’re lobbing rockets at us. And why would they do that? Chomsky:

Such cruelty is to ensure that Palestinian hopes for a decent future will be crushed, and that the overwhelming global support for a diplomatic settlement granting basic human rights will be nullified. The Israeli political leadership has dramatically illustrated this commitment in the past few days, warning that they will “go crazy” if Palestinian rights are given even limited recognition by the U.N.

The craziness is ongoing, unfortunately. It’s just a matter of degree.

We know that Israel can always rely on U.S. support for its being and actions, however egregious and murderous. After all, we keep giving them so much damn money. If Washington, D.C., were really serious about peace in the Middle East it would begin by turning off the spigot.

A Finn in Seattle

November 14, 2012 by davald

Those who regularly follow this blog know of my affection for Finnish Lessons, a book on Finland’s educational reform. Unlike America’s obsession with tests, competition, accountability, and just about anything new and therefore marketable, the Finns set about transforming their schools the old-fashioned way—by adopting the pragmatic views of our very own John Dewey. Yes, that guy.

The author is visiting Seattle for a bit. Pasi Sahlberg appeared last night at the University of Washington, before a room of teachers, principals, professors, and curious citizens. For the Seattle Times, education reporter Linda Shaw writes:

Sahlberg’s message, although he is too polite to put it so bluntly: Stop testing so much. Trust teachers more. Give less homework. Shorten the school day.

Finland, in other words, has become an education star by doing the opposite of what’s happening in many U.S. schools and school districts, including many in Washington state.

I should have put the last sentence in bold letters with a few exclamation points. What the heck, I’ll do it now:

Finland, in other words, has become an education star by doing the opposite of what’s happening in many U.S. schools and school districts, including many in Washington state!!!!

Is the guy a communist? A red? A liberal? Or, is it possible that Sahlberg can speak confidently about what actually works, since his country really did reform its education system and he has the evidence needed to prove it. Shaw:

Forty years ago, Finland was a small, homogeneous country with mediocre public schools. Today, Finland is still small and, although it has grown more diverse, is still much more homogeneous than countries such as the United States.

But no one calls Finland’s public schools mediocre anymore.

In 2000, the Finns surprised the world when their 15-year-olds scored at the top of a closely watched international exam called the PISA, the Program for International Student Assessment. Finland has stayed near the top ever since, while the U.S. scores around the middle.

But, of course, nobody tells us what to do. After all, we’ve got Bill Gates and his foundation, Arne Duncan and his race to the top, and, thanks to dimwitted voters, Washington state now has charter schools. In other words, we don’t know what the hell we’re doing. Yet that doesn’t stop us from throwing shit at the wall expecting different results. Come on. Grab a handful and join the crowd.

Changing demographics

November 14, 2012 by davald

This morning’s Everett Herald includes a map showing how Snohomish County precincts voted in the governor’s race. Red is McKenna; blue is Inslee.

I’ve put circles around two areas. My family used to live in Marysville, which at one time was Democratic. As you can see, it is now mostly Republican. About five years ago we moved to downtown Everett. It is, and always has been, Democratic.

As with the country as a whole, we see vast expanses of red punctuated by pockets of evidently deep blue. With the exception of the tribal reservation (just north of Everett and west of Marysville), the blue areas coincide with urban centers along the I-5 corridor, with a couple of outliers in east county.

Vice presidential candidate Paul Ryan attributed Obama’s victory to “urban voters.” As the New York Times writes:

Mr. Ryan’s concerns follow on the heels of other Republicans who argue that the party’s lack of appeal to minority voters — many of whom live in the nation’s largest urban centers — has made it more difficult to win the presidency.

Here’s what the Snohomish County website has to say about demographics:

The 2010 Census showed that Snohomish County’s population was 74% white and non-Hispanic, while 26% was a racial or ethnic minority.  Among the minority groups, Hispanic and non-Hispanic Asian populations were the largest, with each group individually representing 9% of the county’s total population.

According to IndexMundi whites represent almost 80 percent of the national population. However, about 82 percent of us live in urban centers, with that percentage steadily increasing over time.

Population density tends to correlate with political leanings; the denser an area’s population the more Democratic it is. Marysville has a relatively low density, about 2,600 people per square mile in 2010. My zip code of 98201, downtown Everett, has a density of 4,690 people/sq. mi.

But I hasten to add that density also correlates with income, which confounds the urban-vs-rural breakdown. Paul Krugman adds another possible factor:

As you can see, it’s not just those “urban” voters who seem to vote their identity. Asians and Jewish households are much more Democratic than you might expect given their relatively high incomes, presumably because they see the GOP as believing fundamentally in a white Christian nation from which they will forever be outsiders.

He includes a chart illustrating the above.

Sticking out at the bottom are southern whites who live in more rural areas with lower incomes. The blue line represents a gross and inverse correlation between income and preference for Obama.

I take away some irony from this. The Republicans are doing their best to keep the Rest of Us down. Our incomes have stagnated or even fallen since the beginning of the Great Divergence. If we can’t become rich, then we’ll vote Democratic.

I know. Very simplistic.

We did learn that Romney did quite well among rich white males. Although that cohort has most of the money, it’s a dwindling slice of America. That does not bode well for the GOP’s future, although the party is trying to address its “problem.”

Shakespeare and the CIA

November 14, 2012 by davald

I confess to largely ignoring the scandal extraordinaire: CIA director Petraeus caught with his pants down, in more than one sense. Then again, I don’t watch any of the reality shows, including Jersey Shores, or whatever it’s called. So, my mind does not instinctively gravitate toward all things salacious and ridiculous. To fill in my gaps, I turn to New York Times columnist Maureen Dowd, who puts the scandal in dramatic perspective. Yep, Shakespeare heeds the call.

It’s also worrisome that the nation’s spymaster — who had presided in a military where adultery could result in court-martial — could not have found a more clandestine manner of talking naughty to his biographer babe than a Gmail drop box, a semiprivate file-sharing system used by terrorists, teenagers and authors.

It’s understandable that men accustomed to being away from their families and cloistered with other men in Muslim countries where drinking and blowing off steam are frowned upon might get used to cavorting on e-mail.

But Petraeus should have realized that the Chinese and Russians were snooping and sent Paula Broadwell an Enigma e-mail: “I would like your insights into the debate over COIN versus CT in Helmand Province. Our HVT kills are falling a little short of the mark. Let’s discuss.”

I gleaned a more meaningful insight from Dowd with her comparison between the young Kennedy and the young Obama. JFK inherited a Cuban mess, then deferred to the generals in launching the Bay-of-Pigs debacle. Likewise, Obama gave far too much credence to military brass, Petraeus in particular, in extending the Afghan war, with disastrous consequences.

Petraeus rolled the younger commander in chief into going ahead with a bound-to-fail surge in Afghanistan, just as, half-a-century earlier, the C.I.A. had rolled Jack Kennedy into going ahead with the bound-to-fail Bay of Pigs scheme. Both missions defied logic, but the untested presidents put aside their own doubts and instincts, caving to experience.

Once in Afghanistan, Petraeus welcomed prominent conservative hawks from Washington think tanks. As Greg Jaffe wrote in The Washington Post, they were “given permanent office space at his headquarters and access to military aircraft to tour the battlefield. They provided advice to field commanders that sometimes conflicted with orders the commanders were getting from their immediate bosses.”

So many more American kids and Afghanistan civilians were killed and maimed in a war that went on too long. That’s the real scandal.

Indeed.

Another Very Serious Person

November 13, 2012 by davald

Robert Rubin, who apparently won’t go away, chimes in on the fiscal-cliff discussion. Like David Brooks, he wants “entitlement reform.” But unlike Brooks, Rubin calls for higher taxes on the rich and argues that it’s a good thing.

Raising tax rates for those with the highest incomes challenges the supply-side proposition that even moderately higher rates would hurt growth. President Bill Clinton’s 1993 deficit reduction plan increased income tax rates for roughly the top 1.2 percent of incomes. Opponents said this would lead to recession. Instead, we had enormous job creation and the longest economic expansion in our history.

A recent report by the Hamilton Project, an economic policy project on whose advisory council I serve, reviewed 23 studies of the impact of tax-rate changes on the propensity to work and found that most of them concluded there was no meaningful effect. Tax expenditure reductions, on the other hand, will not raise nearly the revenues needed for sufficient deficit reduction without increasing taxes on the middle class significantly and are likely to disrupt important social and economic goals, though many economists don’t acknowledge that.

Look, if you’re concerned about the federal debt being too high, even if you can’t explain to yourself or anyone else why it’s a problem, then you should be willing to raise revenues by imposing higher taxes on those who have the money. As I’ve mentioned before, Emmanuel Saez estimates that a whopping 93 percent of the economic gains since the end of the Great Recession has gone to the top one percent.

And, no, raising taxes on the rich need not and should not be part of some Grand Bargain in which the Rest of Us have to keep bending over. At what point, dear reader, do you say “enough”? Or, we’re “mad as hell and we’re not going to take it anymore”?

This past election is a start.

Times’ tension

November 13, 2012 by davald

We should not be shocked to learn that two writers for the New York Times, David Brooks and Paul Krugman, test each other’s patience, with the latter thinly veiling his contempt for the former’s opinions. This morning we are treated to a rejoinder of sorts to Krugman’s recent post, wherein he counseled Obama to essentially tell the obstructionist Republicans to go f**k yourself. In specific terms, the economist warned against giving in to the Grand Bargain devotees, those, like Brooks, who believe that success can come only through statesmanlike negotiations. Wasn’t that the failed strategy of Obama’s first term? And what did he get for all that “bipartisanship”? Brooks:

Some on the left are suggesting that he adopt a strategy of confrontation and conquest. The president should use the advantages of victory to crush the spirit of the Republican House majority, they say. Reject the Grand Bargain approach. Instead, take the country over the so-called fiscal cliff. Blame it on the Republicans who are unwilling to even raise taxes on the rich. Wait until they fold, and then you will have your way.

The first thing to say about this strategy is that it is irresponsible. The recovery is fragile. Europe may crater. China is ill. Business is pulling back at the mere anticipation of a fiscal cliff. It’s reckless to think you can manufacture an economic crisis for political leverage and then control the cascading results.

Here’s what Krugman advised:

So President Obama has to make a decision, almost immediately, about how to deal with continuing Republican obstruction. How far should he go in accommodating the G.O.P.’s demands?

My answer is, not far at all. Mr. Obama should hang tough, declaring himself willing, if necessary, to hold his ground even at the cost of letting his opponents inflict damage on a still-shaky economy. And this is definitely no time to negotiate a “grand bargain” on the budget that snatches defeat from the jaws of victory. Krugman reckons that making a deal with the devil is worse than no deal at all. Krugman:

So what should he do? Just say no, and go over the cliff if necessary.

To what effect? Unlike the debt-celing crisis, which really would have been disastrous had Congress not come to its senses, Krugman avers that “nothing very bad will happen to the economy if agreement isn’t reached until a few weeks or even a few months into 2013. So there’s time to bargain.”

Brooks, at the vanguard of the Very Serious, returns to his favorite whipping boys as he pitches dealmaking: minimizing the value of taxing the rich and reforming “entitlements.” Brooks says, “higher tax rates on the rich…wouldn’t even produce enough revenue to cover a tenth of the deficit.” Thus, we should proceed with screwing the Rest of Us again and again.

As if on cue, Krugman produces a chart on his blog this morning that illustrates the consequences of raising the eligibility age for Social Security and Medicare for those in the bottom half.

;

Brooks, who is surely in the top fifty percent of income earners, wants to see that eligibility age increased even further, to the detriment of half the population. As for taxing the rich, we might take another look at the impact of Bush’s policies, including tax cuts, versus Obama’s contributions to the deficits.

;

Cutting taxes during Bush’s terms represented 35 percent of the $5 trillion hit on the federal budget for the 2002-2009 period. Meanwhile, Bush went to war on a credit card.

We can put these numbers in a different perspective (source).

But Very Serious People seem to be uncomfortable with these figures, however presented. They insist, with Romney, that too many of us prefer to live on the dole, mooching from the self-reliant job creators. Thus, any and all efforts to send the proper message to the freeloaders are not only welcomed but deemed salutary. Tough love, you know.

The Republicans, ladies and gentlemen, care not a whit about the Rest of Us. They’ve been quite clear about their attitudes. Too clear for even David Brooks to ignore. Yet, he continues to advocate on their behalf, insisting that entitlement reform remain at the top of the national agenda.

One can hope that Obama pays more attention to Krugman than Brooks. But we shall see.
