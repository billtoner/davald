We take a break…

November 9, 2011 by davald

Need to laugh? Is all the news turning you blue? Did your 401(k) tank? Well, folks, let’s take time out for a couple of humorous asides. At least, I find these funny.

The first I heard from my dad many, many years ago.

A man walks into a tavern with his dog. He places the dog on a stool next to him. The bartender says,

“Hey, ain’t no dogs allowed here!”

The man says,

“But this is no ordinary dog.”

“Oh, yeah,” says the bartender. “What makes him so special?”

The guy turns to his dog and asks,

“Where was I working this afternoon?”

The dog replies,

“Roof! Roof!”

The bartender says,

“Get outa here!”

The man pleads.

“No, wait. Listen.”

He turns to his dog again and asks,

“Who was the greatest baseball play in history?”

The dog barks,

“Ruth! Ruth!”

At which point the bartender escorts them both out of the tavern.

Dejectedly, the guy and his dog walk a few steps along the sidewalk before the dog looks up at the guy.

“Should I have said ‘DiMaggio’?”

Here’s another, purloined from former Atlanta Braves pitcher John Smoltz. It’s got another animal in it.

A duck waddles into a bar, jumps up onto a stool and requests a martini, a little dirty, with extra olives.

The bartender leans over and says to the duck.

“Shall I put this on your bill?”

Okay. Resume misery.

Tax and spend

November 9, 2011 by davald

I’m looking at our state budget, in particular the audited results of 2010. From whom does the state derive its revenues? Where do they go?

Let’s glance at the revenues first.

 

Are you surprised to learn that the state’s biggest source of revenue is from the federal government? It dwarfs all other categories. The 2010 figures, over $12 billion, represent a huge jump from previous years, which were a bit over $8 billion each year between 2001 and 2007, before the Great Recession. Yep. The big jump is a result of the federal stimulus program, much scorned by Republicans. The state sales tax accounted for 19% of total revenues in 2010.

Now let’s see where the money went.

 

No surprise that the biggest chunk was for education. It should also not surprise us that human services were a close second. In 2007, expenditures were just over $11 billion. In 2010 they’d jumped to almost $14 billion.

We here much chatter about the size of government. So what were the costs of running it? Would you believe only 4 percent of total expenditures?

Yet, too many people talk about shrinking the size of government. In this past weekend’s Viewpoints, state senator Steve Hobbs, who evidently is proud to call himself a “moderate” Democrat, and Rep. Deb Eddy, write:

We are constitutionally required to balance our budget, so the reductions are not optional at this point. The cuts hit educational support and safety-net programs particularly hard, because so much of the budget is considered off limits, a characterization that we believe needs to be challenged.

In the meantime, to balance the budget with cuts, more programs would have to go on the chopping block, while attempting to protect the most critical services.

They argue for a three-part strategy to place the state government on a sustainable path. These are: reduce the size of government; reform government through re-prioritization; and then, and only then, boost revenues.

But what to cut, especially when over three-quarters of the budget pays for education and human services? If education is cut further, there will be long-term consequences, all bad. To slash human services when so many of our citizens are suffering from unemployment and high medical bills is not only immoral but obscenely so. Moreover, any reductions in government spending, regardless of where, can only exacerbate this recessionary economy, marked by constrained aggregate demand. As Keynes argued, one doesn’t practice austerity during recessions, unless, I would add, you’re a fool.

Prudence and macroeconomic logic require one of two things: increased revenue or greater debt. They are not mutually exclusive.

Unfortunately, raising revenues, if done improperly, can further drag down the economy. We Washingtonians had an opportunity to do it right a couple of years ago: imposing an income tax on the wealthy. Since they spend a lower percentage of income on goods and services than the Rest of Us, their tax dollars would not reduce overall demand, at least not on a dollar-for-dollar basis. The Rest of Us, however, spend almost everything we make. Not necessarily because we want to but because we must in order to survive.

As for incurring more debt, that may be constitutionally impermissible, even if it might be what Dr. Keynes would prescribe in the short-term. In general, he was appalled by government debt over the long-run, and believed that all sovereign entities should avoid it in almost every circumstance—save during extraordinary periods. (On the other hand, if we can legally increase our borrowing, now is a very good time to do so, since interest rates are so low.)

We are in one of those extraordinary periods. The demand for human services increases during economic recessions, as one would expect. As I said above, millions of people can’t find a job, millions more are underemployed, and millions more suffer from mounting debt occasioned by medical costs with no health care insurance. To whom do these people turn? To us, the government. Boeing’s not going to ameliorate their conditions. Nor is Microsoft, or Amazon, or Paccar.

And speaking of those companies, John Burbank wrote a spot-on essay in this morning’s Herald. He first makes the case that all of these companies and more benefit from public services provided by our tax dollars.

What if you are a business like Boeing, anxious to bring in the next generation of workers as the baby boomers retire? The community colleges, like Edmonds, are training workers for aerospace. The University of Washington graduates a pool of engineers from which you can pick and choose. Highway 526 provides part of the transportation infrastructure to get workers and airplane components to Paine Field. All those things are paid for with taxpayer dollars.

Yet, Boeing paid no taxes. Indeed, as Burbank points out, the company received a massive subsidy from Olympia. (I remember this well, when Boeing was threatening to close down its Puget Sound plants. Boeing leaders had the audacity to demand that they get their electricity free, or at a substantial discount. Other PUD customers would have been forced to make up the difference.) Here’s Burbank:

So you might think that Boeing pays an appropriate amount of taxes for the things from which it benefits, like K-12 education, community colleges, highways, and the state patrol — but sadly, you’d be mistaken. Boeing bullied the Legislature into a $3.2 billion tax rebate package instead, which might be OK, if Boeing actually made the new 787 in Washington. Instead, they have contracted out production all over the world. And now they are building a new 787 site in South Carolina. So our tax give-away has worked out pretty well for them … but not for us!

The aforementioned companies are making profits. Yet they’re not paying taxes, here or to the US Treasury. Worse, as Burbank tells us, “On a three-year profit of almost $10 billion, Boeing actually received $177.6 million from the federal government.”

Burbank concludes:

It is time to turn off the spigot of taxpayer money flowing into already overstuffed corporate pockets. We need it for education, health care and transportation. Those are the public services we expect government to provide. We all benefit from them, and all who are able should help pay for them.

Another Amen.

Dreaming vs. doing

November 8, 2011 by davald

You may share my despair in our country’s inability to go beyond dreams. We have evidently lost the capacity to actually do. I’m not talking small, necessarily, but grand and, forgive me, wonderful.

I am of two minds on small vs. big, however. It may have to do with process. For example, Amory Lovins talks about a lot of smalls making a big. I get that in terms of energy development and consumption. I’m also open to the idea that a relatively small germ of an idea (say a modest dream) can serve as the genesis for something much bigger and, perhaps, better.

On the other hand, there was a time in this country when we could and did do big—from start to finish, from conception to execution. I think of the New Deal projects, for example, of which the Grand Coulee dam was just one. It certainly wouldn’t be built today. Yet generations of regional citizens and businesses continue to reap its benefits. I was alive to witness the great federal highway construction, although I judge it to have done more harm than good. Oh, we’ve always excelled at war making, the bigger and more expensive the conflict the better.

But bear with me a moment to consider an alternative idea on how our everyday lives could be different, with the complete understanding that none of it can be realized in our current fiscal and political climate—if ever. After all, we’re not French. Besides, we’ve grown bitterly cynical.

I bring France into this, prompted by an essay appearing in the Atlantic and featured on Crosscut. It was written by Seattle attorney Charles Wolfe. I was immediately struck by this image:

It’s a photo of a tram running through the southern French city of Nice. The cars themselves are sleek, pure, and functional, with lots of windows. Note, too, the grass strips between the rails. Nice touch, pun intended. Reasonable minds may disagree, but I find this image pleasing and attractive, enough to make me want to live nearby, say in one of the adjacent apartment buildings. I want to be closer, not farther away.

The tram is one part of a very extensive transit plan. A Google translation tells me that the embedded video was shown to Nice’s Community Council, which approved its portion of the plan in 2009. As you’ll note, a full buildout of the transportation system will connect several French cities along the Mediterranean and inland, including Alsace-Lorraine. (I gather from the palm trees that these places have temperate climates.)

The video includes futuristic images of multiple subways and overhead trams. All is neat and tidy, as are the human figures. The goal is to have everything in place by the year 2030. Good luck with that.

Such projects are difficult to accomplish, even in France. As I said above, they’re probably impossible here in the US.

The principal proffered reason would surely be economic. We simply can’t afford it, more so now than in better times. Yet, I have my doubts that we’ll ever see major public works projects in my lifetime if not also my children’s, even if the economy recovers.

I would suggest that the real reason that we can no longer do is that we’ve lost faith in government and in our ability to imagine together. It’s all about the individual rather than the collective we. Voters deny government sufficient revenues to properly fund the public sector, including schools and transportation. The only governmental activity we support is war, of which we can never get enough.

We’ve become mean, brutish, and narrow-minded. No permission is granted to dream to envision to wonder, from our schools to the councils of government. Work for “the man” or don’t work at all.

The rich have siphoned off all the money, and they don’t need trams or busses or public schools. And we said nothing.

Tragic.

Debunking myths: a full-time job

November 8, 2011 by davald

Paul Krugman, no stranger to these pages, has a particularly insightful take on the factors of economic growth. He shows us that a lot of Very Serious People (his term) draw conclusions not supported by the evidence.

Krugman quotes a couple of VSPs. One argued that the economy took off with the dismantling of regulations, especially on the financial sector. The other attributed growth to the rise of the financial sector.

But, as Krugman points out, the economy grew more during the two decades following the end of WWII. Here’s a chart based on data from the Bureau of Economic Analysis.

Financial deregulation began in the 70s under Nixon and continued through Clinton’s terms, with the end of Glass-Steagall. That’s when the economy started its downward trajectory.

As I’ve mentioned previously, the incomes of all fractiles (as Emmanuel Saez puts it) steadily, albeit modestly, rose—until the 1970s. Beginning with Nixon the country experienced what I call ‘the Great Divergence.’ That’s when the income of the top one percent, and especially the top 1/100th percent took off. The Rest of Us, meanwhile, saw wages stagnate or fall in real terms.

Krugman includes his own chart (based on Saez’s data).

 

John F. Kennedy famously asserted that “a rising tide lifts all boats” (actually from the Chamber of Commerce). That was true in the 50s and 60s. However, the tide has been falling since, though not the fortunes of the rich.

Hmmm.

A father’s sweet delight

November 8, 2011 by davald

This past summer our daughter married a wonderful guy, also a lawyer. I had been to weddings before. But this was a first—the marriage of your own child. Yes, I am crying in this photo as I embrace Kelly, in happiness, surely, but also in the recognition that this was a milestone in her life, certainly, but also mine and my wife’s (in the right foreground). Her buoyant husband-to-be looks approvingly from the left.

After the vows were exchanged and the marriage declared official we climbed upstairs to the reception room and a marvelous dinner. At some point people stand up to speak, first the best man, Jesse’s brother, then Jesse’s father, who could easily make a living as a standup. It was then my turn.

I recall saying that my wife and I have been married for 43 years and we both hoped that Kelly and Jesse’s marriage would last at least as long. Then I got a bit choked up, as my mind raced back to retrieve memories. I shared this, paraphrased.

When Kelly was in elementary school I had an office downstairs where I conducted my business. I was almost always home for her when she returned from school, a short block away. I don’t remember how this got started, but when she’d come through the front door and bounce down to the basement where I worked, she’d let out a little whistle (I reprieved it here). I responded in kind. It was a sign from both of us that all was right with the world. And it was, at least for that brief while.

Despite the joyous occasion, everyone in the room was in tears. I think that we all appreciated one of life’s significant passages in that little story.

Beautiful. Right?

When Frazier decked Ali

November 8, 2011 by davald

I was trying to do the hardest job in the world, teach kids who would prefer to be elsewhere. The year, 1971.

We teachers, three nuns and I, had planned an education presentation in the school’s multi-purpose room for parents. Perhaps wanting to put on a show, I had cajoled a few eighth graders to dramatize phagocytosis on stage, since I was “teaching” a physiology elective. (Why? I had gotten a ‘D’ in the subject at Berkeley.)

The room was packed. Tensions were high. Such performances, especially among the young, create both excitement and fear.

Well, the event coincided with the Fight of the Century, the match between two undefeated heavyweights in Madison Square Garden. I happened to have been standing in the rear of the room when a father of one of the students approached me. He whispered in my ear that Frazier had defeated Ali.

At that moment I had one of my brain farts. Assuming that all the men in attendance, and perhaps a few women, were boxing fans, and that the males were there grudgingly, I briskly walked to the front of the auditorium then to the microphone. Apologizing for the disruption, I announced the fight results. I recall hearing some cheers and a few oohs or whatever.

But what sticks out in my mind was the menacing glare from Sister X (I don’t recall her name). If looks could kill…How dare I, she seemed to be thinking in very sharp tones, interrupt this evening with, of all things, a sports score! Indeed.

One of Jobs’s legacies

November 7, 2011 by davald

Apple makes a lot of money. The chart below breaks down mobile phone industry profits. This past quarter Apple iPhone shares were but four percent of total unit sales; with the rollout of the iPhone 4s, Apple will likely be the top seller in the current quarter. Yet, Apple took over half of the profits!

Clearly Apple’s margins are much higher than those of Samsung, Nokia, et cetera. Those who purchase Apple products are willing to pay what turns out to be a small premium for greater value.

But there’s something else going on. Apple has left the rest of the electronics industry in the dust when it comes to manufacturing processes and supply chains. Apple’s costs are simply much lower than anyone else’s. The company builds a better product for less.

Those who own Apple stock can thank Steve once again. (I don’t, however, much to my wife’s chagrin.)

Speaking of nominal Democrats…

November 7, 2011 by davald

Washington’s outgoing governor, Christine Gregoire, faced with more dismal revenue numbers, is determined to cut even more from public education budgets. The Herald article this morning focused on the proposal to terminate the levy equalization program, wherein those districts lacking significant commercial and industrial operations—and therefore property-tax revenues—receive some compensation from the state. (Mercer Island is different from all of us in nearly every respect. Its citizens have always supported public education. They can afford to do so.)

Taking the biggest hit in the county would be Marysville, where my wife has taught for 25 years. If the equalization program does indeed end, the state would cut the school district’s subsidy by over $4 million. Its total budget is $110 million.

This year, after the legislature cut funding for teacher salaries by around two percent, the Marysville school board asked teachers and support personnel to take seven half-days off—without pay. The union accepted, perhaps with few options available.

This is madness. For all the ranting about the importance of education and the perennial “crisis” in the classroom, slashing school budgets is hardly the solution. It is counter-productive. Big time.

Yes, I understand that the state’s revenue forecast has been lowered again and that the governor must submit a balanced budget, but Olympia betrays the constitutional mandate to prioritize public education above all else. Shrinking school budgets is also a recipe for long-term disaster.

While I’m at it, we should understand that the Great Recession, occasioned by wacky Wall Streeters, has disproportionately impacted public services in general and the at-risk in particular. Meanwhile, CEO pay has resumed its meteoric rise. As I write the majority of the unemployed have exhausted their benefits. They’re getting nada. Zilch.

We really should be mad as hell. Don’t you think?

By the way, most of us clearly weren’t thinking when we had the opportunity to impose a very modest tax on wealthy citizens’ incomes. Stupid, we are.
