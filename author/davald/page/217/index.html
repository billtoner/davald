The reserve army

May 24, 2011 by davald

Against the austerity crowd, who continue to warn of inflation “just around the corner,” data disappoint. Inflation remains low, too low, in fact, for the likes of Federal Reserve Board chair Ben Bernanke. Investors keep buying treasury bonds, despite low yields. This is important, since rising inflation and bond yields are the oft-cited arguments for tightening the nation’s fiscal belt.

So why haven’t “soaring” deficits and “burdensome” debt led to inflation and a decline in investor confidence? I think we need look no further than the awful employment numbers.

Corporate America has what it’s always wanted: a reserve army of workers. That millions of people lack jobs keeps a lid on wages. Moreover, those who are working realize that, for the grace of God, they could be receiving pink slips tomorrow. So, they’re willing to work harder and put in more hours to avoid being laid off.

Labor productivity, as it happens, is steadily increasing. But instead of sharing the fruits of doing more with less, corporate heads are skimming off the higher profits for themselves, leaving their employees’ wages stagnant or diminished. They know that they can get away with this, refuse to share the wealth, because of rampant job insecurity in the labor force.

In turn, that insecurity results from extremely low and ever-declining union density rates. Consider this chart from OECD.

Look how far down the list America is, with only 11.9 percent of the workforce belonging to unions. See which countries are at the top.

Now we should look at comparative unemployment rates and, most especially, comparative inequality (the Gini coefficient).

We see that the US has the lowest union density rate, the highest unemployment rate, and the highest metric of inequality of these selected countries. I submit that there’s a causal relationship. The less political clout enjoyed by labor relative to capital’s, the greater the unemployment rate, the greater the inequality, and the more depressed workers’ wages. (Note: The per capita GDP of Americans is higher than most of the above countries’. However, our greater inequality means that income and wealth are more heavily concentrated in fewer hands. We know what happens to average income in a bar when Bill Gates enters.)

The best that ever was?

May 24, 2011 by davald

For long-suffering Mariners fans, the club’s recent spate of, well, winning, marked by spectacular pitching performances, warms the cockles of our hearts, whatever the hell a cockle is. Game after game the starting rotation put up zeroes, ones, but no more than twos—until last night, when Jason Vargas struggled to find home plate, yielding four runs, including two four-bangers by Jim Thome, who, at the age of 40, managed to park one 465 feet into the right-field bleachers.

Ah, but Jack Cust finally recorded his first homer, an off-field drive with a runner aboard. He was signed by the Mariners to provide some punch into a listless offense. We have been waiting, some more patiently than others. When he wasn’t stroking home runs, which was all the time until yesterday, Cust was merrily swishing and walking, but rarely hitting safely. His batting average, like many Mariners’, was below his scale weight.

When I reflect on the Mariners’ mediocrity at the plate, I recall, by contrast, baseball’s biggest sluggers.

Notice that I’ve sorted the players by their seasonal home-run average, based on 162 games. My boyhood hero, Willie Mays, trails the list, despite clobbering 660 over the fence in unfriendly parks (the Polo Grounds and Candlestick). Both Hank Aaron and Ted Williams were just a tick above Willie, averaging 37 home runs a season.

It’s said that timing is everything. That’s certainly the case with Ted Williams, who missed all of three seasons and most of two more fighting wars (WWII and Korean). Had Williams come on the scene later, he could have recorded an additional 185 home runs, giving him 706 for his career, or fourth place on the all-time list.

But what I find most remarkable are the lifetime batting averages of Ruth and Williams, .342 and .344, respectively. And Williams hit into a pronounced defensive shift. Imagine blasting 37 home runs and hitting .344 every year, in Williams’ case. More remarkable were Ruth’s numbers of 46 and a shade lower .342.

The current player most likely to challenge Ruth, Aaron, and Bonds is Alex Rodriguez, who bonks an average of 43 round-trippers a year. Rodriguez is 35 years old. Barry Bonds hit 317 home runs after reaching 35. Aaron was second, at 245. The average of the selected sluggers is 209. If A-Rod manages to hit that number, he winds up with 831 for his career, since he’s currently at 622. If he matches Ken Griffey, Jr., at 147, then Rodriguez still bests Bonds, 769 to 762. So, barring serious injury, Alex Rodriguez should end his career at the top of the home run list.

Yet, A-Rod is no Ruth or Williams. His lifetime batting average, while respectable, is only .303, far below Ruth’s and Williams’s.

I can only marvel at the hitting prowess of the past, the likes of which we’re not likely to see again. Although, there’s a guy down in Texas who hits for high average and routinely smacks balls out of the park. He just turned 30.

There’s blood on my iPad

May 23, 2011 by davald

I have been a devoted Apple consumer since the original Macintosh, with credit card receipts to prove it. Yes, I momentarily strayed to the dark side during Cupertino’s wilderness years, when Steve Jobs was in exile, but quickly returned after immediately experiencing the dreaded blue screen. I have not been tempted away since.

We Apple aficionados have gladly, obsessively paid premiums for our products, believing, with much evidence, that the Macintosh (just “Mac” today), iPod, iPhone, and now iPad are superior to their dark-side counterparts in every respect. And did I mention that we like things to work, with simple, efficient elegance?

I’ve known for some time that those magical products originate in mainland China, having tracked multiple FedEx shipments—from Taiwan, to Anchorage, to Seattle, to Bothell, then “out for delivery.” But I have been unwilling to encounter the ugly underbelly of Apple’s production system.

On the recommendation that I would laugh my head off, I accepted an invitation to see Michael Daisey’s one-man performance of “The Agony and Ecstasy of Steve Jobs.” Yes, I laughed and chuckled and chortled, appreciating the familiar vocabulary of “geekiness” and all things Cupertino. I even learned a few nuggets of Apple lore. But then I started to squirm, as did fellow audience members.

Daisey had contrived to enter the fortress of Foxconn in Shenzhen, China. What he witnessed and the facts he poured out last Saturday evening at the Seattle Rep scrubbed away the carefully burnished Apple sheen. Did you know?

  • The labor costs of an iPhone are 80 cents.
  • Foxconn manufactures or assembles 52 percent of all of the electronics in the world.
  • Foxconn’s Shenzhen plant employs over 400,000 workers.
  • The plant has 23 cafeterias, each built to hold 10,000 workers at a time.
  • The onsite dormitories’ rooms are about 10 by 12 and contain as many as 15 beds stacked vertically.
  • Many of the workers are in their early teens.
  • All of Apple’s products are assembled by hand; there are no robots.
  • Workers put in as many as 18 hours a day, often six or seven days a week.
  • Most are not paid overtime.
  • The Chinese government bans labor unions. Anyone suspected of belonging to one may spend the rest of his or her life in prison. (Karl Marx, where are you?)

For workers, having spent several years on an assembly line doing the same micro task over and over and over again, repetitive stress syndrome becomes the norm. Hands and joints grow gnarled and disfigured. When a worker can no longer perform the menial task, he or she is discarded, like a broken iPod. Ironically, in a terribly tragic way, workers assembling Apple’s products cannot afford to actually buy one, so they’ve never used an iPad, despite touching millions of them.

The Foxconn plant that recently exploded, killing several workers, was erected in 70 days to meet the burgeoning demand for the iPad2. Following a spate of suicides at their giant high-rise facilities, Foxconn installed nets around the top of the roofs. Daisey mockingly calls this “corporate responsibility.”

We Mac fans tend to be more liberal than PC devotees. I, for one, having gladly paid a premium for Jobs’s magical devices, wouldn’t hesitate to pay more to improve the working conditions of Foxconn. Or, perhaps Jobs would be content with a little less profit.

Consider that the iPhone claims just five percent of the mobile telephone market, but 20 percent of the revenue, and a whopping 55 percent of the profits. That’s obscene. The iPad has about three fourths of the tablet market and, no doubt, a huge lion’s share of the net revenues. Apple dominates the music scene, being the largest seller, even greater than Walmart.

Jobs sits on over $60 billion in cash, which has investors wondering what, if anything, he plans to do with all that money. Why not spend a little bit of that wad on the welfare of Chinese workers?

Apple could insist that Foxconn establish an 8-hour-day norm, abolish child labor, provide health care and social security, and a pay scale that would allow workers to make a decent living. The Chinese government won’t lift a finger.

Beijing conceived of a Special Economic Zone for Shenzhen, once a fishing village of 700 and now the third most populous city in the world. If you have electronics in your house, regardless of brand, there’s a good chance they were made in Shenzhen by nearly-slave labor, and very young, at that. No doubt profits are being siphoned off by the enriched party leaders in Beijing, as well as by Foxconn executives and Cupertino, of course.

Michael Daisey told his audience that we Americans made Shenzhen, insofar as we have demanded relatively cheap gadgets. Upon leaving the theater we were handed a piece of paper entitled “What happens next?” Daisey wrote, “That lies in your hands.” He asks at the bottom that we “tell others.”

This is just a monologue [he wrote]—a single voice telling a story of a single experience. But if I have opened the door for you, consider opening a door for others. We do not like to think about China and its implications in our country, but that silence can only exist if we are complicit with it. Talking about it, thinking about it when making purchasing decisions, and understanding it is not just symbolic. They are the first seeds of actual change. Do not be afraid to plant them.

Pass the shovel.

A rare talent

May 21, 2011 by davald

The Mariners, who have enjoyed a recent bit of good fortune along with a skein of marvelous pitching performances by their starters, may have a new, rising star in Carlos Peguero, born in the Dominican Republic. He is 6 ft. 5 in and weighs 245 pounds. He may be the proverbial five-skill baseball player, one who can hit, hit with power, field, throw the ball an accurate mile, and run. Most remarkable, he is fast, shockingly so.

During last night’s game in San Diego, Peguero’s talents were on display. Playing left field, he leapt high for a ball destined for the bleachers, snaring the ball in the webbing of his glove. But the play was not over. After falling back to earth, he readied himself, then threw a strike to cut-off man, shortstop Brendan Ryan, who pivoted quickly with an accurate toss to first baseman Justin Smoak, doubling off the runner from first who was beating a hasty return path to the bag.

But what astonished me were two seemingly routine infield outs. Peguero twice hit ground balls to the right side into the Padres’ shift. Peguero almost, and may well have, beaten the throws to first. He appears to be every bit as fast as Ichiro speeding down the first base line.

If Peguero learns to make consistent contact with the baseball, his power will yield one home run after another. A welcome addition to the Mariners’ anemic offense, where four-baggers are as rare as sympathetic Republicans.

And catch his smile. Such enthusiasm.

A single-payer system in America? Getting close.

May 21, 2011 by davald

Vermont is on the verge of delivering health care for all of its citizens. The path has not been easy, blazed in no small part by one persistent physician, who moved to the state after trying unsuccessfully to push New York legislators.

Many years ago Dr. Deb Richter was struck by a particular experience:

As a medical student, she saw a patient with a life-threatening heart infection caused by an infected tooth that had gone untreated because he lacked dental insurance.

“He was in the hospital for six weeks, and I was like, ‘This makes no sense,’ ” she said.

Yes, indeed, no sense. But that’s what we’ve had in America for so long: those lacking health insurance put off routine procedures because they can’t afford it, only to suffer complications that require expensive emergency treatment later. Under a single-payer universal health care program, that wouldn’t happen.

I’ll be watching to see if one opponent’s opinion is realized.

John McClaughry, a former Republican state senator who is against the new law, said Dr. Richter meant well but did not understand the “long-term damage” it would wreak. In particular, he said the law would drive away businesses that did not want to help pay for it.

“She’ll tell you that putting in single-payer will attract businesses from all over the place,” said Mr. McClaughry, vice president of the Ethan Allen Institute, a conservative research group. “I don’t think she has any appreciation of business decisions at all.”

What Mr. McClaughry doesn’t appreciate is the amount of time, money, and resources businesses’ HR departments spend on their employees’ health care administration today. After the Vermont program is in place for a bit, my guess is that Mr. McClaughry will be feasting on crow.

After Vermont, so goes the nation? We’ll see.

The weeping and wailing over public salaries

May 20, 2011 by davald

Should public employees receive lower wages than their private counterparts, even though they perform essentially the same tasks? Judging by the flaming reactions to both the Herald and KOMO TV stories on PUD General Manager Steve Klein’s compensation, the evident answer is yes. But why?

In my defense of the PUD board’s decision to provide Steve with a retention bonus, I said that his proven track record made him very attractive to utility head-hunters and that his skills and experience were second to none in the industry. In my television interview I even went so far as to suggest that Steve was the best utility manager in the country, whether public or private. (Okay, I concede that may have been a bit hyperbolic, since I am unfamiliar with the performances of Steve’s counterparts outside the Northwest region. But I can’t imagine anyone else possessing his skill set.)

To be sure the KOMO feature was a genuine hit piece, making no pretext to being fair and balanced. It led with “Snohomish Co. PUD exec paid more than the gov., AG combined.” True, but so what? Should viewers infer that KOMO believes that the governor and attorney general’s salary is adequate or appropriate and that anything higher is simply too much? Should KOMO have distinguished between elected officials and appointed CEOs? The station mentioned only in passing that the new CEO of Puget Sound Energy, an investor-owned utility, made $500,000, but that was before she was selected to succeed the outgoing CEO, Steve Reynolds, whose 2009 compensation was $8 million, according to the Herald. Although serving more customers, PSE’s business, and the challenges it faces, are virtually the same as the PUD’s. Yet, I would argue that Steve is far more valuable to the customers of the PUD than Mr. Reynolds was to either the customers or shareholders of the IOU.

Most of what I told KOMO was chopped out. I told the reporter that the public squawks at public employee salaries but turns a blind eye to the multi-million-dollar compensation packages in the private sector. Some hedge fund managers draw salaries of a billion dollars a year. And these are some of the people who nearly brought down the global economy, for which they received federal bailouts, only to resume their wanton ways once people’s attentions were again distracted.

Why does the public evidently believe that teachers should make no more than about $50,000 a year? We say that education is of paramount importance, yet deny schools adequate resources and expect their employees to produce academic miracles. I know, because I’m married to a public school teacher, the most difficult job on the planet, given all the institutional constraints and pressures.

One rationale for underpaying government workers is that people freely choose to work in the public sector. Nobody is forcing them to take low-paying jobs. True enough.

It is an American pastime to rail against “the government.” The Tea Party folks, of course, are over the top; in their eyes, no government equals good government. Yet, we all depend on public services rendered by the harshly criticized government agencies. From transportation to education to environmental protection, governments provide goods and services without which our lives would be even more dismal.

Nevertheless, we consistently underfund our governments, expecting superior performance from employees paid below-market wages. We demand our cake and to eat it too, or, loosely translated, something for nothing. I never cease to marvel at what public school teachers accomplish with limited resources, heightened pressures to perform, and exhaustingly long hours.

But let me suggest another way of thinking about all this. Suppose we started acknowledging all those essential public goods and services. Moreover, and this is the important part, suppose further that we actually adopted the novel attitude that we deserve superior performance from government agencies. Instead of governments being viewed as necessary evils, they would become indispensable to enhance the general welfare.

We would not settle for dilapidated school buildings. We would not put up with expensive and environmentally harmful transportation systems. We would demand clean air, water, and soils. We would expect health care to be a right rather than a privilege. We would insist on livable-wage jobs for anyone who was willing and able to work. We would expect adequate pensions upon completion of our working lives.

If you think that the private sector is going to deliver on these demands and expectations across the board, then you live in the right country, the US of A, and you’ve been duped—big time. The market is not concerned with your well-being. It’s too busy obsessing with profits; and increasingly those profits are being siphoned off by just one percent of the population.

Top ten percent

By the way, the share of the wealth enjoyed by the rest of us has remained stagnant and low.

Meanwhile, and this simply astounds me, the rest of us refuse to subject our privileged few to more progressive tax rates. We had our chance last year to impose an income tax on those making more than $250,000, which, I dare say, includes none of you readers. The tax would have affected only the income above the threshold and the revenues collected would have funded public education. In Congress, the Republicans and far too many Democrats want to preserve the Bush tax cuts, which have contributed the lion’s share of our current deficit—along with the two wars.

So, our current politics of resentment is misplaced. We should not be denigrating public officials for receiving modest salaries while ignoring the accumulating fortunes of a privileged few. Rather, we should expect our governments to work for us and, certainly, provide them with sufficient resources, including competitive salaries, to do their proper jobs—serving the people.

The rich have got you buffaloed. Stop them, before it’s too late.

PUD GM compensation: from there to here

May 19, 2011 by davald

I have no doubt that the front-page article in this morning’s Herald about PUD General Manager Steve Klein’s compensation boost will generate much negative reaction. I’ve been there, done that. So, what’s different about today’s PUD and its general manager than past years’, when I strenuously objected to higher management salaries? Good question, Dave. First, a bit of background may be in order.

In my previous life at the PUD I served as Commissioner Chuck Moon’s policy aide. He had proposed to raise the general manager’s salary to what I believed to be an outrageous amount. If the board concurred, which it eventually did, the general manager would be making more than any other public employee, save state university personnel. Chuck told me that he was “paying the position” rather than the person. Herein lies a difference between then and now. Not long after increasing the GM’s salary, the board was forced to terminate him for both incompetence and other matters I need not discuss. The current PUD board is definitely paying the person and not necessarily the position, although the compensation package should be sufficient to attract future GMs; the board’s policy is to “pay market wages” with respect to all PUD positions.

In my years at the PUD I have worked with a half-dozen different general managers, going back to Charlie Earl (who now superintends the state’s community college system). The GM’s tenure during the last two decades was brief and, with few exceptions, tumultuous. I was not terribly excited about any of them. Before I arrived on the board in 2003, the commission had hired long-time Everett mayor Ed Hansen to replace a general manager who left town under a cloud. This was following the Enron scandal. The commission desired two things: to restore fiscal integrity and attain some level of leadership stability. Hansen performed those tasks well. When he retired five years ago, the PUD was in much better shape than when he started.

Then along came Steve Klein.

I had never believed in the “great man” principle, that a single individual could make a significant, positive difference in an organization. After all, I had not witnessed it at the PUD across several general managers. But, and I have no hesitation to admit this, Steve Klein stands out, far and above the rest. His brief bio on the utility’s web page doesn’t do him credit. His abilities extend beyond his resume, which is ideal for a utility general manager; he combines technical knowledge and expertise with a lengthy track record of superior management experience. Steve brings a set of intangibles, difficult to quantify, to produce remarkable results to any challenge.

The Herald article, which I penned a couple of weeks ago, began with the paper’s leader, describing the PUD as a “well-oiled machine.”

There’s a good reason for the headline: the PUD really is a well-oiled machine, and I attribute that description to a single individual: Steve Klein. And that opinion is not just my own. (During a break at our last board meeting, a prominent member of the community approached me to say that he had appreciated the article. He then turned in the direction of Steve Klein and said that the PUD would not be in as good a shape as it is without “him,” namely Steve Klein.) A week ago, the Snohomish County Business Journal, published by the Herald, named Steve Klein its executive of the year. Keep in mind, that the award did not go to the head of a private utility, or to Boeing, or to Kimberly Clark, or to the CEOs of any number of businesses operating in Snohomish County. The award went to the general manager of a public entity—the PUD.

Is a single individual “worth” the money that the board is giving him? Let’s put it this way. Steve Klein brings more value to the PUD than, say, Alex Rodriguez brings the New York Yankees. And what does A-Rod make? Over thirty million dollars a year, or about 90 times as much as Steve.

Okay, you say, that’s an unfair comparison. A better discussion, perhaps, would attempt to answer some counterfactuals. I’ll suggest two:

  1. What compensation could Steve Klein command elsewhere?
  2. Could the PUD entice a similarly qualified individual to run the utility if the GM salary were no more than the governor’s? (One commenter to the article on Steve Klein’s salary openly suggested that Tim Eyman launch an initiative that would place a ceiling on the salaries of all public employees at the level of the governor’s. By the way, that commenter was one of five opponents I faced in my 2002 campaign for the board.)

The CEO of Puget Sound Energy, a private utility, recently retired. The Herald reported that he made $8 million in 2009. His replacement made half a million dollars last year, before her promotion. None of us should be surprised if her salary is at least doubled as she takes over the reins of the IOU. Would PSE have wanted to hire Steve Klein to replace the outgoing CEO? In my judgment, they would have been fools not to have given him the job—were Steve willing to depart the PUD. If PSE’s CEO is “worth” nearly $10 million a year, then certainly Steve Klein brings as much value, and probably a whole lot more.

That said, by all appearances Steve Klein derives much satisfaction running the PUD, which at least partially explains why he chose to remain rather than leave for richer environs. I’ll offer a bit of speculation.

First off, and as I emphasized in my Herald piece, there is very little bureaucracy at the PUD, which consists of just three elected governors (the board) and the general manager. For someone who has great ideas and is anxious to realize them, there is perhaps no better place than the PUD. Steve has broad latitude and discretion, because he has demonstrated over his initial five years an unparalleled capacity for both keeping the lights on and pushing the energy-resource envelope.

Steve has spent all of his career in the public sector, which suggests that he believes in those inherent principles that distinguish public from private organizations. As a public utility, the PUD cannot extract profits for shareholders, although the PUD maintains healthy reserves to ensure fiscal prudence. Nor, as I’ve said, do public utility managers make private-sector salaries like those of Puget Sound Energy. I would argue that PUD ratepayers are getting quite a deal: probably the best utility executive in the region at public-level compensation.

So, what might happen if the board were to cap the GM’s salary? Suppose the commissioners passed a resolution that prohibited any PUD employee from making more than, say, the governor.

Governor Christine Gregoire receives about $167,000 a year. While that salary places her at the top of state’s elected public officials, it’s below that of the Seattle Port Commission’s CEO. And we should note the “elected” part. For whatever reason, we citizens balk at permitting private-sector salaries for our elected officials. As a PUD commissioner, my annual salary, including per diems, is about $30,000, an amount set by Olympia. County Council members make over $100,000 a year.

But Steve Klein is not an elected official. He is the PUD’s chief executive officer. PUD customers deserve to have their utility run as well as possible. A very good CEO makes that happen. A very good board of elected officials can’t, since we commissioners lack the requisite skills and experience.

Back to our question, what kind of GM could the PUD attract if his or her salary capped at the governor’s? What could we get for $167,000 a year? Unless the board just got lucky, I’d say that the best and the brightest would give the PUD wide berth. Why? Just look at the market for utility executives.

Carl Mycoff, whom the PUD board retained a bit over five years ago to help recruit Ed Hansen’s replacement, was mentioned and quoted in the Herald article.

Klein’s salary is among the low end for executives among comparable public utility districts nationwide, said Carl Mycoff, co-owner of the utility executive search firm Mycoff, Fry, and Prouse near Denver.

…

“The only reason Klein stays there is because he likes that kind of utility,” Mycoff said. “He could make more money elsewhere in a heartbeat with his skills.”

There is an adage: you get what you pay for. We three commissioners want top talent at the PUD, led by a superior general manager. We’ve got one in Steve Klein, and we’re willing to pay for it.

The politics of dirt

May 18, 2011 by davald

The new health care law, derisively called ‘Obamacare,’ is a labyrinth (pdf) of rules and regulations that will keep HR departments busy and close to the aspirin bottle. But it typifies what we should expect from Congress or just about any legislative body. Our governments are crucibles for the politics of dirt.

I do not mean, necessarily, that politicians engage in mud-slinging or inhabit the muck, which they surely do. Rather, I mean here that elected officials generally come from some place, whose well-heeled constituents demand that the bacon be brought home. I contrast the politics of dirt with the politics of ideas, while recognizing that our electoral system of winner take all and a government apparatus of “checks and balances” prevents the latter.

Let’s focus on the federal arrangement. However well-intentioned, the Founding Fathers fashioned an elaborate structure that can’t possibly produce a government “of the people, by the people, and for the people.” Having three separate and “equal” branches dilutes accountability and almost guarantees perpetual gridlock. So, who benefits from such a sclerotic system?

Economic power yields political power. Concentrated wealth readily exploits the politics of dirt to derive benefits unavailable to the rest of us.

Using the health care example, we’ve known for some time that Americans spend more on medicines, doctors, and hospitals than citizens of anywhere else. The spending has been rising exponentially, with no signs of abatement.

One huge problem with both the existing and “reformed” systems is private insurance. Health care in America is a commodity just like iPads and wheat. Thus, it must be marketed and profits derived from sales. Profit, of course, is the difference between revenues and costs. It is therefore in the interest of purveyors to keep costs (in the form of claims) down and revenues up. The consumer has opposite goals.

But health care providers, who are mostly private, also desire increased revenues, which they extract in the form of fees for services rendered. The drug companies, in search of profits, demand government-protected patents, with the intended effect of creating artificial scarcity, which allows the companies to charge exorbitant prices for their pills.

Health care in America, then, is a multilayered system of profit-maximizing firms and individuals ostensibly in the business of rendering sick people well. Yet, despite paying more for health care Americans are not all that well.

Unless, of course, you’re rich. In market systems, those with means can purchase both quality and quantity. The rest of us, if we’re fortunate, obtain health insurance through our employers, albeit with increasing co-pays and deductibles. Those who are neither wealthy nor gainfully employed can pray for health or wait until they’re so ill that the only remedy is a trip to the emergency room, whose costs are borne by others.

So why can’t we Americans have a simple, universal program in which every citizen receives a card guaranteeing access to any and all necessary treatment? Well, it has everything to do with the politics of dirt.

Getting and staying elected requires small fortunes. Like Willie Sutton, politicians go where the money is. Since more and more wealth is being concentrated in fewer and fewer hands, senators and representatives rely on a thin slice of the population. For their investment, the rich expect returns.

Any major piece of legislation under consideration in Congress presents opportunities for the wealthy to extract humongous returns. The more complicated and complex the bill, the more opportunity.

Thus, the health care reform act has thousands of pages, and at every turn we find evidence of opportunities being realized. Think of it as a giant, pyramid-like apparatus, with a tiny orifice at the bottom, from which Americans receive drops of benefits. The rest of the structure has been literally designed for siphoning. There will be trillions and trillions of dollars withdrawn from the “new and improved” health care system, as has been the case with the existing hodgepodge, little of it necessary for achieving its ostensible purpose: making sick people well.

Frankly, I don’t see how a different outcome could eventuate, given the politics of dirt.

Privacy concerns: US government vs Apple

May 17, 2011 by davald

Former comedian and now senator Al Franken is taking Apple and other technology companies to task for tracking the locations of their customers. Apple explains that it doesn’t use the information; it’s merely complying with US law, which requires Apple et al. to record the GPS data for the police state.

Meanwhile, I have to wonder why Franken doesn’t yet seem to be dismayed by the US government’s domestic surveillance, a clear violation of federal statutes. Glenn Greenwald quotes from the New Yorker‘s Jane Mayer:

Jack Balkin, a liberal law professor at Yale, agrees that the increase in leak prosecutions is part of a larger transformation. “We are witnessing the bipartisan normalization and legitimization of a national-surveillance state,” he says. In his view, zealous leak prosecutions are consonant with other political shifts since 9/11: the emergence of a vast new security bureaucracy, in which at least two and a half million people hold confidential, secret, or top-secret clearances; huge expenditures on electronic monitoring, along with a reinterpretation of the law in order to sanction it; and corporate partnerships with the government that have transformed the counterterrorism industry into a powerful lobbying force. Obama, Balkin says, has “systematically adopted policies consistent with the second term of the Bush Administration.”

That New Yorker piece, by the way, is a must read. It certainly reveals Obama to be a major hypocrite. What he said before he became president and what he now sanctions from the Oval Office do not comport.
