How I voted

November 7, 2011 by davald

Not that it really matters, but here is how I marked my ballot this year. I may even provide some rationale for the choices, however quirky.

On the initiatives, I should say first off that any measure run by Tim Eyman earns an automatic nay in our household. In the original Greek sense of the term, he is an idiot, who nevertheless delights being in the spotlight. Such unchecked ego, save for us who defeat his initiatives. But wait. He just comes back for more. So, no on 1125.

The liquor initiative, 1183, garnered two yes votes at the table during yesterday’s voting. I grew up in California, which has few restrictions on the sale of alcohol. If a minimart purveys small bags of potato chips and lottery tickets it probably sells Jim Beam, too. And there is no giant tax, although California could surely use the money. In an opinion this morning the Seattle Times editors cite Attorney General Rob McKenna’s change of heart, after he’d done some research. McKenna voted against the two previous ballot items on liquor sales. Now he supports 1183, discovering that California’s DUI rate is no higher than ours, with our current state-run stores.

I must add that when I moved up here from the Golden State I was taken aback by the backwardness of Washington in some respects, the sale of alcohol being one of them. (Another was the lack of paved sidewalks in new housing developments.) I simply don’t understand the Puritan hold on vice, which is making its way into the ads of initiative opponents. The anti-campaign is a whole lot of bullshit, in my humble judgment. Moreover, the state has no business selling booze or, for that matter, running a gambling operation—ostensibly to fund education. How’s that working out?

Initiative 1163, pushed by advocates of more training of long-term health care workers, received few, if any, newspaper endorsements. It scored two yes votes here.

The other initiatives struck me as innocuous house-keeping measures. I voted for both.

As for the local races, I kept my record intact of never voting for an official Republican. So I pinched my nose and marked my ballot for the nominal Democrat, Aaron Reardon. (Those who read my Herald “Viewpoints” piece know that I have no affection for our system of county government, which is myriad, unwieldy, induces acrimony, and offers almost zero accountability. I would prefer a council that selects the various county officers, including the executive.)

Though I’ve lived in Everett for a half-dozen or so years, I’m less familiar with its city council than I was of Marysville’s when I lived in that city. I’ve met Drew Nielsen and know Jackie Minchew. So I voted for them both.

Now for the embarrassing part. Regarding the other candidates, even after reading the voters pamphlet, I found little to distinguish one from the other. Some races were left unmarked on my ballot.

In my own case, I’m sure that many thousands of people either ignored the PUD race or voted for my opponent—based on the flimsiest of reasoning. However, I choose to believe that those who voted in my favor in 2002 and then again in 2008 did so because they liked what I stood for. Let me harbor illusions, please.

Can’t win for losing

November 7, 2011 by davald

The Snohomish County Public Utility District board adopted a far-reaching, progressive integrated resource plan. It has two major components: achieve as much cost-effective conservation as practicable; and supplement the conservation with renewable resources, preferably in our own backyard.

The rationale for the policy has multiple ingredients:

  • acknowledge climate change and the need to reduce the utility’s carbon footprint;
  • comply with the mandates of I-937;
  • minimize financial risk (e.g., acquire both supply and demand-side resources in small chunks so as to mirror customer growth);
  • reduce reliance on regional transmission, which is costly and increasingly problematic (e.g., integration of intermittent resources like wind); and
  • preserve—and enhance when possible—the area’s environmental attributes and indigenous species.

The PUD has the highest percentage of wind energy in its supply portfolio of any regional utility. However, because of the problems of integrating wind resources (the wind doesn’t always blow when we want it to), the utility will not be acquiring more wind power in the foreseeable future. Rather, to meet continuing load growth and comply with the state’s resource portfolio standard the PUD is focused on harnessing local resources. We’ve identified four:

  • solar;
  • tidal;
  • geothermal; and
  • small, low-impact hydro.

Just recently the PUD received a 45-year license renewal of the Jackson Hydroelectric Project. This involved a decade-long process culminating in agreements with an array of stakeholders, including the Tulalip Tribes, non-governmental organizations, and state and federal agencies. But the utility went over and above the various legal requirements. We’ve enhanced fish habitats, improved river flows, and expanded recreational opportunities.

Over the past several months the PUD has restored and greatly improved the existing Woods Creek and Young’s Creek small hydro projects. In so doing, we have left the sites in better shape than when we initially acquired them. Of critical importance the PUD actually enhanced fish habitat and survival.

The PUD is investigating at least three more small hydro projects, two of which we acquired from Puget Sound Energy. The largest is Sunset Falls, which, if built, will provide 30 megawatts of capacity. The utility filed for a preliminary permit from the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission to investigate the feasibility of constructing and operating the facility. There have been two community meetings thus far, which gave citizens an opportunity to see what the utility has in mind, ask questions of staff, and voice their concerns. (You can view related documents here.)

While it may seem self-serving on my part, since I am on the PUD board, I can say without reservation that the utility will not force this project on anyone. Indeed, we do whatever is necessary to ensure that all interests are not only respected but incorporated in the design, construction, and operating of this or any other facility.

Despite our being only in the exploratory mode, some people have registered strong objections. For our part, the utility will go out of its way to determine their legitimacy.

A few individuals seem to be ideologically opposed to anyone building any new dams or diversions anywhere. To those I would suggest taking a step back to examine these small projects within a larger context. Keep in mind that the utility has a statutory obligation to meet the electricity needs of people living and working in Snohomish County and Camano Island. I’ve already mentioned that the PUD is pursuing all cost-effective conservation. I’ve also mentioned the challenges we face transmitting electricity from distant resources. Also, there are opportunities within our own territory that deserve exploration. The utility has no fossil-fuel resources in its own supply portfolio now or ever. But the energy that we need beyond conservation has to come from somewhere.

Snohomish County is blessed with natural features that can be exploited in environmentally sensitive ways. We’ve got the Sound to the west, so it makes sense to investigate tidal power potential, which the utility is doing. The Cascades sit above the “ring of fire.” Our preliminary investigation has revealed a promising site for geothermal exploration and, if feasible, development. Geothermal, by the way, is an ideal resource: it’s renewable and firm—that is, it runs 24/7.

It also rains here. That combination of falling water and mountains yields hydropower potential.

The utility desires small, incremental projects that pose no danger to fish or wildlife. The PUD will not build a dam or diversion that in any way threatens or interferes with the viability of anadromous fish species. Indeed, it is the utility’s goal to enhance the survivability of indigenous species as we construct and operate facilities.

So, I was taken aback by a letter that appeared in this morning’s Herald. The author wrote:

PUD officials misleadingly compare the Sunset Falls project to their recently completed Young’s Creek dam and proclaim to Herald readers that “the project’s location above the falls would not affect spawning salmon.” Sounds good, except the facts are that salmon and steelhead are trapped by the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife and released upstream where they thrive in the 100 miles of pristine spawning habitat above the falls. The PUD’s public deception about this is very disturbing.

How is the PUD misleading people? In the event the Sunset Falls project is built, fish would continue to be trapped and released upstream where they will continue to “thrive in the 100 miles of pristine spawning habitat above the falls.” And as I said above, the PUD will most certainly enhance fish populations should the project proceed.

On paying teachers for more credentials

November 5, 2011 by davald

I’m aware of no evidence that attaining more degrees boosts teachers’ abilities and effectiveness. Requiring a master’s degree as a condition for either landing an education job or earning a higher salary only ensures that tertiary schools make more money and, more often than not, burdens teachers with much greater debt.

Thus, I very much agree with Yglesias’s recent post. He writes:

The whole system of paying teachers bonuses to acquire largely meaningless extra credentials is a totally wacky back door subsidy to the nation’s master’s degree programs that mostly serves to waste teachers’ time. Even without getting into any more controversial ideas about teacher compensation, it would make much more sense to scrap these bonuses and reallocate the money to higher base pay across the board.

Amen.

Still looking for a job? Good luck with that.

November 4, 2011 by davald

Whoopee. The economy added 80,000 jobs, nudging the unemployment rate down a tick. But don’t get your hopes up.

It appears that Marx was right about a few things, including this point:

The greater the social wealth, the functioning capital, the extent and energy of growth, and therefore also the greater absolute mass of the proletariat and the productivity of its labour, the greater is the industrial reserve army… [Capital, Vol. 1, 798; my italics].

Consider this sobering comment, too:

“We are very gradually moving to a labor-less society,” said Bernard Baumohl, chief global economist at The Economic Outlook Group. Firms, he said, “are making sure that the number of workers they have now are able to produce more if necessary. The problem is that there are going to be a lot of Americans who are going to remain unemployed for a very long time.”

Those lucky enough to be working are producing more with less.

About those one percent, they’re doing quite well, thank you very much. While the bottom 99 percent have seen their incomes stagnant or falling, the very rich are gobbling up more and more of the nation’s total wealth.

Notice that incomes were rising steadily, albeit modestly, following the end of WWII—until the mid-70s. (Chart based on data from Emmanuel Saez, UC Berkeley) The Congressional Budget Office has data to 2007. It’s most recent report includes this graph:

 

By the way, to be eligible for the top one percent you’ll need an minimum income of about $350,000. The first quintile’s income starts at zero dollars and ends at around $20,000. Entrance into the top 10 percent requires an income of almost $75,000. So, 90 percent of Americans make less than $75,000. The average income of those in the top .01 percent is above $4 million.

Yes, the rich are different, extremely so.

Bill’s coming back

November 3, 2011 by davald

Moyers, that is. Yep. He’s returning to PBS this January, having received a generous grant from the Carnegie Corporation. Oh, how I’ve missed him.

Here’s one reason why. Read this talk he gave in honor of Ralph Nader’s group Public Citizen. Some excerpts:

We hold elections, knowing they are unlikely to produce the policies favored by the majority of Americans.  We speak, we write, we advocate – and those in power turn deaf ears and blind eyes to our deepest aspirations.  We petition, plead, and even pray – yet the earth that is our commons, which should be passed on in good condition to coming generations, continues to be despoiled.  We invoke the strain in our national DNA that attests to “life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness” as the produce of political equality – yet private wealth multiplies as public goods are beggared.  And the property qualifications for federal office that the framers of the Constitution expressly feared as an unseemly “veneration for wealth” are now openly in force; the common denominator of public office, even for our judges, is a common deference to cash.

In true Moyers fashion, he gives us the sobering, daunting facts of life, of how the rich have planned their own plutocracy at our expense. But then he calls us to action, rhetorically genuflecting before the occupiers of Wall Street. Nothing achieved by the many has come without struggle.

But if the generations before us had given up, slaves would still be waiting on these tables, on Election Day women would still be turned away from the voting booths, and workers would still be committing a crime if they organized.

It’s time we wiped away the scales from our eyes that cloud the truth. This land, while it may be yours, has been purchased by the very rich, whom far too many of us choose to coddle. Face it. They don’t give a rat’s rectum about your life. Heck, they’d like all of us to live out the lyrics to Dylan’s Tombstone Blues:

Mama’s in the factory, she ain’t got no shoes.

Daddy’s in the alley, he’s looking’ for food.

Yet, Moyers tells us:

Who, in these cynical times, when democracy is on the ropes and the blows of great wealth pound and pound and pound again against America’s body politic – who would dream  such a radical thing?

Look around.

Bust the teachers’ union, saith Steve Jobs

November 3, 2011 by davald

We’re all entitled to our opinions. Those with money can actually give effect to their biases, good or ill. Bill Gates is spending billions of dollars to eradicate malaria. But he’s also out to reform public education in dubious ways.

Comes now Steve Jobs through biographer Walter Isaacson to throw his two cents at the problem of schools. Unlike Gates, however, Jobs didn’t start a foundation or lobby Congress. He just voiced an opinion. His wife, Laurene, deserves credit for establishing successful programs for disadvantaged youths.

Jobs believes that teachers’ unions stymie proper education. If he ran your local school district, he’d give authority and autonomy to principals, who, Jobs said, should be able to hire and fire whomever he or she wants.

I have mixed feelings about Jobs’s opinions. While I would like to see principals assume more responsibility for their respective schools, I also recognize that personalities cloud judgments. Teachers’ unions afford protection against whimsical punishment and discharge. Moreover, how can we know that the principal is appropriate to the tasks at hand?

Teaching is an extremely difficult job, especially in schools with troublesome demographics. My wife is a long-time public school teacher who is now in a high-poverty school. She is struggling mightily to achieve academic progress for her students, most of whom score far below grade level. She would love to have more and better resources to help her succeed. But none are forthcoming. Indeed, school budgets are being cut not enhanced. Do more with less is the implicit message.

There are both very good teachers and regrettably poor ones, and they’re easy to identify. Most, however, fit somewhere in the middle of a normal distribution, where the differences are quite small and variable. By mere chance a teacher may get a roomful of respectful, curious students, making teaching relatively easy if not also satisfying. On the other hand, a new school year may bring the proverbial class from hell. These students would rather be any place than school, and their behavior makes that loud and clear. It is left to miracle workers to achieve inroads here, and few, if any, exist.

That most teachers occupy the middle and are not easily distinguishable from the other, unions are important. In addition to advocating for the interests of their members, unions provide job security against capricious administrators who harbor disturbing biases.

The overwhelming majority of those who got into teaching in the first place received good educations, care deeply about the welfare of pupils, and are willing to work beyond the contract hours. As with my wife, most dip into their own small pockets to purchase supplies and books. They are, as a rule, dedicated public servants charged with the noblest of objectives: imparting knowledge in a variety of subjects to students with diverse interests, backgrounds, curiosity, and native intelligence.

Yet it is the teachers and their unions that incur the greatest wrath. It is misplaced, of course. I’d like to believe that criticisms would be tempered if there were a better appreciation of just how difficult teaching is. Few of us would last a single day in the classroom. I know, because I failed miserably at teaching.

Public education, both its structure and challenges, mirrors the greater society. That we happen to live in one of the most unequal societies in the developed world should give us pause before we start pointing fingers at teachers and their professional advocates.

If you want a deserving target, go to Wall Street. It’s a good start, anyway.

As for the end

November 2, 2011 by davald

I’ve just finished reading Isaacson’s biography of Steve Jobs. So I’ll probably post a few Jobs-related comments from time to time. Like right now.

In the “coda,” Isaacson asked Jobs, who was close to “achieving” death, about the afterlife, did it exist and whether or not there is a God. Jobs said on the question of a deity, “I’m about fifty-fifty…”

Then, after a period of silence and reflection, Jobs said: “On the other hand, perhaps it’s like an on-off switch. Click! And you’re gone.”

Jobs paused again before concluding, “Maybe that’s why I never liked to put on-off switches on Apple devices.”

Learning more brings further confusion

November 2, 2011 by davald

Economics has rightly been called ‘the dismal science,’ both for its subject matter and methodologies. As for the latter, economists, at least those in academia, believe that they are being scientific when they collect data, develop models, and conjure theories. “We’re just like the physicists,” they might claim. Well, no, they’re not.

One reason for the difference is that it’s nearly impossible to conduct controlled experiments, isolating confounding variables and whatnot, as physicists, chemists, or even biologists do every day. After all, the economy is both huge—now global—and dynamic; it doesn’t sit still long enough to study cause and effect.

However, economists can certainly look backwards to seek understanding. The past is not quite as elusive as the present, not to mention the future.

Yet, even here, economists bicker over what caused the Great Depression that was 80 years ago. Worse, they can’t agree on what events or policies ended it.

Many fix the divide in economic thinking between “salt water” and “fresh water” economists. The latter dominated by the University of Chicago, especially under Robert Lucas, a Nobel winner. The former, as one would imagine, occupy faculty positions on the two coasts, but mostly Princeton and Harvard along the Atlantic and Berkeley bordering the Pacific.

Historically, the divergence in thought began during the 70s, when the economy was mired in both high unemployment and high inflation. This conjunction was judged unlikely by those schooled in the Keynesian tradition. Observation of historical data had led to wide acceptance of the Phillips curve. Given this anomaly, the freshwater economists declared Keynesian economics dead.

I have the luxury of engaging a professional economist at least once a week. He holds a PhD from the University of Washington. Let’s call him ‘Hume.’ I delight in our frequent discussions, picking the doctor’s brain while he’s forced to endure my babbling on about Paul Krugman or Brad DeLong or Christine Romer—all saltwater economists.

“Hume” rejects monetary expansion. Increases in money supply, he believes, ineluctably yield higher inflation. But when I counter that the money supply has tripled over the last couple of years without any inflationary effects, he says, “Just wait.” For how long?

Suppose, I ask, that the Federal Reserve commits to increasing the supply of money by the inflation plus two percent (a figure “Hume” often uses to describe his preferred amount). Suppose further that Congress refuses to enact fiscal stimulus. Would the economy right itself? “Hume” says yes.

But would it not be possible, as Keynes himself argued, for the economy to reach an equilibrium (a favorite term of economists) with unemployment sticking at around ten percent? “Hume” again says yes.

What, then, would a recovered economy look like with so many people permanently out of work? Since the end of WWII the average annual rate of unemployment is 5.8 percent. Could nine or ten percent become the new “normal”?

Professor Krugman, Dean Baker, DeLong, and others have been persistently and now almost angrily calling for massive fiscal stimulus to put people to work. Clearly low taxes aren’t doing the trick. Nor are businesses complaining all that much about government regulations. Rather, the complaint is about people not buying enough stuff—that is, insufficient aggregate demand.

“Hume” replies that the problem with fiscal stimulus is that it’s only temporary. Its effects, however salutary, wear off sooner or later to return the economy back to where it began. Well, I wonder aloud, why not a permanent stimulus?

Certainly there are enough public-sector projects to keep millions of people employed for many years, if not forever. We’ve got an aging, crumbling transportation infrastructure demanding urgent attention. Our public school buildings need repair and replacement.

But states are heading in the wrong direction, forced to dramatically cut budgets and, therefore, personnel. The effects are being felt in the schools, I can attest. There’s so much pressure to do more with a lot less. “Hume” knows this too, because his wife, like my wife, is a public school teacher.

I might dismiss my confusion as a trivial gap in understanding, similar to never having learned French. But given our current mess the study of economics seems so very important these days. What is to be done? And will a righteously correct one-armed economist come to the rescue?

Wall Street won’t help the Rest of Us. As Keynes averred, only the federal government can provide the proper analgesic during recessionary periods.

Physicists know how long it takes for a lead ball to hit the ground from ten feet high. (Gosh, I do, too.) Yet economists can’t reach consensus on prescriptions to cure our economic diseases. Dismal, maybe. Scientific? Hardly.

No divisions, less is better

October 31, 2011 by davald

I’ve been reading Walter Isaacson’s biography of Steve Jobs. I’m just at the part where Apple is developing the iPod and iTunes to create a new music ecosystem. Why were Jobs and Apple able to pull this off and other companies, who had the requisite technology, unable to do so?

Jobs suggested that much of the problem with places like Sony, which arguably had all the tools in place (remember Walkman?), was that it was broken into divisions, each with its own profit-and-loss responsibilities. Thus, each division inevitably competed with the others, frustrating the collective effort necessary to put it all together. Apple, on the other hand, had only a single “division” and just one P&L.

But Apple also had Jobs. Key to his success, I think, was not only his exquisite and exhausting attention to detail. He understood that less is better. It was his ability to just say no that enabled Apple to concentrate on doing a few things extremely well.

Microsoft, as we know, charted a different course under the guise of freedom and choice. Bill Gates decided at the very beginning to make his money licensing the software to dozens of different hardware vendors.

When Apple was in the doldrums and at risk of having to shutter its doors, Gates and others strongly urged Apple to license its software to outside hardware manufacturers, just as Microsoft had done. And when Jobs was in the wilderness running both NeXT and Pixar, Apple’s then-CEO permitted a couple of companies to build Mac OS-compatible devices. Upon his return to Apple in 1997, Jobs quickly terminated the agreements.

Jobs believed strongly in closely integrating the software and hardware, unlike the Windows PC side. He reckoned that there was too much fragmentation under the Redmond regime. There were, and continue to be, so many problems with compatibility, as hardware vendors would incorporate different tweaks—and then all those third-party applications and utilities. It’s a wonder that PCs worked at all.

Nor was Jobs’s focus just on limiting the number of devices Apple would produce. The devices themselves were always on diets. Jobs’s creations had fewer features than PC counterparts. Just compare an HP laptop with a MacBook Air. The former is stodgy, ugly, assembled with screws, thick, heavy—but, my, it has a lot of ports and slots. Indeed, PC manufacturers seemed to suffer from “ports gap.” Jobs killed off the floppy drive and, more recently, the optical drive—neither of which will you find in the Airs. (Image from Apple)

As much as Jobs loved Apple and its products he did not hesitate to pull the plug on devices. Nor did he worry about cannibalizing his own products. For example, as iPhone sales skyrocket, iPod sales are down. No problem, since the iPhone includes the iPod functionality—by design. More people are buying iMacs than Mac Pros. Kill the latter.

Though Jobs would behave as a martinet more often than not, he also realized that good products require close collaboration amongst those with different, but complementary, skill sets. So Jobs insisted that the places he superintended be designed to facilitate accidental encounters.

Isaacson writes about Pixar’s being in need of new headquarters, having outgrown its old. Jobs purchased the abandoned Del Monte cannery in Emeryville, at which I happened to work during one college summer (a really crummy job, by the way). Always paying attention to design and function, Jobs ensured that employees would have to run into each other, even on trips to the bathrooms, of which there were only two, at opposite ends of the studio.

Jobs’s org chart differed remarkably from the conventional. Instead of an inverted tree, Jobs had fashioned a wheel. He was at the center surrounded closely by a small hub of direct reports. In most organizations, the branches become insular silos. Jobs would have none of that. (The following graphic from Fortune.

You may have seen the designs for Apple’s new headquarters in Cupertino, on property acquired, ironically enough, from HP. It reminded me of the space station hovering above the moon in 2001: A Space Odyssey. Again, employees, no matter what product they’re working on, will necessarily encounter one another, which yields ad hoc conversations that Jobs believed would stimulate innovative thinking via cross-pollination. (I could imagine Jobs sitting in the center of his new space station, sans shoes, eating apples and drinking Odwalla juice, pondering really cool products.)

Jobs also detested slide presentations inside the workplace; he reserved them for product rollouts, and he was a master at giving them. Jobs expected his leadership team to come to meetings fully prepared and ready to brainstorm. Jobs himself liked to think aloud using a whiteboard and markers. It was important for him to see things. He couldn’t be bothered with spreadsheets. If an employee had an idea for a product, he or she had better have a physical mockup available for Jobs to touch and feel.

It may sound Pollyannaish, but Jobs really did want to marry the arts with technology, and he believed that intuition was more important than intelligence. There are numerous examples of this preference in Isaacson’s book. One stands out so far. Jobs said that most computer vendors let the engineers determine how a machine is designed. The guts would come first, leaving those responsible for the containers to house the engineering contents. The result, thought Jobs, was a whole lot of ugly.

For Jobs, the look and feel and function by the eventual user were most important, qualities that he would intuit. The engineers would be tasked to devise the appropriate components to make them fit together seamlessly and attractively. That’s why Jobs made Jony Ive the second most powerful person at Apple; he is the award-winning industrial designer par excellence. In Ive’s studio was a machine that would convert computer drawings to foam models that could then be painted and even given heft with lead shots, if needed. And that’s where Jobs liked to spend a lot of his time, touching, feeling, holding the prototypes. The artist at work.

Apple, you will learn should you read the biography, is a much different place than any other organizations you may have worked, visited, or read about. As electronics companies try to ape Jobs’s devices, I’m sure that some of them will try to copy his management style and even his workplaces.

They will likely fail at this and produce, in Jobsian lingo, “nothing but shit.”
