Snow as variability [u]

January 19, 2012 by davald

Heavy snow (for us) has blanketed Puget Sound; and it’s still falling, defying yesterday’s forecast—it was supposed to have tapered off last night. Also, it’s cold out there, about 25 degrees. I pity the postal carriers.

We may remember Senator James Inhofe mocking climate change science during a major snowstorm in D.C. How could there be so much white stuff if the planet is supposed be warming?

By way of the folks at Real Climate, I’ve linked to a YouTube cartoon that demonstrates the difference between climate and weather variability. Clever.

Real Climate reminds us:

Success in understanding our universe often depends on how the ‘story’ about it is told, and a big part of that often involves how mental images are presented. Mathematics and statistics can describe nature in great detail and “elegance”, but they are often difficult and inaccessible to the average person. Conversely, the man-and-dog animation is intuitive and easy to comprehend.

Pass it on.

Progressive taxation: a follow up

January 19, 2012 by davald

By way of Krugman, this study (pdf) by Thomas Piketty and Emmanuel Saez concludes that the tax structure has become less progressive over time. In the chart below the ‘P’ stands for percentile, with the last group on the right, P99.99-100, representing the top .01 percent.

In a nutshell, the total taxes paid by the very rich have fallen by almost 60% from 1960 to 2004. The marginal tax rate fell from 91% to 35% in 2004, where it stands today.

Back to SOPA and PIPA

January 19, 2012 by davald

I just received this response from Congressman Rick Larsen:

Thank you for contacting me about the Stop Online Piracy Act (SOPA) and the Protect IP Act (PIPA). I wanted to update you on my views on this important issue.

I am opposed to SOPA and PIPA in their current forms. I believe that these bills create unacceptable threats to free speech and free access to the internet. I have heard from many of you in Northwest Washington who are deeply concerned about the potential impacts of SOPA and PIPA.

Online piracy is a serious problem that costs U.S. businesses billions of dollars. Government agencies must be empowered to stop and prosecute intellectual property thieves. But in doing so we cannot undermine freedom of speech or jeopardize the free flow of information on the internet. I will work with my colleagues to see that any final anti-online piracy legislation protects the internet and does not encroach on free speech rights.

Please be assured that I will keep your thoughts in mind should I have the opportunity to vote on any legislation that would impact online piracy and internet freedom on the House floor.

Again, thank you for contacting me.  I encourage you to contact me in the future about this or any other issue of importance to you.

This got me thinking

January 19, 2012 by davald

Today Apple announced a new, ambitious initiative. It’s all about textbooks for K-12 as well as university. The company released a new version of iBooks, which allows anyone with an iPad to read the books. It also developed a new application for making content, which Apple has dubbed “iBooks Author.” Both apps are free.

Steve Jobs told Walter Isaacson that the textbook industry was “corrupt.” It is also much too expensive, he said. He was working on destroying this system. Today the destruction begins.

But there’s a big problem. Schools don’t have any money. So they won’t be buying a whole lot of iPads. Unless…

…Apple continues producing the iPad 2 but at much lower prices, say down to $200, the price of the Kindle Fire, and gives significant discounts to schools. The iPad 3 is expected to be revealed next month. It is rumored to be major leap above the current version, with a Retina display, faster processor, improved chips, better cameras, and perhaps longer battery life, although the iPad 2 is no slouch in this category.

There is a precedent for this. Last year Apple released its latest smartphone, the iPhone 4S. Yet, it continues production of both the iPhone 4 and the iPhone 3S, priced at $99 and $0, respectively, with two-year contracts.

Apple will soon report its earnings for the last quarter. It is expected to smash existing records, having sold millions of its smartphones, iPads, and Macs. Meanwhile, the company’s pile of cash keeps growing. It can afford to reduce margins on previous device versions, as it retains high margins on its newest models.

We’ll see.

Not all that progressive

January 19, 2012 by davald

American Progress has done some arithmetic using publicly available sources. They produced this chart:

In 2010, according to the U.S. Census, the distribution of total income looked like this:

While this is hardly scientific, why not set the rate according to each quintile’s share of wealth? Or, we might take the average rate (in 2009 that was 17%) as a base, then set the marginal rate for all income above the average of adjusted gross income, which was $49,000 in 2009, for each quintile. Thus, if you were in the top quintile, your marginal rate on all income above $49,000 would be 50.2%.*

Several of the GOP presidential candidates have proposed tax reform strategies, including a flat tax on all income. The flat tax would certainly simplify matters, which is what Jerry Brown argued when he ran for the White House in 1992; he suggested 17%, as I recall, which happens to be the average tax in 2009 (see footnote). However, a flat tax yields hardship for the lower quintiles and a boon for higher-income individuals.

Suppose we assume a flat tax of 17% on all income. For someone making only $10,000, the tax obligation would be $1,700. If the income were $1,000,000, the tax would be $170,000. Under the existing tax structure, the rate is 10% on incomes below $17,000 (married with joint returns). For the millionaire, it’s a bit more complicated. I come up with a shade less than $320,000 that goes to the IRS, slightly different from the figure shown in the footnote, but nearly 90% more than under a flat tax.

After taxes, the person earning $10,000 has $9,000 left over. The millionaire still has $680,000 on which to live. Chances are the poorer individual spends every single penny of the $9,000; but the millionaire can pay all her expenses and still have money to save, invest, and play.

One may ask by what rationale should tax rates be progressive? (Or, in Ron Paul’s case, why should there be any federal taxes at all?) Good question. Here’s one answer:

The real justification for progressive taxation has nothing to do with compassion or fairness or funding the welfare roles. The rationale for progressive is the preservation of competitive markets in which people are not prevented from entering into a business and/or employment because of monopoly or oligarchical control by a few large “owners” of the productive means.  Nowhere is this more evident than in the “financial” sector.  It should be obvious that if corporate income in the billions is taxed much more heavily than corporate income in the millions then earnings per share will be better in smaller corporate entities. And if that is the case then we have a very elegant control on the size of corporations.

I would add to this that the situation that we’re in now, with increasing accumulation of wealth by one small sliver of society, leads ineluctably to mischief. The two great economic upheavals of the past 100 years followed huge increases in wealth by the One-percenters. They have so much money they literally don’t know what to do with it; so they start gambling. Yes, ladies and gentlemen, Mr. Keynes was right: Wall Street is nothing more than a casino. With so much money involved, their mistakes imperil the Rest of Us. We dodged a monumental bullet a couple of years ago. As bad as things are, they could have been much, much worse.

And one more thing, echoing Justice Brandeis: We can either have a democracy or concentrated wealth, but we can’t have both. We pretend to favor democracy. Yet we have allowed a tiny few to amass great fortunes. They operate by different rules than the Rest of Us, and those rules further enrich the powerful while eroding our ability to effect changes that benefit the many. Plutocracy wasn’t what the Founding Fathers had in mind. But that’s what we’ve got.

One way of limiting the power of the few is to tax the hell out of them, then put that money in service to the Rest of Us. Our schools are dilapidated and ill-supplied. The roads are in disrepair. We have no alternative transportation to speak of. Education is too costly. The planet is warming. (Though I write this in the midst of unusual snow showers.)

Time to resurrect genuine democracy. Except there’s all these damn Republicans in the way.

*  As an example, Mr. Bob made $1,000,000 last year, after adjustments. He’s in the top quintile for sure. He would be taxed 17 percent on $49,000, which would be $8,330. The income above that, or $951,000, would be taxed at 50.2 percent, or $477,402. Mr. Bob’s total tax liability equals $485,732. Under the current tax structure Mr. Bob’s obligation to the IRS is $313,222, using this calculator.

Stepping a few parsecs beyond his field…

January 18, 2012 by davald

Whoa. Paul Krugman ranges far beyond his academic discipline in this post, wherein he offers an hypothesis about the Republican presidential candidates. However provocative, I find it plausible.

Krugman believes that the only reason that any of the GOP candidates pose a threat to Obama’s second term is the state of the economy. Were it closer to being a palpably robust recovery, Romney et al. wouldn’t earn serious consideration. That’s not just speculation on Krugman’s part; it’s historical—presidential electoral fortunes closely track the economy’s trajectory.

So we now have a bevy of Republican hopefuls all meeting Krugman’s description of “frauds or fools,” FOF for short. He slaps down Gingrich as both. Romney diligently pursues the title of fraud, flipping and flopping all the way to November. One GOP hopeful, Jon Huntsman, said something to the effect that Romney was “a well-oiled weather vane.” After recently dropping out of the race, Huntsman endorsed…Romney.

Krugman:

So what you have are fairly dim types like Perry, on the one side, and the utterly cynical Romney, on the other. (Gingrich manages to be both a fool and a fraud). Maybe, just maybe, the GOP could have found someone able to achieve Romney-level cynicism while coming across as sincere; but political talent on that level is quite rare. I mean, the various non-crazy-non-Romneys who were supposed to have a shot all turned out to be duds, e.g. Pawlenty.

The weakness of the GOP field is, in short, structural. Without the still-terrible economy, they wouldn’t have a chance.

Notwithstanding the grudging state of the economy, against this FOF field Obama stands out as respectable genius. He’s not, of course. But he really doesn’t have to try; the stark contrast does it for him.

It’s trivial to make this point, but I’ll make it anyway. What we see (or think we see) is largely dependent on our point of view, the perspective from which we observe the world; and it applies not just to ordinary experience (it’s cold or wet outside) but also to the significant issues of the day.

Take this example from the most recent Harper’s Index:

Factor by which an American is more likely to cite unemployment than the deficit as the country’s “most important problem”: 3

Factor by which a wealthy American is more likely to cite the deficit than unemployment: 3

Now consider the media emphasis placed on the deficit vs. unemployment, a drumbeat that even got the White House on board. The fact that 23 million Americans are either out of a job or are underemployed took a back seat to deficit reduction.

Under God

January 18, 2012 by davald

It’s amazing what one can accomplish with a lot of money to spend. Why, the One-percenters have been at it for a very long time, but much in earnest since the Collapse of 1929.

Imagine yourself back then, now struggling to put food on the table along with nearly everyone else in the Great Depression. You’ve lost faith in corporate America, rightly viewed as the problem rather than the solution. The banks were worst of all, busy foreclosing on homes and denying people credit or arrangements.

Well, the One-percenters didn’t just sit back and take it. They organized. But first, what message to spread and who should spread it? Wouldn’t you know that surveys showed that a plurality, if not a majority, of Americans placed their trust mostly in clergymen of the decidedly Christian variety.

As for the message, how about “one nation under God”? Kevin Kruse, writing for the New York Times:

During the Great Depression, the prestige of big business sank along with stock prices. Corporate leaders worked frantically to restore their public image and simultaneously roll back the “creeping socialism” of the welfare state. Notably, the American Liberty League, financed by corporations like DuPont and General Motors, made an aggressive case for capitalism. Most, however, dismissed its efforts as self-interested propaganda. (A Democratic Party official joked that the organization should have been called “the American Cellophane League” because “first, it’s a DuPont product and, second, you can see right through it.”)

Realizing that they needed to rely on others, these businessmen took a new tack: using generous financing to enlist sympathetic clergymen as their champions. After all, according to one tycoon, polls showed that, “of all the groups in America, ministers had more to do with molding public opinion” than any other.

The One-percenters kept at it, all the way to 1953, persuading Congress to include “under God” in the Pledge of Allegiance, written by a socialist, by the way. But they didn’t stop there, of course. Just ask our present-day members of Congress who feeds them.

Internet opposition to congressional initiatives

January 18, 2012 by davald

I admit to not following these matters very closely. But as anyone who has attempted today to use Wikipedia or conduct a Google search can attest, there’s rather sharp opposition to at least two pending bills. One is known as SOPA; the other as PIPA. (You can catch a summary of them here. Usually I’d consult Wikipeda, but the site is down in protest.)

SOPA stands for Stop Online Privacy Act. PIPA for Protect IP Act. If you happened to access Wikipedia today you are invited to enter your zip code, as noted above; the folks at Wikipedia deliver contact information on one’s congressional representatives. In my case they are: Senators Maria Cantwell and Patty Murray and Congressman Rick Larsen. So, I did my civic duty, sending very brief notes to Cantwell and Larsen; Murray’s site must have been overloaded, because I was unable to access it.

I received one reply so far, from Ms. Cantwell. I’m sure that it’s the same letter that you’d receive should you contact her. Here ’tis:

Thank you for contacting me about the internet streaming of copyrighted material. I appreciate hearing from you on this issue.

On May 12, 2011, Senator Leahy (D-VT) introduced S. 968, the Preventing Real Online Threats to Economic Creativity and Theft of Intellectual Property (PROTECT IP) Act. While I am supportive of the goals of the bill, I am deeply concerned that the definitions and the means by which the legislation seeks to accomplish these goals will have unintended consequences and hurt innovation, job creation, and threaten online speech and security. On November 17, 2011, I signed a letter along with Senator Ron Wyden (D-OR) objecting to the bill as it is currently written.

On December 17, 2011, Senator Wyden introduced the “Online Protection and Enforcement of Digital Trade” (OPEN) Act (S. 2029), of which I am an original co-sponsor. The bill has been referred to the Senate Finance Committee, where it is currently awaiting further review. The OPEN Act is a more effective approach to stopping foreign web sites that are found to be primarily and willfully used to infringe intellectual property rights. The OPEN Act builds on the existing legal framework used by the International Trade Commission for addressing unfair acts in the importation of articles into the United States, or in their sale for importation, or sale within the United States after importation.

Our trade laws have yet to catch up to deal with the global digital economy. The OPEN Act recognizes that the Internet has created new opportunities for foreign products to reach the U.S. market and that there is little difference between downloading a pirated movie from a foreign website and importing a counterfeit movie DVD from a foreign company. For those foreign web sites that are determined after an investigation to be primarily and willfully infringing, the International Trade Commission will issue a “Cease and Desist” order. The “Cease and Desist” order may also be served on financial intermediaries that provide services to that foreign web site, compelling financial payment processors and online advertising providers to cease doing business with the foreign site in question.  This would cut off financial incentives for this illegal activity and deter these unfair imports from reaching the U.S. market.

The OPEN Act addresses the same challenges as the PROTECT IP Act, while protecting freedom of speech, innovation, and security on the Internet. The challenge of rogue web sites is one that many nation’s face. The United State has always been seen as a leader on Internet issues. Laws we establish in the United States regarding the Internet are likely to be used as models around the world. And because the Internet is global in nature, it is important that we carefully consider how the laws and policies we adopt in this area may be received and translated by other countries.

Thank you again for contacting me to share your thoughts on this matter. You may also be interested in signing up for periodic updates for Washington State residents. If you are interested in subscribing to this update, please visit my website at http://cantwell.senate.gov. Please do not hesitate to contact me in the future if I can be of further assistance.

Sincerely,
Maria Cantwell
United States Senator

 

Truth be told, I’ve never downloaded an illegal copy of music or video. While I’m aware of bitTorrent, I’ve never used it. But it seems that many people are engaged in placing illicit content on the Internet and many more are downloading it illegally. The government, as always, wishes to protect the property of private corporations, which explains in part our nation’s draconian copyright and patent laws, which likely do more harm than good to the economy by keeping costs artificially high (e.g., prescription drugs).

As for the OPEN Act, you can read a comment here by folks at the Electronic Frontier Foundation, a group dedicated to keeping the web open. That means, among other things, ensuring that there’s limited government interference with the Internet. While EFF judges OPEN better than the above alternatives, it presents us with “the bad”:

The provision for entering temporary or preliminary relief arguably does not require (or contemplate) participation from the targeted site (notably, neither does the Federal Rules). It does require notice to the site before implementation, but we’d much prefer a process that includes both sides, especially because temporary or preliminary relief as contemplated by the OPEN Act can potentially silence fully legal speech. (Sec. 2(f)(2)).

The OPEN Act lacks a public interest provision that would require the ITC to consider the effect on the public interest that its order might have. The rules governing patent actions at the ITC require it to take into account the public interest; unfortunately, the ITC doesn’t always take that requirement seriously. To preserve the balance between intellectual property claims and the public interest, the OPEN Act should expressly mandate that the ITC make findings on the effect that a cease-and-desist order would have on the public interest.

The vigilante provision still lives, which is our biggest complaint about the OPEN Act. The legislation’s current draft includes language that can be read to provide immunity for financial transaction providers and Internet advertising services who cut off payment and services to a site based on a “reasonable belief based on credible evidence” that the site is dedicated to infringing activity and the services do not otherwise breach their contractual relationships with the sites. Unfortunately, the draft legislation fails to define “a reasonable belief based on credible evidence,” creating a scenario where providers could proactively cut off services to avoid potential liability, even if their “reasonable belief” is only based on notice from the big content industries. (Sec.2(j)).

Whatever your attitudes about these measures, today matters came to a head on the Internet. In addition to Wikipedia and Google, even Microsoft, which has forever complained about piracy, joins the protest; it opposes SOPA “as currently written.” As much as I can tell from searching the net, Apple, as is its wont, reminds silent.

The White House has issued a statement that, while expressing support for the protection of intellectual property, finds the competing bills problematic. Here’s an excerpt:

Let us be clear—online piracy is a real problem that harms the American economy, and threatens jobs for significant numbers of middle class workers and hurts some of our nation’s most creative and innovative companies and entrepreneurs. It harms everyone from struggling artists to production crews, and from startup social media companies to large movie studios. While we are strongly committed to the vigorous enforcement of intellectual property rights, existing tools are not strong enough to root out the worst online pirates beyond our borders. That is why the Administration calls on all sides to work together to pass sound legislation this year that provides prosecutors and rights holders new legal tools to combat online piracy originating beyond U.S. borders while staying true to the principles outlined above in this response.  We should never let criminals hide behind a hollow embrace of legitimate American values.

What are those principles?

  • Any effort to combat online piracy must guard against the risk of online censorship of lawful activity and must not inhibit innovation by our dynamic businesses large and small.
  • We must avoid creating new cybersecurity risks or disrupting the underlying architecture of the Internet [emphasis in the original].

Join the protest?
