Republicans will kill us all

May 10, 2013 by davald

Now that I have your attention, consider two items in today’s New York Times. The first reports on carbon dioxide levels reaching 400 parts per million. The second is all about Republican obstructionism in Congress. Why connect the two articles?

Justin Gillis, reporting for the Times, writes:

Indirect measurements suggest that the last time the carbon dioxide level was this high was at least three million years ago, during an epoch called the Pliocene. Geological research shows that the climate then was far warmer than today, the world’s ice caps were smaller, and the sea level might have been as much as 60 or 80 feet higher.

Experts fear that humanity may be precipitating a return to such conditions — except this time, billions of people are in harm’s way.

A majority of Republicans polled believe that global warming is “a hoax.” Thus, they will do nothing to prevent it or even diminish its severity. Indeed, they will push policies that actually increase carbon dioxide emissions, from resisting renewable resource development and conservation to “drill, baby, drill.” Indeed, they likely feel that the impending collapse of the Arctic sea ice is an opportunity for further exploration of fossil fuels rather than a harbinger of disaster.

As the planet warms to unprecedented levels (the highest temperatures while humans have existed), sea levels will rise, agricultural lands will dry up, harmful insects will destroy the crops that are left, violent storms will become the norm, species will disappear, and life will be generally miserable for those who survive.

The overwhelming majority of scientists who study such things tell us that we haven’t a moment to lose. If we don’t act soon and dramatically, the planet will descend into a series of critical feedback loops, exponentially worsening the climate. It may very well be too late already.

Yet, the Republicans stand in the way. Indeed, they welcome the catastrophe that will kill humans and other species of flora and fauna.

Arrest them all, for they pose a clear and present danger to just about everything.

The beatings must continue…

May 10, 2013 by davald

Trying to extract consensus from economists is like trying to get oil to play well with water. A New York Times reporter attempted to seek common ground between Larry Summers and Glenn Hubbard. He came away disappointed. President Harry Truman asked for a one-armed economist, believing that even within the individual the truth was divided.

Here’s a big secret. Economists are biased, despite protests to the contrary. As the Times journalist, Adam Davidson, discovered, from whence you come evidently determines what you study, and what you study makes all the difference.

Larry Summers was born of two academic economists and is the nephew of the late Nobel laureate Paul Samuelson (if you took economics in my generation you read Samuelson’s textbook; I have the 15th edition lying next to me). Glenn Hubbard began life in rural Florida, the son of public-school teachers. Summers is decidedly liberal; Hubbard the opposite.

For us laypersons how do we arbitrate the differences? What shall be our metric?

Economists on Hubbard’s side of the profession worry about deficits and debts and inflation. Those with Summers abhor high rates of unemployment and anemic economies. In both cases it seems that government plays a major role. The former group blames Congress for spending too much money and getting in the way of “the market.” Liberal economists like Summers urge the federal government to spend more money than it receives and would restrain market participants lest they ruin things for the Rest of Us.

My non-expert vantage suggests that Hubbard et al. hold fast to their principles across all circumstances. Krugman, Summers and others acknowledge special conditions, but would otherwise agree that deficits do matter, just not at the moment. Hubbard’s group appears to reject the uniqueness possibility.

Hubbard et al. have been warning of deficits and debts and inflation for the last few years, especially. If government debt continues to mount or if the Federal Reserve expands the monetary base consumer prices will soar as will interest rates. Well, both antecedents occurred. Yet there has been no inflation to speak of and interest rates remain near record-low levels. One might conclude that the conservative’s theories are wrong. One might.

Krugman, Bernanke, Summers and others argued the opposite. Relying on their models, which are based on Keynes’s work, they predicted that higher deficits and monetary expansion would not cause inflation or interest rates to rise. Again, they asserted that the economy was operating under special circumstances, namely a liquidity trap—in which those with capital tend to hoard rather than invest their dollars because of uncertainty about returns—and interest rates running up against a zero lower bound—in real dollars rates are at or below zero. Like Keynes before them they have urged the government to spend more money, preferably on long-term public assets like transportation infrastructure, to stimulate the sputtering economy, even if deficits rise.

Unfortunately, the failure of facts to conform with conservative nostrums has not induced Hubbard and others to revisit their theories. One apostle, economist James McCusker, even wrote a column in today’s Everett Herald titled “Discredited research doesn’t discredit need for cuts.”

The research to which McCusker refers is that of economists Carmen Reinhart and Ken Rogoff. They concluded that rising deficits and accumulated debt deter economic growth. They even went so far as to suggest that there was a threshold beyond which economies decline. That threshold was debt exceeding 90 percent of GDP.

McCusker, though acknowledging the serious mistakes of the researchers, essentially dismisses the criticisms while asserting that their basic point still holds: deficits are bad for the economy, apparently at all times. He writes:

The error in this research paper gave them [critics] what they believed was the perfect sword to slay the austerity dragon.

That belief was mistaken, though. Reports of austerity’s death turned out to be exaggerated.The reason is simple enough. Austerity, like stimulus spending comes in many different flavors. There is a big difference, for example, between an austerity program dictated by a country’s creditors and an independently developed government plan to reduce its dependence on unsustainable debt.

Of the former, I suspect McCusker has in mind conditions of high government interest rates that induce financial institutions to charge more for credit. That would effectively squeeze consumer spending, especially on housing, and dampen growth. Those conditions don’t obtain now. The latter is in play, with governments, especially at the state levels, slashing spending, ostensibly to reduce deficits (see chart below).

Then McCusker lets his ideological blinders misjudge Congress’s American Recovery and Reinvestment Act. He says that “an ill-conceived stimulus program can be totally ineffective at prompting economic growth, employment or private investment. As happened with that program, stimulus spending may instead end up as a kind of mammoth employment security program for some workers. To the extent that it preserved jobs during the difficult early days of the recession, it may not have been totally useless. But it most certainly was not an effective economic stimulus.”

I agree that the program was ill-conceived. Too much of it was in the form of tax breaks, which constrained federal receipts, adding to deficits. Far too little was devoted to public goods and services like transportation and education. Krugman et al. condemned the 2009 program as much too small given the size of the economy’s downturn following the collapse of the housing bubble. However, to assert that ARRA was “totally ineffective” is simply wrong. If Hubbard and other like-minded economists had their way there would have been no federal package at all.

As it is, total government spending has fallen steadily as compared to GDP. We are “practicing” austerity with nothing but misery to show for it.

 

I, for one, would distinguish between the likes of Hubbard and Summers on the basis of sympathy. Either you have compassion for those who are suffering in this dismal economy or you don’t. You can guess which is which.

The Warriors

May 9, 2013 by davald

It was 1975. A friend had scored two tickets to an NBA playoff game that pitted the Milwaukee Bucks against Golden State.  Just before the second half the Warriors’ flaky guard Jim Barnett was leading his team onto the court when a fan-tossed Frisbee flew gently in his direction. He snatched it as if he’d handled the toy thousands of times before then flung it across  the court and back up into the stands. The crowd, already abuzz over the Warriors’ first-half lead, erupted. Golden State would go on to defeat the Bucks in the series and eventually take the championship.

Fast forward to the present. The Warriors are once again in the playoffs, this time seeking to overcome the  heavily favored San Antonio Spurs. I missed their first contest, won in double overtime by the Spurs, but caught last night’s action, which resulted in a 100-91 victory for Golden State. I’m excited.

If asked who I thought would be in this year’s finals I’d have responded the Spurs and the Heat. I like Popovich’s system and how each player knows it and his role within. He mixed and matched his way to the third-best record in the regular season. Ah, but this upstart bunch of very young Warriors has different ideas.

As much as I applauded George Karl for winning coach of the year, one has to wonder why Golden State’s Mark Jackson didn’t get the nod. He’s somehow managed to extract the best effort and performance from his players, mostly no-names, with the exception of Stephen Curry, who led the league in three-point shooting.

1975 was a kind of miracle for the Warriors. After all, they were up against Lew Alcindor, who would ultimately set the all-time scoring record as Kareem Abdul-Jabbar. He was finesse where lumbering Clyde Lee was bully. Lee rattled the hell out of Alcindor, who kept losing his temper and missing his shots.

Could this year’s Warriors stage another miracle? I wouldn’t count them out.

Housing and growth

May 8, 2013 by davald

Economic growth has faltered. When the housing bubble collapsed in the 2007-08 period, several trillion dollars of wealth vanished. (Where it went, I have no idea.) But it may be the housing market that leads a rebound, and there are signs of its revitalization.

First, let’s take a look at the relationship between housing sales and GDP (source: FRED).

I am not suggesting anything profound in the apparent correlation. Intuition alone would suggest that when a lot of people are buying houses the economy improves.

Now let’s take a look at what happened during the Great Recession. Here’s a chart showing housing starts over the last few decades.

One cannot help but notice the sharp, almost vertical decline from the end of 2007 to 2009. However, things are picking up, although we’ve got a long way to go before we get close to the post-war average.

The real estate industry developed a metric some time ago called ‘the affordability index.’ It compares the median home price with median household income. If the index is 100, then the median income is just enough to afford the median-priced home. Any number higher than a 100 suggests we collectively have more income than we need to buy that house, keeping in mind that income statistics are heavily skewed owing to increasing inequality. A factor that I’ve written about extensively. But let’s take a look at the index anyway.

Well, somebody’s got money. Yet they aren’t buying all that many houses at the moment.

The glimmer of hope can be found in that slight uptick at the end of the curve. Will it continue to escalate?

It appears that people are buying houses, after years of staying on the sidelines, and the demand is catching up with supply. This article in the Everett Herald confirms.

It’s 2007 all over again for the county real estate market as antsy buyers rush to buy what’s available in a tight supply of homes for sale.

Northwest Multiple Listing Service members notched their highest volume of new listings since June 2011, but the additions did little to ease inventory shortages. Brokers also reported the highest volume of pending sales since May 2007, along with anecdotes of bidding wars.

Gad. Bidding wars?

So, we should expect housing construction to resume as the available inventory is gobbled up. I have mixed feelings about this.

If, indeed, the economy’s performance is tied to housing, then a more robust housing market (sales and construction) would signal growing GDP. In turn, higher economic growth (people buying things, for example) will yield greater employment and, therefore, increased spending, which leads to more growth—and so on.

So what’s wrong with this picture, if an upturn in the housing market would seem to be a good thing? For me, it gets back to the type of housing and development patterns.

Having recently revisited the city of my previous residence, I came away depressed: little boxes made out of ticky-tacky and they all look just the same, as Tom Paxton wrote many years ago. The people who live in such sprawl have to use their automobiles to go anywhere. There are no stores or parks or restaurants within anyone’s idea of walking distance.

I realize that it’s ultimately about economics. Former agricultural land, especially, is dirt cheap, relatively speaking. Moreover, it’s easy to develop, and if each house is just about a carbon copy of the ones next door, cheap to erect an entire tract of homes. As long as people have no alternative, say living in a vibrant, mixed-use downtown area like much of Seattle, they will flock to exurbia to seek their American Dream.

Then we repeat the sins of the past.

Krugman’s “white whale”

May 6, 2013 by davald

I, for one, would hesitate before taking on Paul Krugman when he’s talking about economics. But this is America, so awards and letters appended to people’s last names hardly deter the benighted.

Let’s take this one post of Krugman. It’s titled “Naive Fiscal Cynicism.” So far, there have been 208 comments. Here’s one from “Lukey”:

Actually the long decline in dept as % of GDP was the result of the post war economy when America was a fast growing net exporting producer. Post 1980 we became a slower growing net importing consumer, the very economic characteristic that Professor Krugman would have us double down on in pursuit of his white whale named “aggregate demand.” I’m betting this won’t turn out too well…

I admit to not being clear about the commenter’s points, especially the second sentence. But let’s explore them just the same.

It’s true that the U.S. economy grew significantly from the end of WWII to Ronald Reagan’s first term. We can see the numbers in the following chart (source FRED):

The annual change in GDP averaged a positive 3.7 percent from 1948 to 1980, inclusive. It has fallen to 2.7 percent since 1981.

What about exports? Here is a chart showing the U.S. current account balance since 1947:

Net exports did not rise during the post-War era. However, they fell sharply after Ronald Reagan took office.

As for debt:

Clearly debt fell during the post-War years until, again, Ronald Reagan entered the White House. But if net exports weren’t responsible for a declining debt, what was?

I’ve written about the decline in federal tax receipts, beginning with Reagan. While the Republicans would have us believe that we have a “spending problem,” the stronger evidence suggests a revenue problem.

 

During the post-war years, federal receipts were actually higher than expenditures for much of the time. Then came Reagan.

Also during those two decades following WWII, a whole lot of men, mostly, returned from the war to enter the civilian labor force. But they had to find jobs. The economy provided them. But how?

During the war the government imposed wage and price controls along with rationing of goods and services. Demand, then, was artificially depressed. After the war ended the controls were lifted. Industries devoted to the production of war materiel retooled to manufacture civilian products, including cars and household appliances. The housing sector mushroomed.

I’m old enough to remember those post-war years, and I think my father and his two brothers were the rule rather than the exception. They joined millions of other men in starting a family, purchasing a house (on the GI bill), acquiring an automobile (no two cars then), and buying clothes washers, refrigerators, and so on.

While it’s true that my father and uncles could not have bought the things they did without an income, and therefore a job, they would not have found employment were it not for millions of people like them buying things. Which comes first, the demand for goods and services or the job?

For the individual, it must be the latter. For the economy as a whole, however, it’s likely the former. Firms will not hire additional workers unless their managers can safely assume that there are consumers who are both willing and able to buy the firms’ products.

The commenter, “Lukey,” calls aggregate demand Krugman’s “white whale,” suggesting a misguided obsession with an elusive object. But even a cursory understanding of the U.S. economy would reveal an obvious characteristic: aggregate demand constitutes the major element of GDP, about 70 percent of the total.

We can see the effects of rationing and price controls during the war era. Personal consumption expenditures (aggregate demand) rose steadily after WWII, reaching a stable 70 percent of GDP since G.W. Bush became president.

The next chart tracks personal spending and GDP. With few exceptions (e.g., during WWII), GDP and aggregate demand coincide.

I confess to not understanding the commenter’s contention that Krugman wishes to “double down” on “a slower growing net importing consumer.” It’s undoubtedly true that something happen during Ronald Reagan’s term that changed the economy’s trajectory from growth to sputter.

If spending by the Rest of Us contributes to, if not causes, economic growth, then it would seem that raising demand is crucial to a healthy (capitalist) economy. So why did spending fall?

Readers of this blog are familiar with my expression ‘The Great Divergence.’ About the time Reagan assumed the presidency the rich started getting much richer while the incomes of the Rest of Us either stagnated or fell. I’ve embedded the following chart on multiple occasions.

If we are to spend, we must have money. But look who’s getting most of it. Yet, why should it matter who extracts the economy’s wealth?

The Rest of US spend nearly every penny we make, whereas the very rich are awash in surplus. They buy everything they need and want and still have millions left over. Increasingly, those dollars are hoarded; they do not circulate. Economists talk about the velocity of money, a measure of a dollar’s changing hands, or circulating in the economy.

Simpleton that I am, I believe that we can rather easily solve many of our problems by converging incomes—squeeze down from the top and push up from the bottom. As Jimmy Stewart said in It’s a Wonderful Life, we need “papa dollars” and “momma dollars” producing lots of “baby dollars.”

The damn rich don’t know what to do with money. We do. And a white whale has nothing to do with it.
