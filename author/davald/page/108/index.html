Eating our own

December 11, 2012 by davald

Thomas Franks had some fun with his native Kansas, asking what the hell happened to the place, a place that used to be home to tens of thousands of socialists; and they were none to shy about it. Today a Democrat in that state is as rare as, forgive me again, a Mariners winning season.

Franks wondered aloud, or on his pages, why so many people would vote against their economic interests. Republicans, after all, and especially the ultra-conservative variety, hold views antithetical to those concerned about putting bread on the table and a roof over their heads while ensuring that their children receive a decent education. A strict diet of government spending cuts, ever lower taxes, and suppression of labor values and expression deliver big piles of crap, actually, with no one on the payroll to clean up the mess.

Michigan, of all states, has now denuded labor, prohibiting unions from mandating dues from its members. Yes, you can still pay them, but you don’t have to. Guess what that will do to labor’s power?

WTF is wrong with Americans? They may rail against the excesses of Wall Street and bemoan the fact that their puny paychecks don’t go as far as they used to. They don’t like their schools, their governments, their lousy transportation systems. Yet, they will elect people to office who give every benefit to the rich and already powerful as they stick it to the Rest of Us.

Harold Myerson is also asking questions. The title of a recent essay reads: “If Labor Dies, What’s Next?” He begins with a dismal background:

Unions have been declining for decades. In the early 1950s, one out of three American workers belonged to them, four out of ten in the private sector. Today, only 11.8 percent of American workers are union members; in the private sector, just 6.9 percent. The vanishing act varies by region—in the South, it’s almost total—but proceeds relentlessly everywhere. Since 1983, the number of states in which at least 10 percent of private-sector workers have union contracts has shrunk from 42 to 8.

Do the people of Michigan, Wisconsin, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and the entire damn South not understand that their last best hope against the plutocrats is labor? Do these rugged individuals who besmirch government have any inkling as to what’s in store for them and their children? Do they love getting screwed every day by corporate America?

Myerson paints a dire picture of the consequences of labor’s demise. Here are some excerpts:

When unions vanish, ordinary Americans lose their right to bargain collectively for their pay and benefits. Even those who have never bargained collectively will feel the loss. Some years ago, when unions were big enough that their effect on the larger economy could be measured, Princeton economist Henry Farber concluded that the wages of nonunion workers in industries that were 25 percent unionized were 7.5 percent higher than they’d be if their industry were union-free. When unionized companies were common, firms that were nonunion had to mimic the wages and benefits of their unionized counterparts for fear that their employees would leave or, worse, organize. That was certainly the practice at General Electric and other largely nonunion giants.

Go ahead, you devotees of Ayn Rand. March into the boss’ office tomorrow and demand a wage increase along with a few extra perks, if you have any now. Do you believe that “The Man” is going to reach into his own wallet to pull out a few bills for you then offer you an instant, fully paid vacation for the entire holiday season? If that same boss decides that he doesn’t like the way you chat amongst fellow workers, he’ll come up with some pretext to make your working life miserable or toss you to the curb. Remember, you ain’t got no union to protect you. You’re on your own, pal, completely at your employer’s mercy.

From 1947 through 1973, when union density in America was at its peak, real wages for nonmanagerial employees rose by 75 percent. From 1979 through 2006, as union density collapsed, real wages for nonmanagerial employees rose by only 4 percent. Unable to get a raise, American households maintained their standard of living during those years by women entering the workforce and by going into debt.

I’ve written ad nauseam about stagnant wages and taking on greater amounts of debt to keep up with the mortgage and car payments. Your boss will not pay you more money or give you more days off because you’re a baseball fan or part your hair on the right side. He won’t pay you a dime more than he has to, just like Mitt Romney takes pride in not paying a penny more in taxes than his lawyers determine he owes.

Oh, but you think that you’re better than your co-workers. You work harder, turning out more widgets or selling a few more used cars. You actually bought into that whole Horatio Alger myth, so you just expect to be recognized and rewarded. Are you interested in a bridge?

Today, wages and benefits make up the lowest share of America’s gross domestic product since World War II. Wages have fallen from 53 percent of GDP in 1970 to 44 percent today. Profits have been growing at wages’ expense. Michael Cembalest, J.P. Morgan’s chief investment officer, has calculated that reductions in wages and benefits were responsible for about 75 percent of the increase in corporate profits between 2000 and 2007.

What’s causing this decline in workers’ ability to claim more of the nation’s wealth? It’s not that they’re less productive. According to a Wall Street Journal survey of the S&P 500, the nation’s largest publicly traded companies, revenues per worker, which were $378,000 in 2007, grew to $420,000 in 2010. Businesses now produce more with fewer employees, but even those workers who’ve kept their jobs haven’t seen their wages rise.

The Man loves your effort. He really does. Over the years, coinciding with the decline in union participation rates, he’s been able to squeeze more and more out of you without having to add a single dollar to your paycheck. But when you get home all tired and cranky whom do you complain about? More often than not, you’ve got it in for the liberals, those who push for more social welfare like Social Security and Medicare. Yeah, that’s the ticket.

And speaking of liberals, Myerson doesn’t let them off the hook. They got too cocky, assuring themselves that the struggles of the 30s, 40s, and 50s secured a permanent place for labor in the political arena. They turned to other matters, all worthwhile, I should add.

But by thinking the labor question had been settled during the decades of postwar prosperity, and then by not thinking about the labor question at all, liberals ceased to address the issue of power: the economic and political power that capitalism concentrates at the top when unions are weak and regulations watered down.

Unions, too, were complicit in their own destruction. Myerson:

The same union leaders who helped create the rift in the 1960s and 1970s between labor and liberals were also largely indifferent to organizing workers in the growing service sector—indeed, to organizing workers at all. As convinced as Daniel Bell that the balance of class power in America had reached a permanent equipoise, George Meany, who presided over the AFL-CIO from 1955 until 1979, told an interviewer in 1972, “Frankly, I used to worry about … the size of the membership. But quite a few years ago, I just stopped worrying about it.”

My grandfather was rumored to have been a member of the Wobblies, the Industrial Workers of the World. The movement’s principals, in particular Big Bill Haywood, preached “one big union.” If labor could not adopt a one-for-all, all-for-one approach and, instead, remained fragmented, it could not and cannot hope to counteract the forces of greed, monopoly, and political hegemony. I’ll end with Myerson’s concluding paragraphs.

Larry Cohen, the president of the Communications Workers of America (CWA), has been a leading proponent of labor’s reunification. He also believes American unions can learn from the successes that labor has experienced in other nations when they helped form broader social movements. In recent decades, he points out, unions in Brazil, South Korea, and South Africa have flourished by becoming linchpins in campaigns for democracy, much as European unions did 100 years ago.

Having played a major role in the unsuccessful 2010 battle to persuade Congress to reform labor law, Cohen is convinced labor needs “to build a movement of 50 million Americans for democracy.” If labor wants to win more rights for workers, he argues, it must first break the stranglehold that big money and conservatives have on our politics by winning campaign–finance reform, abolishing the Senate’s supermajority requirements, outlawing voter suppression, and legalizing undocumented immigrants.

Beyond some visionary officials and activists, does labor recognize how radically it has to change? Too many leaders, says one union veteran, still think that “labor is selling toothpaste, something that’s always needed, that’s not going to go out of business. But we’re not selling toothpaste. We’re selling landline telephones. There won’t be a market for landline phones ten years from now.”

Can unions move beyond landlines? Even if they can, will our corporate-dominated political system still indulge their existence, let alone permit their rebirth? Labor can’t win this fight by itself. It not only needs to make common cause with the broader liberal community; it needs the liberal community to understand and embrace [Arthur] Schlesinger’s dictum that the only alternative to class dominance is class conflict. Heightening that conflict and strengthening unions, or whatever new institutions emerge to fight for working people, must now become liberalism’s primary task.

Punishing fraud

December 11, 2012 by davald

Under our system of government in which winners take all, bipartisanship assumes the default structure. So, our legislatures are generally divided between the two major political parties, and given the ideological litmus tests imposed by the Tea Partiers, those who survive in today’s GOP are both feral and recalcitrant.

Across the aisle, Democrats still seem to abide by big-tent politics; there is room for all, up to a point. This attitude invites incoherence, which is tolerable in a strong majority position; political suicide in an evenly divided house.

Meanwhile, despite appeals to emphasize “the person,” voters continue to select along party lines. This tendency is increasingly important, given the above.

There are differences between the two parties, especially regarding outcomes. We learn each day that some state’s Republican-dominated government has jettisoned a long-standing policy established through blood, sweat, and tears. I have in mind Michigan, Wisconsin, Pennsylvania, and Ohio; there will surely be more, as Republicans control a majority of the states’ houses. Democrats work, with varying degrees of enthusiasm, to preserve those hard-won victories. But numbers matter.

So I return to the matter of the Washington state senate. Yesterday, two Democrats in name only announced that they would be joining the Republicans. As a result, the GOP will assume a one-seat majority in that house.

Reactions have been mixed. Republicans praise the move as a sign of “cooperation” and “collaboration.” Right. Some feckless Democrats have expressed mild displeasure or a rhetorical shoulder shrug. Other Democrats cry foul and vow to avenge the traitors.

And that is how I view the switchers. They have betrayed not just their erstwhile party but also the voters who elected them to office.

When they change their stripes they commit fraud. In the present case, two individuals ran as Democrats then, sensing personal opportunity, switched their allegiance. They have duped their supporters and frustrated the people’s will.

What should be the proper punishment for the crime? Recall.

Pass the word.

Changing stripes

December 10, 2012 by davald

Well, this sucks. Two nominal Democrats (that is, in name only) have effectively decided to become Republicans. This just happened in Washington state’s senate. Now the Republicans, who lost most statewide races, including the governor’s mansion, will control one of the two houses in Olympia, albeit just barely, 25-24.

I do like this immediate response from Dwight Pelz, from the Seattle P-I:

Meanwhile, the chairman of the Washington State Democrats disowned the two defecting senators.

Dwight Pelz said he’s long viewed Sheldon as a Republican, but the party had invested money to re-elect Tom this year. Pelz says that won’t happen again, and the party will draft a candidate to oust him next time.

“This is a decision by Rodney Tom to switch parties back again,” Pelz said. “Rodney Tom is a Republican now.”

Tom was initially elected as a Republican but switched parties in 2006. Pelz said he believes the latest move was simply a way for Tom to fulfill his personal ambitions.

Senator Tom, no doubt with tongue firmly pressed against his cheek, had this to say:

“This is not about power. This is not about control. This is about governing in a collaborative manner.”

Right.

Suppressing wages

December 10, 2012 by davald

Rumor has it that the Federal Reserve has a dual mandate: keep inflation in check and support full employment. As it happens, the two can be mutually exclusive. Focusing on the former keeps wages down and, therefore, consumer spending, which, in turn, retards economic growth.

In one of his posts today Matthew Yglesias criticizes the Fed’s obsession with price stability. That prices have been constrained is not an altogether bad thing; we prefer lower to higher. However, we’d also like to make more money. But increasing incomes is difficult when prices stay low, unless you happen to belong to the top one percent or, better yet, the top 0.01%. Yglesias:

And the problem of the Federal Reserve is that over the past 30 years, it has a perfect track record of never allowing inflation (which is to say a sustained period in which wages rise faster than productivity), but it doesn’t have a perfect track record of never allowing recessions. The inevitable consequence of this asymmetrical success is for the labor share to steadily decline.

He includes the following chart from the St. Louis FED:

In her book Plutocrats Chrystia Freeland cites research on the correlation between a burgeoning financial sector and income inequality. That sector’s prominence exhibits a U-shaped curve, beginning in the first couple of decades of the 20th century. After the stock market crash of 1929, Wall Street’s size and influence waned. Its fortunes rose again in the mid-70s, continuing an upward trajectory until the Great Recession, when it suffered a temporary lull. Don’t worry, they’re back (data from Saez).

Here’s a look at financial sector corporate profits since the end of WWII:

Freeland quotes a management consultant who wrote a book about businesses in emerging markets, including Russia and India. But it’s American businesspeople who have perfected the ability to extract goodies from governments. The consultant:

In the financial industry, the United States has the most co-opted regulatory apparatus. They are so innovative. They are driven to it, and they’re doing a great job of what they’re paid to do. I don’t think this comes out of evil. I think this comes out of what we call runaway effects. The more you get incented [sic] to do it, the more you do it. And because so much of our incentive system is financial, then that’s what we got. We’re getting what we pay for, literally. And so Wall Street’s done a fabulous job of making the world safe for Wall Street.

Freeland adds that between 2000 and 2006 “laws increasing regulation of the finance and real estate sectors had just a five percent chance of passing. Laws that deregulated were three times more likely to pass.” How was this accomplished? The old fashioned way: political bribery.

Lobbying expenditures by the affected financial firms were significantly associated with how politicians voted on the key bills.

As the fortunes of Wall Street rise the incomes of the Rest of Us sputter (data from Saez).

Oh, well.

A progressive square

December 10, 2012 by davald

This may come as a bit of a shock to some readers. Despite being a product of Berkeley in the 60s, I am a social prude. We could chock that up to a stringent Catholic upbringing in which sin was the operative word, and nearly everything one did and thought brought one closer to hell. But whatever the reasons, I neither imbibed nor inhaled. Indeed, I hate smoke of any kind.

Now that Washington state voters have legalized marijuana use, I expect to see a sharp uptick in the level of weed smoke. Folks have already taken to public light-ups, this one at Seattle Center. But I could encounter puffs on my next walk, just as I round the corner of a downtown building.

Those who opposed the initiative warned that citizens would throw virtue out the window and descend into perpetual lassitude. Also, once on pot, the stronger stuff was sure to follow. Kiss law and order goodbye.

I suspect a different eventuality. After a relatively brief period of craziness, behaviors will return to pre-initiative norms, with a few outliers, to be sure. Then we’ll look back and wonder what all the fuss was about.

Neglect

December 9, 2012 by davald

The next time you hear someone proclaim America (is) first, consider the state of our nation’s public infrastructures. You will quickly disabuse yourself of sharing the hoary sentiment.

The New York Times editors provide some measure of how bad things are with the country’s roads, sewer systems, and other public works. We are behind trillions of dollars in making systems safe and reliable, including the electric grid.

Yet, the conservatives have been so successful in demonizing government and its obligations that the Obama administration and dozens of Democratic governors dare not present a realistic proposal to fix the nation. For example:

When the president asked recently for a modest $50 billion for transportation improvements in the “fiscal cliff” talks, Republicans literally laughed out loud. There will be no stimulus in any deal, said Representative Bill Shuster of Pennsylvania, the incoming chairman of the Transportation and Infrastructure Committee.

The amount, the editors said, would amount to only a “modest downpayment” on what’s really needed, more than $2 trillion just to restore public transportation to working order. Forget about high-speed rail or state-of-the art schools.

England proves Keynes right

December 7, 2012 by davald

John Cassidy exposes the UK’s horrible dalliance with austerity. It doesn’t work, just as John Maynard Keynes said some 80 years ago. Cassidy:

One of the frustrations of economics is that it is hard to carry out scientific experiments and prove things beyond reasonable doubt. But not in this case. Thanks to Osborne’s stubborn refusal to change course—“Turning back would be a disaster,” he told Parliament—what has been happening in Britain amounts to a “natural experiment” to test the efficacy of austerity economics. For the sixty-odd million inhabitants of the U.K., living through it hasn’t been a pleasant experience—no university institutional-review board would have allowed this kind of brutal human experimentation. But from a historical and scientific perspective, it is an invaluable case study.

The country’s chancellor of the Exchequer, in both announcing the austerity measures and stubbornly sticking to them, promised a far better outcome. According to Cassidy, “Osborne said that his austerity policies would cut his country’s budget deficit to zero within four years, enable Britain to begin relieving itself of its public debt, and generate healthy economic growth.”

Oh, and don’t listen to the Republicans on this side of the pond.

Barry Bonds and the Hall

December 7, 2012 by davald

A cursory glance at the sports punditocracy reveals a near-unanimous result: sportswriters will pass on Barry Bonds for the Hall of Fame, at least for now, his first year of eligibility. The reason sticks out like a Mariners’ winning season. Remember those? He took steroids, which not only taints his achievements but made them possible. Or so the suspicion goes.

But Bonds has never been convicted of taking performance-enhancing drugs, despite the federal government’s obsessive pursuit of a conviction. Chalk it up to good defense lawyers, or, perhaps, Bonds eschewed the drugs and simply spent a lot of time in the weight room.

If I were voting, I’d put him in the Hall, since I would judge a player by his on-field exploits and not consider allegations of bad people doing bad things. Pete Rose deserves to join the ranks of baseball’s best, though he was a jerk and inveterate gambler. Ty Cobb was also a jerk and a virulent racist to boot. But he’s there, alongside Babe Ruth (hardly a role model) and dozens of other questionable characters.

Let’s take a look at Bonds’s career. Here’s a chart I’ve compiled that fixes his rookie season (1986) at 100. I look at his weight, which we might use as a proxy for strength, natural or otherwise, his home runs and batting average. I hasten to emphasize that bulk or no hitting a round ball with a round bat is still sports’ most difficult challenge.

Bonds began his career with the Pittsburgh Pirates. He weighed 185 pounds and hit just .223 with 16 home runs. The next year he improved his average by 39 points and added another nine home runs to his total. He still weighed 185. His weight would stay between 185 and 190 until 1997, his fifth season with the Giants, after spending his first seven years with Pittsburgh.

In 1997, his first in San Francisco, while still weighing 185 pounds, Bonds smacked 46 four-baggers, or five more than his career average. During his initial dozen seasons, Bonds steadily upped his batting average, hitting above .300 five times. So, he not only got more proficient at hitting the ball, he started hitting home runs with greater frequency.

What I found interesting in reviewing his stats was that Bonds hit more than 50 home runs only once, the year he set the season record at 73 in 2001. That year his listed weight was 228, where it would remain until he retired, at the end of the 2007 season. In 2000 he hit 49 home runs when he weighed 210. Also, he led the league in home runs only twice, despite setting the record for most home runs in a career—762. (Also, if you toss out the record year and assume his average of 41 instead, he still would have broken the career record.)

Here’s another interesting fact. Bonds’ batting average climbed along with his weight. Look at a five-year stretch from 2000 to 2004.

I’ve represented his career average, .298, as a dotted line. Bonds could hit the damn ball, recording a career best of .370 in 2002, when he also knocked 46 over the fence.

Here’s yet another anomaly. Among baseball’s all-time sluggers, Bonds blasted more home runs after reaching the age of 35 than any other player, 42 percent of his total.

Was it Geritol? Vegetables and clean living? Or a secret sauce?

Your humble scribe managed to hit .300 in high school. In all the games I pitched, covering a span of 20 years, I hit just one home run, and I broke my bat doing so. It helped that the game was being played at Lake Tahoe, about a mile above sea level. My blast was only one of several that day.

I mention this personal anecdote to suggest that I have much appreciation for someone who can thrive at the major league level. For me, Bonds reigned supreme—for a while, though his godfather was the best that ever was. He’s next to Ken Griffey, Jr., in the above chart.

Crisis mongering

December 7, 2012 by davald

Not a day goes by that I don’t see a headline in the papers warning of “the debt crisis” or the dire repercussions of “the fiscal cliff.” As economists Dean Baker and Paul Krugman remind us, it’s one thing for us ordinary folks to be ignorant and quite another for media editors and fact-checkers to go AWOL.

Both link us to a Business Insider poll revealing the level of citizen misunderstanding regarding the fiscal cliff, with nearly half the respondents, a significant plurality, believing that if Congress fails to act before the appointed deadline, federal deficits and, therefore, debts, will rise.

Krugman takes the opportunity to deride the Washington Post for its blatant bias on fiscal policies, with the newspaper trumpeting austerity measures for some time now. He writes:

So what’s going on with the subhed? The answer is that the Very Serious People — and there’s nothing as VSP as the WaPo — have spent years crying Deficits! Debt! Danger!, and staff at the Post can’t wrap their minds around the fact that suddenly it’s a too-rapid fall in the deficit that has those very same People terrified.

It speaks to the state of confusion that all the deficit fearmongering has created. And if headline writers at a major newspaper can’t get it straight, how can you expect ordinary voters to get it?

Dean Baker, by the way, performs a much-needed public service by combing media headlines and articles in pursuit of the misleading and just plain wrong. His blog, Beat the Press, routinely exposes sloppiness and, in some cases, egregious errors committed by the Washington Post, in particular, and major newspapers, in general. He’s especially fond of picking on New York Times‘ columnist David Brooks for his fatuous pontification.

There is a larger problem, of course. Those of us who read rely on the media for our sources of information. If the media get it wrong, and we’ve failed to intellectually arm ourselves against falsehoods and misrepresentations, then we also get it wrong. Should too many of us live in ignorance, we’re likely to vote for Republicans. And that’s a very bad thing.

Pay me now or pay me later

December 6, 2012 by davald

The New Yorker‘s James Surowieki offers the Holland experience for a guide to disaster preparedness. Billions of dollars could be saved by favoring storm prevention over after-the-fact restoration. But that’s not the American way. He writes:

Politically speaking, it’s always easier to shell out money for a disaster that has already happened, with clearly identifiable victims, than to invest money in protecting against something that may or may not happen in the future.

Did you see that Obama is asking Congress for $50 billion for Sandy repairs?

Surowiecki adds that Americans are loath to adequately fund infrastructure in general. For whatever defect in our collective psyche, we prefer the erosion of bridges, roads, and schools to extracting a bit more from the rich.

Strange, we are.
