Bastard Baucus strikes again

April 18, 2013 by davald

If we’ve learned anything about our elected representatives it’s that they have neither courage nor conviction. They are pusillanimous poseurs, pretending to act nobly as they pocket dirty money from lobbyists and corporations.

Yesterday the U.S. Senate behaved “shamefully,” allowing a minority of its members to frustrate the will of the people to establish very modest restrictions on the sale of guns that kill people. Lots of them.

Among the minority was Bastard Baucus. Though a nominal Democrat, the man from Montana acts like a very good fascist beholden to the monied interests, which include the National Rifle Association, a group dedicated to the proposition that the answer to every problem is simply more guns, and the more lethal the better.

The Senate, by the way, is itself a tragedy, usurping democracy at every turn. Throw in the gerrymandered House and we’ve got the best Congress money can buy, but nothing close to the people’s body.

The sweet smell of stink [u]

April 18, 2013 by davald

I’m talking about the Mariners, who, despite a dismal on-field performance every year for as long as anyone can remember and record-low attendance figures of late, retain the title of 12th most valuable Major League franchise. If no one is showing up, how does the team make money and, therefore, stay in business? Television, it seems.

The current owners of the M’s have no reason to invest in quality, since they continue to produce profits without having to earn it. Yes, they paid Felix Hernandez buckets of ducats. But that’s because he’s a marketing bonanza unto himself. Several thousand whackos show up when he pitches at home to don yellow T-shirts and gnaw on mutton. Otherwise, not much to show fans in person or on the tube.

It shouldn’t surprise me that the various “pits” in the outfield draw the largest crowds. Why? They have no seats, only a few tables against which young persons stand and chat swilling down beers flirting with each other while paying no heed to what’s happening on the grass below. Perhaps they are the fortunate ones, who depart drunk and oblivious to the Mariners’ travails.

Writing for the P-I Nick Eaton wonders about the dysfunctional arrangement and how long it can last.

Fans are telling the Mariners management that they’re fed up in the only way they know how: Not showing up to Safeco Field.

But is it doing any good? Major League Baseball is a business and the largest shares of revenue these days come not from ticket sales, but from sponsorships and television contracts.

And the Mariners on Tuesday announced buying a majority stake in Root Sports Northwest, giving the franchise another source of revenue as the owner of a regional TV network.

When I watch the M’s on TV is see nothing but empty seats. Why don’t the owners employ dynamic pricing like the San Francisco Giants and airlines? The overhead must be roughly the same for each game regardless of how many show up. They could charge a buck for the seats with the reasonable expectation that the people will buy food and beverages, thus adding to the team’s revenues. But, no. Even if the Astros are in town, smucks are still required to fork over sixty dollars or more for the “good” seats. Or not, since few do. Eaton wonders aloud:

With today’s sports market, the Mariners organization will bring in money no matter how the team is doing on the field. They don’t depend totally on ticket sales and turnstile counts.

But a failing team is a failing team, and it’s hard to imagine a team with no fans could last very long.

Indeed.

Coal’s a-movin’

April 17, 2013 by davald

Those of us who live in the greater Seattle area are justifiably concerned about increasing coal exports via Puget Sound terminals. The trains  extend for miles, blocking intersections for dozens of minutes, if not longer. What many of us may not realize is that those shipments from Wyoming, etc., are already underway. The battle is to prevent further exports.

In the U.S., cheap natural gas is rapidly displacing coal-fired generation. But China can evidently use all the coal it can get its hands on, and companies there are willing to pay a premium.

Yesterday, as I was walking home from the Snohomish County PUD along Hewitt Avenue, I crossed over the Burlington Northern Santa Fe (BNSF) rail line. Here is what I saw:

I had my camera on for only a few moments, so I didn’t capture all of the coal cars. But each car was filled to the brim and carries about 120 tons of coal. A ton of combusted coal emits 2.86 tons of carbon dioxide. A bit of arithmetic reveals that one carload will yield 343 tons of CO2. A coal train has about 120 to 150 cars. That adds up to between 41,000 and over 57,000 tons of carbon dioxide per train. The proposed terminal at Cherry Point, Wash., would handle about 30 miles of coal trains per day transported by BNSF. (More coal train facts here.)

We may not appreciate that wherever coal is burned all of us will experience the effects of increased carbon dioxide. While coal trains certainly pose an inconvenience for motorists, global warming could render the planet inhabitable for future generations of species, including homo sapiens.

Another team?

April 17, 2013 by davald

Basketball fans in Sacramento and Seattle may have to wait a little longer to learn the news. Will the NBA’s Board of Governors approve the sale of the Kings to Chris Hansen’s group? It appears that a competing offer to keep the team in Sacramento has complicated matters.

I should think the owners are conflicted. They don’t want to piss off California’s capital. Yet they must surely relish the idea of re-establishing a team in the much richer Puget Sound. What to do?

Commissioner David Stern has said repeatedly that expansion is not an option. Then I read this tidbit:

But that didn’t stop speculation that the league might use the extra time to at least consider adding an expansion team.

The selling price is above half a billion dollars. With two groups already committed to spending this much, why not let the Kings stay in Sacramento with a promised new arena and create a new team in Seattle, also with a new arena to the owners’ liking? Sounds like a winning proposition. And a very profitable one at that.

Goodbye, Mr. Winters

April 17, 2013 by davald

The scene is a high school library. It’s early morning and yours truly is holding court at one table, surrounded by classmates. While it’s supposed to be quiet, the author is talking, and when he talks the others begin to laugh, at first haltingly, since they don’t wish to incur the wrath of Miss Brubaker, the librarian. But soon they are unable to control themselves, erupting in spasms of side-splitting howls. Now even Miss Brubaker is caught up in the humorous monologues. More often than not the material I presented was stolen from Jonathan Winters, whose one-man skits I had committed to memory. We are in the early 1960s when Winters was king.

As readers no doubt know, Jonathan Winters passed away last week at the age of 87. The news of his death was announced, fittingly enough, on my birthday. Those of you born after Nixon’s fall probably know little of the man. Your comedians are different and, I would argue, less funny. It’s not even close, with the possible exception of Steve Martin.

I was about a third of the way through an homage to Mr. Winters in the New York Times. It was eerily familiar, as the writer shared his experience of watching Jack Parr with his father. On the tube was Jonathan Winters with a stick. I recall vividly watching the same program with my father who rarely laughed. But on this occasion he had fallen off the couch after Winters had thrust the stick into his chest and uttered the words “The United Nations now recognizes the delegate from Nasiland.” The author of the homage was none other than Robin Williams, and most readers know who he is.

One of my favorite skits was “Captain Arnold.” It would be politically incorrect, I suppose, for a modern comic to perform such material these days. Arnold was very gay. Like Ahab, who was a “fantasy,” Arnold was on a quest for the great white whale. When a crew member shouts “thar she blows,” Arnold asks for his spyglass. But he needs “both hands” to hold it, being of less-than-manly stature. And so on.

A few years later I had the good fortune of watching a live performance of Winters at Berkeley’s Greek Theater. The outdoor venue was packed and the weather was calm. If laughter is the best medicine, we thousands were quite well that night.

Not long before this performance Jonathan Winters had climbed the Balclutha, an old vessel moored at the San Francisco Marina. Winters had lost his mind, for which he was committed to a sanatarium. There he managed to overcome depression and, it seems, his alcoholism.

At the Theta Delta Chi fraternity I continued my Winters routines following Monday dinners. The pledges were required to stand up and “tell a joke.” I always received a thumbs up along with the laughter.

Thank you, Mr. Winters. I had nothing else to say.

Rewarding wrong

April 15, 2013 by davald

One might think by now that those who have championed a particular set of policy initiatives, which we can place under the rubric of conservatism for now, and who have nevertheless been proven wrong in nearly every respect would find it difficult to either stay in office or get themselves elected. But one would be mistaken. The power of nonsense retains its ability to inflict increasing damage on the Rest of Us while enjoying our electoral support. Color us schizophrenic.

Among those policy initiatives are “austerity measures.” Governments must restrain spending at all costs, because spending is the problem. (Oh, and high taxes.) Europe’s political leaders can’t seem to get enough austerity, despite overwhelming evidence that tightening fiscal balance sheets exacerbates both debts and unemployment.

Here in the U.S. an entire political party preaches fiscal restraint in religious overtones for no good reason. Our unemployment rate, though not as high as most of Europe’s, remains stubbornly high, with millions of Americans unable to find jobs after months and even years of searching. Many have simply given up trying.

I confess to lots of head scratching over this rewarding-the-wrong phenomenon. I just don’t understand the rationales of those who vote against their economic interests, as in What’s the Matter With Kansas? Why are so many of us evidently comfortable with high unemployment and associated lost lives?

Working poor

April 12, 2013 by davald

The Bureau of Labor Statistics has a new report (pdf) on America’s working poor. To be quick about it, there are lots of them. Here’s a chart on “primary families,” which are defined as families with at least one person working at least half a year.

What the graph shows is the percentage of primary families who are poor despite a member’s working. So, in 2011 8.3 percent of those families live in poverty, up from 6.4 in 2007, just before the Great Recession. Among all persons the percentage of working poor in 2011 was seven, down slightly from  2010.

What’s going on? BLS takes a stab and comes up with a partial list. Among the culprits are the proliferation of part-time jobs and, more important, low wages. A good proxy for both would be so-called burger-flippers. Not only do they work less than full time, they don’t make much money, the minimum wage or perhaps a bit more.

It is a sad testament to our political culture that the needs of the poor go unmentioned let alone mentioned. Certainly the Republicans aren’t about to address the problem. They consistently resist raising the minimum wage and always champion the desires of employers over their past, present, and future employees. So it’s quite easy to jettison workers and deny them both benefits and job security.

While most modern nations have a labor party, America doesn’t. We have the Democrats, who seem just as eager to screw unions as their conservative counterparts, and it’s unions that brought us the eight-hour day, the end of child labor, the minimum wage, and a host of other benefits that are as niggardly as they are taken for granted.
