We are (probably) not alone

January 11, 2012 by davald

Assume that the standard cosmological model is correct, that everything there is began as a singular point just before the Big Bang. It would seem to follow that matter and physical processes are homogeneous throughout the universe. That is not to say that everything looks, feels, or behaves the same. Obviously, one of the defining characteristics of this great expanse is diversity. From essential building blocks emerge incredible variation.

We inhabit a small speck of a planet in the Milky Way galaxy. Astronomers estimate that it is home to over 100 billion stars, including our sun, around which several planets revolve. Again, assuming homogeneity, we would expect that other stars are surrounded by planets.

It should not surprise us, then, that there are likely more planets than there are stars. The New York Times:

The more astronomers look for other worlds, the more they find that it’s a crowded and crazy cosmos. They think planets easily outnumber stars in our galaxy and they’re even finding them in the strangest of places.

A rather whimsical essay appeared as the Everett Herald‘s editorial this morning, “Why ET isn’t phoning us.” The writer, I presume Bob Bolerjack, the editorial page editor, reacts to a couple of observations proffered by columnist Charles Krauthammer and physicist Stephen Hawking, who just turned 70.

Krauthammer wonders why we haven’t heard from extraterrestrial counterparts.

As we inevitably find more and more exo-planets where intelligent life can exist, why have we found no evidence — no signals, no radio waves — that intelligent life does exist?

“So why the silence?” Krauthammer asks. “Carl Sagan (among others) thought that the answer is to be found, tragically, in the final variable: the high probability that advanced civilizations destroy themselves.”

Hawking, too, fears that homo sapiens are making a muck of their planet. Time to colonize other terrestrial bodies, he advises.

Which reminds me of an answer given by a parish priest, who would marry my wife and me. Back then (I don’t know how things go now) couples needed to confer with a priest prior to their wedding, evidently to ensure that we were worthy Catholics. At any rate, the subject of birth control inevitably surfaced. The Church, of course, forbids all forms of contraception, save for coitus interruptus. I posed the question: Well, what about over-population? Without hesitating the priest replied, “We’ll colonize the moon.” He preceded Hawking by almost 50 years.

The Herald editorial concludes:

Perhaps it will occur to Krauthammer and Hawking that there is, in fact, intelligent life out there and they have determined the smart thing to do is to stay the heck away from humans and the earth they are apparently destroying, and will one day seek to escape. With eyes on colonizing someone else’s earth-like planet.

Who exactly would want to befriend us?

Indeed.

A tale of two states

January 11, 2012 by davald

I was fortunate enough to have grown up in California under governor Pat Brown and University of California president Clark Kerr. Education was free, well-funded, and available, even at the tertiary level. Sacramento seemed to have enough money to pay for good schools plus transportation and social services.

But, like Washington state, California allows citizens to write initiatives and place them on ballots. Unlike Washington, however, California’s successful initiatives cannot be repealed or modified by state government. Californians would eventually rue the day that they were given such power, because the halcyon days gave way to a string of crises that now render the state almost ungovernable. It began with the passage of Proposition 13.

With its economy booming (this in the decades of the 50s and 60s), housing prices began their steady climb along with a proliferation of sprawling homes and rising property taxes. Enough, the people said, passing Howard Jarvis’s ballot proposal. Property taxes would be capped at one percent of 1975 assessments, with increases limited to two percent per annum, as long as the owner remained in his house. This had the predictable effect of gradually reducing government revenues. Sacramento was forced to curtail public services.

While the reduction in property taxes would receive the bigger play, the more pernicious feature of Proposition 13 was to require a two-thirds vote of the state legislature to raise taxes. Sound familiar?

Over subsequent years, interest groups, including the teachers’ unions, persuaded the voters to pass a series of initiatives that compelled the legislature to prioritize funding. Fiscal obligations changed, but without new sources of revenue. Sacramento had very little financial discretion. So it robbed Peter to pay Paul.

The Great Recession took a major toll on California, resulting in massive budget shortfalls, government layoffs, and sharply reduced public services. Like Olympia, Sacramento could only cut to balance the budget.

California’s miserable experience is being replicated here in Washington. The one difference, as mentioned above, is that our state legislature can amend or repeal citizens’ initiatives after two years. Washington voters have repeatedly required a two-thirds majority to increase taxes, only to have the matter repealed two sessions later.

Washington is now under the super-majority restriction. Yet, it must balance its budget, which means only cutting is allowed.

However, the state’s constitution makes public education the legislature’s “paramount” duty. After Olympia severely cut funding of public education a group of citizens sued. A few weeks ago the state’s supreme court issued a unanimous opinion. It upheld the constitutional mandate and warned the legislature that it cannot further reduce the education budget. Moreover, the ruling (pdf) states:

  • The word “ample” in article IX, section 1 provides a broad constitutional guideline meaning fully, sufficient, and considerably more than just adequate.
  • Ample funding for basic education must be accomplished by means of dependable and regular tax sources.

The lead plaintiff in the successful lawsuit writes in a Seattle Times op-ed:

Legislators have a sworn duty to uphold that mandate. And they now have a Supreme Court decision that reaffirms their constitutional duty to amply fund K-12 education first. They must therefore take the K-12 budget off the table as they consider cuts this session, and start increasing public-school funding now in order to meet the 2018 deadline of amply funding public schools with “regular and dependable tax sources.”

“The court cannot idly stand by as the Legislature makes unfulfilled promises for reform,” the Supreme Court said in retaining jurisdiction to enforce its ruling.

What can the legislature do, given its inability to raise taxes? Should it stop funding services to the poor? What about transportation funding?*

Governor Gregoire has hinted at two strategies: Ask the voters to increase taxes to fund education (subject to a simple majority vote); petition the supreme court to advise on whether or not the constitutional mandate trumps the two-thirds majority provision.

People’s preferences are as contradictory as they are fickle. There are entire communities that repeatedly turn thumbs down on school levies, Marysville being one of them, even as they complain about the quality of education. My guess is that surveys would show that the general public supports education, but would balk when asked to pay for it. Thus, I don’t have much hope for the public’s agreeing to tax themselves further.

As for the supreme court’s rendering an opinion that would essentially nullify the two-thirds majority requirement when it comes to education funding, I can only guess. Could it carve out an exception for the state’s “paramount” duty? We’ll have to wait and see.

Meanwhile, Olympia faces on ongoing budget challenge: inadequate revenues to offset expenditures. It’s in a short session this year, with few expecting major initiatives to pass, given the need to make further cuts.

If I could play king for a day, I’d abolish the initiative process, establish a unicameral state parliament, the leader of which would serve as governor, and enact proportional representation. I believe that such a government could get things done and fairly quickly. Whether they act for good or ill would depend on whom we elect.

Just dreaming, of course.

______________

* Ironically, she’s also proposed that the legislature pass a 10-year, $3.6 billion transportation package.

To help pay for the package, she asked the Legislature to increase some fees, including adding a $100 fee to electric vehicles and passing a $1.50 fee on every barrel of oil produced in Washington.

Nevertheless, this is hardly adequate to the challenge. As the Times writes:

Gregoire’s proposal is modest compared to a recent report from a transportation task force she convened to examine the issue. The task force determined that state transportation needs in the next 10 years — including road, bridge and ferry projects — would cost up to $21 billion.

Japan vs U.S.

January 9, 2012 by davald

A recent New York Times op-ed has generated a bit of commotion in the blogosphere, including here. Paul Krugman weighed in this morning as did Matthew Yglesias. Both conclude that Japan did suffer a lost decade, or more, but its experience pales beside ours since the Great Recession.

Let’s compare some numbers. Here’s a chart, based on St. Louis Fed data, showing Japan’s GDP/capita in 2010 U.S. dollars. We’ll notice that despite the lost decade GDP per person continued to rise.

How does Japan compare to the US on real GDP/capita?

While I haven’t yet looked at the data I suspect that there’s a similar pattern for all of the western industrial democracies: steadily rising GDP/person until the Great Recession, which affected all these countries.

Yglesias shows us a chart from the Fed on Japan’s average annual hours worked per employee.

But I think this is misleading. Here’s another chart also based on St. Louis Fed data.

Yes, the typical Japanese worker is putting in fewer hours, but she’s more productive. As it happens, the U.S. worker is more productive than his Japanese counterpart.

There are a whole lot of Americans out of work right now, and they’ve been unemployed longer than at any point since the Great Depression. (Part of the reason is that rising productivity. Firms get more bang out of the labor buck than they used to, which translates into fewer workers to achieve the same or even higher output.) I’ll go out on a limb to suggest that those who lack jobs would prefer to have one and one that paid decent wages. It is the unemployment rate that really sets us apart from Japan, as this graph shows.

Notice what happened in the U.S. during Clinton’s years in office: the rate steadily fell. It actually fell below Japan’s, however briefly.

I think that this last statistic may turn out to be the most important of all. There are long-term negative effects on both those who suffer job loss and the entire economy. People out of work aren’t producing anything, and the longer they remain unemployed the more difficult it is for them to jump back into the workforce, especially at the level they occupied before losing their jobs. That loss of productivity weighs heavily on the U.S. and its fifty states. When people aren’t producing they’re not paying taxes, which reduces federal and state revenues.

So, whatever Japan does differently to keep workers on the job is something we might wish to explore. And against the austerians, we should keep in mind that Japan’s government debt is much greater than ours, which presently equals GDP.

Would we trade more debt for a lower unemployment rate? Regardless of your answer, you have no say in the matter.

Conspiracy conjecture

January 9, 2012 by davald

I’m not a big fan of conspiracy-mongering, save for one: JFK’s assassination. On this I defer to a physicist for whom I worked. No friend of the Kennedy’s, he nevertheless judged it impossible that the fatal shot came from the rear, as in the Texas Book Depository. Rather, it had to have come from the front. However, I beg your indulgence as I pull something out of thin air, with absolutely no facts to warrant my speculation.

It begins with Eisenhower’s warning about the “military-industrial complex,” perhaps overlain with Randolph Bourne’s pronouncement: “War is the health of the state.” I’ll add in a generous dose of petrodollars and, voilà, we have the makings of a conspiracy—or maybe just a constellation of interests.

The last century and a half has been the Age of Oil. Its discovery, extraction, and production have literally fueled the modern industrial economy, the one that demands we all buy things and eat like pigs, if that’s not an affront to the Suidae family.

The United States emerged from the second world war unscathed and superior. We demonstrated our ability to rapidly step up manufacturing so as to fight in two theaters: the Pacific and Europe. And I think the captains of industry liked it, the war that is. But with victory declared, our enemies vanquished, what to do with all this military stuff? Besides, the Pentagon brass must have felt depressed, all dressed up for fighting and no where to go.

Ah, but remember our Russian friends, the ones who squeezed the Nazis from the east? Well, they’re communists, after all, and we loathe communists. But, you know, we probably don’t want to actually kill people. Real war can be downright messy. So, what about a cold war? Yeah, that’s the ticket. We get to build more and better weapons, keep our armed forces well-stocked, soldiers saluting, and uniforms bedecked with colorful badges.

Pretexts are easy. Against the Soviet menace we can manufacture “situations” that demand our attention. And these situations happen to arise in areas vital to our economic interests. Most vital, of course, is oil.

Where is most of the oil? In the Middle East, principally in Saudi Arabia. The Saudi family faces constant threats, so we say to them: We’ll buy your oil then use the money to build weapons which you’ll then buy from us. A perfect triangle of national self-interests. Check.

Leaders of nations, like football teams, must compete; it’s in their nature. With a lock on oil supplies we could focus our attention on “the arms race.” Kennedy, the president mentioned in the opening, liked to talk about “the missile gap.” He wrongly asserted during his campaign that the Soviet Union had more ICBMs than we had. We’ve just got to build more of them to keep up; and build we did.

Meanwhile we conducted numerous proxy battles (the situations), framing them as contests between the Red menace and defenders of freedom. We didn’t have to actually fight in these. We just sold the arms and supplied “advisors.” It seems that the Pentagon got carried away in Southeast Asia, and found that there was no simple exit. Eventually we just declared victory and left. Better not try that again.

Then—wouldn’t you know it?—the damn Soviet Union collapsed. Didn’t see that coming. The boys in the Pentagon must have become apoplectic. Now what? We just lost our perfect foil, the Evil Empire, the mother of all pretexts.

Lurking in the wings, the Muslims, long oppressed, came to the Pentagon’s rescue. Ingenious, however diabolical: commandeer commercial jets filled with passengers then slam them into large buildings and the Pentagon itself. In response the world said that “we are all New Yorkers now.” Revenge was a given. But who is the target?

I guess it really didn’t matter, but that Saddam Hussein is one mean son-of-a-bitch, and he tried to kill the president’s daddy. We’re really going to have to sell this one, since there isn’t a sliver of evidence that Hussein, our former buddy, was involved in the 9/11 attacks. A splendid performance by all. With a thin patina of international approval, we launched a massive air assault on Iraq, which led to invasion and occupation. Well, there’s oil there, too.

Once clearly proven that there were no weapons of mass destruction and no ties to al Qaeda, the U.S. could still say that the world was a better place without Hussein, we’re helping Iraqis build a democracy, and there’s the oil.

Yet, Osama bin Laden, the real evil doer, was alive and presumably well inhabiting mountain caves on the border between Afghanistan and Pakistan. The Bush administration had demanded that the Afghans turn him over to us, but the silly people insisted on proof that he did it. Besides, this is a big country and we don’t know where the hell he is. Sorry about that, Kabul, but we’re going to have to invade and occupy your country. That’s how we do things.

Fast forward to the present. We’re still in Afghanistan, our nation’s longest war, though it’s unclear as to whom we’re fighting and what we expect to accomplish. Making good on a campaign promise, Pres. Obama ended our formal occupation of Iraq, although we’ve left behind a fair number of mercenaries and the grandest embassy in all the world. It’s even got a McDonald’s.

Our most recent “situation” is being called “the Arab spring.” After living under generations of tyrants, the people suddenly took matters into their own hands, spawning a series of “spontaneous” revolutions. Oh, and you can bet that we had something to do with these. After all, there’s oil there, too.

Situations don’t typically arise in fields of broccoli. They happen in regions of strategic importance and have everything to do with oil and other raw materials. (Afghanistan is home to a trove of “rare earth” metals used in all those electronic gadgets we obsess over, your’s truly included.)

Noam Chomsky wrote this of the Libyan revolt:

The hope was for a regime likelier to be amenable to Western demands for control over Libya’s rich resources and, perhaps, to offer an African base for the U.S. Africa command AFRICOM, so far confined to Stuttgart.

Now I have no idea if such speculation has even a hint of truthfulness. We’re all free to connect dots to fashion narratives, whether or not justified. If we’re fond of Occam’s Razor, we might say that conspiracies aren’t necessary to explain events; they are what they are.

On the other hand, we can probably learn a lot about what’s going on by following the money and the oil. No need to bother with the broccoli.

Japan’s success story

January 8, 2012 by davald

Many economists, among them Paul Krugman and Ben Bernanke (pdf), cite Japan as a warning for the U.S. That is, we’re in danger of entering a lost decade or two, just as the Japanese allegedly experienced and, indeed, continue to experience. But this article in the New York Times belies this narrative.

As it happens, the Japanese have bettered America in a number of key statistics, including health care, quality of life, and economic strength:

• Japan’s average life expectancy at birth grew by 4.2 years — to 83 years from 78.8 years — between 1989 and 2009. This means the Japanese now typically live 4.8 years longer than Americans. The progress, moreover, was achieved in spite of, rather than because of, diet. The Japanese people are eating more Western food than ever. The key driver has been better health care.

• Japan has made remarkable strides in Internet infrastructure. Although as late as the mid-1990s it was ridiculed as lagging, it has now turned the tables. In a recent survey by Akamai Technologies, of the 50 cities in the world with the fastest Internet service, 38 were in Japan, compared to only 3 in the United States.

• Measured from the end of 1989, the yen has risen 87 percent against the U.S. dollar and 94 percent against the British pound. It has even risen against that traditional icon of monetary rectitude, the Swiss franc.

• The unemployment rate is 4.2 percent, about half of that in the United States.

• According to skyscraperpage.com, a Web site that tracks major buildings around the world, 81 high-rise buildings taller than 500 feet have been constructed in Tokyo since the “lost decades” began. That compares with 64 in New York, 48 in Chicago, and 7 in Los Angeles.

• Japan’s current account surplus — the widest measure of its trade — totaled $196 billion in 2010, up more than threefold since 1989. By comparison, America’s current account deficit ballooned to $471 billion from $99 billion in that time. Although in the 1990s the conventional wisdom was that as a result of China’s rise Japan would be a major loser and the United States a major winner, it has not turned out that way. Japan has increased its exports to China more than 14-fold since 1989 and Chinese-Japanese bilateral trade remains in broad balance.

We should be so lucky.

The inconvenient Noam

January 7, 2012 by davald

I have at least two dozen books by Noam Chomsky, some dealing with his academic field, linguistics and philosophy, but most about politics. He has an uncanny knack for revealing inherent truths and contradictions. In particular, Prof. Chomsky demonstrates repeatedly the abject hypocrisy of U.S. international policies and those of its allies.

In this essay, Chomsky focuses on George Orwell’s expression “unpeople.” By definition, these are groups of individuals unworthy of any society. Thus then can be ignored, at least, or tortured and killed, in the extreme.

Here he discusses the “imperial triumvirate’s” bombing of Libya, ostensibly to support the interests of the rebellion, rising up to overthrow Gaddafi, detested and despised. Chomsky suggests that the actions were “undertaken to eliminate the mercurial tyrant whom they had supported when it was advantageous.” However, Gaddafi—like Saddam Hussein—could no longer be trusted to serve imperial interests.

The hope was for a regime likelier to be amenable to Western demands for control over Libya’s rich resources and, perhaps, to offer an African base for the U.S. Africa command AFRICOM, so far confined to Stuttgart.

Pentagon research

January 7, 2012 by davald

The threatened cuts to the Pentagon budget have many people worried, including, of course, those who have siphoned off enormous profits from defense contracts. There is another concern: such cutting may reduce research and development, the results of which have spawned civilian applications and services. The Internet being chief among them.

According to the New York Times:

The one exception may be Pentagon spending on research and development.

The Pentagon spends about 12 percent of its budget in that area, about $81.4 billion during the most recent fiscal year. That is roughly 55 percent of all federal spending on research and development.

So, why not take some or even all of those dollars and spend them directly on R&D, bypassing the Pentagon bureaucracy altogether?

Not surprisingly, there is debate on the pros and cons of conducting research via the military. The Times article briefly addresses the disparate views. One economist, for example, suggests that having the Pentagon engage in tax-funded research provides additional security and stability. While another argues that going through the Pentagon stifles innovation, since the military has specific war-related objectives.

The Times also points out that the private sector now spends two dollars for every military R&D dollar.

In response, the military has increasingly focused on cherry-picking promising ideas. A 2009 report by an advisory panel of independent scientists concluded that the shift was reducing the chances for the transformative discoveries that once emerged with remarkable regularity.

There is another argument implicit in this discussion. When private firms are large, especially those engaged in technology, they can better afford to establish and sustain research laboratories. I have in mind IBM and AT&T before the breakup. The latter generously funded Bell Labs, yielding technological innovation. Likewise Xerox’s research arm, XPARC, invented the graphical user interface (GUI), which Steve Jobs observed then further developed into what became the Macintosh computer. Apple, being the most valuable technology company in the world and having more cash reserves than the U.S. Treasury, can and does support a large and growing R&D initiative.

While we may disagree over the value of technological innovation (e.g., whether it squeezes the middle class) and its horrible manufacturing processes (again, see Apple), might we prefer iPads to the latest and greatest WMD, or advancing solar technology over the next cool version of laser missiles?

We’re supposed to be hurting

January 6, 2012 by davald

As a follow up to my post on American exceptionalism, I offer these sobering words from columnist Dean Baker:

Globalization has hurt the living standards of the middle class because it was designed to have this effect. Trade agreements like NAFTA were quite explicitly designed to make it as easy as possible for General Electric and other manufacturers set up operations in the developing world and export their output back to the United States. This has the effect of putting U.S. manufacturing workers in direct competition with low-paid workers in the developing world [my emphasis].

Baker is responding to a Washington Post column by David Ignatius, who, in turn, quoted “end of history” Francis Fukuyama:

“What if the further development of technology and globalization undermines the middle class and makes it impossible for more than a minority of citizens in an advanced society to achieve middle-class status?” asks Fukuyama.

That is, we’re all supposed to bend over and just take it in the ass. There’s nothing we can do about it, save become Luddites and destroy the airplanes and ships transporting goods from hither and yon.

Baker doesn’t disagree that globalization plays a strong hand in squeezing the middle class, both here and in Europe. Rather, he argues that it could have played out differently had Congress made different policy choices. Baker:

We could have designed these deals to put our doctors, lawyers, economists and other highly paid professionals in direct competition with their much lower paid counterparts in the developing world. We could have constructed trade deals that remove all the obstacles that make it difficult for students in China, India, and elsewhere and to train to U.S. standards and then practice their professions in the United States.

If globalization had followed this path it would have produced enormous benefits to both the middle class and the economy as a whole. We would be able to get health care, university education and many other services provided by highly paid professionals at much lower cost.

In the same vein it is not technology by itself that has made some people very rich. It is largely government granted patent and copyright monopolies that have made people rich. These polices are becoming increasingly inefficient mechanisms for supporting innovation and creative work.

That is, we could have and perhaps still can fashion different rules that would expand and benefit the middle class. He clearly sees that a big problem with our health care system is supply of doctors and cheap medicines. Bowing to lobbyists and not wanting to bite the hands that feed them, Congress has made health care expensive for Americans; and health care costs take a big chunk out of Americans’ paychecks. The same dynamic applies to technology because of patent and copyright laws, which not only increase costs but allow the One-percenters to extract huge profits.

The money is there. It just needs to be spread around a little bit. At a minimum, the rich shouldn’t receive more entitlements.

Good teachers

January 6, 2012 by davald

Like every reader of this post I attended school, though I skipped kindergarten, but not because I was brilliant; our family’s move occurred after the registration date. From grades 1 through 6 I had six different teachers. Since this was a very long time ago, I have only vague memories of them. All were female, with several newcomers joining veterans.

The one teacher I recall most fondly was Mrs. McKelvy, who taught sixth grade. She had a certain élan, an elegance that suggested to me that she belonged elsewhere, perhaps in fashion design or in the sitting rooms of high society. But she stood out from the rest as an educator, too. I attribute this to her ability to facilitate self-discovery among her students. She seemed to figure us out then exploit our varied interests and talents.

One rather strange classmate had long straight hair and loved horses, so much so that she imitated them in the classroom by galloping to and fro and neighing from time to time. Annoying. But she also loved to draw. Mrs. McKelvy encouraged her idiosyncrasies by first creating floor space, then providing butcher paper and crayons. And draw she did, rather elaborate yet realistic images of countless horses. I have no idea what happened to the horse-girl. However, I suspect that she found self-esteem and self-expression in at least one year of her schooling.

In my case, I must have shown an interest in music. So Mrs. McKelvy encouraged me to join the orchestra, as a string bass player. A couple of points here: I had no formal training in music, including note-reading; and, yes, we had an orchestra in elementary school! This was California in the golden days of education.

There was one other teacher, I think it was in third grade, on whom I had a crush. She was young and energetic, which helped. When she changed her last name via marriage about half way through, my little heart broke. Sad.

In high school there was Mr. Goltra, a graduate of Rutgers University in New Jersey. His head devoid of hair, he’d busily write formulas on the board, demonstrating trigonometry and elementary functions. My table mate and I would pound our fists and shout out, “Rutgers is proud!” Mr. Goltra beamed. He made math interesting, and I did reasonably well at it.

Now to my point: after spending so many years in the classroom I can remember fewer than three “good” teachers, from first grade through twelfth. And something else: there were few tests, and none induced fear and anxiety. How did I and hundreds of classmates learn without them?

I put scare quotes around good, because I have no idea if these three teachers added value to their students. And this seems to be the rage these days, “value-added teaching.” Several states have established criteria to grade teachers. The most controversial is test scores.  What makes a good teacher? Improved test scores. How does one improve test scores? With good teachers. Note the circularity.

Now comes a couple of researchers who believe they can demonstrate that good teachers have long lasting effects on their students. To wit, their graduates earn more money as adults. The New York Times:

Elementary- and middle-school teachers who help raise their students’ standardized-test scores seem to have a wide-ranging, lasting positive effect on those students’ lives beyond academics, including lower teenage-pregnancy rates and greater college matriculation and adult earnings, according to a new study that tracked 2.5 million students over 20 years.

Again, we’re back to a logical circle. If we define “good” teachers by their students’ test scores, then, it seems, to boost scores you’ll need good teachers. And how will you know if you have good teachers in the classroom? By their students’s test scores.

Here’s the tricky part. Aside from this definition, what are the ingredients of good teaching? What do good teachers do differently from their middling or poor colleagues? If we walked into a good teacher’s classroom, what might be the observable indicators of quality teaching?

On to an even stickier widget: Whatever qualities we might discern in good teachers, can they be taught to others? Could we create good teachers out of bad ones? Or, as one researcher suggests:

“The message is to fire people sooner rather than later,” Professor [John] Friedman said.

Ouch. Skip the training and go straight to the pink slip.

The New York Times previously asked the question: What makes a good teacher?

But what makes a good teacher? There have been many quests for the one essential trait, and they have all come up empty-handed. Among the factors that do not predict whether a teacher will succeed: a graduate-school degree, a high score on the SAT, an extroverted personality, politeness, confidence, warmth, enthusiasm and having passed the teacher-certification exam on the first try. When Bill Gates announced recently that his foundation was investing millions in a project to improve teaching quality in the United States, he added a rueful caveat. “Unfortunately, it seems the field doesn’t have a clear view of what characterizes good teaching,” Gates said. “I’m personally very curious.”

Aren’t we all?

Lacking an answer to the question are we left with the great sort? That is, school systems could track teachers over a number of years, collect and analyze data, then produce results similar to the aforementioned study. My guess is that there would be a normal distribution, with one tail of superior teachers and the other tail lousy teachers, with everyone else clumped around a mean—all determined by students’ test scores over time.

So, please tell me. How did I learn anything without all those tests? Or, maybe I’m dumber than a brick.

Macro gets attention

January 5, 2012 by davald

If you visit the economics blogosphere you’ll quickly notice that there is sharp disagreement between what some have called the “freshwater” and “saltwater” economists. The former roughly describes the University of Chicago, led perhaps by Robert Lucas, Jr., a Nobel Prize winner. The saltwater folks occupy the two coasts, Princeton and the Boston constellation on the Atlantic and Berkeley on the Pacific.

These disparate groups also happen to align either for or against Keynesianism, however defined of late. Princeton’s Paul Krugman is the most outspoken champion of Keynes’s theories applied to today’s economic situation.

Recently there’s been quite a bit of chatter regarding a paper written by Chicago’s John Cochrane, who argues that there is no such thing as fiscal stimulus. Here’s an exchange, of sorts, between Brad DeLong, a macroeconomist at Berkeley, and Cochrane.

Now I’m not going to weigh in on this squabble, for a couple of reasons: I’m a lesser mortal than the disputants, and many have already offered their opinions and arguments. I do want to touch on one point made by Cochrane:

The inflation that will result from a trillion dollars of money permanently dropped on the economy, and the real economic dislocation of such a major inflation, is frightful to contemplate.

Cochrane wrote this in 2009. Since, the Federal Reserve has effectively tripled the money supply, adding an additional two trillion dollars. Yet, inflation remains low.

So I thought I’d take a look at two sets of data: M2 and CPI. I calculated the annual change in each then plotted the outcome on a chart.

Now it’s true that between 1960 and 1980 inflation coincided with money supply. But that’s certainly not the case over the last 30 years. Indeed, despite a dramatic jump in the supply of money in 2009, the economy experienced deflation—that is, prices fell; the first time since 1955.

The broader question may be this: Why haven’t changes in the money supply since 1980 affected the Consumer Price Index?
