Tell a good story

December 12, 2011 by davald

Judy Lightfoot tells us about Elizabeth Warren, who is running against incumbent Scott Brown to represent Massachusetts in the Senate. Warren is offering a simple, truth-filled narrative about the Great Divergence. Lightfoot:

Warren told the story, which is becoming familiar around the country, in Seattle Friday night before a crowd of people with $40 tickets to a joint fundraiser with Sen. Maria Cantwell at the Paramount Theatre. The story begins in the Great Depression, Warren said, “a dark point in America’s history,” a colossal bust in the boom-and-bust cycle that had caused panics, bank crashes, and the destruction of ordinary American lives and livelihoods every 15 years or so since the 1790s.

But then “America said we can do better,” Warren said. We instituted FDIC insurance, banking rules, and the SEC to enforce those rules. We also “invested in ourselves and built America’s middle class” by putting money into education K-through-grad school (“for every dollar we put in, we got five dollars back in taxes”) plus into infrastructure like highways, transit, a power grid, and “research, research, research.” Thus “our country got richer, and families got richer right along with us. It worked for more than half a century.”

Sadly, the story goes, about 30 years ago we lost our way. A deregulated financial industry and unfair taxation have “staggered America’s middle class,” said Warren. “G.E., a profitable company with worldwide operations, pays zero taxes.” At the same time, we starve the infrastructure that our millionaires depended on for growing their enterprises, “and we tell young people ‘You’re going to have to take on debt to get an education.’ ”

Pass it on.

The sadness of dying

December 12, 2011 by davald

It is difficult to look at photographs of Christopher Hitchens as he faces certain death from esophageal cancer. While he’s not one to attract sympathy—he doesn’t ask for it and his writings are decidedly pugnacious—Mr. Hitchens has mine. (Photo from Vanity Fair)

My father, as did his father, died from this dreadful disease. My father received an initial diagnosis of “bronchitis.” When his condition lingered, the doctors performed more tests to discover that it was cancer, one whose prognosis sucks—big time. Very few people emerge from the treatments to survive another day.

Hitchens’s disease, while taking its predictable toll, has evidently responded better to the radiation and chemotherapy than my father’s, who lived only five months or so beyond the diagnosis. Hitchens is a year and a half removed from his. But his recent photograph and his writings betray an imminent end.

I began reading Hitchens when he wrote for The Nation magazine. Always provocative, his essays took few prisoners, yet done in a style without equal. The man can write.

Then G.W. Bush decided to launch an assault on Iraq. Though Hitchens shared my disdain for the former Texas governor, he nevertheless endorsed the blatantly illegal aggression—enthusiastically so. When The Nation predictably condemned the invasion, for all the right reasons, Hitchens abruptly resigned, choosing to express his elegant vitriol elsewhere.

He justified his position by an appeal to ridding the world of tyrannical despots, in this case Saddam Hussein. Saddam, as we know, was a very, very bad man. Whatever the official pretext for his disposal, Hitchens didn’t seem to care—just oust the bugger. And the world would be better off for it.

Despite all the evidence that Saddam had nothing to do with 9/11 and that he had no weapons of mass destruction, Hitchens has not regretted his war-boosting. I think this distracts from his otherwise excellent body of work, from politics to literary criticism. Hitchens:

I am typing this having just had an injection to try to reduce the pain in my arms, hands, and fingers. The chief side effect of this pain is numbness in the extremities, filling me with the not irrational fear that I shall lose the ability to write.

Hitchens lives to write. I have read that he types with blazing speed, efficiently turning out one assignment after another in short order. His data-retrieval mechanism astonishes. Hitchens concludes his latest piece for Vanity Fair:

So far, I have decided to take whatever my disease can throw at me, and to stay combative even while taking the measure of my inevitable decline. I repeat, this is no more than what a healthy person has to do in slower motion. It is our common fate. In either case, though, one can dispense with facile maxims that don’t live up to their apparent billing.

He had in mind Nietzsche’s tome The Will to Live and the attributed quote: “Whatever doesn’t kill me makes me stronger.” Poppycock, argues Hitchens, who will not go gentle into that good night.

Irrational choice

December 10, 2011 by davald

You may share my bewilderment over so many of our fellow citizens choosing political candidates whose benefactors and ideologies betray positions and interests antithetical to those of the Rest of Us. No? Then feel free to skip this.

If one has been paying attention to the media and blogosphere since the collapse of the housing bubble and the emergent Great Recession one could not escape the sharp divide in rhetoric, diagnosis, and prescription. While consensus exists that the economy is not like it used to be, none exists beyond that trivial observation.

I have titled this blog “Irrational choice.” By that I mean deciding against one’s own interests, as opposed to a more literal connotation of unreasonable or illogical. Those who vote against their perceived self-interest no doubt possess reason and can wade through a syllogism. I might suggest that what’s going on is more subtle and yet perverse.

In his book Thinking, Fast and Slow, psychologist Daniel Kahneman tells us that most of what passes for cognition is instinctive and unconscious. He calls this “System 1.” Deliberation, inference, conscious mental behavior involves “System 2.” These do not stand for physical locations within our brain, but rather distinct processes. System 1 thinking (“fast”) can be affected by an infinite number of what Kahneman calls “priming effects.” (He didn’t coin the term.) The problem for our species is that System 1 dominates, even to the point that we “see” the world through prior influences and, especially, associations. Why? Kahneman suggests that System 1 mental behavior is easy, requiring less energy than System 2. Besides, it keeps us out of danger, since System 2 can’t react to warning signs quickly enough.

System 2 tends to be lazy and readily defers to System 1. It requires concentration, calories, and selectivity. We’ve got to block out stimuli in order to focus our attention. Kahneman gives the example of trying to calculate in one’s head the product of two numbers, 17 and 24. Unless we’re another “Rainman,” we’re forced to drop everything else to perform the mental arithmetic. System 2 would prefer (I’m being metaphorical here) to take the easy train, just because it is so much more intensive than System 1. It really doesn’t want to overrule our unconscious biases.

I’ll venture out onto a long intellectual limb here: the Republican mind operates almost exclusively in the System 1 realm; the Democratic mind avails itself of System 2 more frequently. Stepping out a bit further, Republicans make more irrational choices than Democrats.

Now, I’m not the first person to draw such an inference. Following the GOP landslide in 1994, Berkeley cognitive scientist George Lakoff was so anguished by voters’ electoral preferences that he decided to write a book. Published two years later, it bore the title Moral Politics. It initially carried the subtitle What Conservatives Know That Liberals Don’t. He replaced this in the second edition with How Liberals and Conservatives Think. (Thomas Frank, now the editor of Harper’s magazine, was also befuddled by the conservative victories, which required so many to vote against their interests. He wrote a book in response, What’s the Matter with Kansas?)

Lakoff set about to describe two broad categories of metaphors to distinguish between the conservative and the liberal, or progressive. The former falls under the strict father morality; the latter under nurturant parent.

The strict father is all about self-discipline, self-reliance, and tough love—I’m doing this or that to you, child, for your own good. It will make you stronger. Good behavior is rewarded; bad behavior is punished. If you fail, chances are it’s your own damn fault.

One also sees this constellation within the conservative Christian religion, at least as practiced in the U.S. There is a definite hierarchy. God, the father, is at the top. All must show obeisance to him, or else incur his wrath. The bible is a set of rules that must be followed.

Likewise, in the family unit the father/husband is the authoritative figure; everyone else defers to him, including the “submissive” wife. In turn, the father/husband is expected to provide for the family. Failure to do so, regardless of circumstances, is a sign of moral weakness.

If you’ve bought into this so far, you can appreciate that the conservative tends to view government as harmful, if not pernicious. The government, especially as provider of public services, contributes to moral weakness by creating dependencies, which lead to sloth and irresponsibility.

Quite opposite, the liberal is the product of nurturant parent morality. The child will be raised in a loving, caring, compassionate environment. Mistakes or misbehaviors are not signs of moral failing but part of normal development. Husband and wife are co-equals. Both are responsible for the child’s well-being.

If the liberal is religious, his or her god will be fair, just, sympathetic, forgiving. There is an emphasis on the New Covenant as described in the bible, rather than the Ten Commandments. The Sermon on the Mount looms large. Disagreements should be resolved by negotiation rather than fisticuffs. Peace is preferable to war.

Government, in the liberal mind, should provide help to those who are victims of circumstances, often outside their control. Government is about safety nets and collective insurance and less about punishment of wrongdoing. Taxes are judged necessary to provide important services, and tax rates should be progressive, so that those who have more pay higher rates.

Lakoff, like Kahneman, opines that most of our thinking is unconscious. We react instinctively, and we’re easily influenced by metaphors, as Lakoff argues. Indeed, that’s how we think—metaphorically. Lakoff discusses certain terms and phrases in our vocabulary and their service as metaphors in evaluating our experiences. For example, we associate ‘white’ with ‘good’ and ‘pure’ and ‘black’ with ‘bad’ and ‘dirty.’ ‘Taller’ is better than ‘shorter.’ Thus when someone says that he “looks up to someone” he is doing two things: elevating the admired individual while subordinating himself.

Both Ladoff and Kahneman attach much importance to associations, which are as strong as they are pervasive. The broad metaphorical categories described above yield relevant associations that affect how we view the world around us. Since System 1 is always “on” during our waking moments, we have a strong propensity to make snap judgments about nearly everything. (As Malcolm Gladwell put it, we decide in a “blink.”)

What I find perverse about all this is that the mental associations and the priming effect actually determine what we see—even after our judgments have been falsified by objective proof. Worse, there is some research that suggests that our prejudices are reinforced by the counterfactual evidence.

Consider the topic of global warming. Those who deny the science and its conclusions will do so in even stronger tones when confronted by more scientific evidence confirming that global warming is real and largely human-caused. That is to say, we cannot appeal to the denier’s reason, since his System 1 mode blocks out his System 2. The prejudices intensify.

Now let’s return to behaviors that undermine one’s self-interest. In the economic realm, most people belong to the 99-percenters. They have virtually nothing in common with the One-percenters, whether it’s accumulated wealth or the company kept. Most Republicans belong to the larger group. Yet, the Republican Party, as evinced by policies emanating from the House, not only ignore the interests of the 99-percenters; they eviscerate them. The GOP prefers to coddle the very wealthy, and stick it to everyone else.

That attitude becomes clearer with each proposal issuing from the White House. Most recently, the Republican-dominated House is holding hostage Obama’s proposals: extended unemployment benefits, the payroll tax cut, and about $20 billion in funding for the new health care law. The New York Times:

House Republicans moved Friday toward a showdown with President Obama over the Keystone XL oil pipeline, which they included in their bill to extend tax relief for 160 million American workers through 2012.

Republicans said the pipeline would create jobs and had support from dozens of Democrats in Congress. And they welcomed the opportunity to split two Democratic constituencies: labor union members who stand to benefit from work on the pipeline and environmentalists, who oppose the project.

The House Republican bill, scheduled for a vote next week on the House floor, would deal with three urgent issues: extending a payroll tax cut scheduled to expire next month; continuing jobless benefits for the long-term unemployed; and averting a 27 percent cut in Medicare payments to doctors that would otherwise occur on Jan. 1.

The bill, unveiled Friday, would also block certain air pollution rules and eliminate more than $20 billion of spending planned under Mr. Obama’s new health care law.

The House Republicans, and all current GOP presidential candidates, favor policies that benefit extremely wealthy citizens at the expense of the Rest of Us, including most conservatives. So why do so many Republicans support policies adverse to their own interests?

It may have everything to do with Lakoff’s strict father morality, which is dominated by System 1 cognition. Financial success, the conservatives hold, deserves praise and honor rather than opprobrium. The rich should be worshiped rather than vilified. They have earned their money through discipline, self-reliance, and dint of hard work—or so the myth goes.

Thus, in the conservative mind, the Occupy Wall Street crowd are infidels; they would seek to subtract from the vast fortunes of the wealthy. By this System 1-thinking, extracting more taxes out of the rich violates the mores of the strict father metaphor. Likewise, using tax dollars to fund “entitlements” is equally pernicious to the conservative mind, since government benefits reward the weak and, therefore, the morally degenerate.

Irrational, to be sure, but nevertheless understandable within the above-described context. It would follow, then, that no attempt to demonstrate the folly of undermining one’s self-interests can succeed. More likely, such efforts only harden the resolve.

The liberal Rest of Us waste precious time and resources engaging the conservative in argument. There is a battle going on, and it’s time to forcibly press the nurturant parent. Here’s Lakoff:

Conservatives have, to a significant extent, come together on the question of their central values. Progressives [and liberals] must also unite, if they are to have a chance of holding their own against the conservative juggernaut. I think this issue to bring progressives together should be this most central of all issues—raising children to become responsible, empathetic adults.

Religious extremism

November 30, 2011 by davald

Via Neflix streaming I am engrossed in episodes of the BBC series MI-5, subtitled “spooks.” It’s an intelligent alternative to, say, 24. The acting is superb, the characters interesting, and the writing brilliant. That’s what the Brits like to say—brilliant. The series began in 2002 and is still going, I think, although I’m just now finishing up season 3.

While England lacked its own 9/11, it has suffered numerous bombings and serious threats, placing the country on a state of high alert, clearly manifest in the telecasts. The real MI-5 has had its hands full trying to foil would-be attacks, much as our FBI-cum-Homeland Security apparatus does here. England has an awful lot of CCTVs, too.

I’ve just watched a couple of episodes dealing with Muslim terrorist bombings and close calls. The BBC, in a nod to equal opportunity, engages Christian fundamentalists in similar attacks.

Indeed, there are true believers in nearly every sect bent on a holy war to settle things once and for all. I’m appalled by such extremism and the rationalization of violence in furtherance of a greater cause. (To be sure, MI-5 carries out the same calculus, at least on TV, with the agency’s head instructing a subordinate to “kill him,” on more than one occasion.) One reason for my revulsion is that I am generally a peaceful man, eschewing physical violence and abhorring state-sponsored murder—capital punishment. I don’t care much for war, either.

Moreover, I simply cannot fathom the primacy of religious beliefs in savage behavior. MI-5 will have a Muslim cleric tell an ardent follower to bomb the hell out of a train station and himself to boot. It’s all in a good cause, you see, because the martyred follower is promised celestial virgins, or some such crap. So the Muslims run around saying “Allah” while killing innocents. “Death to infidels.”

Christianity, of course, is no stranger to these attitudes. A very nasty bunch down through the ages: the Crusades, Protestants against Catholics, Catholics purging other Catholics, and so on.

Nearly every religion can be called upon to sacrifice, murder, maim, or imprison others or their own. There are few exceptions that come to mind, perhaps the Amish or the Quakers. But Nixon was a Quaker, so what the hell.

I was baptized and raised in the Roman Catholic church. Through my childhood I was a dutiful member, confessing my sins and then taking holy communion. (It’s really, I mean really, the body and blood of Christ Jesus. Okay.) One good thing about being an adult Catholic is that you could drink, unlike, say, the Mormons and the Muslims. I’ve witnessed a few drunken priests in my day. Heck, you can even drink wine during Mass.

The church has come in for a lot of deserving heat of late. It seems that far too many young men enter the seminary so that they can later fool around with boys, permanently damaging their pubescent psyches.

Nor is the church hierarchy timid in issuing directives to the faithful, although I’ve yet to hear “death to infidels.” In addition to the biblical commandments, there’s a seemingly endless list of don’t dos.

But my biggest disappointment in the Vatican and its direct reports (cardinals and bishops) is their failure, if they’re even wont to try, to loudly and publicly condemn wars and violence. Hell, they even have priests in the barracks. Yes, there is the just war doctrine. That is, it’s okay to slaughter provided you follow the rules.

Here’s what I’d like to see from Rome, although I certainly won’t hold my breath. It would be in the form of a proclamation, something like this:

Henceforth, it shall be a grave mortal sin to kill another human being, regardless of circumstances!

There are an awful lot of Catholics in the military and in public office promoting the use of force and the stealing of precious resources from the Rest of Us. I have in mind Eisenhower’s speech wherein he said: “Every gun that is made, every warship launched, every rocket fired signifies, in the final sense, a theft from those who hunger and are not fed, those who are cold and are not clothed.”

Why couldn’t those words emanate from the mouth of the Pope?

In the final analysis, however, John Lennon said it best: “Imagine no religion.”

What if we’re too dumb for democracy

November 28, 2011 by davald

Let’s call it ‘scientific intelligence.’ I don’t have in mind scientists, who are obviously smart and know a thing or two about their field of study. I’m thinking of the non-scientific cadre, which is most of us. How are we to appreciate some very complex topics so as to inform our policy judgments?

The most obvious topic here is climate change, or global warming. The overwhelming majority—close to unanimity—of climate scientists concur on several fundamental points:

  • atmospheric carbon dioxide warms the planet, serving as a “blanket,” to use Al Gore’s term
  • too much CO2 can render the planet too warm to support species as we know them; these first two are about physics
  • if the earth warms more than a couple of degrees centigrade, there will most certainly be extreme weather, prolonged droughts, habitat erosion, and the end of species

I’ll leave the facts there. Now what to do about it?

Yet we can’t go from fact to policy because far too many of us are ignorant of science in general and climate change in particular. Not only do we fail to comprehend, we Americans have a proclivity to call scientists liars and their “theories” fabrications. It’s as if we’re making this deduction: if we can’t or don’t understand science, then science is false. It evidently doesn’t occur to us to look in the mirror and wonder about the state of our grey matter.

Such things are the subject of a new book, Fool Me Twice: Fighting the Assault on Science in America, by Shawn Laurence Otto. I haven’t read the book, but the author has been interviewed by Rolling Stone magazine. Julian Brookes, writing for RS, begins:

“Whenever the people are well informed” an optimistic Thomas Jefferson wrote, “they can be trusted with their own government.” Sure – but what if the people have no clue? 

In response to one question Brookes replies:

Look at the 94 of 100 newly elected GOP members of Congress who have either said flat-out that they believe climate change is a vast hoax or that they have signed pledges to oppose any mitigation efforts. And this goes against all the evidence presented to every government around the world, including our own. This also extends to people like John Boehner, who has advocated in the past for teaching creationism in science classes, and who claims to believe that climate scientists are saying that carbon dioxide is a carcinogen.

My concern runs beyond the elected officials, who are entitled to their own opinions and, it seems, their own fact. They were elected, after all, which means that a majority of their constituents put them in office. The representatives and senators are scientifically illiterate because too many voters are even more so.

Although U.S. colleges and universities continue to churn out brilliant and innovative scientists, the bulk of American society stumbles on simple math and couldn’t tell the difference between Newton’s laws of motion and his fig-filled cookies. (I am kidding about the latter, of course.) If you ask the man on the street what happens if something goes up, he’d be unlikely to respond that “it must go down.” What’s gravity got to do with Sarah Palin or Rick Perry or just about any Republican, for that matter?

I have virtually no confidence in the American breed of homo sapiens to think its way out of climate change. Other species will either succumb or adapt to altered conditions occasioned by an ever-warming planet. Humans must rely on our brains, which, alas, appear to be in short supply.

Sorry about that, descendants.

The death of suburbia

November 26, 2011 by davald

I grew up in the suburbs, specifically in Concord, Calif., about 35 miles east of San Francisco, via the Bay Bridge and Caldecott Tunnel. It would not surprise me to learn that our housing tract was one of the first in the region, since the suburbs really didn’t take off until after WWII and the start of the boomer generation. I was five years old at the time of the move from a North Richmond apartment project. It was not until a few years ago that I departed suburbia for the higher-density of downtown Everett, Wash., which is in the throes of spasmodic transformation. It’s not quite a revitalization.

Over time I came to detest suburbia, with its endless sprawl, induced enervation, and necessary reliance on the automobile. Those few found walking along the sidewalks there were judged, in Jim Kunstler’s parlance, “short of their meds.” Unless they were teenagers, who were forced to walk long stretches to school or the local strip mall, since they had no cars of their own but the insatiable desire for social interaction.

And speaking of the automobile, in my last post I discussed briefly the notions of ecological footprint and carrying capacity. When the aggregate of the first metric exceeds the rather fixed volume of the latter, shit happens in the form of pollution, climate change, obesity, and, of course, wars. Planet Earth is a serious zero-sum game.

There is a strong correlation inverse between population density and gasoline consumption. How could it be otherwise? Consider this graph posted on Wikipedia:

Houston sits atop this chart, a big Texas city whose political leaders jettisoned the terms ‘zoning’ and ‘planning’ from their vocabularies. Along with Phoenix, it is the essence of auto-dependent sprawl.

In the lower left corner we find European cities, marked by high population density and robust public transport. To be fair, Europe’s land-use patterns reflect historical politics and economics developed long before the advent of the car—indeed, during the age of kings and queens and fuedalism. In America the car dictated land use.

Now that the housing bubble has collapsed and municipal governments struggle to pay bills, what’s to happen with suburbs? Christopher Leinberger, professor of urban and regional planning at the University of Michigan, believes that suburbia is dying, if not already dead. Writing for the New York Times, Leinberger tells us:

DRIVE through any number of outer-ring suburbs in America, and you’ll see boarded-up and vacant strip malls, surrounded by vast seas of empty parking spaces. These forlorn monuments to the real estate crash are not going to come back to life, even when the economy recovers. And that’s because the demand for the housing that once supported commercial activity in many exurbs isn’t coming back, either.

The truth of the last sentence must still be proved. Americans have been in thrall to their Dream since the end of WWII. It remains to be seen if the housing bubble collapse permanently tossed the Dream into history’s dustbin.

Leinberger avers:

The shift is durable and lasting because of a major demographic event: the convergence of the two largest generations in American history, the baby boomers (born between 1946 and 1964) and the millennials (born between 1979 and 1996), which today represent half of the total population.

Many boomers are now empty nesters and approaching retirement. Generally this means that they will downsize their housing in the near future. Boomers want to live in a walkable urban downtown, a suburban town center or a small town, according to a recent survey by the National Association of Realtors. [My wife and I fit this profile.]

The millennials are just now beginning to emerge from the nest — at least those who can afford to live on their own. This coming-of-age cohort also favors urban downtowns and suburban town centers — for lifestyle reasons and the convenience of not having to own cars.

He reports that only 12 percent of future homebuyers favor the conventional suburbs; most prefer higher-density urban centers. Perhaps the trend is toward a Europeanization of America: moving closer together, aided and abetted by attractive amenities and public transportation, including fixed rail. Leinberger cites Portland, Seattle, and several other cities that are in the midst of such transformation.

Yet, we can appreciate that old ways die hard. Suburbia attracts because it is relatively inexpensive, at least initially. The dirt under the houses is typically converted farmland, requiring little preparation for foundations, lumber, sidewalks, and streets. The houses themselves, as Leinberger affirms, are poorly constructed (as in fast as possible) out of cheap materials. Heretofore low gasoline prices, ready credit, and government-funded roadways have also conspired to make the suburban house affordable.

The embedded costs, we should understand, have been artificially constrained by governments at all levels. The price we pay at the pump does not include the cost of war or damage to the environment. The roads upon which we drive have long been subsidized. Mortgage interest deductions, uncommon around the globe, encourage home ownership, as do low-credit thresholds and government-backed loans. These homeowner enticements may not survive the current economic doldrums.

If we agreed that the suburbs have met a deserving death, what might we do to facilitate, if not accelerate, the transition from suburbia to urbanity? Another professor, Louise A. Mozingo, suggests we focus on offices. Also appearing in the same issue of the Times, Mozino writes:

Rethinking sprawl might begin much more effectively with these business enclaves [i.e., suburban offices, mostly of the expansive campus variety]. They cover vast areas and are occupied by a few powerful entities, corporations, which at some point will begin spending their ample reserves to upgrade, expand or replace their facilities.

Here, too, we find massive subsidization. Local governments, anxious to attract new business, routinely grant tax exemptions, modify zoning, and build connecting roads. Employees of these suburban offices usually pay nothing for parking, which further encourages the use of automobiles and consumption of gasoline—contributing to greenhouse gas emissions. But subsidies, however noble in intent, impose costs on others, as I’ve discussed above.

Mozingo concludes:

All three steps — a halt to agricultural land conversion, connecting dispersed employment centers with alternative transit, and encouraging downtown development — are needed to create renewed, civic-minded corporate workplaces and, in the process, move toward sustainable cities. Even leaving aside climate change, very soon the price of energy will make the dispersed, unconnected, low-density city-building pattern impossibly costly. Those jurisdictions and businesses that first create livable, workable, post-peak-oil metropolitan regions are the ones that will win the future.

We’ll see.

About that spending

November 25, 2011 by davald

That our economy requires people to spend lots of money is both a truism and ultimately self-defeating. Spending is part of an overall process the planet cannot sustain.

The problem relates to the amount and extraction of raw materials, pollution-causing manufacturing, and ultimately disposal of non-biogradeable stuff—garbage that sticks around for hundreds if not thousands of years. The more things produced, the greater the waste.

In between, of course, there is all that energy needed at almost every stage of the process. And insofar as we continue to buy and use motor vehicles, we’ll continue to burn billions of tons of carbon. Even our industrial agricultural practices rely heavily on fossil fuels.

While the short-term solution to economic recovery demands that we somehow increase our spending, the long-term viability of earth and its inhabitants necessitates the reverse, much less spending and perhaps more saving. Otherwise we’ll destroy the only planet we have.

What might such an economy look like, one that relied far less on consumption and the rest of the aforementioned process? Would it necessarily entail many steps backward, if you will, with people ultimately shunning cars, electronics, and modern appliances? Would we have to return to the Stone Age? Your guess, by the way, is as good as mine, probably much better.

You’re probably familiar with the concepts of ecological footprint and carrying capacity. The World Wildlife Fund‘s 2010 report includes this graphic.

As you’ll note we humans exceeded the earth’s carrying capacity way back in the mid-70s. The increase in the total ecological footprint since translates into unaccommodated waste, habitat destruction, species elimination, heavier pollution and ultimately a huge environmental debt to successive generations of people, plants, and animals.

Not to be too depressing, but we’ve headed in the wrong direction fast with scant hope of reversing this process. Even if a critical mass of us finally awoke to the disturbing trend and called for concerted action to arrest the human onslaught, our modern, energy-intensive, consumer-dependent capitalist system precludes responsible collective behavior. We are all creatures of the inner dynamics of a global economy that pushes and pulls us along in the same earth-destroying direction.

We’re up to seven billion now, we humans, and while the rate of population growth has declined, we’re still on track to add another two billion or so by the middle of this century. If everyone of us today lived in the manner of North Americans, say, the human species would need another three or four planets. But as I said above, we have only the one.

There is a significant matter of fairness, then. Even our relative poor have much more than most of the rest of the world. The large majority of the planet’s human population want and deserve a higher standard of living. But clearly this is a zero-sum game, though a very serious one. If the poor of the earth are to have more, the rich must have less.

Yet we know that the opposite is happening. The world’s wealthiest are getting even richer, which means that everyone else must struggle with less.

Is it any wonder there is so much political strife? The egregious inequity between the global Haves and Havenots cannot be sustained as the planet itself risks destruction by its own inhabitants.

However, despite the gloom and doom I’d like to end on a more hopeful note. First, consider this graph, also from the WWF report:

Look at the gray area, which represents carbon emissions. The graph suggests that if we could curb those emissions, we could fall back within the earth’s carrying capacity. Indeed, all of the other measures’ contribution to the expanding ecological footprint has been rarely stable over time. It’s those damn carbon molecules.

As always, it’s a matter of political will. And you know how that’s going.

Okay, not all that hopeful.
