Social media

October 11, 2011 by davald

In my previous life I was social. That is, I was busy being around lots of different people depending on the venue and purpose, from protesting U.S. involvement in Central America and the “Auschwitz of Puget Sound” to helping create liturgies and dancing crazy into the night.

Today, not so much. Indeed, save for my official duties as an elected official and a weekly lunch with a couple of “philosophers” I’m a hermit. Guess what? I like it.

Several months ago it was suggested that I join Facebook. I was reluctant, perhaps mostly because I didn’t understand the phenomenon—and I’m not sure that I do now. I did sign up, then after a short while, disappeared into my blogging and routine, which consists of reading, walking, dieting (Type 2), watching sports and a few TV programs, fixing meals, and cleaning house—but no Facebook. As I said, this suits me fine.

Yesterday Facebook finally agreed to Apple’s terms and conditions, making its app available for iOS. To those living in the darkness, that’s Apple-speak for the operating system that runs the iPhone, iPod Touch, and the iPad. I’m an inveterate gadget guy, possessing both the iPhone and the iPad, so I downloaded the free app, which, to be useful, means I had to rejoin the Facebook community(?).

Truth be told, I’d like to instigate a revolution, as long as I don’t have to get off my butt. Revolutions require a critical mass of people dedicated to change. Social networking, we’ve discovered with increased frequency, can help. Indeed, it may be crucial to organizing, delivering critical information to the masses. Occupy Wall Street. Battle Seattle. Overthrow dictators. Elect your favorite politician. Social media makes shit happen, or so it seems.

So I’ve rejoined Facebook, as much to try out the new app, as to start that revolution.

Now, everybody, raise your right fist. Higher.

Inevitable, yes, but…

October 6, 2011 by davald

…still a shock. Steve Jobs is dead.

I was sitting in front of the television watching the Phillies-Cardinals game with my iPad in my lap. A notice from MacWorld flashed on the display: “Steve Jobs has died.” I immediately went to the NY Times website. There it was under “breaking news.” Then to Apple’s website. Simple. Elegant. Final.

I had intended this morning to write about my memories of baseball. I’ll leave that to another day. What better occasion, now that Steve has succumbed to “Life’s best invention,” to share my Apple experiences, of which there are plenty.

It was in Bellevue that I first encountered an Apple product—the IIe. Clunky by today’s standards, but more polished than anything else in the storeroom. I was the owner of a dual-floppy (and I do mean floppy) CP/M machine, complete with a keyboard and a black-and-white monitor. Put in the system floppy. Voila, the ‘A’ prompt. Henceforth, nothing but keystrokes. Yet I did not purchase the IIe; a bit too expensive for me back then.

Fast forward to 1984. I’m in the Everett Mall, which then had a computer store. There it was: the Macintosh. You all remember the commercial directed by Ridley Scott. The 1984 Super Bowl brought the computer for the rest of us. I could not wait to get up close and personal. There on that tiny 9-inch screen was Microsoft Word. That’s right. Gates et al wrote for Apple before the PC. And it had a mouse! Type a few lines. Italicize this. Put these letters in bold. Copy, drag, and paste. Wow!

I finally bought my first Apple product two years later—the Macintosh Plus. I added a LaserWriter printer. Lots of money. But, hey, I was going into desktop publishing. Besides, I could write off the products as a business expense, which I did—legitimately. However, it was frustratingly difficult to draw accident scenes on it. (I consulted forensically on traffic accident reconstruction.) Slow with that tiny screen. But the printed product, after much painstaking work, looked neat, clean, and more professional than drafting old school, even if I did have to tape several letter-sized pages together. Also, I sold my IBM Selectric, since I could now do my reports and invoices on the Mac and LaserWriter. Heaven.

I have not stopped buying Apple products since that first Macintosh purchase. Here’s a list, which may not be exhaustive:

  • Macintosh II
  • PowerBook
  • Macintosh IIci
  • iMac (turquoise)
  • Newton
  • G4 desktop (PowerPC)
  • G5 desktop (PowerPC)
  • G4 laptop
  • MacPro (current)
  • MacBook Pro (passed on to my son)
  • iPad (first edition)
  • iPad 2
  • MacBook (for teacher-wife)
  • MacBook Air (for teacher-wife)
  • Time Capsule
  • Apple TV (1st and second editions)
  • iPod (80 GB)
  • iPod nano (for teacher-wife)
  • iPhone 3
  • iPhone 3s (for teacher-wife)
  • iPhone 4

(Note: If any of these get stolen, proof of my inventory is now in the clouds.)

During Apple’s dark days, between Steve Jobs’s tenures, the Marysville School District, where my wife teaches, decided to abandon Macs in favor of Wintel machines. I spoke against it at a public meeting, no doubt ineffectively, since passion ruled my mind. I warned the district that it would soon see a bloated IT staff and budget with technical problems that would overwhelm support personnel. Besides, I said, the cheap PCs would likely crash, break down, or just stop working altogether. I was right. To this day, unfortunately, the district has stuck with its unworkable and unmanageable PC-cum-Windows (still using XP) system, and teachers have to wait months for fixes. How much better for students and teachers alike if they could use MacBook Airs, iPads, Apple TVs connected to widescreen televisions—with everything connected wirelessly.

The PUD also jettisoned its mostly-Mac installed base a year or so before the school district. That decision—which probably made sense at the time given Apple’s downward trajectory—also led to much higher IT costs and a larger support staff. The utility is still using Windows XP. It’s familiar and most of the bugs have been worked out. There are a few isolated Macs in the communications department, which employs graphic designers.

Jobs never pushed his products to the enterprise. Instead, they are serving as Trojan Horses. Employees buy Apple products for their own use, then bring them to work. If they’re high enough in the food chain, they insist on IT’s accommodating their iPhones and iPads. Gradually, corporations are becoming more Apple friendly.

This includes the PUD, which is set to adopt iPhones rather than Android devices; iOS is more secure. This past week the board witnessed a demonstration of iPads, which will eventually be used by commissioners and upper management in lieu of printed binders. Of course, I was all over this, although my colleagues are a bit wary. The Trojan Horse has entered.

Apple products just work, an expression I’m sure that you’ve heard more than once. All of my old devices have either been sold or handed down to family and friends. They have not been tossed, because they still function—although I don’t remember what happened to the Newton.

While I am terribly saddened by the news of Jobs’s demise, I think Apple is just starting. While it’s the most valuable company on the planet, it will continue to be insanely great. Just look at all the patents the company has either registered or secured.

We ain’t seen nothing yet.

In the minority, and proudly so

September 22, 2011 by davald

We appear to be a nation infected with bloodlust. According to the polls, nearly two-thirds of us support the death penalty, despite increasing evidence that its application is race-biased and fraught with investigative and evidentiary mistakes.

No matter. Georgia killed Troy Davis last night.

Was he innocent? He says so. The off-duty police officer he allegedly shot was killed by bullets from a gun Davis did not own. Seven of nine witnesses to the incident recanted their statements. One confessed to the killing.

No matter. Georgia killed Troy Davis last night.

The pope said no. Harry Belafonte said no. Sister Helen Prejean (“Dead Man Walking”) said no. Former FBI director William Sessions said no. Congressman John Lewis said no. Amnesty International said no. Jimmy Carter said no. Desmond Tutu said no. Members of the European Parliament said no.

No matter. Georgia killed Troy Davis last night.

Witnesses said that they had been coerced by the police into giving false testimony.

No matter. Georgia killed Troy Davis last night.

I am an unequivocal opponent of capital punishment.

No matter. Georgia killed Troy Davis last night.

Kill, even if you’re wrong

September 21, 2011 by davald

Troy Davis, having exhausted his options, is scheduled to die today in Georgia. Many prominent people here and around the globe have called for clemency. But, hey, this is the South, and southerners, especially, like to kill people, even if it’s a mistake.

The editors of the New York Times call the execution “a grievous wrong.” Why? Let us count the ways, taken from the editorial:

Seven of nine witnesses against Mr. Davis recanted after trial. Six said the police threatened them if they did not identify Mr. Davis. The man who first told the police that Mr. Davis was the shooter later confessed to the crime. There are other reasons to doubt Mr. Davis’s guilt: There was no physical evidence linking him to the crime introduced at trial, and new ballistics evidence broke the link between him and a previous shooting that provided the motive for his conviction.

Yet, there’s nothing like a good old-fashioned state murder. Why let these silly facts get in the way?

Say, you gonna die?

September 15, 2011 by davald

Well, of course. We’re all going to die—someday.

I try not to think about it, because death has reared its ugly head on several occasions, from grandparents to siblings and in between; and I didn’t care for those experiences very much. Yet, the philosopher in me used to wonder about life’s meaning, as if the ego of one pervaded the ego of all. Hey, we’re “God’s children.” Right?

I eventually abandoned the arrogance of such an enterprise, concluding that life, yours or mine, has no more meaning than a rock’s. I don’t feel lessened by that epiphany. It’s rather liberating, actually.

Roger Ebert is a much different man than he was while working with Gene Siskel. The thumbs still work. But cancer has stolen much of his jaw and below. He can only write, which he’s done well and amply for several decades.

His doctors had all but given up on him during one of his circulation-related episodes. His wife, however, knew he was still “there.” Evidently the physicians were persuaded. Ebert lived…

…to write his memoir, for one. An excerpt appears in Salon magazine. A worthy read, I say. For example:

What I expect to happen is that my body will fail, my mind will cease to function and that will be that. My genes will not live on, because I have had no children. I am comforted by Richard Dawkins’ theory of memes. Those are mental units: thoughts, ideas, gestures, notions, songs, beliefs, rhymes, ideals, teachings, sayings, phrases, clichés that move from mind to mind as genes move from body to body. After a lifetime of writing, teaching, broadcasting and telling too many jokes, I will leave behind more memes than many. They will all also eventually die, but so it goes.

O’Rourke’s had a photograph of Brendan Behan on the wall, and under it this quotation, which I memorized:

I respect kindness in human beings first of all, and kindness to animals. I don’t respect the law; I have a total irreverence for anything connected with society except that which makes the roads safer, the beer stronger, the food cheaper and the old men and old women warmer in the winter and happier in the summer.

You could do worse.

The Great Divergence and the withering of empathy

September 13, 2011 by davald

We are, of course, entitled to our own opinions. Some might go further to argue that we’re equally entitled to our own facts, which belies ignorance of the the term ‘fact.’ However, it’s always been the case, and seemingly more pronounced in these times, that passions rule reason, as Hume suggested. In other words, what we may lack between our ears is more than offset by the quantity of our guts.

In response to my recent post on “So Why is America So Great?,” a reader offered these words:

Okay, best national defense in the history of earth.  That’s a plus, though it is a bloated bureaucracy.

This is obfuscation.  The issue is medical care, not health care.  There’s more MRI machines in Philadelphia than in all of Canada and England . . . combined.

The richest (owned houses, cars tv’s, cable, autos, electronics, health) and fattest “poor” in the history of humankind, and with a standard of living higher than the average (not “poor”) person in, say, Paris or Rome or Madrid.

The rich keep doing what made them rich and the poor (the few who remain such for as much as a decade) keep doing what made them poor.

My congratulations and admiration to the billionaires.  keep up the good work.

I really don’t know what to make of this. But it’s clear that the writer and I live on different moral planets. It also seems as if presidential candidate Ron Paul lives on the same planet as the commenter.

Last night emcee Wolf Blitzer asked the congressman, “Are you saying that society should just let him [a quite sick man near death who opted not to buy private health insurance] die?” Several members of the audience yelled out, “Yes!”

Despite having studied several years’ worth of philosophy, I would not expect to put forth an argument that would persuade Mr. Paul and the commenter to change their minds. Rather, I suspect that we either have empathy or we don’t. It’s not so much a matter of the gut as a function of two other factors: evolutionary biology and early childhood development.

I read the Wikipedia entry on empathic concern. Consider this excerpt from the article:

Human beings are strongly motivated to be connected to others.[4] In humans and higher mammals, an impulse to care for offspring is almost certainly genetically hard-wired, although modifiable by circumstance.

To be fair to Congressman Paul, in answering the question he recounted his professional medical practice and charitable works. He implied that he cares about others. But he also said something to the effect that freedom means assuming personal responsibility for your own welfare and not relying on “socialism.”

One common thread that runs through all of the Republican candidates for president is abhorrence for governmental programs, especially public social programs like Medicare, “Obamacare,” and even Social Security, although there seems to be some retrenchment from the latter after Texas governor Rick Perry called it a “lie” and “a Ponzi scheme.” And while Ron Paul is decidedly libertarian in his views, which places him a bit more out there than others in his cohort, he believes with his competitors that government should keep its hands off the private sector, which brings me back to my commenter and his praise of billionaires.

I happen to think that highly unequal societies—and the U.S. is at or near the top of this category—are unhealthy societies. There is considerable research to support this view. At the forefront of this research is Prof. Richard Wilkinson of the UK. With Kate Pickett, a professor of epidemiology, he has written his latest book, entitled The Spirit Level: Why Greater Equality Makes Societies Stronger. Here’s an excerpt from the dust jacket:

Further, more unequal societies are bad for everyone within them—the rich and middle class as well as the poor.

Interesting. As it happens, inequality is bad for your health, even Mr. Paul’s and Mr. Buffett’s.

Wilkinson and Pickett show that inequality tracks with the following syndromes:

  • obesity rates
  • morbidity rates
  • mortality rates
  • and a host of psychological diseases.

Consistent readers of my posts know that I focus on inequality. For example, I recently wrote about what I call the ‘Great Divergence.’ It began in the 7os and has continued unabated, enlarging the gap between the extremely rich and the Rest of Us. (Coincidentally, the Washington Post has begun a series on inequality, replete with charts and graphs.)

I am deeply disturbed by the brazen selfishness of the Tea Party, as evinced by members of the debate audience. Likewise, all of the Republican presidential hopefuls exhibit what I believe to be a perverse disregard for the wellbeing of others. We will recall that at the previous Republican debate some in the audience cheered governor Perry’s unseemly record of state executions.

Robert Reich, now teaching at my alma mater, talks about America being two societies. One of the very rich and the other populated by the Rest of Us. (He also wrote the foreword to the Wilkinson book.) The Great Divergence may have already reached a point of no return. The very rich and their odd supporters have become increasingly inured to the misfortune of others, no doubt for a very simple reason: they have no exposure to the Rest of Us. There is no overlapping of these disparate societies. Yes, we know of the rich. We see them on TV and in magazines. But they do not, perhaps cannot, know us.

And that’s a problem, because the rich have all the money, and money translates into political power, which is put to the further service of the rich, and so on. Yet, rather than inducing a revolution we get “admiration.”

I don’t get it.

Reflections on 9/11

September 12, 2011 by davald

Over the past few years I’ve received three frantic phone calls from my daughter. The first came at the end of her initial day at a new summer job. As I recall she said, in a frightened voice, “What’s going on here?” This, we all learned, was the beginning of the Battle of Seattle. The second phone call had a similar refrain. She was in New York City for another summer job. The electricity had gone out in Manhattan. This was the East Coast Blackout.

Ten years ago she called the third time. Again, the same question, but with a directive, “Turn on the TV.” The first plane had struck a tower. The second came shortly thereafter. This was, of course, “9/11.”

Almost 3,000 people lost their lives that fateful morning, a crisp, clear day. Their deaths would be avenged with at least one order of magnitude more casualties. The wars of Iraq and Afghanistan, launched in retaliation, have caused death and destruction far beyond the scope of 9/11.

A couple of comparisons may be in order:

  • Between 1993 and the end of 2009, over 590,000 people were killed in automobile accidents.
  • There are at least 8,000 gun-caused homicides each year in the U.S.

I suspect that most of the victims of car wrecks and gun shots are mourned by relatives and close friends. A death, however suffered, is a tragedy, inducing sorrow and sympathy. Likewise for those who lost loved ones in the 9/11 attacks.

Yet, what is it about the deaths of 9/11 that are so different from roadway fatalities or homicides by firearm? It can’t be the volume, since the 9/11 lives are but a tenth of annual highway slaughter. Over 4,400 soldiers have died in Iraq and another 1,700 in Afghanistan, according to the Department of Defense. Combined, the wars have cost more American lives than the heinous attacks of 9/11. Is this a fair exchange?

Could the country’s reaction to 9/11 have much to do with the manner of killing? Probably, I’d say.

Attacks by foreign agents on U.S. personnel have regularly led to war. “Remember the Maine!” The sinking of the Lusitania provided impetus for America’s entry into WWI. When the Japanese attacked Pearl Harbor, Congress immediately voted a declaration of war. The Gulf of Tonkin incident, which we have since learned never happened, became a pretext for war in Southeast Asia. Two commandeered commercial jets penetrate the twin towers of the World Trade Center, causing a subsequent collapse and the deaths of almost 3,000 people. President G.W. Bush attacks Iraq, which had nothing to do with 9/11.

Paul Krugman’s recent comments on the last point have created a storm of vitriol in the blogosphere. He writes:

The memory of 9/11 has been irrevocably poisoned; it has become an occasion for shame. And in its heart, the nation knows it.

The shame, he explains, is about this:

The atrocity should have been a unifying event, but instead it became a wedge issue. Fake heroes like Bernie Kerik, Rudy Giuliani, and, yes, George W. Bush raced to cash in on the horror. And then the attack was used to justify an unrelated war the neocons wanted to fight, for all the wrong reasons.

I concur.

Also, I hope to never receive another frantic phone call from my just-wed daughter, unless it has to do with the imminent arrival of a grandchild.

Killing as if there were no tomorrow

September 8, 2011 by davald

I did not watch last night’s Republican debate. No surprise here to readers of this blog. But I did catch Glenn Greenwald’s column just now.

NBC’s Brian Williams asked Texas governor Rick Perry about his presiding over the highest number of state executions in U.S. history. There have been 234 under his watch. As Greenwald reports:

…the mere mention by Williams of this morose record triggered an outburst of cheering and applause from the audience.

Greenwald, as do I, thought this disgusting and “base.” But, again, no surprise. This was a GOP audience, after all.

Many of us, though not this writer, also openly cheered the assassination of bin Laden. Initial reports were chaotic and varied, beginning with an early statement to the effect that bin Laden’s compound was heavily fortified and that his body guards, and perhaps even his wife, opened fire on the approaching SEALS. We now know that the assailants faced no opposition whatsoever, and the directive from on high was to shoot and kill at first sighting. We also know that bin Laden’s body was swiftly buried at sea.

Although bin Laden had quickly claimed responsibility for the 9/11 attacks, the evidence is hardly conclusive. Yet, whoever was responsible for the heinous crimes should have been arrested and prosecuted. His assassination precluded such procedures.

What might a proper trial of bin Laden revealed? We can only speculate, of course. My mind conjures a couple of possibilities:

  1. that bin Laden was not the perpetrator in chief; someone else was responsible;
  2. that bin Laden was the mastermind but had assistance from foreign governments with which we are “friendly.”

It’s no secret that al Qaida has received huge sums from Saudi Arabia, one of the “friendly” nations. I suspect that had bin Laden been apprehended alive and bound for trial, his attorneys would have had the resources to mount a significant defense, including, we can imagine, collecting reams of official documents from the U.S. and its “coalition partners.” There would have been sharp cross examinations of prosecution witnesses. Rather soon in the proceedings a great deal of information would have been revealed, no doubt much of it the governments would prefer not see the light of day.

Let’s say that it would have been awkward.

As I was saying…

September 7, 2011 by davald

Few believe that the Republicans will endorse any major initiative that Obama may propose tomorrow night. Indeed, Mitch McConnell said as much. The GOP rightly concludes that their chances of improving their numbers, including the cherished one in the Oval Office, depend inversely on the economy’s health. As the unemployment rate remains stubbornly high and as marginalized individuals go off the rails, next November’s electoral prospects loom large for Republicans. Never mind that they are chiefly responsible for the mess.

And I think they know that. It even occurred to me that Boehner, and McConnell, and Cantor actually understand Keynesian economics, or at least that point about massive government spending being the only way to recovery under these extraordinarily dismal conditions. In this cynical, albeit speculative, interpretation of conservative groupthink among congressional leaders, Boehner et al. will do all that they can to deny the American people what they so desperately need—which are mostly jobs and financial security.

Mike Lofgren used to drink this vile Kool-Aid, but he evidently lost his taste, having “left the cult.” From his former insider’s perspective, Lofgren lists three principal tenets of today’s GOP:

  1. The GOP cares solely and exclusively about its rich contributors.
  2. They worship at the altar of Mars.
  3. Give me that old time religion.

The first requires no explanation or elaboration. This has always described the Republican Party.

The second, of course, warrants clarification. Lofgren is talking about the Roman god of war. Republicans, he says, have never met a country they wouldn’t invade.

However, I think the worshiping here is bipartisan. The Democrats have had their share of presidential warmongers, among them: Wilson, Truman, Kennedy, Johnson, Carter, Clinton, and now Obama. Our own Henry M. Jackson is at the top of the congressional list. The Pentagon loves both political parties.

As to the last point, it’s become clear that the Republican Party needs the Christian fundamentalists. They are the most feral, loyal, and boisterous Americans. Moreover, along with the Tea Party, they are a most gullible collection—the GOP, with the able assistance of Fox News, Rush Limbaugh, and Matt Drudge, can tell these people anything and they’ll believe it. Global warming is a hoax. Old people don’t need Social Security or Medicare. The unemployed would prefer not to have a job. People of color are dumber than whites. Obama is a socialist. Unions are responsible for the recession. The list goes on.
