Quality of life

March 24, 2014 by davald

The Everett Herald‘s editors opined on the county’s quality of life, or, more accurately, the lack thereof. The paper cited economists at a recent event sponsored by the Economic Alliance of Snohomish County. They were asked how Everett and the area might reduce the risks of another recession. “Be a place where people want to live,” reported the editors.

Downtown Everett, which should be such a place, appears to be struggling with its identity. Historically supported by maritime interests along the port, including a series of pulp and paper mills, their demise presents new and potentially interesting challenges for the powers that be. What to do?

There are always those who believe that “the market” will and should dictate the appropriate answer. But laissez-affaire probably got us into the Great Recession and inhibits economic recovery. The so-called “job creators” have only enlarged their bank accounts while leaving the Rest of Us to struggle with uncertainty, low wages, and diminished expectations. Why should a hands-off attitude work here?

A proper vision may be necessary but hardly sufficient to build a place where people want to live. No matter, there are absolutely no visible signs that public officials and business leaders care a whit about attracting people to downtown and keeping them here. Yes, there are new apartment buildings and more to come, but they are neither attractive nor part of any integrated pattern of development that promises economic vitality.

My daughter and her husband live on Capitol Hill. They are just a few blocks from everything they need, including grocery stores and upscale restaurants. Public transportation can be had at the closest corner. Their combined incomes afford them a not-so-cheap home and walkable access to amenities that have so far eluded denizens of Everett.

Okay, you may not wish to buy in Seattle. After all, prices are much higher than here. How about renting?

As it happens, my wife and I looked briefly at that possibility. Would you believe $2,700 for a two-bedroom unit? Yikes.

Rents, like house prices, are subject to market forces. Property owners can extract such sums because demand is sufficiently high. And why is demand higher in Seattle than Everett? Because more people want to live there.

Seattle escaped the Great Recession relatively unscathed. Its unemployment rate is lower than the state’s and much lower than the that of the nation as a whole.

Seattle itself experienced 4.8 percent unemployment as of January 2014. More likely than not, those who live in Seattle work in Seattle. Also, their incomes tend to be higher than Everett’s households. The combination of work, play, and home encourages dollars to exchange hands with greater frequency than in less affluent areas like Everett.

The Herald‘s editors:

In an era when the best paying jobs are going to scientists and computer whizzes, a community needs to invest in the things that attract not only those kinds of businesses, but also those kinds of workers. Recreation and entertainment “infrastructure” can be as important as transportation or communications infrastructure. Excellent schools and libraries can be as great an economic boon as a streamlined regulatory or tax system.

County leaders should take the lesson to heart. Quality of life is not a mere byproduct of prosperity ­­— it can be an important catalyst for it.

Still, what to do, and who goes first?

I believe that government officials, including mayors and city council members, should pay attention to the built environment. How things look and fit with each other attracts would-be residents.

Yet, design standards seem nonexistent in Everett’s code. A search of “setbacks” for example in the city’s online document entitled “Design and Construction Standards and Specifications” came up empty. Setbacks, as any urban designer will tell you, are important for pedestrians and commerce. Now let’s take a look at a new apartment building constructed along Pacific Avenue, one of the city’s major arterials.

While the sidewalk is wide and the building entrances recessed from the front, there is no buffer between the heavily travelled street and sidewalk. The same property owner is erecting a massive set of buildings a couple of blocks away. They, too, will lack buffers.

Pacific Avenue consists of two travel lanes and a left-turn lane in each direction. It was not designed with pedestrians in mind, though in 2006 the city adopted a plan (pdf) to one day convert the arterial into a “boulevard.” The document is eight years old, and there is no evidence of progress toward its realization.

To be sure, the “Downtown Plan” is littered with pleasant words. Take, for instance, the document’s “goals and objectives”:

To be fair, I absolutely love the plan. It’s clear that those who fashioned it know that Everett needs a lot of work and that they have good ideas on how to proceed.  Yet, I fear that the document sits somewhere on a shelf. Remember that the plan is almost a decade old. The final section is about implementation. Here is “Step 2. Plant a Seed”:

Initiate a High-Visibility, Transformational Project

(Begin planning within one year; complete within 3-4 years) [my emphasis]

The purpose of this action is to show significant City commitment to downtown with a project that will foster substantial development and/or add a new dimension to downtown activities. Two recommended projects meet these criteria: a major streetscape improvement of Rucker Avenue (Action S-2) and the development of a multipurpose focal park or plaza (Action O-3). Since the Rucker Avenue improvements are directed toward fostering a new in-city residential neighborhood, this project might be timed to coincide with substantial mixed-use residential development along that street. The City might begin planning and design of the street and commit construction funds when the private investment occurs. Creating a unifying central park or plaza is directed at adding a whole new set of activities, recreational attractions, and business opportunities in downtown. The construction of the new park should be complemented by joint City and private efforts to program events for optimizing its use and to address security and maintenance needs. The success of downtown parks is as dependent on good management as it is on good design.

Here’s a photograph of Rucker, that I recently took.

 

The city is way behind.

Enthusiastic ignorance

March 24, 2014 by davald

As I write I’m listening to Herbert van Karajan and the Berlin Philharmonic. It’s a recording of Tchaikovsky’s Sixth Symphony, Pathétique. This piece has everything to do with the title of my post.

A few years ago I read an essay by Alex Ross, who writes for The New Yorker. His topic was audience applause. I’ll quote:

Up until the beginning of the twentieth century, applause between movements and even during movements was the sign of a knowledgeable, appreciative audience, not of an ignorant one. The biographies of major composers are full of happy reports of what would now be seen as wildly inappropriate applause.

Before our grandson was born (he just turned one), my wife and I would treat our family to a Christmas-time evening, first at the Wild Ginger restaurant then at Benaroya Hall across the street. At one of the performances we heard pianist Olli Mustonen perform Prokofiev’s Third Piano Concerto. We were seated on the left just a few rows back from the stage. I was somewhat familiar with the piece, but my wife had never heard it. The first movement is a doozy, building then retreating again and again until the music reaches a climax, really. Exciting stuff. Well, my wife clapped. Unfortunately, she was the only one in the hall to do so, and her reaction brought a stern glare from conductor Gerard Schwarz.

Yesterday my wife and I attended the symphony, this time to hear Tchaikovsky’s Pathétique. It’s been a while since I played the piece, but the theme in the opening movement is instantly recognizable. Well, I lost track of the progression. The third movement, like the Prokofiev first, ends with what seems to be a dramatic finale. A considerable number of attendees burst into applause and hurrahs. So did I. Whoops.

Maestro Ludovic Morlot was not finished. Nor was the orchestra. They proceeded into the fourth and final movement above the enthusiastic ignorance, of which I was a part.

“Now we’re even,” said my wife.

Why Republicans?

March 18, 2014 by davald

My wife and I got around to watching the movie 12 Years a Slave. While I was under no illusions about the savagery and utter inhumanity of that “peculiar institution,” the movie lays bare both the moral depravity of physically and emotionally abusing blacks and the pervasive air of superiority adopted and sustained by white gentry. The picture serves as a stark reminder of just how cruel our species can be.

I find no accident in the stranglehold of conservative ideology and practice in the South, the most persistent venue of slavery. The politicians who wield power there manifest the behaviors and attitudes of their forebears, though the whippings and lynchings have abated. What’s going on in the South is but the latest mark on the long continuum of man’s inhumanity to man.

Of course, the pernicious sentiments are not wholly confined to the southern region. Northerners exhibit similar cruelties, as much to themselves as to others deemed unworthy of compassion and rightful consideration. (I have in mind Thomas Franks’s Whats the Matter With Kansas?)

Writing for the New York Times, Timothy Egan captures the more modern expression of depraved indifference in the person of Paul Ryan, a nominal Catholic of Irish descent who appears to have eschewed the New Testament in favor of the myriad rationalizations of cruelty found in the more ancient texts—one, by the way, employed by Michael Fassbender’s character in the aforementioned movie to justify slavery. Egan compares Ryan’s (and Romney’s) assaults on the poor to England’s Sir Charles Trevelyan, the public official charged with administering relief during the Irish famine. From Wikipedia’s entry:

During the height of the famine it is suggested that Trevelyan deliberately dragged his feet in disbursing direct government food and monetary aid to the Irish. In a letter to Thomas Spring-Rice, Lord Mounteagle, he described the famine as an “effective mechanism for reducing surplus population” as well as “the judgement of God”.

Egan quotes Ryan:

“We have this tailspin of culture, in our inner cities in particular, of men not working and just generations of men not even thinking about working or learning the value and the culture of work.”

Tevelyan had said in regards to the starving Irish:

“Dependence on charity is not to be made an agreeable mode of life.”

So he let a million Irish die rather than feed them. The Irish were to the British what enslaved blacks were to southern white plutocrats, with one exception: slaves were property and no good to their owners if dead.

We are a decidedly mean breed, we humans. An entire political party gives evidence of embracing the devil within us, apparently believing that caring for others is not only ideologically forbidden but itself an exacerbation of lazy predilections. Where the GOP holds sway meanness abounds in multiple forms, from refusing to expand Medicaid to draconian reductions in social services. In the logic of the conservative, giving hurts.

It may be the case, then, that the Republican Party is the political expression of humanity’s dark side. My question at the outset should be revised: Why are there so many mean people?

Noam Chomsky and Edward S. Herman collaborated on the book Manufacturing Consent. They take us back to the advent of modern advertising as developed by Sigmund Freud’s nephew. He believed and subsequently confirmed that people can be enticed into buying things they would otherwise ignore. Propaganda was a short skip and a jump beyond. The state could, and would, manufacture our consent for war. Public sentiment was changed almost over night from strongly opposed to U.S. entry into World War I to virulently supporting what became a senseless slaughter of millions.

How can we resist the entreaties to buy and fight? Intellectual self-defense, argues Chomsky. We should question motives, look beyond the superficial words and images, and decide what is in our best interests, which are likely different than those of the manufacturers.

While I have often commented on “the relative halcyon days” of the immediate post-war (II) decades, I am only kidding myself. The fifties and sixties were an aberration in economic terms, with inequality at historically low levels. They were also culturally nasty years, when blacks were still openly treated as inferior beings, poverty was severe and made largely invisible to others, and we were just a crazy general removed from the planet’s annihilation.

Perhaps America, in particular, is essentially comprised of mean-spirited people, and that it has always been the liberal’s conceit to believe otherwise. After all, slavery not only took root in fertile political soil, it tenaciously endured, even to its present-day manifestations. (Consider, for example, how many blacks are unemployed, underpaid, and over-represented in our nation’s jails and prisons. That’s no accident.) Almost a quarter of us live below the official poverty line, and a staggering half are either poor or near-poor. Wages for the Rest of Us have not even kept pace with inflation. The entire political system is unwieldy, dysfunctional, and basically corrupt, with the desires of the few outweighing the needs of the many.

We the conceited have every reason to despair. Columnist E.J. Dionne summarizes the dismay as we approach November’s elections:

Listlessness is bad politics. Defensiveness is poor strategy. And resignation is never inspiring.

You can feel elements of all three descending around President Obama as he fends off attack after attack from his conservative foes who vary the subject depending on the day, the circumstance and the opportunity.

As I’ve done here, Dionne points the finger directly into Obama’s chest. The mean people have always hated Obama and everything he does. No surprise they despise Obamacare and will do anything to derail it. Obama contributes to his own political demise. Dionne:

Then the Obama administration threw their adversaries a lifeline with the disasters that befell HealthCare.gov, empowering Republicans to remount their favorite hobbyhorse.

Chomsky, for one, avers that our enthusiasm for political candidates is misplaced, that a truer democracy would be about the Rest of Us, or, to paraphrase Lincoln, be a government of, for, and by the people. I, too, have written about leaders following and the followers leading.

But I’m right now feeling listless, defensive, and resigned. It’s far too easy to whip up the morally depraved into a hateful frenzy. Heck, this lot may be the real Americans. Now, that’s depressing.

Societies of one—random thoughts

January 22, 2014 by davald

It is perhaps in our nature, those who engage the news and politics in general, to find fault with just about everything. The attitude recognizes no partisan boundaries.

The news, of course, is relentless and infinite. As a New York Times subscriber I can simply glance at the digital headlines to see problems everywhere. This morning’s sample attests:

  • Photo Trove Is Said to Show Widespread Torture in Syria

  • States Cutting Weeks of Aid to the Jobless

  • Food Banks Anticipate Impact of Cuts to Food Stamps

  • Former Virginia Governor and His Wife Are Indicted

Ah, but suppose I viewed the pages through different filters. Could someone vitally interested in food, for example, mentally block out the headlines that disturb? The Times has a section devoted to “dining and wine.” It’s chock full of recipes, restaurant reviews, and seemingly endless stories of wines from all over the world. That should keep one busy.

Or, perhaps I’m suddenly obsessed with music of all genres. The Times does not disappoint. The paper has many sections having little, if anything, to do with problems of the world, unless you’re troubled by the antics of Justin Bieber or the string section of the New York Philharmonic.

There are certainly less serious publications with an online presence. The once proud Seattle Post-Intelligencer has transformed itself from a once-competitive newspaper delivered to my door each morning to a portal for voyeurs, featuring constant slideshows of cheerleaders and movie celebrities and weird people doing weird things. Not much there to boil one’s political blood.

I can only imagine that there are people in the world who possess Pollyannaish blinders. Their minds filter out the wrong and let in only the right. If such people exist, they probably don’t subscribe to the New York Times or read other newspapers or consult their local television news.

The Internet has enabled, if not encouraged, the politically ignorant. One can focus on one’s passion or obsession at the exclusion of all else. Certainly the “series of tubes” has changed us.

When I was growing up nearly every doorstep, or porch, or driveway, or front lawn received a daily newspaper tossed with troubled accuracy by a boy on a bicycle. In the Bay Area that was my home households could choose from an array of publications, but almost all chose at least one. In the evening the television was tuned to a national news program delivered by one of three networks; this was long before cable. If I dare say so, the consumption of “the news” provided a largely common experience, the stuff of society.

Now we are divided by ignorance, the default mode, since it’s nearly impossible to find commonality even in the news. Accounts of events differ, depending on ideology. If there was ever objectivity in reporting, it vanished long ago. Today each of us sees the world through blinkered eyes, and my world is not yours.

How, then, to function as a Great Society when we are all societies of one, replete with personal Weltanschauungs and disparate interests marinated in ignorance? Margaret Thatcher may have been right, but sardonically so.

We may thus understand the apparent need of political leaders to embrace ritual and ceremony. Jointly singing the Star-spangled Banner or reciting the Pledge are intended to unite, even if the venues in which they’re practiced are either sports arenas or tiny city council chambers, the former decidedly apolitical and the latter sparsely attended.

Yet, history records grand efforts to corral the masses into giant spaces, from the circuses of Rome and the spectacles of Hitler to modern Olympics and the Super Bowl (Go, Hawks). The noble aspirations of the commonweal give way to the pernicious and trivial.

Ironically, the libertarian impulse for individual freedom can find expression only after concerted efforts by the many to thwart  oppression by a few. Those who rail against actions to keep the rabble in line exhort us to organize, protest, and resist—en masse. It is they who imagine a critical mass of voluntary associations overcoming the power of plutocrats and politicians.

Herein lies a conundrum. How does one achieve such a critical mass amidst societies of one? You may appeal to my moral sensibilities, but if I’m not open to your entreaties because I am otherwise distracted, then your efforts will have been in vain. Now multiply the arrangement by millions and you will readily appreciate the enormity of the task before you. More likely than not, our consent to the status quo has been ably manufactured.

Besides, there is no rabble with which to reckon. The jury remains out on whether or not this is a good thing.

Envisioning the future

January 18, 2014 by davald

I’m a bit of a sci-fi consumer, beginning with the original Star Trek television series in which creator Gene Roddenberry explored a few ethical issues set in space and the distant future. But there were always the gadgets that intrigued. Could we be using those things some day?

Curiosity compels me to keep watching new programs with the same question before me. With evolving computer-generated imagery, we see fanciful technology appear real and, therefore, believable. Why, science should be able to build that now or at least soon.

In a recent episode of Almost Human, whose main character is played by the “Bones” actor in the latest Star Trek movies, some very bad people purloined software that enabled special bullets to unerringly find their intended targets regardless of conditions, terrain, and so on. Scary stuff.

I imagine that we have thousands of scientists working in government-funded research laboratories intent on developing the next big thing in weaponry, surveillance, and more. And if we’re doing it other countries are too.

There’s much discussion these days about the National Security Agency and its massive, intrusive surveillance schemes. Dozens, if not hundreds, of super-computer systems are no doubt chained together to sift through billions and billions of electronic messages. The television series Person of Interest has a giant computer that does precisely that, spitting out “a number” that identifies either a would-be perpetrator or victim of such.

The broad surveillance has been justified time and again as necessary to prevent further acts of terrorism on U.S. soil. But surely it doesn’t stop there.

I suspect, but can no way prove, of course, that much of the security apparatus is devoted to preventing presumably bad people (or governments) from getting their hands on the latest gadgets and technology designed to kill and wreak havoc, from James Bond-like pens to grid-disabling electromagnetic pulses. Meanwhile, mad scientists beaver away inside U.S. facilities to fashion gadgets just like the ones on TV.

Gun culture

January 16, 2014 by davald

The New York Times reports on schools adopting a new culture in response to recent tragedies. There will be more.

For students across the country, lockdowns have become a fixture of the school day, the duck-and-cover drills for a generation growing up in the shadow of Columbine High School in Colorado and Sandy Hook Elementary School in Connecticut. Kindergartners learn to hide quietly behind bookshelves. Teachers warn high-school students that the glow of their cellphones could make them targets. And parents get regular text messages from school officials alerting them to lockdowns.

Since this is my blog, and blogs are all about opinions, I’ll offer mine. I detest guns, find them absolutely unnecessary for “the security of a free state,” and cannot begin to fathom their justification, especially after yet another massacre. Even the slaughter of dozens of young children fails to rein them in. Indeed, in a perverse way, each travesty triggers an increase in gun purchases on the pretext that the government will react to the bloodshed with controls, however minimal and ineffective.

Among the gun nuts is the counterargument that the Swiss have lots of guns, yet they don’t go around shooting one another. So keeping and bearing arms, per se, does not necessarily produce violence and mayhem.

I would suggest that we have an unwell society, unlike the Swiss. Certainly we tolerate pervasive poverty and inequality—absent among the Swiss, whose Gini index (level of income maldistribution) is much lower than ours, 29 to about 47, respectively.

Inequality is important, I believe, because it’s virtually impossible that extreme wealth can peaceably coexist with vast numbers of poor and near-poor. Moreover, the U.S. is a giant country compared to, say, Switzerland, and its huge expanse contributed to America’s well-deserved reputation as a “gunfighter nation.”

Inequality explains almost half the variance. We note that the District of Columbia exhibits the highest inequality and murder rate. It’s also under the jurisdiction of Congress, which has been loath to pass meaningful restrictions on guns.

That said, given the current complexion of the Supreme Court and the considerable influence of the gun lobby I do not see much hope of restrictive legislation in the near future. The violence will continue—along with inequality, about which Congress is also loath to address

Being apolitical

January 13, 2014 by davald

Bad men need nothing more to compass their ends, than that good men should look on and do nothing.

— John Stuart Mill

Is it in my genes? My grandfather was heavily involved in politics from the left, being a strong union man when that meant something. My father followed in his footsteps, belonging to the Oil, Chemical, and Atomic Workers union for most of his adult life. (I recall the dark days of the extended strike at the Richmond, Calif., refinery when belts were severely tightened and food judiciously distributed at the table.) We were, as a breed, Democrats to the bone. The only mention of Republicans was in cursed language. The favored adjectives were “damn” and “bastard.”

With such background I inherited a history of workingman politics, in which the rich were scorned, the poor pitied and assisted, and the Rest of Us struggled to make a better life. It helped that I was raised in California, then under the governorship of the first Brown, where the education system was the envy of the world and good-paying jobs plentiful. I matriculated at Berkeley—tuition free.

To be sure, I did not quickly take to the political life, largely skating through high school and even college, generally oblivious to events around me, save for the Kennedy assassination, which struck me deeply. I pursued baseball, eschewing protests of any kind, though there was surely much to oppose, from overt racial discrimination to the Vietnam War, which I managed to avoid by lottery luck.

I married early, while still a junior at Cal, became a father before I graduated, then set out to earn a living at whatever. I had no plans and nothing, really, to suggest a career, let alone a successful one. Unlike my father and his father before him, the jobs that I landed required a coat and tie rather than overalls and gloves. I did not sweat. Nor did I get dirty, save for whatever food and drink may have dribbled onto my clothing during lunchtime.

But I eventually became political. When? I would say that it happened the moment that I decided to form a union at Pemco Insurance.

That was a fool’s errand back in 1977-78. The CEO at the time, Stanley O. McNaughton, would often tell the employees that capitalism was the “greatest human invention.” He detested unions. But I naively soldiered on, gradually gaining reluctant adherents to “the cause.” In the end, after months of frustrating efforts to win the hearts and minds of a majority, we canceled the vote, realizing that we potential unionists would lose big time. Within the year I was gone. Yet now I was hooked. Damn.

The title of this post is “being apolitical.” Let’s say that I’m imagining what it might be like to fit the description.

Now I imagine that one can be apolitical (as in not paying all that much attention to current events and governments at any level) because one is otherwise engaged in something really interesting that consumes all one’s waking hours. Being a musical director of a major symphony orchestra comes to mind.

Then, I suppose, there are millions of us who just don’t give a darn about who wins elections or what the legislature or city council is up to. You may believe that you can go about your everyday activities seemingly untouched by political acts. Your pursuits may be trivial or profound leaving scarce minutes to do anything else, including casting your ballot.

Many of us are likely cynical of politics in general. This article in the New York Times triggered my post. It’s about buying elections. Shocked? Well, we shouldn’t be. Yet, the amount of money and the degree of sophistication involved in skirting campaign finance laws, however toothless, could make cynics of us all.

Exploiting a loophole in the state law and a network of political action committees in Alabama and Washington, Mr. [Mike] Hubbard [head of the Alabama Republican party] shuffled hundreds of thousands of out-of-state dollars into the Republican organization in Alabama, vastly outraising the state Democratic Party. On Election Day, Republicans won majorities in both the State Senate and House of Representatives for the first time since Reconstruction — and Alabama joined the rapidly growing fraternity of states where government is controlled by a single political party, now the largest it has been in more than half a century.

Alabama’s transformation was the product, in part, of a sophisticated political apparatus designed to channel political money from around the country into states where conditions were ripe for Republican takeover. In 2010, the effort achieved striking success, moving a dozen states to sole Republican control, including presidential swing states like Ohio and Pennsylvania.

Oh, and you contributed $25 to your favorite candidate expecting to make a difference? Good luck with that.

So, why not simply turn off the political faucet, the one that gushes out at me every day? I could play computer games or watch reality TV or read the classics, start a hobby—do anything but let the cesspool of politics disturb my life, liberty, and pursuit of happiness.

Well, there are a couple of nagging considerations that prevent me from doing so. First, I am, by definition, a politician. I hold elective office, and it’s a responsibility I take seriously. Besides, in my tenure I believe that I have made a positive difference for the community.

Second, politics is really inescapable. We may choose to ignore what government officials do, but their impacts can be huge, from how much we pay in taxes and to what ends, to whether or not we kill thousands of civilians in foreign lands, to the degree of suffering visited on citizens existing at the margins.

Politics, then, is ultimately a moral endeavor. Those we elect (with or without our vote) can do good or evil. That’s why it matters.
