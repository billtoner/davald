Following clues that lead to nowhere

January 23, 2012 by davald

I avidly followed the TV series Lost, which I found engrossing, compelling, and at times riveting. Its multiple layers of intrigue, science fiction, and impenetrable mystery belied a seemingly simple storyline: a plane crashes somewhere in the South Pacific; its survivors marooned on an island. That was the surface narrative overlaying stupefying events and characters, which had me saying “WTF?” after each episode. But, to my dismay and disgust, the six-season program ended with the most godawful dreck; I felt utterly cheated. There was so much there for so long then this bathetic “climax”?

Heather Havrilesky, writing for the New York Times, shares by disappointment. She begins with this metaphor:

Imagine that a laboratory rat has just been introduced to a maze. We know that rats will navigate mazes for a reward — a pellet of food, say, or a dose of cocaine — but what if the reward of this particular maze is only the promise of a mystery pellet of unknown origin?

And what if the rat finds a new mystery pellet every time it successfully navigates the maze, and each pellet offers a clue to where the next pellet will be found? Will the rat keep searching, collecting clues and mystery pellets until it’s exhausted and undernourished? Will this endless flow of pellets and clues about pellets inspire the rat to form a wiki site on the Web, encouraging other rats in the laboratory to list the dimensions and colors and locations of the mystery pellets that they’ve found? Will this wiki site, in turn, encourage the rats to become increasingly obsessed with these mystery pellets, despite the fact that said pellets turn out to have no nutritive or stimulative benefits whatsoever?

Now imagine that the lead researcher enters the lab one day and tells all the rats (in rat language) that there is no answer to the mystery-pellet question. All those clues were arbitrarily placed, based on the whims of each lab assistant, and they don’t mean anything. Still, the lead researcher sincerely hopes that all the rats have thoroughly enjoyed their time navigating this maze. Their loyalty and efforts are much appreciated, and they are now free to go about their lives as if none of this ever happened.

What will the rats do then?

As I read this I immediately thought of Lost and how the writers conspired to treat me and millions of others to a tawdry joke. Well, she, too, was thinking of Lost.

A similar process is under way in the post- “Lost” television world. The first three seasons of “Lost” may have approached the imaginative charms of the original “Star Wars” trilogy, but the next three were nearly as awful as George Lucas’s catastrophic prequels. You could easily picture the stumped writers of “Lost,” helpless in the face of an ever-growing pile of unsolved mysteries, madly skimming Wikipedia entries on space-time geometries and black holes.

The show’s finale was the crowning disaster, the Scooby-Doo ending to end all Scooby-Doo endings. After hinting for years that their nonsensical mess would add up to something, not only did the producers Carlton Cuse and Damon Lindelof fail to address a tiny fraction of the trillions of mysteries they introduced, but they threw out the Lostpedia with the bath water, scrapping all of those riddles for the equivalent of Lucas’s teddy-bear victory dance: a celestial moment with the survivors, hugging and holding hands in the afterlife.

There have been a couple of other television series that played games with my psyche, frustrating me at nearly every turn. You remember X-Files, the chronicles of Fox Mulder and Dana Scully. They teased me with one, interesting narrative (“cancer man”) that the sadistic writers left hanging on multiple occasions as they took the audience into completely irrelevant diversions, single-episode stories designed to shock and disgust but having nothing to do with the only redeeming tale, one that suggested alien colonization aided and abetted by a mysterious, officious chain-smoker.

I allowed myself to get hooked on Fringe, another J.J. Abrams creation (Lost being one of now many creations). Again, we have at least one strain of storytelling that captivates: multiple universes, shape-shifters, and the Observers. But the writers sent us into unrelated digressions; so much so, that I forget where the principal narrative left off. The result is confusion and annoyance. I’m not alone; the ratings have fallen with each new season.

Havrilesky:

What we’re witnessing is the cost of hollowing out nutritive meaning or weight from your mystery pellets and suspense pellets and horror pellets. Whether it’s a 26-hour long episode of “Law and Order,” a 12-hour-long episode of “24” or a series of snuff-film vignettes featuring Jessica Lange and a basement full of creepy stuff preserved in chloroform, subtlety and refinement are not exactly winning the day. The problem lies in the fact that the dead-end suspense and pill-popping, knife-wielding darkness of today’s TV dramas shove more subtle pilot candidates out of the way. The empty thrills, the ticking clock that never runs down, the pointless twists and turns that are neither motivated nor resolved, all degrade the audience’s palate until all we can taste is blood, all we can see is teenagers in hot pants, all we can hear is flat dialogue and all we can expect at the end of season is a giant, flashing question mark.

And that’s sad, because it’s also true that television writers are taking big risks in their laboratories these days. They’re experimenting with speeding up and slowing down the timeline, trotting out unlikable characters and testing our tolerance for crazy, for demented, for morbid. Instead, they should focus on testing our tolerance for smart, complex characters and nuanced stories, just as “The Sopranos” and “The Wire” and “Mad Men” did. Because some of us have been burned too many times to head back into that jungle maze yet again.

I believe the solution is rather simple, but unconventional for the networks. Commit to one season and one season only. Charge the producers, directors, and writers with one task: create a series that is tight, intelligent, well-acted, and complete. Entice the audience to follow along from episode to episode, sticking with the main theme, admitting no departures.

Instead, it seems that the studios and networks are always betting on a multi-season blockbuster that will yield large dividends over time, enough to subsidize crap. In so doing they risk the ultimate—and literal—turnoff.

Under God

January 18, 2012 by davald

It’s amazing what one can accomplish with a lot of money to spend. Why, the One-percenters have been at it for a very long time, but much in earnest since the Collapse of 1929.

Imagine yourself back then, now struggling to put food on the table along with nearly everyone else in the Great Depression. You’ve lost faith in corporate America, rightly viewed as the problem rather than the solution. The banks were worst of all, busy foreclosing on homes and denying people credit or arrangements.

Well, the One-percenters didn’t just sit back and take it. They organized. But first, what message to spread and who should spread it? Wouldn’t you know that surveys showed that a plurality, if not a majority, of Americans placed their trust mostly in clergymen of the decidedly Christian variety.

As for the message, how about “one nation under God”? Kevin Kruse, writing for the New York Times:

During the Great Depression, the prestige of big business sank along with stock prices. Corporate leaders worked frantically to restore their public image and simultaneously roll back the “creeping socialism” of the welfare state. Notably, the American Liberty League, financed by corporations like DuPont and General Motors, made an aggressive case for capitalism. Most, however, dismissed its efforts as self-interested propaganda. (A Democratic Party official joked that the organization should have been called “the American Cellophane League” because “first, it’s a DuPont product and, second, you can see right through it.”)

Realizing that they needed to rely on others, these businessmen took a new tack: using generous financing to enlist sympathetic clergymen as their champions. After all, according to one tycoon, polls showed that, “of all the groups in America, ministers had more to do with molding public opinion” than any other.

The One-percenters kept at it, all the way to 1953, persuading Congress to include “under God” in the Pledge of Allegiance, written by a socialist, by the way. But they didn’t stop there, of course. Just ask our present-day members of Congress who feeds them.

MLK was dangerous

January 16, 2012 by davald

Meek men don’t get assassinated. That fate is reserved for those who dare to pester the rich and the powerful. Martin Luther King annoyed the hell out of our nation’s elite. It was not for his stand against racism, though he played a significant role in the eventual passage of the Civil Rights Act. Rather, MLK condemned the fortress of capitalism, calling for a massive redistribution of wealth.

In a 1968 Memphis speech he called it “criminal” that a nation so rich would force men and women to work for “starvation wages.” He advocated a guaranteed income, and it was Richard M. Nixon who pushed Congress to pass relevant legislation; the idea of a basic income has been championed by those on the left (Karl Marx) and the right (e.g., Frederich Hayek and Milton Friedman).

Here are some quotes of King that appeared in a Socialist Party USA pamphlet:

The dispossessed of this country — the poor, the white and Negro — live in a cruelly unjust society. They must organize a revolution against that injustice, not against the lives of the persons who are their fellow citizens, but against the structures through which society is refusing to take means which have been called for, and which are at hand, to lift the load of poverty.

…

We must rapidly begin the shift from a ‘thing’-oriented society to a “person”-oriented society. When machines and computers, profit motives and property rights, are considered more important than people, the giant triplets of racism, materialism and militarism are incapable of being conquered.

…

I am now convinced that the simplest approach will prove the most effective — the solution to poverty is to abolish it directly by a now widely discussed measure: the guaranteed income.

In 1965 Martin Luther King wrote a letter for Pageant magazine. The essay was titled “The Bravest Man I Ever Knew.” That man was Norman Thomas, the leader of the Socialist Party. In a preface he apologized for being unable to attend the 80th birthday of Thomas; King was on his way to Oslo to accept the Nobel Prize for Peace. Here are some excerpts from the essay:

I can think of no man who has done more than you to inspire the vision of a society free of injustice and exploitation. While some would adjust to the status quo, you urged struggle. While some would corrupt struggle with violence or undemocratic perversions, you have stood firmly for the integrity of ends and means. Your example has ennobled and dignified the fight for freedom, and all that we hear of the Great Society seems only an echo of your prophetic eloquence. Your pursuit of racial and economic democracy at home, and of sanity and peace in the world, has been awesome in scope. It is with deep admiration and indebtedness that I carry the inspiration of your life to Oslo.

…

During our historic March on Washington in the summer of 1963, when 250,000 Negro and white Americans joined together in an outpouring of fellowship and brotherly cooperation for a world of freedom and equality, a little Negro boy listened near the Washington Monument to an eloquent orator.

Turning to his father, he asked: “Who is that man?”

Came the inevitable answer: “That’s Norman Thomas. He was for us before any other white folks were.”

…

Yet America has never fully utilized Thomas’s great abilities. He has been a marvelous unofficial ambassador-at-large to our friends in Europe, Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Would it not make sense to make him our official representative to the United Nations?

In his column today Paul Krugman acknowledges Dr. King’s contribution to ending legal sanctions against blacks and other people of color, though racism still casts a pernicious pall over our society. As an economist, also a Nobel Prize winner, Krugman concludes that de jure racism has given way to economic classism. Krugman:

Economic inequality isn’t inherently a racial issue, and rising inequality would be disturbing even if there weren’t a racial dimension. But American society being what it is, there are racial implications to the way our incomes have been pulling apart. And in any case, King — who was campaigning for higher wages when he was assassinated — would surely have considered soaring inequality an evil to be opposed.

King spoke of the growing chasm between the Haves and the Have-nots. In one of his speeches (you can view it yourself by clicking on the last link) he said that America spends over $320,000 for “each enemy we kill in Vietnam,” whereas we spend only $53 for each poor person in the War on Poverty. Krugman:

Mitt Romney says that we should discuss income inequality, if at all, only in “quiet rooms.” There was a time when people said the same thing about racial inequality. Luckily, however, there were people like Martin Luther King who refused to stay quiet. And we should follow their example today. For the fact is that rising inequality threatens to make America a different and worse place — and we need to reverse that trend to preserve both our values and our dreams.

Dr. King “refused to stay quiet,” for which he paid the ultimate sacrifice. MLK, rest in peace.

Blessed be brown fat

January 13, 2012 by davald

It seems that regular exercise turns white fat brown, which is a good thing, say researchers. Brown fat is associated with lower obesity and diabetes rates.

Brown fat, as many of us have heard, is physiologically desirable. While white fat cells are essentially inert storehouses for fat, brown fat cells are metabolically active. They use oxygen and require energy. They burn calories.

Until recently, it was thought that human adults did not have brown fat, that we lost our stores after babyhood. But beginning in 2009, a number of studies showed that grown-ups do harbor brown fat. Some people just have more than others.

And it may be that irisin, and exercise, partially determine how much brown fat each of us contains, the new study suggests. In perhaps the most compelling of the many separate experiments detailed in the Nature paper, the scientists injected irisin into white fat cells removed from mice. Afterward, genetic changes in the cells signified that they were browning. The fat cells also increased their respiratory rate, an indication that they were burning more energy.

So, get thee off thy duff. Or, maybe we’ll eventually take a pill to transform our fat and skip exercise altogether.

American “exceptionalism” debunked

January 5, 2012 by davald

Americans derive a perverse sense of pride in their self-delusion. They refuse to believe obvious truths (e.g., nearly anything scientific) while embracing falsehoods like angels.

Among the latter is the myth of social mobility. Against the evidence, too many of our citizens rationalize rank inequality of outcome because each of us has equal opportunity.

Along the way to our misguided notions we lazily accept the shibboleth that “socialist” Europe—with all of its taxes, generous public services, and history of feudalism—squelches the entrepreneurial spirit and denies inhabitants the chance to get ahead.

Well, it just ain’t so. As the New York Times headline tells us, “[It’s] Harder for Americans to Rise From the Lower Rungs.” Harder than whom?—you may ask. From the linked study (pdf):

Mobility is lower in the U.S. than in the U.K., where it is lower again compared to the Nordic countries. Persistence is greatest in the tails of the distributions and tends to be particularly high in the upper tails: though in the U.S. this is reversed with a particularly high likelihood that sons of the poorest fathers will remain in the lowest earnings quintile. This is a challenge to the popular notion of ’American exceptionalism’. The U.S. also differs from the Nordic countries in its very low likelihood that sons of the highest earners will show downward ’long- distance’ mobility into the lowest earnings quintile.

Why is this? The New York Times:

One reason for the mobility gap may be the depth of American poverty, which leaves poor children starting especially far behind. Another may be the unusually large premiums that American employers pay for college degrees. Since children generally follow their parents’ educational trajectory, that premium increases the importance of family background and stymies people with less schooling.

We may think of the latter in terms of a steadily rising conveyor belt. The offspring of the privileged already occupy a spot. Many will not have to lift a finger to remain on board; they have earned their money the old-fashioned way—via inheritance. Others, say those with supportive, educated parents, are provided with the necessary conditions for the upward rise. Many more will have to struggle mightily to stay with it.

There’s another conveyor belt, this one headed downward. It’s really easy to remain on the belt. You can’t really fall off this one. The occupants are already poor from birth and are likely to remain so, as the study reports. Their parents lack education and probably a steady job paying adequate wages. The schools they attend are old, dilapidated, and ill-supplied. Teachers there try their best to engage unmotivated pupils; many give up. The children live in squalid conditions and suffer food insecurity.

Some on the upper belt fall off. They may drop into a kind of purgatory, occasionally getting a break to jump back on the rising conveyor. But the longer they stay in this in-between realm, the more difficult it is to rejoin their former peers; the gap grows larger over time. Others who fall land on the lower belt, from which they may strive to reach purgatory. It is nearly impossible to get from the lower to the upper belt.

The American middle class used to be larger and more confident. They did not need college to land family-wage jobs. With their incomes they could afford a home, a decent car, a vacation, and an occasional trip to a restaurant. They could also set some money aside and perhaps even help pay their children’s college expenses. That was my family.

Today’s middle class has shrunk; its occupants worried about the future. They are in this purgatory of unrealized dreams and diminished opportunity. If they are laid off, chances are they will be out of work for many months if not years, and if they do find another job it will be at a much lower wage. They risk falling to the lower belt, forever doomed.

It really doesn’t have to be this way, as the Nordic countries attest. We are supposedly a free citizenry living in a democracy. We could decide our futures. But it seems that too many of us have become fatalists, as if the savage inequalities are just part of “the system,” just the way things are.

A pervasive sense of profound futility looms large. That should bother us all.

Losing weight

December 28, 2011 by davald

A couple of years ago I experienced chest pains. When my wife arrived home I calmly asked her to take me to the hospital. Given my symptoms, the hospital staff admitted me. My blood pressure was dangerously high, and it wouldn’t decline. After staying overnight, I was subjected to a stress test the next day. Hours later a physician entered my room to report “good” news and “bad” news. The good: I have a healthy heart with no arterial blockage. The bad: I had Type II diabetes. I was then finally weighed. The scale revealed 276 pounds! Take a look. I’m standing next to my daughter, Kelly. The shirt is 3X.

 

I followed up with a visit to my regular doctor, who warned me of the dangers of diabetes. I had to lose weight by reducing calories and burning more through exercise. He referred me to a dietician, who, ironically, was as big around as she was tall. She gave me some reading materials, including a chart showing the carbohydrates of various foods. She also filled in a form that broke down what I was to eat and how much each day.

I learned what I had long suspected: there are many factors, including heredity, that play a role in both gaining and losing weight. My family, most of whom are dead and gone, carried a surplus of fat on their bodies. As a child, my weight fluctuated, though I was usually declared “husky,” wearing appropriately sized clothes. Here’s an early shot of me in the year of my birth.

As for wearing “husky,” my waist required pants befitting a much taller child. You can see for yourself in this photo.

I’m the one with the jeans rolled way up. Standing to my left is sister Nancy, who, though a year younger than I, was a bit taller for a few months, which caused me considerable grief. She, too, was prone to pudgy. Our father is standing between Nancy and me. My sister Liz is on the far left, with our brother Jim between the two of us. I should add the obvious: my shirt amplified my girth—bad choice.

I carried the “husky” label through the first couple of years of high school. Then I started growing up rather than out. Here I am on the far left at the age of 16. As you can see, I was now taller than sister Nancy to my left.

My college years at Cal yielded several ups and downs in my weight—from 165 lbs. as a high school senior to about 185 playing baseball on Evans Field in Berkeley. Once I left college, my weight tended to increase far more often than the opposite.

On two occasions, however, I managed to quickly shed bushels of pounds, at one point getting below 170. Otherwise, the waist expanded. I used to delude myself into believing that my body had a certain set point; I would simply stop gaining weight. Didn’t happen.

So let’s get back to the post-diabetes diagnosis. Frightened about the prospects of that insidious disease, I began walking three miles every day, then upped it to 4.5 miles, which I now traverse each morning at a 15-min/mile pace. Following a rigid diet, which I recorded dutifully in a spreadsheet along with my weight and blood pressure, I lopped off 70 pounds at one point, bringing me to just a bit over 200. I’ve since added back about 15, though I no longer keep track.

While I’m still considered overweight I’m a far cry from “morbidly obese,” which is what I was at 276. My vitals are within normal, as is my blood-sugar reading—my A1C dropped from over 7 to under 6, actually 5.5. That, too, is a normal level.

Readers who have struggled with their weight can attest to how difficult it is to lose pounds and keep them lost. It seems that we chubby people have bodies that don’t especially care to lose mass. After shedding a few pounds, our chemistry changes, making it difficult to stay slim or slimmer.

Such thoughts were triggered upon reading “The Fat Trap” in the New York Times. The author, who also struggles to keep off the pounds, offers us a bit more sympathy.

If anything, the emerging science of weight loss teaches us that perhaps we should rethink our biases about people who are overweight. It is true that people who are overweight, including myself, get that way because they eat too many calories relative to what their bodies need. But a number of biological and genetic factors can play a role in determining exactly how much food is too much for any given individual. Clearly, weight loss is an intense struggle, one in which we are not fighting simply hunger or cravings for sweets, but our own bodies.

I know that each pound on my body represents 3,500 calories. If I subtract 500 calories from my daily intake, I’ll lose one pound after a week. But the article suggests that this simple calculus may not work so well for chubby bodies. We may have to reduce the caloric intake a bit more and we won’t burn off the calories as efficiently as a “normal” person at the same weight.

Well, time for my walk.

Rising inequality [u]

December 27, 2011 by davald

New York Times columnist David Brooks tends to write in broad generalities (a penchant he shares with fellow columnist Thomas Friedman) coated with a thick layer of moralism. Today’s column is more of the same, which is titled “Midlife Crisis Economics.”

He correctly notes that the United States and other industrial democracies are much different societies than they were a half-century ago. Brooks cites rising inequality as one factor marking the difference.

Inequality is growing in nearly every developed country. According to a report from the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development, over the past 30 years, inequality in Sweden, Germany, Israel, Finland and New Zealand has grown as fast or faster than inequality in the United States, even though these countries have very different welfare systems.

As we see Canada, Finland, and the Netherlands have experienced only a moderate rise in inequality since the mid-1970s (data from OECD). It may help to consider more inequality statistics. The higher the index the greater the inequality.

The United States is the most unequal society in the group. Indeed, very few countries (e.g., Mexico and Brazil) have higher Gini indices. Even before the Great Divergence in the mid-70s, the U.S. was much less egalitarian than the Nordic countries and Canada.

So, what accounts for the growing gap between the Haves and the Have-nots? The OECD suggests (pdf) several plausible explanations:

  • globalization
  • technological innovation
  • policy choices, including regulations

OECD dismisses the first.

This report finds that neither rising trade integration nor financial openness had a significant impact on either wage inequality or employment trends within the OECD countries.

As for technology and capital flows, the OECD writes:

The study also shows, however, that increased financial flows and technological change had an impact on inequality. Growing outward FDI [foreign direct investment] was associated with increases in wage dispersion, albeit only in the upper half of the wage distribution, while technological progress contributed to the increase in overall wage dispersion, chiefly in the upper half of the distribution.

I am not surprised that the OECD concluded that education-derived skills explain most of the wage gap.

The growth in average educational attainment thus appears to have been the single most important factor contributing not only to reduced wage dispersion among workers but also to higher employment rates. On the basis of these results, the evolution of earnings inequality across OECD countries over the past few decades could be viewed mainly as the difference between the demand for and supply of skills or, as neatly summarised by Tinbergen (1975), the outcome of a “race between education and technology”.

It also occurred to me that there may have been a decline in tax revenues, which, in turn, may have reduced wealth transfers from the Haves to the Have-nots. Let’s look at tax rates (percentage of total worker compensation) of selected countries. They’ve declined for the countries selected, especially in Sweden.

The OECD publishes two sets of data on the Gini index of multiple countries. One pertains to inequality before taxes and government transfers; the other after taxes are collected and spent on public services benefiting the less wealthy. I’ve created a chart that shows the difference in the two as a percentage change and over time. The higher the percentage change the greater the wealth transfer.

There was not much change over time for Canada and the US. However, after rising from the mid-70s to the mid-90s, the Nordic countries’ saw a decrease in the difference between the before and after (taxes and transfers).

Yet, whatever the causes of income inequality the OECD authors conclude that governments must enact policies to reduce the gap between the Haves and the Have-nots.

Rising income inequality creates economic, social and political challenges. It can stifle upward social mobility, making it harder for talented and hard-working people to get the rewards they deserve. Intergenerational earnings mobility is low in countries with high inequality such as Italy, the United Kingdom, and the United States, and much higher in the Nordic countries, where income is distributed more evenly (OECD, 2008). The resulting inequality of opportunity will inevitably impact economic performance as a whole, even if the relationship is not straightforward. Inequality also raises political challenges because it breeds social resentment and generates political instability. It can also fuel populist, protectionist, and anti-globalisation sentiments. People will no longer support open trade and free markets if they feel that they are losing out while a small group of winners is getting richer and richer.

Those countries with high inequality, including the United States, experience low “intergenerational earnings mobility” and reduced economic performance. Moreover, public support for public institutions wanes most in unequal societies. We’ll note that David Brooks wrote: “According to a Gallup survey, 64 percent of Americans polled said they believed that big government is the biggest threat to the country.”

What should be done?

The OECD:

…policies that invest in the human capital of the workforce are key. Over the past two decades, the trend to higher educational attainment has been one of the most important elements in counteracting the underlying increase in earnings inequality in the long run. Policies that promote the up-skilling of the workforce are therefore key factors for reversing the trend towards further growth in inequality.

Human capital policies comprise two main strands. First, better job-related training and education for the low-skilled (on-the-job training) would help to boost their productivity potential and future earnings. This requires measures to ensure that training markets perform better, as well as ensuring sufficient incentives for both workers and firms to invest more in on-the-job training (OECD, 2006). To compensate for mobility (staff turnover), corporate tax policies that encourage employers to make additional investments in the human capital of their employees are warranted (e.g. deduction of training expenses as business costs).

The second strand is equal access to formal education over working life. Access to tertiary education is important for improving the prospects and living standards of lower-skilled people and giving individuals the opportunity to acquire the skills needed in the labour market. Educational or learning accounts can be a means to help achieve this objective (OECD, 2005), but tax incentives need to be designed in such a way that they do not disproportionally benefit higher-wage earners in high marginal tax rates.

Alas, a huge segment of our country, including almost all Republicans, are loath to adopt such policies. Indeed, the GOP in both houses is determined to increase inequality. As for accessing tertiary education (college), good luck with that. It’s expensive, with less and less government aid. Thus, the average college graduate faces over $25,000 in accumulated debt.

I wonder if I could have afforded a college education today. When I attended Berkeley in the 60s there was no tuition—just small “incidental fees.” When I entered as a freshman an academic semester cost me $60 in fees plus books and living expenses. When I graduated four years later the fees had risen to $100 a quarter. (Berkeley switched from the semester to the quarter system during my tenure.) Today it will cost $13,200 per year for tuition and fees (still a bargain, I suppose). The university estimates that total costs will exceed $30,000. That’s $120,000 for four years.

But if college is evidently so important to avoid poverty, why does a student have to become a pauper in the process? Shouldn’t the state invest in human capital, thereby facilitating an inexpensive tertiary education?

I have an idea. Take billions from the Pentagon and transfer the dollars to universities to make education free.

Pass it on.

Hitch arraigns

December 19, 2011 by davald

Christopher Hitchens, commissioned to cover our national party conventions for the London Review of Books, found much to be desired. He took little comfort in this, because he declared America his new home, officially so, and thought we deserved better. Here he uses his journalistic assignments to inveigh against our electoral system and its evident follies. There is no candidate left unscathed. As always, the writing is preciously provocative.

He regrets what has happened to presidential elections, particularly the respective parties’ anointment of their chosen ones long before the November balloting.

A Republican National Convention had been arranged, as a sort of sound-stage or a mixed-media event, entirely for the convenience of the press and TV. The delegates were mere extras on the set, the coronation of the two nominees was a sure thing, the feral extremists and fundamentalists had been tidied out of sight, the corporate-sponsorship logos were beautifully placed, the camera angles and background briefings were the chief preoccupation of the Party managers and – there was exactly nothing to cover, nothing to transmit, and nothing to write about.

While he would eventually champion the invasion of Iraq, he found nothing to like in George W. Bush, whom he excoriates for his gubernatorial  murders and the glee with which he approached this dreadful practice.

I say ‘twisted’ about the dislikably boyish Governor of Texas, because he has signed more than a hundred death warrants during his entirely undistinguished tenure, and because he was only criticised (and that faintly) for two of these capricious crowd-pleasing atrocities. In one case, a Canadian citizen was condemned to death without any notification to the Canadian Embassy, as is reciprocally mandated by both US and Canadian law. In another, a woman was executed despite good evidence that she had undergone a transforming redemption while on Death Row. Bush met the Canadian objections by saying that the man ought not to have committed a crime while in Texas (which would mean that as President he would not care about consular representation for Americans arraigned overseas). He countered the Christian conservative petition on behalf of Karla Faye Tucker by mimicking her last-minute entreaties (‘Oh, please don’t kill me’) to a reporter for Rupert Murdoch’s Weekly Standard. That does it for me, and I don’t even believe – as Bush is supposed to – in Biblical repentance.

It’s worth going through the list, providing Hitchens’s unique perspective.

John McCain – nobody’s idea of an intellectual but likes to talk dirty and got himself shot down while scattering high explosives over someone else’s country. (People keep describing him as a POW but I think that there must be some mistake – no war on Vietnam was ever declared.)

Donald Trump – a ludicrous figure, but at least he’s lived it up a bit in the real world and at least he’s worked out how to cover 90 per cent of his skull with 30 per cent of his hair.

Bill Bradley – boring and pompous and tenth-rate but used to play basketball and take showers with African Americans.

The weird one is [Al] Gore. When he isn’t robotically normal he is decidedly odd. Who would claim, as he did, to have been (with his wife) the model for Erich Segal’s treacly novel Love Story? Who would opt, as he did, for paid advice from a simpering Clintonoid feminist about how to become an ‘alpha male’? Who would make as little as he does of having had the ravaged and husky Tommy Lee Jones for a college room-mate? Who would get his wife to proffer an on-the-record interview, asserting that he was a secret Tamburlaine in the sack? He’s not a psychopath like his boss, and he inspires piercing pity for his writhing attempts to break free, but still.

Alas, we won’t see this kind of writing again.
