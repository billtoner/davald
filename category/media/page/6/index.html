Leaks

June 10, 2013 by davald

The government spies. I know, that shatters the earth. Governments lie, too, as I.F. Stone declared, after decades of revealing the deceptions. So we should not be surprised that the current executive branch, led by an erstwhile defender of privacy and liberties, should both eavesdrop on its citizens then initially deny the same.

Throughout this latest scandal I’m reminded of the television program Person of Interest. A brilliant computer scientist designed an incredibly sophisticated program that instantly sifts through terabytes of data to reveal “a number,” the lone identity of someone who is either about to be harmed or will perpetrate a despicable act against another. Perhaps the premise is not so far fetched, as we’re learning of late.

And speaking of identity, the individual who spilled the beans on the National Security Agency has come forward with an admission, also telling us that he is willing to face the consequences of his actions. And we know that there will be consequences.

Have you noticed, then, that the nefarious deeds revealed rather quickly fade, replaced by an obsessive focus on the leakers? We soon lose sight of the pernicious spying, or the “secret” conversation about evil intentions and conduct, to focus exclusively on those who exposed the shenanigans in the first place. I have in mind, for example, the following:

  • Nixon going after Daniel Ellsberg
  • the Republicans going after Jim McDermott when he distributed a purloined tape recording of misdeeds
  • the government’s over-the-top prosecution of both Private Bradley and Julian Assange in the Wikileaks revelations

We consumers of news gladly allow the message to change from bad people doing bad things to pursuing the whistleblower. Why, he broke the law. He captured the conversation illicitly. They broke national security.

Next scandal.

A media future

May 14, 2013 by davald

I’ll go out on a short limb here. My guess is that in a more perfect media world, all content, and I mean all, would be instantly available at any time on any device of our choosing. That world does not exist, but I think it inevitable.

What prevents the ideal from becoming reality sooner rather than later is money, of course. It’s always about money. Right now the major factions divide between content and providers. The former include creators and owners. Cable and satellite dish companies compose the latter. The networks occupy both, more or less. Against the tide of consumer demand, all are jockeying to either hold on to what they have or exploit the inevitable, albeit grudgingly.

As things now stand, I can watch some sporting events live on my iPad. I have a subscription with MLB.com that allows me to view major league baseball games, including my beloved Giants, on my iPhone, iPad, computer, and Apple TV-cum-television. The streaming quality is still inconsistent, but getting better. ESPN also streams live to my portable devices, except the Apple TV.

I pay an exorbitant amount of money to Comcast each month. Channels, in the hundreds, are “bundled” with each other and with a land line and Internet. I can also watch some programs on my Apple devices, with emphasis on some. There’s also “on demand.” But that’s a hit-and-miss proposition. Comcast doesn’t provide all episodes of all seasons of all series.

Frustration rules. Why can’t I just watch this or that when I want to?

First, baby steps. ABC recently said that it would begin streaming live content via cable providers to phones and tablets. Will that move spur others to follow? Probably.

There’s another problem with my ideal provisioning scenario: the lack of a central hub or portal through which content is acquired then streamed. I’m sure that Apple would like to play that role, and it’s already captured the lion’s share of digital music. Many of us buy television programs and movies from the iTunes store. But there’s nothing “live” in the online library.

Amazon, I suspect, stands the better chance of becoming that digital hub, since Jeff Bezos seems intent on dominating the retail world. He began with books several years ago. Now I can have just about anything delivered to my front door. As a “prime” member I can also stream media content for no extra charge. Still, nothing live.

The Nation magazine used to print a double-paged spread illustrating in graphic form the concentration of the media companies. I haven’t seen one for a while, but I recall there being only a handful of corporations pretty much owning everything. They have lots of money, which means that they can pay for bushels of lawyers and lobbyists, not to mention purchase their requisite lot of senators and representatives. They are unlikely to let the future catch them with their pants down. Rather, they will make sure that whichever way the consumer winds blow, they’ll steer things in their direction, with dollar bills attached.

But it will happen, ladies and gentlemen. We will have everything everywhere anytime. It just won’t be cheap.

Determining b.s.

May 13, 2013 by davald

On occasion I cross breaths with climate change skeptics, although I strongly suspect that in my absence they descend into group-thinking denial, following the lead of that noted eminent scientist Jim Inhofe, who has repeatedly called global warming “a hoax” and likely something stronger when he’s surrounded by fellow imbeciles. A preponderance of facts should never get in the way of a good story, as Ronald Reagan opined—he of “trees pollute” and “ketchup is a vegetable” fame. The stories range from carbon dioxide is as natural as flower petals to god will decide humanity’s fate, so might was well not worry about it. Oh, and it seems that those who hold one conspiracy embrace dozens, all whackier than mimes on meth.

The combination of social media and the Internet breeds incoherent screeds that instantly infect idiots. Have you heard the latest on the Clintons’ murders? Did you know that the moon landing was fake? Vaccinations will kill you, of course.

Suppose that you have the good fortune of having landed a position in a university, and it really doesn’t matter which, save for those founded and run by aforementioned idiots. Let’s further assume that you teach political science or sociology, fields that don’t easily lend themselves to formulas and laboratory observations as physics or chemistry do. Now you pass out your first assignment, asking students to explore a topic, reach a conclusion, and base findings on research. What should you expect from your students?

A midwestern professor of political science, who goes by “Ed,” has a blog that I’ve referenced several times, ginandtacos.com. He prefers to remain somewhat anonymous, and who can blame him? His prose is colorful. His tone irreverent. Today he mourns the state of student stupidity.

He mentions an actual paper submitted by an undergrad, replete with footnotes!

Many years ago a student submitted a research paper that I continue to use (without personal information, obviously) in classes as an example today. Unfortunately it’s not a positive example. The paper is a ten-page treatise on how the American presidents who died in office were all secretly murdered by the Freemasons.

The student was quite unhappy with the (failing) grade this mess of internet conspiracy theories received. “I did research and cited all of the sources”, s/he stated. It was true; the paper was exhaustively cited and clearly represented a good deal of research. The problem – and we/I did cover this in class repeatedly throughout the semester – is that not all “sources” are made equal. Some are legitimate, some are questionable, and some are flat-out nonsense. And back when I was still learning how to teach, it surprised the hell out of me to find that a non-trivial minority of students cannot tell the difference.

“Ed” goes on to discuss how we can distinguish between obvious bullshit and trustworthy sources. As he puts it:

My best advice, I think, is to err on the side of caution when the objective is to cite supporting research. Sure, government or major media outlet sources will not always be correct and may be flawed. But if the choice is between the Benghazi story on CNN or a post on something called WheresTheBirthCertificate.com, it is in students’ interest to play it safe.

Indeed.

The “sensible center”

May 2, 2013 by davald

Alliterative, to  be sure. That’s Peter Jackson, writing this morning in the Everett Herald. He came to his keyboard not to bury Steve Hobbs but to praise him. Hobbs, a nominal Democratic senator in Olympia and member of the Road Kill Caucus, presumably a group of sensible centrists, annoys the hell out of liberals and progressives for his stubbornly conservative positions on key issues affecting the Rest of Us. Here’s Jackson, the son of Henry M., if you don’t know:

Two recent examples involving Hobbs merit flagging. Leaders of Washington CAN! gave the senator an ultimatum to renounce his leadership role in a bipartisan national group called “Fix the Debt” or they’ll begin to leaflet and campaign against him. (Hobbs wants to start a conversation about our colossal national debt. Oh, the shame.) Note to pressure groups: Ultimatums only irritate harried lawmakers who are trying to do their best. Hobbs also has been the target of WEA activists for his support of education reform (a teacher doorbelled his family home in Lake Stevens, Hobbs said.)

I’ve discussed “our colossal national debt” on these pages (e.g., here). It’s a bogus problem, as several prominent economists have painstakingly explained (one example here). It’s especially so in the midst of the anemic jobless recovery from the worst economic downturn since the Great Depression. The “sensible centrists” love to exploit our ordinary sensibilities about debt (sinful) to whittle away at public services, including education and so-called entitlements, the programs that most of us will rely on as we enter our golden years.

As for the “conversation,” it’s occurring amidst the numbers, which are dismal. European leaders trumpeted austerity (or fiscal consolidation, if you prefer) as an antidote to “colossal national debt.” It’s not working, Messrs. Jackson and Hobbs. Indeed, cutting federal spending during liquidity-trap conditions actually increases deficits. Why? Because governments continue to suffer from inadequate revenues occasioned by depressed aggregate demand, and consumers buying goods and services accounts for the majority of GDP.

Besides, those like Jackson and Hobbs ignore a more important consideration about debt: its affordability. With interest rates at or near zero, debt service payments impose minimal challenges to the fiscal balance sheets.

 

Before departing, I have to comment on this quote from Jackson’s editorial:

This Editorial Page has disagreed with Hobbs on a few issues, including the wisdom of a coal-export facility at Cherry Point near Bellingham. Nevertheless, Hobbs embraces positions that align with his core principles.

To offer an extreme example, Hitler embraced positions consistent with “his core principles.” He did not like Jews, so he killed as many as he could get his hands on. Any fair reading of Mein Kampf or the history of Der Führer’s rise in Weimar Germany reveals that fundamental hatred of all things Hebrew. That he acted in accordance with this core principle is hardly commendable. It was and continues to be unreservedly condemned.

The problem with Steve Hobbs and others of his ilk is precisely a matter of “core principles.” In my judgment, they’re decidedly wrong for the people of Snohomish County, Washington state, and the nation.

Boston vs. West

April 23, 2013 by davald

I wrote a bit on the differences between the events of Boston and West, Texas. The former, of course, involved deadly bombs detonated near the finish line of the fabled marathon. The latter, which took more lives, was deemed an industrial accident—a fertilizer plant explosion.

Writing for the New York Times, Bruce Machart expands on a similar theme.

We tend to discount that which is accidental as somehow less tragic, less interesting, less newsworthy than the mayhem of agency. Lives have been “lost” in Texas, but in Boston, by God — lives have been “taken.”

But this distinction means nothing to the victims or, I imagine, to their families. In Boston, in West, whether by sinister design or by accident, whether on a television-ready stage or hidden away in a rural factory, when people are hurt, when lives are lost, the essential human cost shouldn’t be lost on the living.

Indeed.

The nature of death

April 22, 2013 by davald

There were two major events that once again shattered everyday lives. First there was Boston. Then there was Texas. In each event, people died: three from the bombs set off near the marathon’s finish line; perhaps dozens killed by a massive explosion at a fertilizer plant. Dying defined their commonality. All else was different.

Our reactions differed, certainly. We immediately spoke of “tragedy” and “senseless violence” when describing the Boston bombs. Who did this? Who could have done this, and why? The media quickly filled the airwaves; there was much to be said. The ensuing dragnet focused on two suspects. One was killed by police. His younger brother was eventually apprehended. We were interested—big time.

In West, Texas, a fertilizer plant exploded. There was nothing much left, and hundreds are reported missing, with more than a dozen confirmed dead. It was described as “an accident,” as if no one is responsible for what occurred. Boston had perpetrators. Exploding fertilizer plants don’t. But I would ask: Who did this? Who could have done this, and why?

There will be a post-accident investigation, of course. People will endeavor to identify the “proximate cause” of the explosion and elements of the causal chain. This will no doubt take a very long time. In the end, there will likely be litigation. Fines may be assessed. By then we may have forgotten about “the accident.” After all, accidents happen all the time and we no longer remember them.

We will remember Boston and derive satisfaction from knowing that the evildoers faced justice. Exploding bombs in crowds is not the stuff of accidents. Exploding fertilizer plants, or petroleum refineries, or coal mines, or nuclear power facilities are. And that makes all the difference, save for the victims and their families.
