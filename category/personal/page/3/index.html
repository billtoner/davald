Elderly signs

February 3, 2014 by davald

The mirror gives clues, which my ego chooses to ignore. The face is noticeably rounder, the hair almost completely gray—what’s left of it. My teeth evince regular visits with coffee. Moreover, my neck belongs on a linebacker and not on someone long past his prime.

With the advancement of age, I should not be surprised if cognitive faculties fail from time to time. Research suggests that as we get older we process things more slowly than we did in our youth, if we process things at all. Here is where I notice the aging most acutely: translating mental images and events into ordinary words.

These days, I would prefer to point and grunt rather than try to produce the relevant verbs and nouns. When my wife asks me a question about where stuff goes in the kitchen, for example, I gesture with my hands and emit unintelligible sounds. When she doesn’t understand my directions, I get irritated—not with her but with my inability to instantly express myself in English.

When I type I need not speak, which is a good thing. At least until the arthritis immobilizes my fingers.

Being apolitical

January 13, 2014 by davald

Bad men need nothing more to compass their ends, than that good men should look on and do nothing.

— John Stuart Mill

Is it in my genes? My grandfather was heavily involved in politics from the left, being a strong union man when that meant something. My father followed in his footsteps, belonging to the Oil, Chemical, and Atomic Workers union for most of his adult life. (I recall the dark days of the extended strike at the Richmond, Calif., refinery when belts were severely tightened and food judiciously distributed at the table.) We were, as a breed, Democrats to the bone. The only mention of Republicans was in cursed language. The favored adjectives were “damn” and “bastard.”

With such background I inherited a history of workingman politics, in which the rich were scorned, the poor pitied and assisted, and the Rest of Us struggled to make a better life. It helped that I was raised in California, then under the governorship of the first Brown, where the education system was the envy of the world and good-paying jobs plentiful. I matriculated at Berkeley—tuition free.

To be sure, I did not quickly take to the political life, largely skating through high school and even college, generally oblivious to events around me, save for the Kennedy assassination, which struck me deeply. I pursued baseball, eschewing protests of any kind, though there was surely much to oppose, from overt racial discrimination to the Vietnam War, which I managed to avoid by lottery luck.

I married early, while still a junior at Cal, became a father before I graduated, then set out to earn a living at whatever. I had no plans and nothing, really, to suggest a career, let alone a successful one. Unlike my father and his father before him, the jobs that I landed required a coat and tie rather than overalls and gloves. I did not sweat. Nor did I get dirty, save for whatever food and drink may have dribbled onto my clothing during lunchtime.

But I eventually became political. When? I would say that it happened the moment that I decided to form a union at Pemco Insurance.

That was a fool’s errand back in 1977-78. The CEO at the time, Stanley O. McNaughton, would often tell the employees that capitalism was the “greatest human invention.” He detested unions. But I naively soldiered on, gradually gaining reluctant adherents to “the cause.” In the end, after months of frustrating efforts to win the hearts and minds of a majority, we canceled the vote, realizing that we potential unionists would lose big time. Within the year I was gone. Yet now I was hooked. Damn.

The title of this post is “being apolitical.” Let’s say that I’m imagining what it might be like to fit the description.

Now I imagine that one can be apolitical (as in not paying all that much attention to current events and governments at any level) because one is otherwise engaged in something really interesting that consumes all one’s waking hours. Being a musical director of a major symphony orchestra comes to mind.

Then, I suppose, there are millions of us who just don’t give a darn about who wins elections or what the legislature or city council is up to. You may believe that you can go about your everyday activities seemingly untouched by political acts. Your pursuits may be trivial or profound leaving scarce minutes to do anything else, including casting your ballot.

Many of us are likely cynical of politics in general. This article in the New York Times triggered my post. It’s about buying elections. Shocked? Well, we shouldn’t be. Yet, the amount of money and the degree of sophistication involved in skirting campaign finance laws, however toothless, could make cynics of us all.

Exploiting a loophole in the state law and a network of political action committees in Alabama and Washington, Mr. [Mike] Hubbard [head of the Alabama Republican party] shuffled hundreds of thousands of out-of-state dollars into the Republican organization in Alabama, vastly outraising the state Democratic Party. On Election Day, Republicans won majorities in both the State Senate and House of Representatives for the first time since Reconstruction — and Alabama joined the rapidly growing fraternity of states where government is controlled by a single political party, now the largest it has been in more than half a century.

Alabama’s transformation was the product, in part, of a sophisticated political apparatus designed to channel political money from around the country into states where conditions were ripe for Republican takeover. In 2010, the effort achieved striking success, moving a dozen states to sole Republican control, including presidential swing states like Ohio and Pennsylvania.

Oh, and you contributed $25 to your favorite candidate expecting to make a difference? Good luck with that.

So, why not simply turn off the political faucet, the one that gushes out at me every day? I could play computer games or watch reality TV or read the classics, start a hobby—do anything but let the cesspool of politics disturb my life, liberty, and pursuit of happiness.

Well, there are a couple of nagging considerations that prevent me from doing so. First, I am, by definition, a politician. I hold elective office, and it’s a responsibility I take seriously. Besides, in my tenure I believe that I have made a positive difference for the community.

Second, politics is really inescapable. We may choose to ignore what government officials do, but their impacts can be huge, from how much we pay in taxes and to what ends, to whether or not we kill thousands of civilians in foreign lands, to the degree of suffering visited on citizens existing at the margins.

Politics, then, is ultimately a moral endeavor. Those we elect (with or without our vote) can do good or evil. That’s why it matters.

When I was 17 [u]

November 22, 2013 by davald

Fifty years ago today I was seated in my high school German class when a student from “the office” entered the room, beckoning Herr Boyden. Upon receiving the message, Mr. Boyden then turned solemnly to us students to make an announcement. “President Kennedy has been killed.” What?

We were all of 16 or 17, this class of Guten Tags and other gutturals, too young to appreciate the fact and implications of Camelot struck down under a Texas sun. The weekend was filled with quiet grief and somberness all around, as we watched the televised scenes of Jackie, John-John, Caroline, horses, canons, soldiers, and the casket. Abruptly in the midst, we were taken to the Dallas police station to witness live the shooting of the alleged lone assassin by a man named ‘Ruby.’ Violence atop violence.

I always had my doubts that Oswald acted alone or was even part of the events on November 22, 1963. My former colleague, a PhD in physics, said that it was impossible for the fatal shot to have come from the rear, he having tested the lethality of bullets for the U.S. Army at the Aberdeen Proving Ground in Maryland.  Oliver Stone’s JFK amplified my suspicions, and Fidel Castro recently conversed with The Atlantic‘s Jeffrey Goldberg:

I asked Fidel why he thought Oswald could not have acted alone. He proceeded to tell the table a long and discursive story about an experiment he staged, after the assassination, to see if it were possible for a sniper to shoot Kennedy in the manner the assassination was alleged to have happened. “We had trained our people in the mountains during the war”—the Cuban revolution—“on these kind of telescopic sights. So we knew about this kind of shooting. We tried to recreate the circumstances of this shooting, but it wasn’t possible for one man to do. The news I had received is that one man killed Kennedy in his car with a rifle, but I deducted that this story was manufactured to fool people.”

He said his suspicions grew especially pronounced after Oswald was killed. “There was the story of Jack Ruby, who was said to be so moved by the death of Kennedy that he decided to shoot Oswald on his own. That was just unbelievable to us.”

I then asked Castro to tell us what he believes actually happened. I brought up the name of his friend, Oliver Stone, who suggested that it was the CIA and a group of anti-Castro Cubans (I used the term “anti-you Cubans” to describe these forces aligned against Castro) that plotted the assassination.

“Quite possibly,” he said. “This is quite possibly so. There were people in the American government who thought Kennedy was a traitor because he didn’t invade Cuba when he had the chance, when they were asking him. He was never forgiven for that.”

So that’s what you think might have happened?

“No doubt about it,” Fidel answered.

Whether or not Kennedy fell victim to Oswald or an elaborate conspiracy, whose participants have managed to keep quiet all these years, I share Chomsky’s misgivings about his legacy. He was as much a cold warrior as Nixon and LBJ and would likely have escalated U.S. involvement in Southeast Asia.

But we’ll never know, and JFK’s untimely death shook a nation and shattered what little innocence it may have had.

UPDATE (November 24, 2013):

Well, I slightly fudged on my age. I was 16 when Kennedy was killed. But the song says “When I was seventeen,” and not “sixteen.” Forgive me.

Also, I watched Nova‘s piece on using modern forensic science to answer key questions about the assassination, including whether or not a single gunman using the gun implicated in the shooting could have pulled this off. The conclusions:

  • the shots were fired from the School Book Depository Building with the rifle Oswald acquired online
  • the fatal shot came from the rear using the same rifle; it did not come from the front.

It’s the second conclusion that intrigued me most, given my former colleague’s opinion. A group of forensic pathologists and firearms experts, after reviewing the archival evidence, including x-rays and photographs of Kennedy’s skull, opined that the bullet entered from the rear producing extreme pressures within the cranial cavity that exploded the top and upper front of the president’s head. Fractures of the skull were consistent with rear entry. As for Kennedy’s head moving back and to the left, this could be explained again by the forces pushing outward from the skull interior and against Kennedy’s upper spine, creating a rearward jerk of the torso and head.

Nevertheless, if Oswald was the lone assassin, he pulled off a truly remarkable feat, given the type of rifle, the motorcade’s moving away from the building as the shots were fired, the partially obscuring trees, and the incredible accuracy required within a very short time frame.

Oswald himself claimed that he was a “patsy,” suggesting that he was being used by others. Jack Ruby’s gun made sure that Oswald would talk no more.

America sucks—a qualification

November 21, 2013 by davald

In a previous post I suggested that America sucks. I should explain.

First off, America is a country or nation or geographical location. The term ‘America’ is often used to describe the sum total of what and who exists within 50 states and includes our economic and political systems along with considerable real estate. Moreover, whether or not the expression ‘America sucks’ fits depends in large part on one’s point of view.

Truth be told, I’m doing okay, as is my family, including two grown children. We all have sufficient incomes on which to live comfortable lives with some left over to secure decent retirements. Well, then, if I’m okay then you’re okay. Right?

Not really, as I’ve written about on these pages on countless occasions. Millions of Americans struggle mightily to put food on the table and a roof over their heads. It bothers me that rampant economic insecurity exists at all in a country that is so rich in the aggregate. Take this chart, for instance. It shows the Gini index along with the share of income enjoyed by the top ten percent of Americans (data from Emmanuel Saez and FRED).

As the country has grown more unequal (the Gini ratio) those at the top increase their wealth, taking an ever greater share of the economy’s total income. Now think a moment about the red line and where it ends on the right. Just one in ten of us now capture half of all income. One of out of every two dollars made in America goes to that top 10th of households.  Again, these numbers bother me.

Yet, many of us don’t seem to care about such statistics. Perhaps we believe that we are brought into this world as individuals dependent exclusively on our own abilities and fortunes for survival. Few prosper; most do not. Such is life. This ideology promotes suspicion of government, which exists only to ensure property rights and common defense. Though, if I should care about someone else, I am free to help. If I want to join a group or exclude others from the ones to which I belong, I’m free to do so, and no government should say otherwise. Nor, of course, should the government confiscate my wealth to give to Those People. Besides, giving money or services to the destitute only encourages laziness and dependency, moral failings far worse than avarice.

I am describing, loosely, a collage of right-wing attitudes and beliefs that includes the Tea Party, libertarians, devotees of Ayn Rand, and the more conservative strains that still call themselves Republicans. I should probably add those who are apolitical, who avoid the ballot box and choose to ignore those outside their in-group—these are the solipsists.

I concede that one can get through life from birth to death without regard for the well-being of others. It’s also possible that this lifespan may be relatively free of misfortune. Lucky or God’s will. Take your pick.

In that same post linked above I shared my conflicted feelings about watching Bill Moyers’s television program. Each week he interviews people—some famous, most not—who cite appalling numbers quantifying the problems faced by many in America (and the world). The pictures they rhetorically paint make me angry or sad. Almost all of Moyers’s guests devote the majority of their daily lives in service to others, either directly or through advocacy. I am simultaneously disturbed and overwhelmed. What can I do? What should I do? What happens if I do nothing?

I may delude myself in believing that there was a time in America when the notion of commonweal mattered, a stronger sense of society among relative equals. Regardless, it’s dramatically clear to me, at any rate, that we are now a nation deeply divided by religion, race, socioeconomic status, and surely politics. In brief, we cannot agree. Instead, we argue and bicker and get altogether too angry with each other to solve problems collectively, with many of us condemning the very concept of public.

If I’m only slightly correct in this surmise, the future bodes ill.  The problems faced by the Rest of Us are many and acute requiring urgent, concerted action. They will not be resolved by Bill Gates or the Koch brothers or any of the plutocrats who must rely on exponential numbers to record their wealth. Indeed, their intrusions into education, energy, and the political arena as a whole only exacerbate the challenges.

But how depressing this all is. That is, if you care.

26 years ago

October 24, 2013 by davald

Yesterday marked the 26th anniversary of my sister Nancy’s death. Cancer finally beat her, after two and a half years of treatments, surgeries, and a series of unrecoverable setbacks. She was only 39.

I believe that in each family there’s a member that holds everyone together. For ours, Nancy was that person. Her demise unraveled the rest of us, beginning with my mother, who was never the same afterwards.

Nancy Nurse, as we called her, had much to live for, which may explain why she insisted on all those futile surgeries right up to her final days. Her daughter was not yet five, and what mother doesn’t want to live on to share her child’s experiences, from the first day of school to a wedding and a child of her own? Nancy missed out on all of those significant events, and her daughter never had her mother.

Still thinking of you, Nancy.

Chemex coffee snobbery

October 23, 2013 by davald

My grandmother used a Chemex to make her coffee, which she ground from beans purchased at the local grocery store. The white filters she used were carefully folded by her, then often re-used.

As I recall from my youth, she would fill a saucepan with water, then bring it to a slow boil. At the same time she’d heat water in a kettle, which she then poured over the ground coffee in the Chemex that had been placed in the sauce pan with the already hot water. Several pours from the kettle yielded a pot of delicious coffee, or so my grandmother and grandfather believed.

My parents, however, wondered about all the fuss. They were content with Alta coffee (pre-ground in a brown tin can) that they dumped into an old-fashioned percolating coffee pot atop the stove. It looked like mud and probably tasted like it too. No matter. They consumed it from morning to night, cup after cup.

I purchased my own Chemex years ago. The filters are now pre-folded. I grind my own beans with a coffee mill that resembles something from Star Trek. While I love the taste of the coffee from the Chemex I don’t use it all that much, preferring the simplicity of a single-cup pour, repeated as necessary, using #2 filters and a ceramic cone. (My wife generally detests coffee, unless it’s mixed with liquid chocolate and milk in a “mocha”; yes, I have an espresso maker, too.)

Spatial

July 16, 2013 by davald

We sometimes hear the expression, “She’s spatial.” This can be viewed as a good thing, but, on occasion, the adjective can disparage, since we place so much emphasis on “quantitative skills,” the kind measured in achievement tests like the SAT.

I recall receiving the same label as an elementary student. I did well on those tests that required the ability to virtually manipulate objects or to see how different forms found expression in alternative dimensions. Whatever. While I did okay on the standardized tests, my scores did not catapult me into full-ride scholarships at prestigious universities, though throwing a baseball past most hitters did.

As it happens, students that show an aptitude for spatial reasoning tend to me more creative as adults. I missed the memo. Then again, there are exceptions, and I must be one of them. The New York Times:

A gift for spatial reasoning — the kind that may inspire an imaginative child to dismantle a clock or the family refrigerator — may be a greater predictor of future creativity or innovation than math or verbal skills, particularly in math, science and related fields, according to a study published Monday in the journal Psychological Science.

Buy your kid some Legos. You’ll be doing him or her a favor.

“Champ”

July 3, 2013 by davald

I should have mentioned that my grandfather’s nickname for me was “champ.” That delighted yours truly but forced my sisters into counseling. It didn’t help that my grandparents bought me an A2000 baseball glove, after I had conspicuously drooled over an ad for the same in Sports Illustrated. The glove cost over $30 bucks, and this was in the early 60s. Today it costs more than $200 at Amazon.

After seeing only a few of my games, including semi-pro, my grandfather died in 1965 from emphysema, ending 60+ years as a heavy smoker. That was the year I graduated high school and the New York Mets drafted me in the first ever baseball draft. That was also the year that I nearly pitched back-to-back no-hitters. How close? Would you believe one strike? Let me tell you about it.

I recorded two outs in the bottom of the seventh inning (this is high school). I had two strikes on the hitter. He swung and missed at a curve ball. But the catcher missed the pitch entirely, which rebounded off the wooden backstop straight to him. He picked the ball up in plenty of time to throw out the batter running to first; however, he threw it well over the first baseman’s head. Runner safe. The next guy promptly lined a single to left, the only hit his team collected. Damn.

The next day’s newspapers reported that I had “slammed my glove to the ground in disgust.” Yep. The same A2000. My sisters may have derived some satisfaction from the account.

Grampa

July 3, 2013 by davald

I never pronounced the word properly, Grandpa, that is. Too lazy, I guess. But whatever I called him, he was my intellectual mentor, albeit unwittingly—or at least so I’m told. It’s not as if he lectured me on anything or regaled me with stories of his activities or accomplices. Rather, it was more a matter of my being with a genuinely smart guy who read books and talked politics, usually with “colorful metaphors” punctuating his frequent tirades. As a younger adult I, too, became political, somewhat Sixties-style. Thus, the apparent connection across a skipped generation; neither my father nor his brothers seemed at all engaged in current events. 

So, who was Grampa? It was whispered to me on a couple of occasions that he had been a Wobbly, not quite a communist. It was also said that he associated with Chicago radicals during the Great Depression and its preceding decade or two. He even played poker with Clarence Darrow, the notorious and brilliant lawyer of the day. I remembered Grampa as always old, his head enveloped in tobacco smoke, and strong words of rebuke for “the damn Republicans,” otherwise characterized as “bastards.” In full agreement there.

William Hartson Aldrich was born in 1897 somewhere in Iowa, probably near Spencer. I’ve mentioned previously that his family came from money and that he was likely the black sheep, having steered to the left as everyone else was busy making fortunes. Here’s a photograph of him with his older sister, Jessie. 

 

As a young man, perhaps then a member of the International Workers of the World, he came west to work on the railroads and in the Pacific Northwest lumber industry. I gather the sojourn, if it occurred at all, was brief, since he learned early a trade as a linotype operator, having apprenticed with a newspaper. (In the picture below, he’s on the far left.)

 

Grampa subscribed to several radical journals, among them The Masses, a socialist publication, and its successors The Liberator and The New Masses. These were never on display in his apartments, but my grandmother must have kept copies of them, and they were later given to me some years after he died.

Here’s a shot of the September 1911 cover of The Masses:

By the time I was old enough to be cognizant of Grandpa he had evidently abandoned his outward support of radical causes. He voted for Truman and every Democrat who ran for president afterwards, though he was fond of Henry Wallace. There were standard newspapers lying around and lots of books, including a multi-volume biography of Churchill. I would ultimately receive copies of his giant Webster’s Dictionary and the Beards’ History of the United States. (The books fell victim to one of several moves, since I no longer have them. However, I still have his two-volume set of The People Will Judge, an anthology of original writings on American history, going back to pre-colonial times.)

While my grandfather had a love of tobacco, his first love was golf. His three sons would take up the game, with my father developing a life-long obsession. Here’s a photograph of the golfer, with a cigarette in his mouth, of course:

 

I never shared an enthusiasm for golf, believing that it was a waste of a nice walk, in the manner of Twain. But I love baseball, and so did Grandpa.

He saw the greatest in his day and told me about them. Living in Chicago he attended games at Wrigley and Comiskey. He also caught a number of Negro Leagues contests. So I heard about Honus Wagner’s huge hands, the tenacity of Ty Cobb, and Babe Ruth’s home runs. But his favorite players were black: Satchel Paige and Josh Gibson. Of the former Grandpa said that he could “hit the corners of a gum wrapper.” Gibson was without equal. Yet, when I asked him who was the best player of all time, without hesitating he exclaimed, “Willie Mays.” Amen.

I’ll close with several photographs of Grampa.

 

In this next shot my dad is on the left, with his younger brother J.B. standing beside him. Dad would get no taller than this; he was 5’8″ at the age of 13 and the same height when he died in 1978.

 

 

 

These last two photographs are of 50th wedding anniversaries. The first is Grandma and Grandpa’s; the second my mom and dad’s.

 

I can’t resist ending with my parents’ wedding photo:

 

Hands still clasped together after a half-century. That’s a good thing.
