Stripes

June 26, 2013 by davald

In this third installment of an elder’s story (second one here), I’ll mention briefly my parent’s obsession with stripes. A series of photographs of me, beginning at an early age and running through grade school, depicts yours truly in shirts with horizontal layers.

When you’re young, as I was in the above photograph, taken of me near my first birthday, stripes might be considered cute. Here’s another at Thanksgiving in 1952.

I dreaded indoor photographing back then, because the flash bulbs would blind me for minutes. You may have seen Hitchock’s Rear Window wherein Jimmy Stewart attempts to ward off Raymond Burr by setting off a succession of camera flash bulbs. Well, I could certainly relate.

I don’t know the year of this photo; it may predate the previous one. I’m in stripes again, this time wider. My sister Nancy is on the left. I look anxious, with signs of early chubbiness in the face.

And one final picture of the striped young man.

I consider this priceless. Not only am I in stripes, but look at those cuffs! You see, back in the day my pants were “husky” sized, which meant that they were big enough to fit around my waist but much too long for my stubby legs. So I rolled them up from the bottom. Quite the fashion statement for a boy just reaching adolescence. And that posed another problem, as my year-younger sister Nancy was actually taller than I was. Deflating.

I was begot

June 13, 2013 by davald

In this second installment of an elder’s story (the first is here), I’ll talk a bit about my father, who passed away from cancer in 1998 at the age of 76. Were it not for this dreaded disease I’m certain he’d still be playing golf, which was his passion.

He was a member of Tom Brokaw’s “greatest generation” having fought in World War II. Here’s a picture of him looking rather scared in uniform.

He was a pilot for what was then called ‘the Army-Air Force.’ Assignments took him over the Mediterranean, where I presume he bombed and shot at targets, mostly German or Italian. I’m sure that he felt lucky to have survived. He no doubt shared that feeling with his brothers, both of whom came home in one piece, more or less.

In all my conversations with my father and uncles there was no discussion of the Great War. There was nothing about exploits or near-misses or the fact that they must have been frightened at times beyond description. I gather that my dad was the least decorated of the two, as the photo above attests. After my uncles’ deaths, I came across a couple of items describing achievements and acts of heroism of J.B., on the left, and Gene, in the center, the latter receiving a purple heart, as I recall.

With the war over my father wound up near West Point, about 60 miles north of Manhattan. There he met an attractive young secretary who worked for a general and they soon married.

Notice the smile on my dad’s face. It looks forced, and it’s a pose evident in many of the photos I have of him. As I said, there was a marriage.

A year or so later I became the first born of Donald and Florence Aldrich. That was in April of 1947. My father traveled to the San Francisco Bay area to join his brothers, all in search of employment. My mother and her three-month-old son—that would be me—took a non-stop flight aboard a propeller-powered DC 9. 

We would establish a household in a North Richmond rental, part of a large apartment complex populated by young ex-military and their wives. We lived on the bottom floor of a four-unit building that was connected to a line of others along one side of the street.

Precious, isn’t he. I would guess several pounds and months out of the womb. And here is where we lived. Dad, Mom, sister Nancy, and I sitting on the front porch of the Richmond apartment.

 

I have only two distinct memories of the place. One involved a fire that had started in an adjacent unit. No injuries, as I recall, but the event seemed to cause much commotion. The other was about a neighbor’s dead cat, which I discovered in the gutter. I nobly reported the event to the owner. The other memories were vaguer, having to do with climbing up and down stairs to visit the Catanesis, who would become life-long friends. I was told years later that my parents would spend many a weekend evening partying with the neighbors while we were presumably asleep. For shame.

My mother, Florence Dingee, was born and raised in upstate New York. Here is a picture of her parents. Proper.

 

I never saw Grandpa Dingee, though Grandma visited us in the East Bay on a couple of occasions. I do recall the fateful phone call to my mother from one of her siblings announcing the news of Grandpa’s death. The only time, really, that I saw her cry, and there would be several opportunities to do so, including Nancy’s death.

It’s a cruel thing to move far away from your parents and families, especially with a new baby in tow. Back then, travel was either expensive or lengthy, though my parents did drive all the way from California to New York, a journey of some five days. That was when we children were left in the care of my two great aunts, the one resembling a linebacker.

Aunt Nell

 

Before leaving this installment, I’d like to show you two pictures. The first is of my dad and me. 

 

The second is of me with my son, David, twenty-two years later.

An elder’s personal story

June 7, 2013 by davald

Watching the interview with singer Patty Griffin about her newest album, American Kid, I concluded that I’ve been remiss in my duty toward history. I’ll explain.

Ms. Griffin gave some thought to the imminent passing of her father, which led to several songs for the album, aided and abetted by Robert Plant, formerly of Led Zeppelin. She spoke of “elders” and how we tend to ignore them, often by sequestering them in places like Florida, where they go to die. Instead, we should encourage our elders to tell their stories, which would enrich our own lives.

I am an elder—officially. While nothing monumental has occurred in my life so far and there is no chance that I’ll have a page on Wikipedia, I begin here a sporadic account of yours truly—as a record for those who survive me, my children and grandchildren, in particular. For the casual reader, I promise nothing of significance, nothing that will excite or enthrall. Truth be told, you’ll find it rather boring. But, hey, it gives me something to do as the clock ticks away.

As with most biographies, we begin with my ancestors. Since they evidently told no stories, I don’t have much to report. I do have some pictures, however.

Let’s start with my father, who would have been 92 years old today. Here’s some proof that he was born. Note the date.

 

Donald J. Aldrich, my father, was born in Elgin, Illinois, to William Hartsford Aldrich and Mary Louise (Devine) Aldrich. My grandfather was born in Iowa, I’m told. Here’s a photo, the oldest I have, showing W.H. with his sister Jessie, whom I never met and knew nothing about.

A rather striking figure, don’t you think? I’m told that his father came from money, which probably accounts for the professional photograph. My great grandfather, Avery, also struck quite a pose.

My grandfather’s mother belonged to the Dennis family. Again, I know very little about her, other than her divorce from Avery after the birth of my grandfather. Here’s a photograph of Mr. Dennis, who was W.H.’s maternal grandfather. (I could have used “begets,” but that’s already been done.)

The Dennises had a farm. Here’s proof:

The Dennises may have had money, too, judging by the number of farmhands.

I mention the money angle because family lore suggests that W.H., my paternal grandfather, was something of a black sheep. He was more Bohemian than Rockefeller, a reported ancestor. Indeed, William Hartsford Aldrich became a political radical, essentially denouncing property; he never owned real estate and referred to Republicans with the adjective “damn.” We might get a sense of why he chose a different path by looking at his mother and aunt below.

Run for the hills, Grandpa! These women meant business.

W.H. learned a trade. He would support himself and family as a linotype operator, and, I’m told, a darn good one. Here’s a photograph of him working for a local newspaper. My grandfather appears on the far left.

My paternal grandparents, surprise, met then got married. I’ll show you a delightful photograph of the young couple, first.

They were married in Chicago, where they would live for several years.

Now, the announcement says that they moved first to Racine, Wisconsin. News to me. I presume that my grandfather’s job was there, but he would eventually work for one of the Chicago major dailies, before an extended strike forced him to go west, where he joined his three sons after the war.

My paternal grandmother, who was called “Molly,” was born in DeKalb, Illinois, of an Irish immigrant, John Devine, and a woman from New Jersey, named Ellen Dolan. Here’s Molly’s birth certificate.

My grandmother had two sisters and a brother, Uncle Ed. He was a bit daft, shall we say, though not always so. I’ll say a bit more about him later. But first I’ve got to show you this picture of W.H. and the three sisters.

Molly is at the far left. Aunt Margaret is in the middle, with Aunt Nell on the right. (Nell was a battle axe, having forced me to eat corn flakes with water—no milk or sugar—during a brief baby-sitting experience. As you can see, she could have played a linebacker for the Bears.)

I’ll sign off now, but will return with more pictures and narrative. Oh, about Uncle Ed.

The story goes that he was a bright, outgoing young man who fell head over heels for a woman. They became engaged, then she broke it off. That was literally too much for Ed, who suffered a mental breakdown. He never recovered, and was essentially in the care of my two great aunts above. I recall his sitting in front of the television watching a ball game eating soup, which he slurped messily. I think I have a picture of him in his later years. Here is it. He’s in the middle. Spoiler alert: that’s my mother on the far right. Aunt Margaret is just behind Uncle Ed’s left shoulder. I have no idea who the others are.

If only…

May 29, 2013 by davald

Walk or run? I used to do the former but am now confined to the latter.

As it happens, my prior running days may have had much to do with a thinner self, as this article suggests. Now—with the advance of age, arthritis, and bad knees—I walk, though getting up and down stairs offers pain and suffering. An interesting takeaway from the article:

After both sessions, the volunteers were set free in a room with a laden buffet and told to eat at will.

The walkers turned out to be hungry, consuming about 50 calories more than they had burned during their hourlong treadmill stroll.

The runners, on the other hand, picked at their food, taking in almost 200 calories less than they had burned while running.

I can relate.

Spanish wine

May 13, 2013 by davald

I am hardly an enophile, although I generally enjoy the product of fermented grapes. I usually stick with known vintners and varieties. However, I recently took the bait from the local wine shop. It featured a Spanish wine, marked down until supplies run out. I tried one. Then another, and so on. Good stuff, and happy to oblige.

Spain—along with Greece, Portugal and other European peripheral nations—is suffering mightily under imposed austerity. Before the Great Recession, business was booming. Too much, it seems. Capital flowed into Spain from the core nations, funding major asset expansion in the private sector. Spaniards earned rising incomes and spent freely. Then the spigot suddenly closed. Although the government’s debt was minimal before the crisis, it soon rose once tax receipts waned as a consequence of diminished economic activity.

Another consequence is very high unemployment. Among working-age youth the rate is 50 percent. But the government can’t provide much assistance to those out of work, since the heads of the Eurozone insist on “fiscal consolidation” to reduce deficits.

If you’re not working and you get little, if anything, from your government to tide you over, you can’t buy things, including your country’s own wine. Spanish vintners must try to sell their output to foreigners like me.

This piece by Gavyn Davies in the Financial Times addresses the improving Eurozone trade imbalances, which mask underlying problems. He writes:

…the reason for the trade improvement is not wage disinflation or structural reform, but the impact of the crisis and austerity programmes on domestic demand in the south [the southern peripheral nations of Spain, Greece, Italy, and Portugal].

People long out of work become desperate. Desperation leads to disruption both in parliaments and the streets. Nothing left to lose, it would appear. And I’m sure that they could use some of their own wine right now.

The living-downtown experiment

May 1, 2013 by davald

I was born and raised in the suburbs. That’s all I knew until a half-dozen years ago, when my wife and I (though mostly me) decided to take up residence in beautiful downtown Everett, a smallish burg about 30 miles north of Seattle. Of course, there’s nothing beautiful about Everett, an old mill town sputtering to transform itself into a respectable city. But that process is evidently difficult, since nothing has changed since our arrival, save for more boarded-up storefronts, several shuttered restaurants, and two more apartment complexes, one housing low-income “artists.”

The reason people choose to live in cities (as apart from those who must) is the presumed amenities, those things that make urban living not only tolerable but, as in the case of Seattle, for example, enjoyable. My daughter, her husband, and their new baby live on Capitol Hill, just a few minutes’ walk to myriad restaurants, coffee shops, bakeries, hospitals, grocery stores, and places in between. There is a hustle and bustle, where there is pervasive glumness and wanting for things to do in Everett.

In my case, I desired to be within walking distance to my part-time job, serving on the elected board of the public utility. It’s just 15 minutes away, and closer if I get favorable traffic signals. Our one car is a Prius, which my wife drives about a dozen miles roundtrip to a school where she teaches. So, we’re pretty green, as they say. We don’t use much electricity or gasoline.

Unfortunately, Everett is home to forlorn-looking single men, who plod along the sidewalks heading god-knows-where, dog shit, and cigarette smokers. By my informal reckoning, one out of every two people I encounter in my daily walks has a cancer stick hanging out of his or her mouth or clutched between fingers. Their congregations can be mistaken for a building fire, the smoke is so thick.

Oh, and there are banks, lots of them. As I understand, a couple of decades ago the wise city officials thought that it would be a splendid idea to designate downtown Everett the financial hub of the county. At the same time they permitted the construction of a mall several miles south. The predictable result was that longtime retail establishments like The Bon (now Macy’s) and J.C. Penney shut their urban doors to set up shop at the mall. Downtown, then, has dozens of nattily attired bankers and support personnel who, during the noon hours, walk from their offices to the handful of lunch counters then back again. At other hours of the day, the streets are occupied by the aforementioned forlorn, and businesses turn out the lights at five, as employees head for their suburban homes.

You may be getting the picture by now. Everett sucks.

It wasn’t always this way, and there is a large, wall-sized photograph at a non-Starbucks coffee shop of a once-bustling city, with lots of busy pedestrians, automobiles, and street cars. Yep, Everett had trollies on tracks a few decades ago. But that was then.

With one exception, which I’ll get to shortly, this downtown experiment has been a bit more than tolerable, since we live in a rather large apartment on the top floor of a fairly new building. We have splendid views of Mount Rainier (during sunny days), the Snohomish Valley, two mountain ranges, and Puget Sound. Lovely. Except…

When we first moved into our place our immediate neighbor, a single guy, thought the whole world would benefit from his obsession with playing his guitar and singing at the top of his lungs for hours on end. He wouldn’t shut up. To make matters worse, he electrically amplified his instrument. You see, we who live on the top floor at the south of the building share a large deck, with see-through metal railings between our respective spaces. On warmer days, the windows and sliding doors come open. The neighbor’s incessant bellowing and plucking crowd out what is normally a very quiet ambience. And I like quiet, because, as you’ve guessed, I spend a fair amount of my day writing and reading and researching. I don’t like disruption, and the neighbor was all about disrupting my peace.

I complained on several occasions to the building manager. I don’t know if it was cause and effect, but after a few months the guy abruptly moved out. His replacement is the best kind of neighbor. I don’t even notice her. Perfect.

A couple of days ago I experienced my worst nightmare. The guy was back in the building. As I met him coming out of the elevator on the top floor he said that he was living down the hall temporarily. Then, in two months, he would be returning to the same next-door apartment. There was glee in his voice and a hint of sweet revenge. He will once again give me hell.

When I confronted the building manager, reminding him of the troubled history, he merely shrugged, saying that the neighbor’s noise was permissible from early morning to nearly midnight. I disdainfully replied, “Thanks a lot,” before angrily walking away.

My wife and I have concluded that we are merely monthly rent checks to the manager, whose principal task is filling the apartments with anyone willing to pay. What to do?

We will likely return to the suburbs after my wife retires in a couple of months. I can’t say we’ll miss downtown Everett. After all, who likes loud neighbors and piles of dog shit?

Goodbye, Mr. Winters

April 17, 2013 by davald

The scene is a high school library. It’s early morning and yours truly is holding court at one table, surrounded by classmates. While it’s supposed to be quiet, the author is talking, and when he talks the others begin to laugh, at first haltingly, since they don’t wish to incur the wrath of Miss Brubaker, the librarian. But soon they are unable to control themselves, erupting in spasms of side-splitting howls. Now even Miss Brubaker is caught up in the humorous monologues. More often than not the material I presented was stolen from Jonathan Winters, whose one-man skits I had committed to memory. We are in the early 1960s when Winters was king.

As readers no doubt know, Jonathan Winters passed away last week at the age of 87. The news of his death was announced, fittingly enough, on my birthday. Those of you born after Nixon’s fall probably know little of the man. Your comedians are different and, I would argue, less funny. It’s not even close, with the possible exception of Steve Martin.

I was about a third of the way through an homage to Mr. Winters in the New York Times. It was eerily familiar, as the writer shared his experience of watching Jack Parr with his father. On the tube was Jonathan Winters with a stick. I recall vividly watching the same program with my father who rarely laughed. But on this occasion he had fallen off the couch after Winters had thrust the stick into his chest and uttered the words “The United Nations now recognizes the delegate from Nasiland.” The author of the homage was none other than Robin Williams, and most readers know who he is.

One of my favorite skits was “Captain Arnold.” It would be politically incorrect, I suppose, for a modern comic to perform such material these days. Arnold was very gay. Like Ahab, who was a “fantasy,” Arnold was on a quest for the great white whale. When a crew member shouts “thar she blows,” Arnold asks for his spyglass. But he needs “both hands” to hold it, being of less-than-manly stature. And so on.

A few years later I had the good fortune of watching a live performance of Winters at Berkeley’s Greek Theater. The outdoor venue was packed and the weather was calm. If laughter is the best medicine, we thousands were quite well that night.

Not long before this performance Jonathan Winters had climbed the Balclutha, an old vessel moored at the San Francisco Marina. Winters had lost his mind, for which he was committed to a sanatarium. There he managed to overcome depression and, it seems, his alcoholism.

At the Theta Delta Chi fraternity I continued my Winters routines following Monday dinners. The pledges were required to stand up and “tell a joke.” I always received a thumbs up along with the laughter.

Thank you, Mr. Winters. I had nothing else to say.

The perils of PayPal [update]

April 10, 2013 by davald

My wife and I bought a piece of furniture in December that we’ve found to be particularly uncomfortable, as in dangerous to our health if we sit in it for too long a stretch. We paid a handsome price, but now want to shed the recliner for something less injurious. So I took a few photographs then posted one on Craigslist.

Years ago we managed to find a buyer through the service for a room’s worth of couches and chairs and tables and book cases. The person paid cash. He was happy and we were happy. Let’s see if we’ll succeed again.

Not.

Rather quickly after I posted, I got an email from someone who asked initially if I was stuck on the offer price. I replied that the furniture was just four months old and in excellent condition; I’d like to get as much as possible. Now it gets interesting.

The man (at least his email address suggested as much) wrote that he would be acquiring the item via a shipping company. Here was his plan. He would send me the full price plus an additional $500 that I was to wire to that company. Strange. I got suspicious.

I would not be able to access the sale amount presumably deposited in my PayPal account until I had completed the wire transaction. Stranger still, the combined amounts (the one to me and the other to the shipping company) actually exceeded the price that I had originally paid for the item from Macy’s back in December. WTF? The guy could buy the same piece of furniture brand new for less than the total involved in our exchange.

All right, I can be a bit forgiving if not also dim at times. Why would anyone purchase an item for over a thousand dollars sight unseen (save for a single photograph)?

I searched these phrases: “PayPal,” “scam,” “shipping company.” I got back this little gem from another near victim:

I just got several emails from a potential buyer, who says that he is out of town and needs to have a “shipping agent” pick up the merchandise and that he will deposit into my Paypal account.  After giving my PayPal information for the payment, he says that his shipping agent office was closed over the weekend, so he sent me $700 more than my asking price  to my account and asked me for help in wiring money to them for him.  In the meantime, FAKE emails from Service@Paypal start showing up saying that the funding is on hold until I verify that the shipping is paid to the agent via Western Union – thenthe full  funds will be released to my account.  Of course, no pending notifications or anything in my legitimate PayPal account show up.  After my response to the buyer that I did not agree to these terms, he informs me that payment has been made and that if I dont follow through, that legal action will be taken.  I then get another FAKE paypal email that states this will be reported to the FBI unless I comply with payment (out of my own pocket) etc….     Obviously a scam, as the fake legal action paypal email shows a completely different classified ad agency than what I had posted (Craigslist instead of KSL).

Beware of anyone paying the full amount without first inspecting the merchandise or using a “shipping agent” for a pickup.

Such ingenuity of a diabolical kind. Beware.

To answer your question, I did not go through with this charade. Are you interested in some furniture?

Blogging for free

March 6, 2013 by davald

Matthew Yglesias tells us that if you have something to say, say it. But don’t expect to get paid for it.

He started out in college blogging for nothing, then eventually landed paying jobs, the latest of which is at Slate. He writes:

The fact remains that if you have things to say that you think are worthwhile and nobody is offering to pay you to say them, you ought to say them anyway for free. If enough people agree with you that those things are worthwhile, it just may lead to something.

That worked out fine for him. I presume he’s getting a comfortable paycheck now; enough to buy a new house in the D.C. area. I, however, am under no illusion that the New Yorker will be extending me an offer to write for its magazine. Ain’t gonna happen.

As you may have notice from reading this site, there are no ads. I actually have to pay WordPress for the privilege of being ad-free.

I detest ads, for one thing. So, I make no money researching issues, then expressing my viewpoints. Besides, I’d hate to become dependent on people’s visiting The Observer so that a few mills are sent my way upon each click of the mouse.

And speaking of ads, I often wonder, more so recently, why Google’s stock continues to climb while, for example, Apple’s falters. After all, Google gives away its Android operating system to the likes of Samsung and HTC and a myriad other cellphone manufacturers. So it doesn’t make any money off its OS. The lion’s share of Google’s revenues come from mobile ads, the kind that populate webpages—the good, the bad, and the ugly. It also charges retailers to appear in its search findings. I understand that Google’s price-to-earnings ratio is about three times that of Apple, and Apple makes more profit in a quarter than Google makes in a year.

This article in Bloomberg News offers an explanation for the divergence.

Investors are willing to pay more for each dollar of Google’s earnings relative to Apple amid optimism that Google, with more than 40 percent of the U.S. online-advertising market, will command even more of the $37.3 billion that businesses spend each year to reach Web audiences. [Note: Apple’s last-quarter profits exceeded this annual total.]

But I have to ask: Does anyone actually buy something as a result of seeing a Google ad? I, for one, don’t even notice them. Or perhaps it’s all subliminal, that I’m drawn to make my next car a Jaguar and my next vacation in the Bahamas. But there’s a problem for the advertiser. I will likely never buy another car; the 2nd-generation Prius has low miles and will surely outlast me. Nor do I like to travel, anywhere. The joke around here is that I’ve been to Bothell.

I do understand that it would be easy to join the crowd. I could invite companies to post their ads on my blog. They’d pay Google for the privilege, I guess, in the apparent hope that one out of every 10 readers (about the total thus far) would click on the ad then purchase a whatever.

As I said, I don’t get it. Blogging for free I get. Alms, anyone?
