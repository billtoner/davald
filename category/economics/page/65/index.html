Politics as economics

April 10, 2012 by davald

Our political system is a duopoly; only two parties call the shots. It will likely stay that way, because vested interests so desire.

A principal interest is the “money-and-media election complex.” As media coverage of elections wanes, increasing revenues flow into the coffers of mostly television stations and networks.

One acquaintance we know who was running for governor of a Midwest state as a Democrat in a general election complained to a TV station manager that his campaign was not getting any news coverage. “You want to get on the air?” the broadcaster replied. “Buy an ad.” Whether intentional or not, commercial broadcasters have little incentive to give away for free what has become a major profit center for them.

That quote appears in a comprehensive look at the “complex” by Robert McChesney and John Nichols. Writing for Monthly Review magazine, the authors report that political advertising is a “bull market.”

The United States is in the midst of its quadrennial presidential election, a process that now extends so long as to be all but permanent. The campaign is also drenched in more money given by a small handful of billionaires than has been the case in the past. Since the 1970s the amount spent on political campaigns has increased dramatically in almost every election cycle. It has led to the formation of what we term the “money-and-media election complex,” which has a revenue base in the many billions of campaign dollars donated annually, and has effectively become the foundation of electoral politics in the United States. Moreover, the rateof increase in campaign spending from 2008 to 2010, and especially from 2008 to 2012, is now at an all-time high.

They tell us that Madison Avenue eventually took control of political campaigns, coinciding with Joe McGinniss’s The Selling of the President in 1969. Pitching a candidate was much like pitching toothpaste, and in advertising repetition works. Message. Repeat. Repeat. Repeat.

The book, which involved McGinniss spending time with Nixon’s television advertising advisors including Roger Ailes during the 1968 presidential campaign, seemed shocking and a sharp departure from the political-driven campaign narratives provided by the likes of White. McGinniss documented how Nixon came to rely upon TV political commercials, based on Madison Avenue marketing principles, as the foundation of his campaign. In the book Ailes presciently concludes immediately after the November victory, “This is the beginning of a whole new concept. This is it. This is the way they’ll be elected forevermore.” It is ironic that today, when one reads the book, it seems downright quaint, even homespun, in comparison to subsequent elections. The liberal McGinniss is able to wander through the corridors of power in Nixon’s campaign like a serendipitous hippie roaming around at Woodstock.

Nor does political propaganda, like commercial advertising, attempt to differentiate products in terms of facts and figures. Watch any television commercial to gain factual data and you will be disappointed. It’s all about attitude and emotion. How do you feel after watching a piece on Product X? Similarly, how do you feel about Candidate Y after watching his commercial?

McChesney and Nichols focus on negative advertising, the emerging staple of political propaganda. The objective is to incense the viewer, making him or her angry at the other guy. This strategy departs from the commercial realm. The reason becomes clear: negative advertising about a competitor’s pickup truck, for example, will engender cynicism about all pickup trucks, even yours. In politics, however, creating cynicism and, better yet, complete voter apathy is a good thing.

Negative advertising can be tremendously effective, a smashing over-the-top boffo success, even if it does not generate a single new voter for the candidate (or supportive independent group) placing the ad. If it simply takes voters leaning toward the opponent and makes them less likely to vote for the opponent, maybe not vote at all, that is a victory. After all the point is to get the most votes and if you lower the number for the opponent, that has the same effect as increasing your own total. Moreover negative advertising can have the delicious side effect of forcing an opponent to respond to charges, no matter how spurious. Negative advertising can amplify spectacularly the classic political move captured by the story of the politician who told his campaign manager to start a rumor that his opponent was a child molester. “But he isn’t a child molester, is he?” responded the aide. “Of course not,” said the candidate, “but I want to hear him deny it.”

With the Supreme Court’s disastrous ruling in Citizens United we ain’t seen nothing yet. The hundreds of millions of dollars accruing in Super PACs will give new meaning to negative advertising.

In the April 2012 issue of Harper’s Magazine editor Thomas Frank tells us that “It’s a Rich Man’s World: How billionaire backers pick America’s candidates.”

While visiting Kansas City last December, I read a local newspaper story lamenting the gradual transformation of Missouri into a reliably Republican citadel—a red state, as we like to say. In the past, I read, Missouri had been different from its more partisan neighbors. It had been a “bellwether” state that “reflected national trends,” rather than delivering votes for any particular party. But now all that was over, and I assumed the article would go on to mourn the death of judicious public reason—the tradition of giving rival arguments a hearing and testing them with that famous “Show Me” skepticism.

I was wrong. Forget the death of open-mindedness. What was actually being mourned that day in the Kansas City Star was a possible loss of advertising revenue by the state’s TV stations. If Missouri was no longer a battleground state, then the two parties and their various backers would no longer fight their expensive electronic war over the airwaves between St. Louie and St. Joe, and “spending on TV ads in the state [would] plummet.”

Franks provides us with a clear example of the “money-and-media election complex.” In tightly contested political races, even at the local level, campaign dollars flow directly to the TV stations. McChesney and Nichols:

Wall Street stock analysts can barely contain themselves as they envision the growing cash flow. “Voters are going to be inundated with more campaign advertising than ever,” one investor service wrote in 2011. “While this may fray the already frazzled nerves of the American people, it is great news for media companies.” “No one loves a good political brawl like a U.S. broadcast company. The fiercer the fight, the more money broadcasters can expect from campaign advertising—particularly in an era when political rhetoric grows more heated every day,” Moody’s Investors Service wrote in a special 2011 report touting media stocks as a good buy. “There are good political years, and then there are years like 2012, when speculative-grade, pure-play television broadcasters expect an unprecedented frenzy of political advertising amid an intense battle for control of both the White House and a closely divided Congress.” As Carl Salas of Moody’s put it, “Virtually all U.S. broadcasters will benefit from spending on political ads in 2012.”

One analyst likened political ads to Christmas shopping for retail outlets. The bulk of television profits are from campaign checkbooks, and it’s easy to see why. There are virtually no costs assumed by the stations; the political ads are already in the can ready for broadcast. And this year, a presidential one, will likely generate massive and record net revenues for TV stations across the country.

It wasn’t supposed to be this way, of course. After all, the airwaves are “owned” by the public. Television networks and local stations receive licenses from the government on the condition that they operate in the public’s interest. One public service envisaged by Congress and the FCC was political coverage. Television was expected to report on electoral issues. But major media have essentially blocked efforts to give effect to the original intent. As mentioned above, if a candidate wants coverage he can pay for it. There is almost zero incentive for television stations to cover campaigns.

McChesney and Nichols conclude thusly:

The U.S. electoral system is wallowing in a sea of money, idiocy, and corruption precisely at the moment the nation’s growing problems demand solutions that work to the benefit of the vast majority of Americans—the 99 percent—who have no role in the current regime except to be manipulated and exploited. This is going to be a defining political struggle going forward, until it is resolved.

Green vehicles

April 5, 2012 by davald

The New York Times reports that owners of more efficient vehicles may not recoup their investment before they replace them. That holds true at even $5/gal. gas prices; they’re currently at $4 or so.

But the article ignores externalities. Burning gasoline emits harmful pollutants, including greenhouse gases. Would incorporating an implicit carbon tax justify the additional expense of buying a hybrid version? As you’ll see, if you can stick with me for a bit, is that the Prius is a better deal than, say, a Camry—even without imposing a carbon tax. But the Civic hybrid may or may not justify its higher costs over the non-hybrid model. The Times says no; my calculations suggest barely. So, let’s take a look at carbon emissions.

Each gallon of combusted gasoline releases about 20 pounds of carbon dioxide, the principal greenhouse gas. If a conventional vehicle averages 20 mpg, a 60-mile round-trip commute would consume three gallons and contribute 60 pounds of carbon dioxide. Let’s assume a five-day workweek for 50 weeks a year. That adds up to 15,000 pounds of carbon dioxide annually, or 7.5 tons.

What is the “value” of a ton of CO2? Estimates vary. The Carbon Tax Center includes this table showing different proposed taxes.

Looking at the 2020 column we see that the tax ranges from a low of $22.31/ton to $115/ton. In our above example, the lowest tax would add $167.33 to the cost of an annual commute. Using the highest figure, that additional cost would be $862.50.

Assuming gas at $4 a gallon, a year’s worth of commuting in a 20-mpg car would cost $3,000 at the pump (3 gallons/day x five days/wk. x 50 wks. x $4/gal.). Adding in the carbon tax yields a range of $3,167.33 to $3,862.50, or between 5.6 percent and 29 percent more than the fuel costs alone.

My wife and I happen to drive a Toyota Prius. I’ll assume the New York Times‘ price of $23,537, which is roughly what we paid for ours. The Times estimates that the car pays for itself in 1.8 years, without factoring in a carbon tax. We’ve been getting about 41 to 45 mpg, depending on which one of us is driving and the nature of the trip, whether it’s mostly freeway or in-town. The Times assumed 49.7 mpg, which may be the case with the latest model.

For comparison’s sake, I’ll assume 40 mpg for the Prius, or twice the rate used in the above example. With the same commute numbers, I’d use half the gasoline, or 375 gallons per year rather than 750. At $4/gal. for gas, I’d save $1,500 a year.

But I’d also cut carbon emissions in half, from 15,000 pounds/yr. to 7,500 pounds (3.75 tons). At the lower tax rate, the Prius’s carbon tax would be $83.66; at the highest, the tax would be $431.25 per year.

The Times assumed that the average vehicle owner keeps his or her car for six years. Over that period the Prius costs $36,804, including a $50/ton carbon tax. A sample vehicle, one costing $28,400, the average price American’s spend on an automobile, and obtaining 20 mpg, would cost $44,464 over the same period. Clearly the Prius saves us money. (I’ve assumed a $5,000 down payment and 5% interest over 48 months.)

Let’s look at another comparison, one done by the Times. In this case we’ll use a Honda Civic and its companion hybrid. The former costs $18,675 at the dealer, according to the Times; the hybrid model costs $23,999. Assuming a $50/ton carbon tax and $4 for a gallon of gasoline, the six-year costs of the Civic would be $32,512; the hybrid, $31,503. The Times calculated a payback for the hybrid 12.1 years, or twice as long as most people hang onto their vehicles before replacing them. When I ran the numbers, even assuming no carbon tax, the Civic Hybrid costs slightly less over the six-year period than the conventional model. (I can’t explain the disagreement, although it’s likely I messed up—somehow.)

It appears, then, that if one wanted to save money and reduce emissions, stick with the Prius. Electric vehicles, says the Times, won’t pay back their premium for two and a half decades. But, once again, I ran the numbers.

The Nissan Leaf, for example, gets about 3 miles per kilowatt-hour. The 60-mile commute used in the above example would require 20 kWh. At a retail rate of 8.3 cents/kWh (the PUD’s retail rate), that works out to $1.66, versus the Prius’s $6 for the back-and-forth trip. The Times compares the Leaf with the Nissan Versa, a gas-powered car. I calculated the six-year costs of the Versa at $25,242; the Leaf, $31,788—again, assuming a $50/ton carbon tax. With no tax on carbon, the six-year difference is about $8,000 in favor of the Versa.

Thus, a purely electric vehicle neither saves money nor captures the externalities of carbon dioxide emissions, even with a $50/ton tax. How high a carbon tax would be needed to equalize the six-year costs between the Versa and the Leaf? Would you believe $294/ton? This is more than twice the highest proposed tax in the above table.

By the way, my wife and I love our Prius. It’s roomy, comfortable, with surprising spunk. Love the hatchback, too.

A debate on inequality

April 4, 2012 by davald

There has been some recent research (and here) purporting to demonstrate that income inequality retards economic growth. Moreover, in the U.S. especially, the very poor tend to stay that way as do the very rich, belying claims for social mobility.

The Atlantic‘s Derek Thompson summarizes studies on both sides of the question: Does income inequality constrain economic growth? He begins with a quote from Pres. Obama:

What drags down our entire economy is when there is an ultra-wide chasm between the ultra-wealthy and everyone else.

After reading the summaries the reader is left frustrated. If economics is an empirical science, why so much variance around a rather straightforward proposition?

At the end of his piece, Thompson turns to James K. Galbraith on the subject of growing inequality.

What explains this? Maybe it’s the nature of the global economy. In a fascinating review of data from the University of Texas Inequality Project, James K. Galbraith revisits the famous Kuznets curve … and adds another curve. Income inequality rises in an industrial revolution, falls as the country grows and develops, and then rises again. “For some of the richest countries, notably capital goods exporters and the small oil producers, rising incomes are associated with rising inequality once again,” he writes. Here’s that theory in a graph:

Dean Baker, author of The End of Loser Liberalism: Making Markets Progressive, wrote in that book:

Money does not fall up. Yet the United States has experienced a massive upward redistribution of income over the last three decades, leaving the bulk of the workforce with little to show from the economic growth since 1980. This upward redistribution was not the result of the natural workings of the market. Rather, it was the result of deliberate policy, most of which had the support of the leadership of both the Republican and Democratic parties.

He submits that the rising inequality experienced elsewhere is also the result of the same or similar policies, part of a concerted effort to siphon money from the Rest of Us and into the pockets of the very rich. Baker did not make Thompson’s list. But you can download your own free copy here (pdf).

Baker, returning to Obama, excoriates the president:

How could progressives have allowed themselves to be beaten back? The root of the problem is that President Obama and the leadership of the Democratic Party backed away from telling the truth about the economic collapse. By refusing to “demonize” Wall Street and the rest of the financial industry for the damage inflicted on the economy and the nation, the president and his party left a huge void that has been filled with alternative stories.

Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson, authors of Winner-Take-All Politics, offer a similar narrative. Four decades ago conservatives, who were clearly in the minority at the time, mounted a systematic effort to reframe the debate to gain electoral victories. Money from the One-percent flowed into think tanks, whose policy wonks fed the media, who dutifully spread the new gospel, priming the electorate to accept a different ideology. Its salient features are familiar to us all: unfettered markets are good, regulation is bad, unions reward unproductive workers, government is essentially evil, the minimum wage costs workers jobs, “entitlements” must be reformed, public education is riddled with lousy teachers—and so on.

Most fascinating about the new conservative strategy is that so many citizens set aside their socioeconomic status to embrace these features. That is, people ignored the fact that they had little in common with the One-percenters as they championed tenets of religious fundamentalism, political libertarianism, and rugged individualism. Entitlements were out. Unions were out. The government was out. Democrats were out. (Thomas Frank wondered aloud “What’s the matter with Kansas?”—the title of one of his books, this one about his native state. At the turn of the last century, Kansas had been home to rabid populism, including large swaths of socialism. Today, the state is as conservative as they come, and in Frank’s view, the people there have voted against their own economic interests by electing Republicans.)

Baker et al. believe that progressives are in the same boat as conservatives were forty years ago. But instead of adopting a “loser-liberalism” attitude, progressives should take lessons from the resurgent conservatives. Among other things, that means shifting the debate in favor of the Rest of Us by first pushing an alternative narrative, then developing and executing a coherent strategy to win the hearts and minds of the electorate. First and foremost, this back-from-the-wilderness effort should clearly demonstrate that people are better off under progressive leadership than they are under a “you’re-on-your-own” ideology. It’s in our self interests to reject the conservative mindset and adopt a more sociocentric attitude, one that appreciates that government can and should be a mechanism for increasing equality and protecting the Rest of Us against the vagaries of life.

It’s a plausible argument: if government can be co-opted by the One-percent for their benefit, it can be reclaimed for the Rest of Us. Do you wish to be a winner or a loser? We make the call.

Here comes China

April 2, 2012 by davald

“It is now a question of how many years, rather than how many decades, before China replaces the United States as the largest economy in the world,” he [Wang Jisi] adds.

China’s financial successes, starting with weathering the 1998 Asian financial crisis and the 2008 global financial crisis, the execution of events like the Beijing Olympics in 2008 and the Shanghai Expo in 2010, contrast with America’s “alarming” deficit, sluggish economic recovery and polarized domestic politics, Mr. Wang says.

— New York Times

The above chart, based on data from the Economist magazine, vividly illustrates America’s sore thumb. We’re importing far more than we’re exporting. Germany is the opposite, followed closely by China. Our negative account balance hasn’t always been the case, though.

Bill Moyers had Andrew Bacevich on his program again. Bacevich, a retired military officer and now a professor at Boston University, believes that America’s century is over, and the sooner we accept that fact, the sooner we’ll be able to make the necessary adjustments. Empires may come and go, but nation states survive, for better or worse.

BILL MOYERS: So what does it mean if the United States is no longer the sun around which everything else orbits?

ANDREW BACEVICH: It means that history has moved on. It means that the 21st century is in all likelihood, to the extent that we can foresee the future, and we must all acknowledge that our capacity to do so is very limited, but to the extent that we think we can glimpse the future, the 21st century is going to be a multipolar order. There are going to be some number, bigger than one, some number of powers who together will either create order or replicate the catastrophes that occurred in the first half of the 20th century, when the last multipolar order collapsed.

China’s going to be one of those powers. Probably India is going be one. In its way, in its peculiar way, Europe is going to be one of those powers that we have to reckon with. To some degree Russia, maybe Brazil. And emphatically, the United States of America. But we are not going to call the shots.

In addition to its sheer size, China isn’t hampered by “polarized domestic politics.” Congress can’t get anything done. Part of that could be deliberate among those who are in Washington to wreck the government. But most of the inertia owes to sharp ideological differences.Whatever the causes, Congress is stymied. The Chinese government moves quickly, forcefully, and in pursuit of specific goals.

We might get some sense for this if we imagined that there were only one political party in America, say the Democratic Party. Regardless of what you think of the Democrats’ priorities, we can all agree that if it could act in the same manner as the Chinese government does, things would be much different in the U.S. We’d likely have universal health care coverage (probably single payer), free education, generous pensions, higher union participation rates, better transportation systems, less poverty, and much less inequality, which means far fewer and smaller fortunes.

We could do the same on the other side, imagining a Republican political monopoly. Both education and health care would be judged a privilege rather than a right. Thus, all schools would be private and subject to market forces. Many federal agencies would be abolished, including the EPA and probably any department charged with taking climate change seriously. Tax rates would be lowered even further, perhaps with zero corporate tax. Federal spending, especially on entitlements (Social Security, Medicaid, and Medicare), would be slashed.

However we imagine a future under one-party rule, our existing system of “checks and balances” ensures stagnation. That could place us at a decided disadvantage vis-à-vis China.

Yet, no matter how soon China overtakes the U.S. economy, we’re not likely to alter our system of government—perhaps ever. Certainly in the short term dominant U.S. politics will condemn anyone who dares to suggest that America has lost its hegemony and is no longer Number One. We’ll have to go through this messy period of recriminations before the evidence overwhelms, forcing us to acknowledge the obvious. Or we may find it impossible to break the shackles of tradition and myth. We may be victimized by our perceived exceptionalism, unable and unwilling to entertain thoughts of inferiority, even though we are clearly inferior in may important respects (e.g., health care, education, transportation).

In the final analysis, what “we” think probably doesn’t amount to much. As always, the most important consideration is the well-being of the One-percent. Given the global scale of the economy, the extremely wealthy have no doubt put in place measures that protect and grow their portfolios in a scheme of heads they win, tails the Rest of Us lose. Even the sovereignty of nations pales against the priorities of the rich, who respect no borders, only their wallets, pitting one nation against another, if deemed expedient. Our role is to salute the flag, as if it mattered, thus perpetuating the fiction.

Econ 101

April 2, 2012 by davald

The recent financial crisis, in particular the collapse of the housing bubble and its fallout, has generated renewed interest in the “dismal science.” Students who flock to Econ 101 are anxious to learn what happened, how did the world get itself into such a mess? This mini-forum in the New York Times (may be subject to paywall restrictions) provides some incites into lessons learned and lessons ignored.

One casualty of economic thinking, and good riddance, I say, is the heavy reliance on mathematical modeling, as if a global economy of over six billion agents and trillions of dollars in transactions could be mapped and its outcomes predicted. One of the forum participants fled the U.K. academia in favor of broader perspectives offered in French universities. She included this formula in her brief essay:

It’s from an introductory textbook on economics, and it presumes to predict financial crises. She was not convinced. Nor should we.

Another casualty is hubris. Economists do not know what they claim to know. Moreover, attempts to equate the profession with natural sciences like physics are not only wrongheaded but dangerous. Doing so invites economists to shoehorn data or make rather silly assumptions about human behavior—to fit the models, judged sacrosanct, regardless of the evidence.

A further difficulty with trying to model different economic policy prescriptions is the problem of counterfactuals. What might have happened if there had been no TARP, or federal stimulus package, or an even larger package advocated by many economists (e.g., Christine Romer and Paul Krugman)? This from Jeffrey Miron, one of the forum members:

The principal reason for our imperfect understanding is that macroeconomists and policy makers do not get to “run experiments” with the economy, and that is the only way to learn its true structure and determine how policies affect its performance.

Another commenter warns of what Hyman Minksy called “speculative euphoria.” When times are good, memories recede, optimism reigns, and here we go again—with another bubble.

In fact, when times are good, then each loan seems like a sure bet, and firms that were previously constrained from borrowing can borrow – and overborrow. Easy access to credit further reinforces the good times, and an unsustainable lending boom ensues – unless it is constrained by regulation or some sort of countervailing policy. The crisis highlighted the existence of these self-reinforcing feedback loops, and we ignore them at our peril.

Perhaps the biggest casualty of the Great Recession is retrenchment. Both policymakers and economists seem frozen in their intellectual nakedness. The profession was “wrong,” or at least inadequate to the task. While policymakers are either reluctant to commit resources to the challenge of putting people to back to work or are hopelessly mired in flawed economic analysis (see, for example, Paul Ryan’s “fraudulent” budget).

One thing may be certain. More people will take up the study of economics. But unless they do so with the qualifier “as if people matter,” they will repeat the sins of the past resulting in more of the same—the One-percenters against the Rest of Us.

Pouvez vous dire des pauvres?

April 1, 2012 by davald

Even the French are feeling the pinch of broad European austerity measures. This is a depressing article from the New York Times, which reveals increasing marginalization of the people in a country known for its generous welfare state and haute cuisine. Consider some statistics from the Times:

  • In France, half the nation’s workers earn less than $25,000.
  • While the country seems to exude prosperity, the number of working poor is up from 6.1 percent in 2006, and experts predict it will grow.
  • Today, up to 120,000 people are living in French campgrounds, according to Observatoire des Inégalités, a social watchdog group. While it is not a new phenomenon, officials say it is accelerating.

But like the United States and most of Europe, increasing public debt along with widespread belief that governments must curtail spending have made matters worse, especially for the growing ranks of the poor. The Economist magazine also turns a spotlight on France:

The awkward truth is that France, the second-biggest economy in the euro zone after Germany, faces a public-finance squeeze. French public spending now accounts for 56% of GDP, compared with an OECD average of 43.3%: higher even than in Sweden. For years France has offered its people a Swedish-style social model of services, benefits and protection, but has failed to create enough wealth to pay for it.

The Economist‘s recommendation is for France to institute labor reforms (i.e., lower wages and benefits) to increase the competitiveness of its workers. Certainly its unit labor costs are much higher than those of Germany, as this chart from the magazine makes clear:

Moreover, France’s current account balance is negative, as is the U.S.’s. Germany is running a trade surplus of over $200 billion; France’s deficit is over $63 billion (Economist). By the way, the U.S. is running a trade deficit of more than $470 billion.

It could be a matter of timing: recession, high unemployment, along with debt overhang from better days. Perhaps austerity measures, which are understandable reactions to high public debt, may make matters worse. Perhaps further these are extraordinary times that undermine conventional wisdom. Paradoxically, the solution to excessive debt may be more of it.

Paul Krugman cites a new study by Paul McCulley. From the report (pdf):

Fiscal austerity does not work in a liquidity trap and makes as much sense as putting an anorexic on a diet. Yet, “diets” are the very prescriptions that fiscal austerians have imposed (or plan to impose) in the U.S., U.K. and Eurozone. Austerians fail to realize, however, that everyone cannot save at the same time and that in liquidity traps, the paradox of thrift and depression are fellow travelers that are functionally intertwined.

…

The importance of fiscal expansion and the impotence of conventional monetary policy measures in a liquidity trap have profound implications for the conduct of central banks. This is because in a liquidity trap, the fat tail risk of inflation is replaced by the fat tail risk of deflation. In turn, the fatness of the deflation tail is a function of the government’s willingness and ability to pump-prime, i.e. to borrow and spend.

McCulley concludes:

Unless fiscal stimulus is deployed with force to offset the loss of demand from private deleveraging in a liquidity trap, enduring economic slack, deflationary pressures and the risk of depression may arise. In the topsy-turvy world of liquidity traps, adherence to fiscal and monetary orthodoxies can be costly, and acting irresponsibly relative to orthodoxy can work.

But few policymakers will dare jettison conventional wisdom, believing that the present situation falls within normal business-cycle trends. They make excessive debt a moral issue, requiring stern medicine in the form of fiscal belt-tightening and, in effect, the persistent suffering of the poor and near-poor. They still fear inflation just around the corner, again because they fail to recognize just how extraordinary are the current circumstances. Liquidity traps, for example, are extremely rare.

As long as we continue to think that we’re either in a new normal or that the economy will simply recover spontaneously, we will eschew proper prescriptions, thus prolonging the misery.

Ideology baked in

March 30, 2012 by davald

The House of Representatives is currently in the clutches of very conservative Republicans. Yesterday the House passed a conservative budget, which stands little, if any, chance of gaining traction in the Senate. The budget, predictably, is all about cuts to entitlement programs and even lower taxes on the wealthy. Ideology triumphs over compassion. (What an oxymoron that was, “compassionate conservatism.” Today’s definition of conservative is precisely about imposing more suffering on the less fortunate so as to raise the fortunes of the One-percenters.)

Yet, even in the House, ideology gets tested or countered. Nor is it permanent.

Not so in the Supreme Court. Once an ideologue is emplaced, it’s baked in until he or she dies, or can’t hold it any longer while sitting on the bench.

In his column this morning Paul Krugman accuses the conservative Supremes of bad faith and willful ignorance. Krugman:

Let’s start with the already famous exchange in which Justice Antonin Scalia compared the purchase of health insurance to the purchase of broccoli, with the implication that if the government can compel you to do the former, it can also compel you to do the latter. That comparison horrified health care experts all across America because health insurance is nothing like broccoli.

Why? When people choose not to buy broccoli, they don’t make broccoli unavailable to those who want it. But when people don’t buy health insurance until they get sick — which is what happens in the absence of a mandate — the resulting worsening of the risk pool makes insurance more expensive, and often unaffordable, for those who remain. As a result, unregulated health insurance basically doesn’t work, and never has.

I was especially struck by Krugman’s quote of Charles Fried, which comes from an interview with the Washington Post:

“I’ve never understood why regulating by making people go buy something is somehow more intrusive than regulating by making them pay taxes and then giving it to them.”

But you never know. This Supreme Court is capable of leaping from a narrow judgment to an expansive ruling that gives new meaning to judicial activism. I have in mind the decision on Citizens United, which could have been narrowly interpreted, leaving essential campaign restrictions in place. No, these guys (and the conservatives are all guys) opted to use this case to: (a) affirm that corporations are persons; and (b) equate money with speech. Now we have the predictable emergence of “super PACs,” which have no limits on receipts or expenditures, provided the entities keep arms length from particular candidates. Wink. Nod.

This Court, then, could perversely respond to Fried’s argument with a decision that makes government taxation unconstitutional. If everything the government does can be related to broccoli, then surely the ability to tax us is fair game.

Krugman worries:

…But it’s hard not to feel a sense of foreboding — and to worry that the nation’s already badly damaged faith in the Supreme Court’s ability to stand above politics is about to take another severe hit.

Increasing inequality

March 29, 2012 by davald

 

Following WWII, the U.S. population was increasing income equality. The trend reversed direction in the late 60s and early 70s. Inequality has been steadily increasing since then. (I’ve downloaded the data from the St. Louis FED site.)

We hear much lately about structural changes in the U.S. employment picture. During the 50s and 60s we built things. These manufacturing jobs were mostly union-wage, giving rise to a robust middle class of blue-collar workers. But manufacturing jobs began to fall.

 

The downward plunge began in earnest under Ronald Reagan, held steady for several years under Bush I and Clinton. When Bush II entered the White House, manufacturing jobs left.

The decline in union participation preceded the decline in manufacturing. Did the former contribute to the latter?

There appears to be a correlation between union density rates and the Gini index in international comparisons.

 

Sweden has the highest union rates and the lowest Gini index. At the other end of the scale, the U.S. has the lowest union rate and the highest Gini index.

Just saying.
