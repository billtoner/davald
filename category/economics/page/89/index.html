The housing bubble

August 30, 2011 by davald

Economist Dean Baker, who blogs here, both identified the housing bubble and predicted its collapse, then estimating a drop in asset value of some $8 trillion. He was prescient, and now reminds just about anyone who would listen or read that we’re currently in such a mess because of the collapse.

The reasons are simple: with stagnating wages, people treated their homes like ATMs, allowing them to continue spending money on goods and services (representing 70 percent of GDP), while sharply dropping their savings. With home values plummeting, so has spending; the savings rate has risen, also. With reduced spending comes reduced industrial activity comes layoffs and wage freezes—and so on.

This recent housing bubble was the mother of all housing bubbles, as this graphic depicts. (The original chart prepared by the New York Times. It was updated—the red dotted line—by Steve Barry for The Big Picture.)

Some creative individuals have produced a clever animation. You’re on a roller coaster, following the curves of the Case-Shiller Index. Hold on tight.

Do facts matter?

August 29, 2011 by davald

I’ve often thought it a liberal conceit that one need only present facts to prevail in arguments. Joe believes x, but is told that the truth is y. Therefore, Joe abandons x and embraces y. Unfortunately, that’s not the way people decide issues. Like George Bush’s infamous quote, it’s more a matter of “the gut.”

In his column this morning Paul Krugman scares us. He merely recounts some sobering poll results that show Republicans believing the darndest things. Among the statistics:

According to Public Policy Polling, only 21 percent of Republican voters in Iowa believe in global warming (and only 35 percent believe in evolution).

Republicans, as a rule, also believe that Obama is responsible for most of what ails us. Now, I have my truck with the president, as I’ve revealed here on numerous occasions, but I know—don’t I?—that Bush spent more and took a federal surplus into the toilet.

The folks at Truthout.org are being helpful. They provided me, and you, with three charts that show my view of the world is more accurate than the Republicans’.

Whose administration spent more federal dollars?

What about the deficit?

And as for jobs?

Before I get carried away with these comparisons, I don’t think it’s appropriate to either credit or blame a president for all events that happen on his watch. However, it is appropriate to take issue with those who practice “the blame game,” especially if they’re obviously unfamiliar with the facts—or, more likely, choose to ignore them.

It would be comforting, nonetheless, to know that Americans, by and large, decide on whom to vote for by consulting the more obvious truths. For example, it would be silly to elect one of the Republican candidates because one thought that the GOP was better at creating jobs. The last chart, certainly, should disabuse one of that notion.

Also, if you believed that innovation in science and technology would help spur economic growth you really shouldn’t vote for Michelle Bachman, who said that hurricanes were signs from God. Nor should you select Rick Perry, who dismisses both evolution and global warming.

That said, Perry, the dimmer version of G.W., is now leading all other GOP candidates by double digits. Who are these people, the Republicans?

Krugman concludes his column thusly:

Now, we don’t know who will win next year’s presidential election. But the odds are that one of these years the world’s greatest nation will find itself ruled by a party that is aggressively anti-science, indeed anti-knowledge. And, in a time of severe challenges — environmental, economic, and more — that’s a terrifying prospect.

But, hey, Americans elected G.W. not once but twice. Well, that’s what the Supreme Court said, anyway, in the first go around.

In times of economic gloom, too many people lose their heads. Unfortunately, there’s always a sufficiently large pool of professional candidates who are willing to exploit the madding crowd. After all, they’re one of them.

More on cities

August 26, 2011 by davald

James McCusker, who writes a weekly column for the Everett Herald, devoted today’s entry to “urbanization costs have taken a heavy toll,” the heading for his piece. Therein, McCusker, an economist living in Bothell, writes:

Ordinary people recognized that simply adding population didn’t translate into economic growth. Instead, most often it would translate into higher taxes.

The underlying cause of higher taxes also relates to something that the American people understood a lot better than their politicians: It costs more to live in the city.

This begs the question: Do increasingly denser urban centers yield lower or higher economic growth? I’d pose a related question: Are cities more or less efficient than either suburban or rural environs?

The September special issue of Scientific American is all about cities, most of it positive and optimistic. Physicists Luis Bettencourt and Geoffrey West authored one of the articles, entitled “Bigger Cities Do More with Less.” They write:

The numbers show that urban dwellers produce more inventions and create more opportunities for economic growth. Often large cities are also the greenest places on the planet because people living in denser habitats typically have smaller energy footprints, require less infrastructure and consume less of the world’s resources per capita. Compared with suburban or rural areas, cities do more with less. And the bigger cities get, the more productive and efficient they tend to become.

The authors concede that housing prices are higher in cities than elsewhere. But the overall benefits exceed aggregate costs.

In a city with high rents, only activities that add substantial value can be prof- itable. These economic pressures push urbanites to come up with new forms of organizations, products and services that carry more value added. In turn, higher profitability, excellence and choice tend to attract more talent to the city, pushing rents higher still, fueling the need to find yet more productive activities. This feedback mechanism, in a nutshell, is the principal reason cities accelerate innovation, while diversifying and intensifying social and economic activity.

Data suggest that a doubling of a city’s population increases economic output by 15 percent than if the same population were split in two. Size does matter.

Likewise, the larger and denser a city the lower the per capita environmental footprint. The reasons are fairly straightforward. Owning and driving a car in New York City is a burden rather than a necessity, as it is elsewhere. People walk or use public transportation or get purchased items delivered; and living in apartments requires less energy to heat and cool vis-a-vis a suburban house, where all walls are exposed to the elements.

Also, as a city’s population increases so do wages. However, we should not draw the conclusion that merely living in a city translates into a bigger paycheck. Rather, cities are more productive and innovative, thereby attracting more capable citizens, those with more education and, therefore, greater earning ability. (On a personal note, my wife and I could not afford to buy a house on Seattle’s Capitol Hill, but our lawyer daughter and her lawyer husband can and did.)

I wish to address a few gratuitous comments of McCusker. He writes:

The higher costs of urban societies are aggravated by the fact that most of these costs are the domain of government, a traditionally inefficient organization.

I don’t pretend to understand what McCusker means by this. Are rents and housing prices, the biggest component of urban living’s costs, the “domain of government.” Wouldn’t real estate value be the product of supply and demand rather than municipal government? The obvious attractiveness of Seattle combined with geographical constraints lead to higher rents and housing prices than in, say, Marysville, which has few amenities, little commercial activity, and automobile-dependent densities.

McCusker also writes:

Costs and productivity limitations are the reasons why we can’t buy way out of the fix we are in. To get out we must shift resources away from federal, state and local government and into manufacturing, mining, agriculture and petroleum industries so that we can regain some productivity growth.

The recessionary economy is doing its part to reduce the size of governments across the board, along with a ridiculous aversion to taxes. State, county, city, and school district governments have been slashing budgets right and left. Why? They’re losing revenues.

And how might “we” transfer resources to the private sector? Americans are among the least taxed citizens in the industrial world. If the money isn’t going to governments, where is it being spent? Well, McCusker has apparently failed to notice that nobody is spending money, which is the source of the economic problem. Consumers aren’t consuming because they’re either out of work or their wages have stagnated or they’ve lost the value in their homes, which had enabled continued buying during the housing bubble’s expansion. Firms aren’t spending money to expand their business because they have no confidence that people can or will buy their goods.

McCusker also fails to appreciate that labor productivity is up in almost every sector. Corporations are getting more and more output per worker, which helps explain why they’re not hiring. That said, it’s true that growth is mediocre and far below capacity in each of the industries he mentions. Again, that can be explained by a multitude of reasons, including a concerted effort to shift manufacturing off shore.

And what shall we mine? I suspect that McCusker is thinking of oil, coal, and natural gas. He evidently hasn’t noticed that the U.S. reached its peak oil production decades ago (it costs too much to extract relative to retail price). Nor has he noticed that we’re in the midst of a natural gas glut, with prices steadily falling. Further, McCusker appears to dismiss global warming either as science or threat. There’s one thing the planet needs less of and that’s more carbon-rich resources.

Finally, McCusker tosses out a non sequitur. He writes:

We will also need a new economic model—not a Keynesian hand-me-down…

What does Keynes have to do with anything he’s been discussing? Moreover, how could he with a straight face assert that “we” are living under a “Keynesian hand-me-down” economic model? No one in the White House, no Republican, and only a handful of Democrats espouse Keynesian doctrine. That’s too bad, since Keynes’s model explains our current economic problems, one defined by constrained aggregate demand and pervasive uncertainty (see Keynes’s chapter 12).

I infer from McCusker’s “hand-me-down” expression that he believes that all of Keynes can be summed up in government giveaways. My guess is that McCusker criticized the stimulus package, because it was both Keynesian and “hand-me-down.” It was neither. Real Keynsians understood that the economy needed a huge infusion of dollars, at least three times as much as the Recovery Act, which was inadequate in size and form—too much of it was tax cuts and not enough in public infrastructure.

So, does McCusker think that the economy will spontaneously recover? If so, what will be the trigger? What event or events will restore confidence and induce spending and hiring?

I’m listening, Mr. McCusker.

Want to save money? Raise the Medicare eligibility age.

August 25, 2011 by davald

Not.

The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities (CBPP) published a report on this very proposal, which we hear from the mouths of Republicans and Democrats alike. Why, we’re living longer, right? So we’ll simply push access to Medicare a few birthdays later. We’ll start at, say, two, then gradually up it to…what? A slippery slope, I suggest.

To be sure, the federal government could save a nickel or two, although that’s dubious, as I’ll discuss briefly below. But costs will surely shift to vulnerable citizens, those who are in poor health and without private medical coverage. They’ll have to wait a few more years to receive Medicare benefits. The CBPP writes:

The principal study of the effects of raising the Medicare eligibility age, by the Kaiser Family Foundation, estimates that its increased state and private-sector costs would be twice as large as the net federal savings.  If the proposal were fully in effect in 2014, Kaiser estimates, it would generate $5.7 billion in net federal savings but $11.4 billion in higher health care costs to individuals, employers, and states.

Let’s think a bit more on this.

Suppose you’ve been working at a blue-collar job, one that has required lots of physical labor. You finally retire at age 65. However, the thirty years on the job have taken their toll on your body. You’ve got a bad hip that requires replacement. Perhaps you’ve developed a heart condition. Whatever, you don’t get Medicare. You’ll have to wait until you’re 67 or maybe even older. Well, you don’t have any money for the necessary medical treatments. So you put off getting them. The months and years pass and you’re only getting worse. Now your condition has deteriorated to the extent that corrective procedures will be far more involved and much more costly than they would have been when you retired. Now you’re eligible for Medicare. You’ve become, because of the longer wait period, a greater financial liability. Your claims against Medicare are much higher because of the delays in getting treatment.

One of the compelling arguments for universal health care, or even “Medicare for all,” is that people can and will seek medical attention after initial symptoms. No need to put off seeing a doctor, because you don’t avoid out-of-pocket expenses. You’re covered. Period. Well, early prevention and treatment are less expensive in the main than letting conditions go untreated.

Consider a diabetic. If the person can’t afford regular physical examinations, his problem gets worse. He may have been able to effectively counter the diabetes through diet and exercise, provided he had an early diagnosis. Now that he’s forced to wait, because he lacks health insurance or cash, the diabetes requires insulin or, god forbid, amputation of an extremity. He’s much worse off, an invalid, and taxpayers get a much higher bill.

The rational approach to medical care is to cover everyone on an equal basis, from pre-birth to death. Receiving medical care should be a right rather than a privilege. It would certainly be cheaper for all of us.

Fallacy of composition

August 24, 2011 by davald

Let’s suppose that as a result of the Great Recession you find yourself in debt with little savings. This is not hard to imagine, as a general consequence of the housing bubble collapse people lost wealth in their homes, which they had relied upon to buy things rather than save. So, what do you do?

A now-frugal individual would probably try to do two things: pay off his debt then start saving money. Three cheers for him. But what happens if a whole lot of people behave in the same manner?

If one does either or both of these two activities, spending on goods and services—almost by definition—declines. Then what?

Should too many of us stop buying things, companies are forced to cut back on production. After all, why build more widgets if no one is going to buy them? If production falls, then the company needs fewer workers. The now unemployed workers have no wages, and they probably lack savings, as we mentioned above. They may receive unemployment benefits, but those are less than the wage they were earning at their jobs, and at some point the benefits run out. To be sure, the unemployed worker is probably spending all those benefits on necessities: rent, food, etc. But he’s surely not buying a new car or a new refrigerator or a vacation in Hawaii. It’s doubtful that he can save much, because he’s not bringing home as much as he was while working.

We can see that a whole lot of money is now being withdrawn from the economy in a massive, vicious cycle. Lower incomes lead to less buying, which reduces production, which increases unemployment—and so on.

The problem here is a prime example of the fallacy of composition. That is, what applies to or is suitable for the individual is not necessarily good for all individuals as a whole.

John Maynard Keynes invoked “the paradox of thrift.” In recessionary periods, especially, if everyone saves, no one saves. That’s because increased savings means reduced spending, which lowers income (the vicious cycle), which forces people to use their savings to pay bills. What’s good for one is bad for all.

On the international sphere we hear well-meaning political leaders calling for Spain, Italy, and just about every other Euro nation to act just like Germany. That won’t work, because of the fallacy of composition. Not every nation can have a positive account balance, where they’re exporting more than importing. Some nations have to import the exports.

In the United States the dominant theme is austerity: We all have to tighten our belts, and if the people have to do it than so should the government.  Reducing the nation’s debt now, while the people practice thrift, can only exacerbate our misery and prolong the tiny recovery.

Nearly all Republicans actively preach austerity and ignore the fallacy of composition. Should they prevail in next year’s elections…well, you can write your own narrative.

Predictive theories

August 22, 2011 by davald

Milton Friedman, of all people, argued that the value of an economic theory lay in its ability to predict human behavior. That is, what would people, in the aggregate, likely do under particular circumstances. This is somewhat ironic to the extent that some of Friedman’s own theories fail to account for what the heck is going on today.

Today, of course, we’re in a mess, arguing over what, if anything, can and should be done about it. One intrepid economist, whom I often cite, appears to have been prescient about not only the diagnosis but also the cure.

Paul Krugman was recently asked by a few readers whether he had anticipated the protracted economic stagnation. In particular, did he express concerns over the efficacy of Obama’s stimulus package. He linked to three separate columns he penned for the New York Times.

The first prior assessment was made in early January 2009, a couple of weeks before Obama assumed office. He wrote:

But Mr. Obama’s prescription doesn’t live up to his diagnosis. The economic plan he’s offering isn’t as strong as his language about the economic threat. In fact, it falls well short of what’s needed.

Bear in mind just how big the U.S. economy is. Given sufficient demand for its output, America would produce more than $30 trillion worth of goods and services over the next two years. But with both consumer spending and business investment plunging, a huge gap is opening up between what the American economy can produce and what it’s able to sell.

Krugman believed that the stimulus package was only a third of what was needed to close the output gap. That is, instead of $800 billion (nearly half of which was in the form of tax cuts, which are not all that stimulative), Obama should have proposed and Congress should have adopted a measure of almost $2.5 trillion. Even then, it seems, despite Democrats controlling both houses, Obama was seeking a pragmatic, bipartisan solution. He and his political advisors thought that any package had to be less than a trillion dollars and much of it had to be tax cuts in order to woo Republicans.

His second and third previous columns are more of the same, although he ups the amount the stimulus should have been to almost $3 trillion.

But there was one point he made that caught my attention back then, in early 2009. Krugman wrote:

So here’s the picture that scares me: It’s September 2009, the unemployment rate has passed 9 percent, and despite the early round of stimulus spending it’s still headed up. Mr. Obama finally concedes that a bigger stimulus is needed.

But he can’t get his new plan through Congress because approval for his economic policies has plummeted, partly because his policies are seen to have failed, partly because job-creation policies are conflated in the public mind with deeply unpopular bank bailouts. And as a result, the recession rages on, unchecked.

O.K., that’s a warning, not a prediction. But economic policy is falling behind the curve, and there’s a real, growing danger that it will never catch up.

Well, that’s precisely what has happened, although Obama has never come forward to propose further stimulus, especially after the Republicans “shellacked” the Democrats in 2010. Worse, Obama sold the actual package on the promise that it would reduce unemployment and spur the economy.

He’s cooked his own goose with several different recipes.

Some more inconvenient facts

August 20, 2011 by davald

It has become the standard mantra of conservatives that the economy is in the doldrums because of excessive federal spending. Hewing to this line serves as a narrative glue binding the different shades of the GOP, twinned with a pledge not to raise taxes, even should the ratio of cuts to revenues exceed 10-to-1.

Now no one ever accused Republicans of letting facts get in their way. Indeed, they can yelp whoopers ’til the frogs cease to croak, then simply ignore or slough off the gaffes should a proximate sentient being mention the error. However, the Rest of Us should not shirk from our responsibility to record the blunders and, if possible, dissuade the weak from following the idiots down the dark holes of ignorance.

About that spending (chart from the St. Louis Fed):

 

Writing for ProPublica Michael Grabell does more service to our cause (how presumptuous) by dispelling several myths that are also part of the GOP’s narrative glue. He throws a few glancing blows at mainstream Democrats on the stimulus’ effects, for balance’s sake, I guess.

But I must take issue with his comment on the belief that the stimulus should have been bigger. He calls this “a red herring.” Well, no. There are many competent economists who argue that, given the size of the housing bubble collapse, among other factors, the stimulus really should have been much larger. I emphasize ‘should,’ which is different from ‘could.’ Yes, the politics were horrible, and it’s clear that Obama refused to accept the advice of his own economic advisors, Christine Romer being one, to push a larger stimulus package. So, it’s doubtful that a divided Congress would have acceded, as Grabell points out. That does not detract from the argument that the package should have been larger.

It’s become more obvious with each report on the dismal economy that Congress and Obama were wrong. I’d go further.

It was not only the puny size of the stimulus that failed to overcome the recessionary dynamics. It was also its limited duration.

Economic players, both consumers and producers, crave stability and predictability. Had Congress built in an indefinite stimulus, keyed to obvious metrics (e.g., the unemployment rate), the economy, I would argue, would be recovering much faster. Once growth returned, ease back on the stimulus. (Sweden does this.)

Yes, there is a time for austerity. Keynes said that it was appropriate during “boom” cycles, but hardly during recessionary periods, such as we’re in now. Austerity only makes matters worse.

In retrospect, at least, it was a humungous mistake to cut taxes, as Bush did. Launching two wars—without raising revenues to pay for them—added to the burden. The debt, which had been growing sharply since Reagan’s tax cuts, was being paid down during the Clinton administration and headed toward an actual surplus. (Graph from the Washington Post)

By the way, notice how little the stimulus (2009 Recovery Act) contributed to the problem. The tax cuts ($1.7 trillion) and the two wars ($1.3 trillion) are responsible for a huge chunk of the current deficits.

The solutions to our fiscal problems, as I’ve suggested previously, are relatively simple in concept, however difficult to achieve in this political cauldron:

  1. end the Bush-era tax cuts (better yet, get them back to the pre-Reagan days)
  2. end the wars
  3. invest in public infrastructure (e.g., fix dilapidated schools, non-automobile transportation sector)
  4. adopt Medicare for all
  5. eliminate sprawl-inducing subsidies (e.g., mortgage deductions, oil subsidies)

Piece of cake, I say.

A simple remedy

August 19, 2011 by davald

For many of us, the problems we currently face are easily remedied. What’s in the way is not a lack of proper ideas but political will. Indeed, we have at least one party intent on throwing the entire economy under the bus rather than see another Obama term.

Writing for the London Review of Books professor Corey Robin offers a serious yet familiar solution to what ails us. He writes:

Had politics not intruded and had Obama heeded the advice of centre-left economists [among them, his own advisors and Prof. Krugman], these are the steps he might have taken. First, do nothing about the debt, at least not now. The debt can be significantly reduced only if the economy improves. The best way to encourage that is for the government to spend, which will add to the debt in the short term but reduce it in the long term through revenues generated by growth. Second, once the economy is healthy, increase taxes, particularly on the wealthy. As a share of GDP, revenues are at their lowest levels since 1950. With the exception of a brief interregnum in the late 1980s and early 1990s, top marginal rates are the lowest they’ve been since 1931. Corporate taxes in the US are the lowest of any OECD country. The notion that taxes shouldn’t be raised, not only to fund necessary and desirable expenditure but also to cut the debt, runs counter to common sense. Last, cut military spending. As the economic journalist Doug Henwood has observed, if the US simply returned to the spending levels of 2000 – when ‘the Pentagon didn’t have to hold bake sales’ and spending was 3.7 per cent of GDP as opposed to the current 5.4 per cent – it could save $3.6 trillion in the next decade, 72 per cent more than the debt-ceiling deal negotiated by Obama and Congress will save.

But, as he condemns the measures taken by Bush II, Robin excoriates Obama, who, he believes, holds much responsibility for prolonging a stagnant economy. It was Bush, as Robin reminds us, who cut taxes and launched two wars on borrowed money. Instead of paying off the massive U.S. debt, as would have occurred by merely perpetuating the Clinton policies, Bush added more to it than any president in history. As for Obama’s failures, here’s Robin:

Instead, Obama and Congress took the opposite path, which was paved 40 years ago by the anti-tax philosophy of the American right. In February 2010, Obama convened a bipartisan commission to balance the budget by 2015, effectively making debt reduction a top priority. After the November midterm election, when the Republicans took back the House with the help of the Tea Party, Obama froze the pay of federal workers and endorsed a more aggressive austerity programme. Cuts were proposed and tax increases dismissed: not just once (with the extension of the Bush tax cuts in December) but twice (with the first phase of the debt deal, which eliminates $900 billion solely through spending cuts) and now potentially a third time (with the second phase of the deal, which eliminates an additional $1.2 trillion solely through spending cuts if a congressional committee can’t produce an alternative package of tax increases and spending cuts by November). While the deal does include defence cuts – though it’s unclear if these are cuts or simply slower rates of growth, and whether and how most of them will happen – Obama’s latest comments, and those of his defence secretary, suggest they could be traded for benefit cuts.

Robin considers the widely divergent interpretations of Obama’s character, giving partial credence to all, while ultimately dismissing them as almost beside the point. These, too, are familiar to readers: Obama’s “preternatural aversion to conflict”; the now-evident fundamental flaws in our federal political system; and that Obama is not the progressive that many had hoped for.

However, the current problems, Robin suggests, should be viewed within a wider, historical context. Populations facing similar situations—war and associated fiscal crises—”have catalysed politically progressive advances and even precipitated revolutions.” The focus has now shifted to the few genuine progressives among us who allowed the present tragedy to unfold over decades. Robin concludes:

And here Democrats like Obama and his defenders, who bemoan the stranglehold of the Tea Party on American politics, have only themselves to blame. For decades, Democrats have collaborated in stripping back the American state in the vain hope that the market would work its magic. For a time it did, though mostly through debt; workers could compensate for stagnating wages with easy credit and low-interest mortgages. Now the debt’s due to be repaid, and wages – if people are lucky enough to be working – aren’t enough to cover the bills. The only thing that’s left for them is cutting taxes. And the imperialism of the peasants.

That last phrase, by the way, comes straight from Karl Marx.
