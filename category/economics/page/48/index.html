Wasting electricity

September 24, 2012 by davald

The New York Times is running a series on data servers, the massive centers that house bits and bytes generated by millions of Internet users. They started out small a few decades ago; today, they are massive and proliferating.

Though I am intimately engaged in energy issues from my perch on a utility board, I was unaware that, according to the Times, these data centers waste more than 90 percent of the electricity they consume to perform their intended function. Worse, they rely on dirty diesel backup generators, which themselves are giant and plentiful. And we should use “backup” advisedly. The Times:

Energy efficiency varies widely from company to company. But at the request of The Times, the consulting firm McKinsey & Company analyzed energy use by data centers and found that, on average, they were using only 6 percent to 12 percent of the electricity powering their servers to perform computations. The rest was essentially used to keep servers idling and ready in case of a surge in activity that could slow or crash their operations.

Microsoft has built huge server farms in Quincy, Wash., to take advantage of cheap electrical power provided by Grant County Public Utility District, which owns two of its own dams on the Columbia River that generate nearly 8.5 million megawatt-hours a year. The town is small, with fewer than 7,000 residents. It’s a farming community, both traditionally and technologically.

Before the arrival of Microsoft and Dell and other Internet behemoths the town’s residents and agricultural enterprises consumed about 9.5 MWa, or about 83,000 MWH. Last year Microsoft and Yahoo combined to consume 41.8 MWa, or roughly 366,000 MWH.

If the Times‘ article is correct on the waste numbers, and if Microsoft et al. continue adding capacity, then there could be implications for the region down the road, especially if the economy recovers and demand for all things increases. At the moment, we may be collectively unmoved, because there is so much supply of electricity amidst stagnant consumption. But if demand rises, those waste figures could loom large.

Right now, the electricity is cheap. The Times‘ article says that the technology giants pay between 2.5 and 3.8 cents per kilowatt-hour. Yet, even at those low rates, there’s got to be opportunity for efficiency measures that are much less expensive than the power.

I predict, then, that sooner or later a niche of energy conservation firms will step in to sharply diminish wasteful consumption and reliance on dirty diesel backup. The region, certainly, would be better off.

Mitt’s tax rate

September 22, 2012 by davald

I must say that I was taken aback by Matthew Yglesias’s defense of low tax rates for Mitt Romney. Taxes on capital, Yglesias said, should be lower than taxes on labor, since the capitalist invests while the laborer spends, perhaps profligately.

He provides us with a story of two make-believe doctors under two different scenarios. They both earn roughly the same. But one buys things in the here and now while the other prudently saves (invests) for the future. If taxes are higher on the saver, then he is has more incentive to spend. If they are lower, he prefers to sock money away.

— In the world where investment income isn’t taxed, the second doctor says to the first doctor “all those fancy vacations may be fun, but I’m being much more prudent. By saving for the future, I’ll be comfortable when it comes time to retire and will have plenty left over to give to my kids.”

— In the world where investment income is taxed like labor income, the first doctor says to the second “man you’re a sucker—not only are you deferring enjoyment of the fruits of your labor (boring) but when the money you’ve saved comes back to you, it gets taxed all over again. Live in the now.”

I thought this much too simplistic, if not also imaginary. Do the Mitt Romneys of the world contribute more to society by investing their dollars than the Rest of Us do by spending almost all that we earn—because we have to?

Today I read Doug Henwood’s post on Yglesias. He, too, found the reasoning suspicious and the story even more so. (Note: Henwood is an economist; Yglesias is not.) Henwood:

Most investments are in previously existing financial instruments. As Alan Abelson used to say in the old Barron’s ad, someone buys a stock because he thinks it’s going to go up—but the person he buys it from thinks it’s going to go down. Almost all corporate investment in real things like buildings and machinery is financed internally, through profits. For the last few decades, corporations have been generating more money than they know what to do with, so they’ve been shoveling out to shareholders. (See here for more.) In Romney’s case, Bain largely invested in existing corporations—turning some of them around (mostly with other people’s money) and bleeding others dry (at great profit to Bain partners).

Really, Matt, you didn’t know this?

It’s this part about savings equally investing that I find most troublesome. Just because I put money into an account, wherever it may be, does not necessarily mean that the dollars are being invested in “real things” that do indeed create jobs and “spread the wealth around.”

 

With the Great Divergence, beginning in the latter 70s, money velocity fell rapidly as the Gini index rose, suggesting, at any rate, that a more equal distribution of income leads to a greater turnover of money. Here’s the FRED definition of money velocity:

Velocity is a ratio of nominal GDP to a measure of the money supply. It can be thought of as the rate of turnover in the money supply–that is, the number of times one dollar is used to purchase final goods and services included in GDP.

But when more and more dollars are siphoned out of the economy into the hands of a few (like Mitt Romney), the Rest of Us have less income to spend on goods and services that make the whole thing go ’round. And this is precisely the problem we face today, too little aggregate demand.

So, one solution is to increase taxes on capital gains and lower taxes on ordinary income like wages so as to induce more spending. Under existing tax rates, Mitt Romney et al. have an incentive to save more than spend. The problem is that those damn savings never trickle down to the Rest of Us. More likely, they wind up in the Cayman Islands or, as Henwood says, being “shoveled to shareholders.”

Who are these people?

September 21, 2012 by davald

Paul Krugman, in my humble opinion, nails it with his take on Mitt Romney and the new Republican Party, “what has become a party of the wealthy, by the wealthy, and for the wealthy, a party that considers the rest of us unworthy of even a pretense of respect.”

I would only add that the modern-day GOP, whether by design or via gaffe-ridden rhetoric, makes no apologies for being so. Despite the growing inequality in America, the Republicans would increase the gap. Despite remarkably low taxes on wealth, the Republican would prefer to pay no taxes at all—they’re for “the little people,” who exist only by virtue of the “job creators.”

The tragedy, perhaps peculiar to this country, is that far too many who have no business in the GOP will vote a solid Republican ticket this November, with their man “Mitt”—the would-be “Gaffer-in-Chief”—at the top.

A housing comparison

September 20, 2012 by davald

The New York Times ran a piece yesterday about “Forbes billionaires” snapping up apartments in Manhattan’s newest and largest residential tower. So far the largest purchases exceed $90 million. This got me thinking.

The median net worth in the U.S.—obviously skewed by the aforementioned billionaires—is $77,300, according to this. The median 2010 housing price, according to the U.S. Census, was $221,800. That purchase represents 287 percent of the median net worth.

If a billionaire buys that penthouse described in the Times article, he or she will have spent less than ten percent of his or her wealth. Someone with a fortune of five billion need spend only two percent of it on the Manhattan penthouse.

Suppose the ordinary guy spends only 10 percent of his wealth on a home. What could he buy? Well, of course, he could buy a $7,430 house. Not too many of those, however.

According to Wikipedia the United States is home to 412 billionaires, or about 34 percent of the world total. There are 13.2 billionaires here for every 10 million of the Rest of Us. What is the net worth of these very special people? Forbes provides an estimate.

Suppose that each of these billionaires spent just 10 percent of his or her net worth buying up median-priced homes ($221,800). How many could they acquire?

 

Bill Gates could purchase almost 24,000 homes that cost the median-income household almost three times its net worth. Even the least wealthy person on the above list, Abigail Johnson, could buy more than 5,000 such homes. The Walton family could pool their dollars to snap up almost 38,000 homes; the Koch brothers slightly less than 16,000.

Wikipedia reports as many as half a million Americans are homeless. There are about 130 million housing units in the U.S. The current vacancy rate for homes is 2.5 percent, which means that at least 3.3 million homes are unoccupied (I’m sure that there is a churn factor here). So there are over six vacant homes for every homeless person.

Only in America.

Stepping in it

September 19, 2012 by davald

Mitt Romney has a very large shoe he likes to shove into his very large—and always open—mouth.

The Internet is abuzz with Romney’s remarks uttered in the presence of wealthy benefactors. I need not reprise them here, other than to point out the obvious: while his shoe is otherwise occupied, he’s blowing all kinds of crazy flatulence out of his ass.

Romney essentially believes that half of Americans aren’t properly so; they lack the self-reliance and perseverance of successful people like, well, Romney. Maureen Dowd, writing for the New York Times:

It’s literally rich: Willard, born on third base and acting self-made, whining to the rich about what a great deal in life the poor have.

We thought Romney was secretly moderate, but it turns out that he’s secretly cruel, a social Darwinist just like his running mate.

Half of Americans, Romney intoned, don’t pay income taxes and depend on government handouts. Let’s say that his assumption is correct. Does it follow that the recipients are “moochers,” in Paul Ryan’s argot? That if only these people would get out of their hammocks and ape the values of David Brooks they’d join the One-percenters, then say goodbye to Medicare, Medicaid, unemployment benefits, and low-income credits.

But not so fast. Matthew Yglesias reminds us that some very wealthy people benefit handsomely from direct government programs. Take copyright laws. They have enabled a single man to become the richest of them all. Yglesias has Bill Gates in mind.

He links to here for information on who else benefits from U.S. “entitlements.” Some highlights, or lowlights, depending on your viewpoint:

  • three-fourths of those receiving benefits are the aged or the disabled
  • a plurality of government benefits go toward the middle class, though woefully shrunk since the Great Divergence
  • mortgage-interest deductions (a government program) benefit the rich more than the relative Have-nots
  • indeed, the rich, like Mitt Romney, have far more opportunities to avoid paying taxes while enhancing their capital gains—because of government laws and codes

And Romney-the-rhetorical-fart-monger will get about half the votes cast in November.

Only in America.

The way we were

September 19, 2012 by davald

Tocqueville remarked some 60 years after the Revolution that America had a very large class of property owners, who were neither rich nor poor. He believed that its existence ensured stability, since there was every incentive to preserve wealth and, thus, the status quo. The rich, by the way, he found to, “daily rise out of the crowd and constantly return thither.”

This article in The Atlantic reveals that the America Tocqueville observed was indeed a more equal society than certainly his native France, which had suffered through a bloody revolution of its own. Here’s a visual comparison of Gini coefficients* (the source is a referenced study):

Here’s U.S. income distribution over time, going back to 1774.

 

In commenting on the perceived equality in post-Revolution America, Tocqueville wrote:

…equality naturally leads men to go in for industry and trade and that it tends to increase and distribute real property…and inspires every man with a constant and eager desire to increase his well-being.

That “constant and eager desire” fueled self-aggrandizement, leading to skyrocketing new wealth in the decades following the Civil War. Yet, the rich were still not satisfied. Beginning in the 1970s they steadfastly plotted the Great Divergence. The gloves were off, as were regulations and taxes. The rich have never looked back.

* Sweden’s current Gini index is about .26; the U.S.’s is around .38—both figures reflect the effects of taxes and transfers (OECD).

As depressing as they come

September 17, 2012 by davald

While the collapse of the housing bubble was bad, very bad for millions of us who lost homes to foreclosure, it was a perfect opportunity for those with extra money to exploit misfortune. The following excerpt from Thomas Edsall’s piece in today’s New York Times:

At the height of the housing collapse, Tarver saw an opportunity. “This moment right now,” she told Desmond, “it’s going to create a lot of millionaires. You know, if you have money right now, you can profit from other people’s failures. … I’m catching the properties. I’m catching ‘em.”

Edsall ends with these terribly depressing paragraphs, which serve as a follow up to my previous post:

This dependence on moneyed interests effectively precludes exploitation as a theme for either major party to develop. These sources of campaign cash would dry up if they became the target of policies or positions they found threatening.

Even as polarization poses more sharply defined choices to the voter, pressing issues remain off limits. Poverty and hunger have been dropped from the agenda. The range of policy and electoral choices remains confined to what fits comfortably into a world of muted ethical concern, a world in which moral relativism has permeated society not so much from the bottom up, as from the top down.

The unshackling of moneyed interests — in the name of first amendment rights — from restraints on campaign contributions has, in fact, constrained the free speech of the disadvantaged. It empowers those whose goal is to hinder consumer-protection legislation, to forestall more progressive tax rates and to quash populist insurgencies.

This skewing of the odds in favor of the rich comes at a time when the Democratic Party is already inhibited by accusations that it likes to foment “class warfare” and to play “the race card.” The result has been a relentless shift of the political center from left to right. The two most recent Democratic presidents, Bill Clinton and Barack Obama, have pursued agendas well within this limited terrain. There is little reason to believe that Obama, if he wins in November, will feel empowered to push out much further into territory the Democrats have virtually abandoned.

The reform movement that would not die

September 14, 2012 by davald

Cries to reform public education have been with us since, well, the beginning of public education. One can find all sorts of examples, from both the right and the left, that schools were lousy, producing an inferior product, and woe is the nation that can’t compete because its workers lack the requisite skills.

The Chicago teachers’ strike is just the latest event to draw attention to reform, with much at stake, including how teachers and principals are evaluated, school autonomy, and rewarding the good while punishing the bad. David Brooks predictably weighs in on the side of the reformers, praising Mayor Rahm Emmanuel for tackling the intractable.

But, of course, Brooks waxes pretentious, writing about the world’s being divided into two economies. Economy I is all about supply and demand and prices and competition. Economy II encompasses investment without output and includes “entitlements” and public education. He warns that unless significant reforms occur in the latter economy, the former will suffer from the unsustainable drag of the other.

Brooks, like Kristof and the New York Times editors before him, assumes the present situation, then invokes all the platitudes and prescriptions so beloved of the after-the-fact reformers. Once again, there is no mention of the before-the-fact reforms of Canada and, as often mentioned here, Finland.

Okay, Finnish guy, what would the Finnish Way look like in America?

Well, first of all, Finland’s reform movement, which began decades ago, takes its intellectual cues from our own John Dewey, the American philosopher with a lot to say on how we learn and how we should teach. Fair warning, however: Dewey was a “progressive,” which in the U.S. is tantamount to being a communist or worse. Let’s assume, for the sake of argument, that we can get beyond the toxic labels to actually consider what the man advocated.

After some deliberation one would understand that a proper before-the-fact reform takes time, measured in decades if not generations. One reason for this, even beyond establishing a consensus on the correct educational goals, is that, metaphorically speaking, we’ve got to wait for the pig to make its way through the anaconda. I mean no disrespect, please, to those toiling in our public schools today. As I’ve said elsewhere, they deserve sanctification, not vilification. But any reform movement that focuses first and foremost on selecting and training the best and the brightest to teach our children yields benefits in waves: the new eventually replace the old.

I believe it is counterproductive to squeeze out a better educational system from the status quo through a painful, bitter process of sifting the good from the bad. After all, the teachers, whether they are “top performers” or merely mediocre, come to the profession with noble intentions. Moreover, they have received training in college and beyond and no doubt participate in regular professional development sessions. For someone or some test to tell them that they’re awful at their chosen profession is problematic on its face, benefiting no one, as diminishing morale takes its toll on students, with deleterious effects on learning.

But reformers are an impatient lot, always ready for bear, and quick to pillory teachers as greedy government workers who seek nothing more than to protect their weak as they perpetuate academic disparities. They would not come easily to a reform movement that is gradual and gives them nothing to do. Once the emphasis is placed on the front end—in universities—reformers will have to find something else to occupy their time.

That, my friends, would be progress.

Housing

September 13, 2012 by davald

According to economist Dean Baker and others the collapse of the housing bubble eroded upwards of $8 trillion in wealth. Now we have an overhang of both debt and houses.

Few dispute the above facts. Where things get dicey, as in controversial, is what, if anything, can and should be done.

Some would argue that the economy is in the midst of a major “correction.” All that housing and all that debt was unsustainable; so we’re collectively deleveraging as houses sit unoccupied. Meanwhile, people who were busy building housing stock suddenly lost their jobs, and most have been unable to find new ones—especially in construction.

So I’m going to show a few charts that illustrate where we’ve been and where we are today. First will be residential construction employees.

There ’tis. After a steady buildup from Clinton’s first term and continuing through Bush II’s second (until the last year, of course), housing construction fell off a cliff.

Housing starts show a similar pattern.

 

Yet, houses are more affordable now.

 

An index of 100 means that the average household income qualifies for the average household price, assuming a 20-percent down payment. Of course, those who read this blog know about how averages (mean) can distort. Somebody’s making money, but it’s not the Rest of Us. And after you’ve got your first million or two and purchased your second or third home, you really don’t need another house in your personal portfolio.

There may be an interesting explanation for the upward blip during G.W. Bush’s second term. We can see that the affordability index was inching down from roughly 2004 to 2006, no doubt the result of escalating home prices combined with lower real wages. Bush, we will recall, then declared that America was”an ownership society,” so he pushed to realize his description through low interest rates and minimum, if any, down payments and, for that matter, proof of income. During the Great Recession, housing prices fell sharply, which increased affordability, even as many lost jobs and income.

For those in construction, wages fell dramatically, while their unemployment rate skyrocketed.

These last three graphs suggest that we still have some ways to go waiting for “the correction.”

The home ownership rate has also tumbled.

If we are of a mind with those who suggest that the economy needs to correct—without government assistance—then we’ll likely cast a reproving eye toward those who manufactured and facilitated the “ownership society.” These individuals and firms, along with their suckers, deserve to suffer the consequences of their actions.

Harsh medicine, some might say.

Those who argue that our society cannot long abide this lingering high unemployment, with all its attendant pain and suffering, advocate strong government action—far beyond the monetary policies of the Federal Reserve Board. Paul Krugman is decidedly in this camp, and has been since before Obama took office.

Krugman believes that the economy is actually in a “lesser depression.” He invokes Keynes to describe our current malaise: “a chronic condition of subnormal activity for a considerable period without any marked tendency either towards recovery or towards complete collapse.” His most recent book, End This Recession Now!, urges fiscal stimulus, this time serious enough to meet the task at hand. (The quote is from that book.)

Let’s say that the Great Recession (or “lesser depression”) resulted in a “loss” of $2 trillion. The Obama stimulus package was far less than this amount, perhaps a quarter of what was needed, according to Krugman et al. And keep in mind that over 40 percent of the package was in the form of tax cuts to “job creators.”

In the chart below we see that the economy is doing far less than its potential: the difference between the red and blue lines. Without any external shock, and fiscal stimulus would be one source, the trajectories remain constant and parallel, with a permanent economic loss. That loss, we should understand, is manifest in lingering high unemployment, stagnant wages, and a continuing erosion in public infrastructure. (In a depressed economy like ours, tax revenues decline, making it impossible for state and local governments to adequately fund schools, transportation, and other social services.)

To no reader’s surprise, I’m in the Krugman camp. I believe that action should be taken to jump start the economy (the “magneto effect,” as John Maynard Keynes put it) so as to put people back to work, get the dollars circulating, and increasing tax revenues so that we can repair schools, bridges, roads, railways, etc.

How’s this for a campaign slogan? “Let’s build the country we deserve!” It worked for FDR. Why not us?
