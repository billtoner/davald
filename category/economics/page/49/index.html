Taxes and death spirals

September 12, 2012 by davald

Mayor Bloomberg says that Congress should get on with it and just pass the Simpson-Bowles recommendations, even though the committee’s report failed to gain sufficient support from its members. The mayor also believes that lower taxes help businesses.

Well, let’s see. I’ll try a little thought experiment.

Businessman A, who makes Widget X, believes that he could become more competitive if his company’s tax burden were less. To be sure, his costs would decline, all other things being equal, which would free dollars to, say, hire more workers to boost production, even though aggregate demand remains weak.

Businessman B also makes Widget X. If A gets a tax break, so will he. So, both A and B will have no comparative advantage against the other—again, all other things being equal.

Now we could introduce Businessman Chang. He’s Chinese, of course, and he also makes Widget X. Both A and B find it difficult to compete with Chang. So they push for lower taxes in hopes of taking some market share from Chang. A does not better B, nor B, A, but A and B, with lower taxes, better Chang—to limited extent and only temporarily.

Chan has a government that builds all the infrastructure he needs, from high-speed rail shipping, to modern factories, to adequate schools that train his workers. But A and B, because their taxes have been lowered—along with every other business person in America—have lousy infrastructures. The roads are congested or crumbling. Railways are clogged and unable to accommodate high-speed trains. Nor do the would-be workers have the requisite skills to improve production, because the schools are underfunded.

Both A and B press for even lower taxes to compensate for Chang’s advantages. All levels of government comply with their request. But the governments, in turn, have less and less tax revenues to provide those things that Chang takes for granted. Schools, roads, and other transportation options continue to fall apart. Nevertheless, A and B complain about the lousy infrastructure and the failing schools, as they keep lobbying their government representatives to do more with less and, of course, to further lower their taxes.

A and B have created their own death spiral. The more they clamor for and receive tax breaks, the more difficult it is for them to compete against Chang.

But A and B are idiots, of course. They never see beyond the tip of their noses. Indeed, they shout and scream that “they built it” and that they are “job creators” answerable to no one—if only government would reduce their tax burdens and stay out of their way.

For their parts, the different levels of government have no choice. The elected representatives depend on A and B for their campaign donations. Nor do they have any money to get in A and B’s way. They can’t pay for schools and roads and railways. And the governments can no longer invest in research to spur technological innovation.

Mr. Chang’s future looks bright. Not so much Mr. A and B’s.

Why so many poor?

September 12, 2012 by davald

Keeping with my naming-the-evil theme, I pause to ask the fundamental question above. The U.S., as we know from writing and reading this blog, has a disproportionately high percentage of its people living in poverty—relative to other industrial democracies. Why?

Some argue that Americans don’t have the requisite skills to land and maintain higher paying jobs. Okay, even if true, and I’m not sure that it is, again, why? Why do so many Americans lack the “right stuff” to earn decent incomes?

Well, they have only high school degrees, which pay less than those jobs requiring a college education. Then why do so many fail to advance beyond the secondary level?

It’s because they lack the proper attitudes or, as some would put it, “character.” I’m thinking here of David Brooks or Charles Murray. If only more of us had self-discipline, curiosity, and the desire to get ahead, then more of us would go on to college then earn more money.

To repeat, why? Why don’t more of us have those traits?

Well, it’s the parents’ fault. They don’t provide for their children, they don’t enrich with language or the right experiences.

Again, why? Why are so many parents lacking, assuming the rationales for the sake of argument?

How far back should we go? To the amoeba?

Such an infinite regression, and I can see how easy this one is, helps no one. At each step the finger is pointed at this or that culprit. But just as we can’t hardly blame the first cell, what good does it do to fault the child or his parent?

For that matter, what do we gain by criticizing the teacher or public education?

How can we end poverty, should that be our goal? There are at least two schools of thought: we either directly eliminate poverty or we invest more in education so that tomorrow’s students succeed where there predecessors fell short. Or, we do both.

I’ve written before that poverty itself interferes with learning. There are both psychological and physical assaults on a poor child that prevent normal development—deemed necessary for proper learning. So taking the second approach may be like pushing a string.

Better, it seems, to focus on poverty itself, while boosting education investments. That would entail far greater provisioning of social services, including adequate nutrition, health care, shelter, and overall economic and emotional security.

Oh, gosh, you’re talking socialism. Fie on you.

Demonizing

September 12, 2012 by davald

A recent post prompted the following comment:

attacking the rich makes me very uncomfortable, and sadly that has become the theme of many discussions these days. also, most speeches at the RNC and DNC tried to celebrate poverty and struggle…Is this the right way forward? Comparisons that demonize an accomplishment lead to divisions and ill- feelings.

I admit to watching not a second of either convention, so I can’t speak to their content and whether or not there was an attempt “to celebrate poverty and struggle,” something I doubt would emanate from party officials on either side of the political aisle—at least uttered in earnest tones. Indeed, my readings of recent statements by Ryan et al. is that the poor are essentially “moochers” and ne’re-do-wells. Such an attitude is evidently permissible to those who are rendered “uncomfortable” by attacks on the rich.

But here’s the point: if there are “divisions” and “ill-feelings” amongst the populace, and there certainly are, they are not the result of the successful rich being demonized. Rather, and as I’ve tried to make plain on numerous occasions, it is the rich themselves who have created the disharmony and disparities.

This is not merely an idle opinion on my part; many have chronicled the concerted efforts to extract ever-larger rents from the Rest of Us, mostly through the best Congress money can buy. I have in mind recent works by Joseph Stiglitz, John Cassidy, Dean Baker, and others. The Great Divergence, in other words, was neither an accident nor the inevitable outcome of “the market.”

That said, does engaging in “class warfare,” as the Right accuses, improve the chances of attaining a more humane society, one less marked by extremes of both wealth and poverty? I have no idea.

I am reminded, however, of a Catholic nun who told me many years ago that the first step toward achieving social justice was “to name the evil.” One such evil, she continued, was the growing gap between the Haves and the Have-nots. To ignore the glaring inequality was to undermine the Gospel, in her opinion.

I have long since abandoned religion and my Catholic heritage. Yet, the nun’s advice  strikes me as both reasonable and necessary.

So, I shall continue to rail against the excesses of wealth and privilege, especially as they deprive the Rest of Us of dignity and economic security. If that makes some “uncomfortable,” so be it.

Jobs context

September 11, 2012 by davald

When we see the monthly press releases from the Bureau of Labor Statistics we typically lack context. We’re treated with raw numbers, generally of the form, “the economy added 80,000 new jobs in August,” or something similar. Is that good or bad? We’ll need more data to determine which.

I might suggest that we compare numbers to the population. After all, more of us are living in the U.S. each year. If employment held steady, then the employment-to-population ratio would fall, which suggests an unhealthy economy—all other things being equal.

Let’s look again at the government numbers, since people on the political right make it a point to argue that because government is “the problem,” it shouldn’t have any workers.

When you hear politicians or newspaper editors decry the rise in government spending or advocate slashing budgets, they are typically blind to the numbers. Besides, they betray a total disregard for education, in particular, and public welfare, in general.

The big waste

September 7, 2012 by davald

In my previous post I mentioned Japan and how that country’s economy has remained flat for some time now. Krugman, after criticizing Japan’s central bank (its Federal Reserve Board) for failing to build in higher inflation expectations, among other actions, has come to “apologize” to the Japanese because the U.S. reaction to its own recession has been even worse.

If we plot the annual change in Japan’s GDP since 1960, we get this:

As for the U.S. economy:

Both economies are seeing a downward trend. However, we see much more volatility here in the states. One hypothesis: manufacturing.

While both countries continue to shed manufacturing jobs as a percent of the total labor force, Japan’s economy still employs more of its people in manufacturing than does the U.S. Manufacturing jobs, typically paying family wages, would support consumer spending, the biggest component of GDP. After all, families need housing, food, transportation, clothes, and so on. And they would spend almost all of their income on such necessities.

The financial sector is different. Let’s look at employment in banking, finance, and real estate (source: Bureau of Labor Statistics).

What about corporate profits (source: NIPA tables of BEA)?

I’ve set 1950 at 100. Manufacturing profits have remained fairly flat over the ensuing decades, whereas financial sector profits soared. Not surprisingly, with the near collapse of Wall Street, those holding financial instruments lost plenty during the Great Recession. But thanks to the government’s bailout, the profits of hedge-fund managers and traders have rebounded. (Those of you underwater on your mortgages are shit out of luck.)

Krugman and Ben Bernanke both wrote papers on the Japanese experience (pdfs). Bernanke:

I will argue here that…there is much that the Bank of Japan, in cooperation with other government agencies, could do to help promote economic recovery in Japan.

The two economists believed then (in the late 90s) that Japan’s central bank should have expanded the monetary base. Bernanke added that financial leaders should commit to a higher inflation rate. Krugman and Bernanke said that Japan was in the midst of a liquidity trap; that is, the economy was—and still is—suffering from inadequate aggregate demand, which retarded growth opportunities, inducing investors to put their money in financial safe havens rather than to productive use. Bernanke invoked FDR:

Franklin D. Roosevelt was elected President of the United States in 1932 with the mandate to get the country out of the Depression. In the end, the most effective actions he took were the same that Japan needs to take—- namely, rehabilitation of the banking system and devaluation of the currency to promote monetary easing. But Roosevelt’s specific policy actions were, I think, less important than his willingness to be aggressive and to experiment—-in short, to do whatever was necessary to get the country moving again. Many of his policies did not work as intended, but in the end FDR deserves great credit for having the courage to abandon failed paradigms and to do what needed to be done.

When Bernanke wrote his paper he was the head of Princeton’s economics department. Krugman, whom Bernanke brought to Princeton, contrasts Professor Bernanke with Fed Chair Bernanke, arguing that he should act like the former in his current role. In other words, Mr. Bernanke, practice what you preached.

Japan didn’t take the advice of either economist. Nor do America’s Republicans support any efforts to devalue our currency. Yet, a weaker dollar (relative to other currencies) would make U.S. goods more economically attractive to trading partners, thus spurring exports. An increasing demand for U.S. products would stimulate domestic production and require additional workers, whose pay would be used to buy things—and so on.

Yet we sputter along, disdaining policy prescriptions that some experts, including the aforementioned, believe would right the listing economic ship. In our collective state of suspended animation we desperately seek signs of life, the so-called “green shoots” that portend good times ahead.

Thus, we react to today’s employment numbers:

Yes, the trend is inching downward. But, ladies and gentlemen, we’ve still got over eight percent of the work force involuntarily idled. It helps to see where we’ve been.

In the Bureau of Labor Statistics’ press release this morning we find that the financial sector added 11,000 new jobs but that manufacturers laid off 15,000. The employment-population ratio is the same as it was a year ago, at 58.3 percent.

It occurs to me that those who don’t give a damn about the unemployed, now labeled “moochers,” see no reason whatsoever to have the government step in, either with monetary or fiscal stimulus. Others, and I would hope that it’s a majority of us, sympathize with the plight of those seeking work but can’t find it.

Few, I should imagine, want to be on the dole; we’d much prefer to have a job, perhaps any job, that paid us enough to meet our immodest needs. In the belief that benefits to the unemployed would be temporary, we supported them and even their extensions when it became clear that things weren’t getting back to normal any time soon.

The minor good news is that there are about a million fewer would-be workers out of a job for longer than 27 weeks this year over last. Still one out of four teenagers can’t find work, nearly 15 percent of African Americans have no jobs and over 10 percent of Hispanics. If you should have a bachelor’s degree, you probably have work, since that cohort’s unemployment rate is 4.1 percent.

Bernanke and Krugman argued that persistent stagnation of the Japanese economy would take a long-term toll. Yet, the country has managed to keep its people employed, though its poverty rate, at 15.7 percent, is now the highest among the 34 OECD nations.

In the United States we impose high rates on both poverty and unemployment. Surely these figures do not bode well for our future. The unemployed lose skills, grow frustrated, and are unable to contribute. Let’s call it a very big waste.

Recovering

September 6, 2012 by davald

OECD warns that Germany, the main engine behind the Eurozone, will lapse into another recession this year. Europe could use better news.

As for the U.S., our economy is expected to grow, albeit anemically (source: FRED).

Economists tell us that this recovery will take longer than usual because the recession had much to do with the collapse of the housing bubble. About eight trillion dollars in home values evaporated, leaving millions of Americans with unprecedented levels of debt and houses are no longer available to use as ATMs. It doesn’t help that we don’t have as much money to spend as we used to, since many of us lost our jobs and everyone else hasn’t seen a raise in years. The numbers tell us that we’re busy paying down that debt, which means were buying less.

 

At some point, though still many months if not years ahead, the economy could right itself. Having paid down their debts, people will once again start buying houses, provided they have jobs. The housing surplus will end, spurring new-home construction. Happy days may return.

Or not.

Some economists, including Paul Krugman, suggest that we are already in a Japan-like stall, and that our economy will grow only by tiny fits and starts. Investor confidence will remain weak, manifesting itself in minor stock price adjustments, both up and down. We’ve known that since the Great Divergence, economic growth has been much more modest than during the relative halcyon post-war period.

 

Unfortunately for the Rest of Us, we don’t see higher wages and benefits unless the economy really grows. This happened briefly during Clinton’s second term—after he raised taxes, I might add. But, again, there are few, if any, plausible reasons to expect significant economic growth.*

____________

*  And should the economy almost miraculously recover to achieve post-war trends it will happen at the expense of the planet. In America, at least, economic growth equals greenhouse gases—oil, cars, and suburban housing tracts.
