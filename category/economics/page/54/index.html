The productivity of American workers ranks at or near the top in comparison with other western industrial democracies. Moreover, it has increased over time, enriching someone. Unfortunately, it’s not the workers whose efforts are being reflected in this metric.

Paul Krugman touches on the disconnect in this blog post. He includes the following chart from the Economic Policy Institute:

 

I had previously written about this disturbing phenomenon here. You’ll note that the disconnect coincides with the Great Divergence, which began in earnest during Ronald Reagan’s first term. Until then workers’ compensation paralleled their rising productivity. Then the rich bastards decided that they would start stealing productivity gains from the Rest of Us. Here’s a look at productivity and compensation over different historical periods.

 

The Reagan administration and a compliant Congress detested Labor, evident in the 1979-1990 period. Labor’s share of the productivity gains reached their lowest point. The recent decade is nearly as bad.

We can imagine what might have been. Say that worker compensation continued to follow the same trajectory as productivity as it had in during the relative halcyon days of post-war America. A few obvious outcomes:

  • real wages would be much higher today
  • tax revenues would be greater, and, thus, deficits smaller or non-existent
  • consumer spending would be higher, and that’s 70 percent of GDP

  • the Gini index would be lower, since the wealth would be “spread around”

But the bastards would have none of this. They prefer that the Rest of Us be mired in misery and desperation so that they can have more expensive toys and rule the world.

About those very special people

July 18, 2012 by davald

Not a day goes by that we don’t read about this or that financial firm engaging in blatantly illegal activities, from submitting false data to produce a bogus Libor to screwing underwater homeowners to defrauding municipal governments on bond issues. The frequency and gravity of the wrongdoing belies the “few rotten apples” trope trotted out to preserve the status quo. The system, we are told, is essentially sound; just purge the rotten apples and we’ll resume “doing god’s work.”

Who are they kidding? Well, surely the self-serving message is intended for the Rest of Us. Sadly it resonates, either because we are too distracted to pay attention, too inured from the innumerable reports of iniquity, or we’re just too damn stupid to appreciate the enormity of the crimes. And I do mean crimes.

Okay. Say that you’re among the few who follow these travesties of justice and you have built up your intellectual self-defense over the years. You will not see these transgressions as anomalies or aberrant behaviors. You will properly understand that the capitalist-cum-political system is putrid to the core. And the source of the putrefaction is plain old opportunistic greed. If someone feels that they can exploit this or that for another buck, they’ll do so with no moral compunction. Indeed, the rules of engagement verily dictate devious acquisitiveness.

Philosopher Peter Singer in his book A Darwinian Left accuses progressives and liberals of ignoring human nature, specifically the tendency of our species to order ourselves into hierarchies and to behave without regard for others’ interests. He suggests that conservatives have a better grasp of human nature; they don’t believe in the inherent goodness of man or his malleability. After all, Adam ate the apple.

So here’s the irony. If Singer is correct, and Berkeley cognitive scientist George Lakoff seems to agree, then conservatives by and large give in to such negative impulses and even go so far as to enshrine them. “Greed is good.” We could add: by any means necessary.

Singer argues that those on the left must first acknowledge moral frailty then do something to limit or remove the circumstances that induce misbehavior and create those that yield better fruits. What the left gets right is understanding the inherent value of cooperation and mutual benefit, creating win-win rather than win-lose situations.

Lakoff’s book Moral Politics parallels Singer’s. He, too, distinguishes between the liberal and conservative mind, describing the differences in metaphors, the nurturant parent and strict father, respectively. But Lakoff is less reserved, less academic, if you will. Lakoff concludes that the nurturant parent is preferable to the other. We’d all be better off if we were raised in families led by adults who support and foster rather than punish and reward, and he thinks that the left should do something about it.

Lakoff chronicles, accurately I think, the rise of political conservatism in America, culminating in electoral victories. The advances resulted from a comprehensive and systematic focus on promoting a conservative worldview, a philosophy. Liberals and progressives, on the other hand, have naturally advocated for society’s more vulnerable members. But, unlike the conservatives, they’ve acted issue-by-issue for the most part and spent an inordinate amount of time, energy, and resources combatting the worst of conservatives’ specific proposals, and, I would add, they’re all bad. Consequently, liberals and progressives conspicuously lack an overarching ideological thrust, a coherent and cohesive set of broad principles that both informs and invigorates appropriate actions. Lakoff:

There is Black Pride and Gay Pride, but no Liberal Pride or Progressive Pride. It’s about time liberals got some.

…

Conservatives have taken the term ‘moral’ for themselves and liberals have let them keep it! It’s time to take it back.

All too often, Lakoff argues, liberals confront conservatives on the latter’s intellectual turf, allowing themselves to be co-opted by the shibboleths of “big government” and “government spending,” for example. As a cognitive scientist, Lakoff talks about framing and context and, yes, metaphors. Narratives are important.

Right now, judging by the recent election results, conservatives have the upper hand. Look at Congress, the state houses, and governors’ mansions. Still-Democratic areas feel threatened. Progressives and liberals find themselves on the defensive, unable or perhaps unwilling to counter the conservative ideology with one of their own, one that Lakaff and Singer believe is vastly preferable to the social Darwinist Republican motif, in which the rich get richer and poor, poorer. The Rest of Us would surely be better off, and we’d get there on our own terms rather than as a byproduct of the elite’s plundering.

Progressive Pride. I like it.

Trickle down

July 18, 2012 by davald

A recurring theme amongst the G.O.P. is that there are very special people, as in superior, whose success should be praised. Not because they necessarily serve as exemplars for the Rest of Us; we’re to know out station in life and accept the fact that we’re just not good enough. Rather, our obvious betters are principally responsible for anything that comes our way, from our jobs to the food on our table. As such, we should be grateful, and envy is clearly the wrong attitude, since we stand no chance of joining the elites.

Mitt Romney said as much in a speech before the N.A.A.C.P., suggesting that we live in a merit-based society that rewards those with superlative talents, including natural intelligence, of course, who work hard, take risks, and at all times pursue the almighty dollar. From this faith, really, it follows that government must favor the the best in its various policies. That means lower taxes and fewer regulations (too constraining, don’t you know).

What about the Rest of Us? Well, you see, extending any benefits and “entitlements” to the 99-percenters produces a lazy, discontented rabble who neither appreciate their lot in life nor exert sufficient effort to make the capitalist engine work. Besides, the government subsidies to the vast majority of citizens subtracts from the resources that should and must find their way to our superiors. If they don’t succeed, evidently spectacularly so, the Rest of Us are shit out of luck.

This theme figures prominently in the behavior of several Republican governors, notably Texas’s Rick Perry, who have pugnaciously declined to participate in the federal government’s Medicaid program. They may cloak their actions in the rhetoric of opposing Washington encroachment, somehow interfering with “states’ rights,” but in so doing they are unashamedly consigning our most vulnerable brothers and sisters to lives of further desperation and misery.

The New York Times editors call this a “rush to abandon the poor.” Apt words. The editors:

Mr. Perry, too, opposes the existing Medicaid program, and, in a recent letter to Washington, said that expanding it represented “brazen intrusions into the sovereignty of our state” and would “threaten even Texas with financial ruin.” The truth is that Washington will pay all the cost of the expansion for three years (then scale back to 90 percent). Mr. Perry’s bold resistance may play well politically in his state, but it’s nothing more than a stomach punch to the millions of uninsured in Texas who will have to stay that way.

The editors remind us of some egregious statistics about Texas meanness. If any state could use federal assistance for health care it is Mr. Perry’s.

The state with the country’s worst health care record just happens to have a governor who has been the loudest voice against national efforts to improve it.

A quarter of the residents of Texas, 6.3 million people, are uninsured, by far the highest percentage in the country. (That number includes more than a million children.) Texas ranks last in prenatal care and finished last on a new federal assessment of overall health quality that examined factors like disease prevention, deaths from illnesses, and cancer treatment.

It’s always been true that Republicans don’t give a damn about the poor and infirm. Hell, care and compassion are utterly foreign concepts. But to be so “brazen” about it.

Those who fail in our winner-take-all society are not only responsible for their own fate. Any diversion of funds from the coddled rich to help them threatens the aforementioned capitalist engine. Insofar as we adopt liberal policies, god forbid, we’re only killing the golden goose. That means even more misery for the Rest of Us.

Only in America.

Mitt’s folly, Brooks’s obsequiousness, Obama’s hypocrisy

July 17, 2012 by davald

Mitt Romney spoke before the N.A.A.C.P. a few days ago. Yes, it’s difficult to wrap your brain around that image, a very privileged white Republican addressing an audience of mostly Democratic African Americans. But he did, and this is what he said, as quoted by Thomas Edsall, writing for the New York Times:

In a merit-based society, people achieve success and rewards through hard work, education, risk taking, and even a little luck. The founders considered this principle to be one endowed by our Creator, and called it the ‘pursuit of happiness.’ We call it opportunity, or we call it the freedom to choose our course in life. A merit-based, opportunity society gathers and creates a citizenry that pioneers, that invents, that builds and creates. And as these people exert the effort and take the risks inherent in invention and creation, they employ and lift the rest of us, creating prosperity for us all. The rewards they earn do not make the rest of us poorer, they make us better off.

While one of the points may have been subtle, it’s not too much of a stretch to appreciate that Mr. Romney is a living avatar of those he had in mind, the “people [who] exert the effort and take the risks.” And these special people “employ and lift the rest of us, creating prosperity for us all.”

As such, they should be revered, supported, unleashed. For the better they do, the better we do. Trickle down, you know.

A while back New York Times columnist David Brooks wrote an annoying essay on Americans’ lack of follow-ship. We have leaders, those special people. But too many of us fail to appreciate their superiors. Instead, we’ve let the liberal canard get in the way, such stuff as that we’re “created equal” and, by golly, who says you’re better than anyone else, including me? Declining to follow our betters, Brooks suggested, only worsens circumstances for the Rest of Us. For shame. Brooks:

Then there is our fervent devotion to equality, to the notion that all people are equal and deserve equal recognition and respect. It’s hard in this frame of mind to define and celebrate greatness, to hold up others who are immeasurably superior to ourselves.

It’s not difficult to discern a particular narrative here. The rich are different. The very, very, very rich that much more so. Most gain their difference through birth. Others, like Brooks, apparently, come to their superciliousness from aspiration and contrary experience.

Brooks graduated from the University of Chicago then began his journalistic career as a police reporter. Perhaps in that occupation he witnessed first hand the failure of follow-ship. Since then, as manifest in his writings, he’s been on a journey toward superiority, if not in himself, then in his naked obsequiousness to presumed “greatness,” a trait he admires above all others.

In his column this morning Brooks comes to the defense of Romney, the superior being, who has been ridiculed by fellow columnists, especially by Paul Krugman, and by Romney himself, actually, as he awkwardly and, likely, unsuccessfully appeals to ordinary mortals. Mr. Romney the businessman, as imagined by Brooks, was all about pursuing “modern capitalism” under the intellectual cloak of Joseph Schumpeter, who preached the doctrine of “creative destruction.” In brief, the weak and obsolete should be replaced by the strong and relevant. Thus, if you attack Romney’s career at Bain Capital, you attack our economic system, for which you deserve condemnation in the strongest terms. Brooks:

The president is now running an ad showing Mitt Romney tunelessly singing “America the Beautiful,” while the text on screen blasts him for shipping jobs to China, India and Mexico.

The accuracy of the ad has been questioned by the various fact-checking outfits. That need not detain us. It’s safest to assume that all the ads you see this year will be at least somewhat inaccurate because the ad-makers now take dishonesty as a mark of their professional toughness.

What matters is the ideology behind the ad: the assumption that Bain Capital, the private-equity firm founded by Romney, should not have invested in companies that hired workers abroad; the assumption that hiring Mexican or Indian workers is unpatriotic; the assumption that no worthy person would do what most global business leaders have been doing for the past half-century.

This ad — and the rhetoric the campaign is using around it — challenges the entire logic of capitalism as it has existed over several decades. It’s part of a comprehensive attack on the economic system Romney personifies.

Well, what could be more un-American than attacking Romney-style capitalism? See, Obama really is a socialist.

To be sure, Brooks continues by revealing Obama’s hypocrisy, and rightfully so. The president has no qualms about surrounding himself with executives who have practiced outsourcing as religion. I’ll name two of his “competitiveness” adviser: Jeffrey Immelt and Ursula Burns.

Immelt, as we should know, is the CEO of GE, that bastion of “modern capitalism.” From Wikipedia:

Since taking over, GE’s stock has dropped nearly 60%. General Electric has closed more than 31 plants since 2008 and let go of more than 19,000 employees, but Immelt still took home $15.2 million in 2011.

For her part, Xerox’s Ursula Burns seems determined to systematically unravel one of America’s iconic companies. Here is one blogger’s take on Burns’s performance. An excerpt:

The bottom line is this: Ursula Burns is exactly the sort of bottom-feeding CEO that her buddy Obama complains about. She has squeezed the profit, life and future out of Xerox. She has betrayed a community which just last month bent over backwards to give her the world on a platter.

And she has fired thousands of employees after pushing them to make profits in hopes of saving their jobs. As quarter after quarter has shown profits, she has cut more and more, punishing workers who have sacrificed to enrich her and the others at the top.

…

In an industry where knowledge and discovery are the keys to future profitability, Xerox decided to lose the people who pave the way to that future. In an odd hybrid of shortsightedness and greed, wrapped up in a screw-America mentality, Xerox has decided to kill the goose that lays the golden egg.

It is a decision that indicates there is an emotional balance in the company. The workers hate Ursula, and as this decision shows, she hates them, too.

A friend of mine, who will be reading this post, recently left Xerox, mostly due to the frustration with the above. He posted the following on the Internet (edited version):

The support teams have been decimated and the work, while originally promised to be performed by HCL, is now being picked up by development groups who cannot afford to rely on HCL support for assistance.  The skill-set just isn’t there.  If the question at hand cannot be found on a script somewhere, they have no idea how to answer or otherwise improvise.  Sr. level engineering talent is being replaced with very Jr. level engineering talent from HCL who are not up to the task…

Ouch.

Come to think of it, Obama and Brooks share a marked trajectory: rising from relatively humble origins to rub shoulders with elites. In so doing they have taken on the airs and effrontery of Wall Street barons and industrial tycoons. The Rest of Us still have some crumbs, but no representation from any branch of government, including the White House.

More on taxes

July 14, 2012 by davald

The Tax Policy Center has released a study of Mr. Romney’s tax proposal. Ezra Klein, writing for the Washington Post, provides a chart comparing tax rates between Obama and Romney. Here ’tis:

Ladies and gentlemen, please understand that Mr. Romney intends to lower taxes even more on the wealthy, who already enjoy the lowest tax rates in over 80 years, and raise taxes on the poorest fifth of Americans. Nevertheless, you may like that he would toss most of us a bone, those in the next few quintiles. Here’s a chart based on Tax Policy Center data showing mean, or average, income by quintile (2009):

Those of us who have even a passing acquaintance with the Occupy Wall Street movement know about the One-percenters and the 99-percenters. The former have most of the money; the latter not so much.

On these pages I have often mentioned Emmanuel Saez, a professor of economics at UC Berkeley. He studies income inequality, and has become quite the expert on the topic. I will go out on a very short limb here to predict that he will eventually win the Nobel prize in economics. Like Paul Krugman before him, he’s already won the John Bates Clark Medal, awarded to the top economist under the age of 40. That was in 2009. Two years ago he received a MacArthur Foundation award. Saez spends a lot of his time poring over income data from multiple sources.

I want to show you a couple of charts based on his work. The first shows median income (2010 dollars) of what Saez calls ‘fractiles.’

That first column to the left shows the average income of the bottom 90 percent. In 2010 dollars it was only $30K. More on this segment in a bit. You’ll see that the average income rises as we move up the “fractiles.” Those in the bottom portion of the top 10 percent made an average of $126 thousand in 2010. The next four percent, up to the top one percent, made an average of $205.5K.

Let’s pause to think about this for a moment. We’re already nudging the top one percent of household incomes (figures include capital gains income, by the way) and yet we’re still a long way from the millionaires. Indeed, as we can see as we move right along the chart, even the top .04 percent don’t average a million dollars in income. It’s only when we inch next to the top .01 percent that we see average incomes above a million, and here they’re $2.8 million.

Before I slice these fractiles even smaller, a word about that bottom 90 percent. Here’s a disturbing chart, again based on Saez data:

Yep. The average income for the bottom 90 percent in real dollars has remained essentially flat all the way back to 1965, the year I graduated high school. You may find this interesting, or not: the highest average income for this group, which is most of us, after all, was a bit over $34,683 in 2000, the last year of Clinton’s presidency. Just before the economy dropped into the toilet in 2007, the income level for this class had edged back up to $34K. It’s fallen by 12.5 percent since. (I don’t have 2011 or 2012 data.)

As for the very rich, things are much, much different.

The top 10 percent are still making modest salaries, certainly as compared with professional athletes, for example. Even the top five percent average just (?) $150K. The One-percenters pulled down $352K on average. When we skip to the top 0.01 percent, the figure skyrockets—all the way up to an average income of $7.9 million.

How big is that, you might ask? Well, it’s about 265 times the average income of the bottom 90 percent!

Back to taxes before we end.

Romney proposes to reduce the taxes of the very wealthy. The top 0.1 percent would see a reduction of 8.7 percent in federal taxes. Let’s suppose that, like Romney, these very rich people pay an effective tax rate of 15 percent. If you’re fortunate enough to be in that bracket, you would have made roughly $1.5 million before taxes. At 15 percent, your tax liability would be $225,000. Should Romney’s proposal prevail, then you would save about $20K.

Remember, the average income of the bottom 90 percent is a bit less than $30,000. Thus, Romney would give the top 0.1 percent “relief” equal to two-thirds the income of 90 percent of American workers.

If we dare jump up to the 0.01 percent, and I doubt that anyone reading this is in that bracket, we’d find that they pay an average federal tax of $1.2 million at a 15 percent effective rate. Should they receive the same 8.7 percent reduction in taxes, they’d save more than $100 thousand, or over three times the average salary of the bottom 90 percent.

So, I found it most disturbing that in the paper this morning a recent poll showed a majority favoring an extension of the Bush tax cuts even on the very wealthy! Here’s McClatchy News:

Young voters ages 18-29 favored tax cuts for everyone by a margin of 69-29, the largest margin of any age group.

Latinos favored tax cuts for all incomes by 62 percent to 36 percent. Whites supported tax cuts for every income by 50 percent to 44 percent. African-Americans split, 48 percent for limiting the tax cuts to incomes below $250,000 and 47 percent for extending them to all incomes.

And those making less than $50,000 supported tax cuts for all incomes by 53 percent to 41 percent.

Huh? A couple of final comments.

Do our youth have their collective ass stuck up their smartphones? Or is this the clearest sign of the massive failure of our public education system? Perhaps both.

Last time I checked, the rich are predominantly white, by choice, I should imagine. There aren’t too many Latinos and African Americans in the upper strata. Yet the former favored giving the rich even more money by a whopping margin. Blacks, as it turns out, were the least supportive of the racial groups in further coddling the wealthy.

Only in America.

Economic conversation

July 13, 2012 by davald

Most Thursdays you’ll find me at a local restaurant for lunch. Opposite me sits an economist and a lawyer. No, this is not the start of a joke. While our topics are wide-ranging, from evolution and genetics to politics and philosophy, the bulk of the give-and-take involves the economy. And why not? The economy is front and center for obvious reasons.

Yesterday we got around to discussing two questions:

  1. Should the government have rescued the banks?
  2. What can the government do to stimulate the economy and, if it can, should it? Our answers varied.

The economist, first off, believes that the government should not have intervened following the collapses of the housing bubble and Lehman Brothers. He offered two reasons: First, the banks under duress, those principally responsible for the housing bubble, should not have been bailed out; they took risks, so they—and they alone—should have suffered the consequences. Second, the economy would likely have shrunk without a bailout, and the downturn may have been more pronounced, but it may have recovered more quickly.

I responded by referencing the HBO movie Too Big to Fail. I previously wrote about it here—and from that post:

There is plenty of drama in this HBO production, none of it contrived. As Treasury Secretary Hank Paulson, Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke, and New York Fed chair, Timothy Geithner, gradually realized the enormity of the problem, desperation set in, along with countless sleepless nights. They first tried to persuade the tottering financial firms to fix their own mess by buying out Lehman Brothers, whose CEO, Dick Fuld, went from cocky to terrified in a matter of days if not hours. Citi, Goldman Sachs, et al. refused the deal. Paulson, Bernanke, and Geithner had only one option left: persuade Congress to rescue the banks, an action they all loathed. But it was absolutely necessary to immediately inject billions of dollars into these companies to avoid global catastrophe.

We might gain some sense of the scale of the global challenge by looking at the value of outstanding derivatives. According to Wikipedia, that figure stood at $220 trillion at the end of 2004. By the end of 2007, it had reached almost $600 trillion. Think about that for a moment. The U.S. GDP is around $15 trillion. GDP for the entire world totals $62 trillion. So, the value of derivatives is ten times as much as global GDP! Paulson et al. eventually realized that they were on the edge of a financial cliff. If these financial institutions, especially A.I.G., which had insured many of the derivatives, including those built on mortgage-based securities, declared bankruptcy, as they surely would have without T.A.R.P., the planet’s economies collapse.

Of course, I’m not privy to the negotiations or the numbers dramatized in Too Big to Fail. I do recall Princeton economist Paul Krugman appearing on PBS’s News Hour during the early days of the crisis. He did not like the idea of bailing out the banks but thought it absolutely necessary to avert a complete global meltdown. No doubt he had in mind the value of those derivatives.

Returning to my previous post, I wrote:

Recall the scene from It’s a Wonderful Life when Jimmy Stewart’s character rushes into the lobby of the Building and Loan to assuage the fears of his depositors. They were there to withdraw their money. Paulson et al. envisaged a global run on the banks as depositors lost confidence in the financial institutions.

We can imagine a counterfactual, that the governments of the U.S. and Europe stood on the sideline, refusing to give in to moral hazard—heads the banks win, tails we lose. Suppose this attitude had prevailed in the 1930s.

Following the stock market crash of 1929, the world’s economies suddenly plummeted. Roosevelt expanded the monetary base. While that helped, the economy was still a long way off from full recovery. It took the mother of all fiscal stimuli—World War II—to finally push the economy into robust recovery. The chart below, from Christine Romer’s 1992 paper on the post-crash economy, illustrates the extraordinary plunge in real GDP. The pre-war gains, Romer argues, were mostly the result of monetary expansion.

The U.S. and other economies are many times larger today in both nominal and real dollars. There was much more to lose in 2008 than in the 1930s.

Here, then, I rely on those with greater insight and understanding of the enormity of the challenge presented by the twin collapses. If the value of derivatives played a role in concentrating concern, then I cannot fault the logic of Paulson et al. Something had to be done and done very quickly. A bailout, however distasteful, was deemed necessary by those in a position to know the stakes involved.

What about the role of government in stimulating the economy? The economist, again, argues against such a role. Why? Because he believes the effects are only temporary and, moreover, will lead to inflation.

In Europe, the peripheral economies continue to feel the effects of the Great Recession. There has been little fiscal stimulus. Rather, led by Germany, the core nations are demanding the opposite—austerity—in exchange for monetary rescue. It’s not working. Europe is likely to fall into another recession. Meanwhile, unemployment rates, especially in Spain, Greece, and Italy, remain stubbornly high. Among Spanish youth, the figure is 50 percent.

As for the United States, the Federal Reserve’s tripling of the monetary basis has neither increased inflation nor raised interest rates. Nor has it had much effect on the economy. Firms continue to hoard cash rather than invest in productive activities. Krugman et al. cite a decline in aggregate demand: people lack the money to spend on goods and services—at least in sufficient amounts to spur hiring and expansion.

As I mentioned above, we do seem to have an historical record of government stimulus working, namely the huge military buildup, beginning in 1939-40 (the U.S. had already stepped up defense spending to aid the British and other eventual allies in their initial challenges from the axis powers).

We can see this blip in the following chart:

The economist argued that any stimulus must be permanent rather than temporary. Why? Because individuals and firms will adjust to the increased government spending by assuming that taxes will have to be raised to pay for the higher expenditures.

But, again, we see in the above chart that federal spending fell as sharply and as quickly as it rose in the WWII era. Spending increased again in the early 50s to fund the Korean War. (We’ll also note that despite the “guns and butter” programs of LBJ, federal spending as a percentage of GDP fell during his administration. Indeed, since the end of the Korean War, spending-vs-GDP has fallen almost every year, which calls into question the GOP claims of runaway government spending.)

In his blog yesterday Paul Krugman reminds us of John Maynard Keynes’s diagnosis of the 1930s economies. Here’s Krugman:

Consider the extended version of the “magneto trouble” metaphor I use in my recent book. Keynes argued that the Great Depression could be thought of as a failure in the car’s electrical system; so let’s think of it as a situation where your car won’t run because it has a dead battery — that is, you could get it running again with a fairly trivial and easy intervention: just buy a new battery, which costs only a tiny fraction of the expense of a new car.

Following the crash of 1929 the national unemployment rate ranged from 15 to 25 percent. It fell to 4.6 percent in 1942 and 1.2 percent in 1944, a year before WWII’s end. It remained below seven percent until 1974 (Bureau of Labor Statistics).

Did the U.S. raise taxes after the fact to pay for the war-time stimulus? Here’s a chart showing the marginal tax rate from 1929 to 1974:

In 1944 and 1945, the marginal tax rate was 94 percent. It fell in 1946 through 1949, reaching a low of 82.1 percent in 1948 and 1949. In 1950, Congress bumped it up to 91 percent, where it remained until 1964, when it fell to 77 percent. Despite the Vietnam War and his Great Society domestic programs, LBJ’s Congress reduced the marginal rate to 7o percent from 1965 to 1967.

We can conclude that the marginal tax rate was not increased in the post-war era following military Keynesianism. If “rational” economic players modified their financial decision in anticipation of higher tax rates, they did so in error.

If the history of the post-Great Depression era can serve as our guide, then, yes, a massive infusion of federal dollars will right the economic ship. But should the government do so?

The economist first began with a brief example. Suppose that a high school baseball phenom is drafted. He signs then begins his career in the minor leagues. We’ll assume that he advances a couple of levels. But when he reaches AAA his skills prove inadequate to the challenge. The major league club that drafted and signed him let’s him go. Now what? His only job has been playing baseball.

Is the government responsible for putting this man to work in something else? If we answer no, then why should the government assume responsibility for the 12 million or so who are out of work and want to? If not responsible for the one, then not responsible for the many.

I didn’t get a chance to respond to this before time ran out. But had I the opportunity I might have argued as follows.

We can assume that a single individual losing a job, though a hardship certainly to that person, can hardly be measured in terms of total economic output. If ten people lose their jobs, same thing. We can go on, adding first a hundred, then a thousand. Still there is no significant impact to the economy, though these jobless personally suffer.

At the end of 2011 there were about 109.3 million people in the private labor force. Another 22 million worked in government at all levels. That’s over 130 million men and women in jobs. If a million people are given their pink slips, then the unemployment rate is less than one percent. Even as many as five million unemployed is about four percent, which happens to be a level widely considered to reflect full employment.

In June of this year over 13 million Americans were out of the work force. Yes, most of these people are going to be miserable. But I would suggest, and others have too, that this many people out of work for extended periods is also bad for the Rest of Us. They are not making any money, save for time-limited unemployment benefits or whatever savings they may be able to draw from. So, they’re not paying taxes. Worse, they’re not spending money as they use to. And that’s a real problem.

It’s a problem because when people don’t spend money, as I mentioned above, firms don’t hire and capitalists don’t invest in productive activities. Why expand operations if there aren’t enough people to buy the goods and services?

Thus, the government and the economy suffer. The former from diminished tax receipts; the latter from lower-than-desired growth.

Did either the recent monetary expansion or Obama’s stimulus package boost inflation, as the economist warned? Let’s look at a chart from the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

The stimulus package was passed in 2009, though forty percent was in the form of tax cuts, not the best way to induce spending. The annual change in both GDP and CPI fell into negative territory from 2008 to 2009. Also, keep in mind that we’ve got a $15 trillion-a-year economy and that A.R.R.A. was about $800 billion, give or take, or just five percent of GDP. Inflation, as we see above, moved upwards, peaking at just over three percent in 2011. Since last year the inflation rate has fallen to 1.4 percent, a very low number, indeed, by historical standards.

I should end with another quick point about the 1930s in the context of inflation. Christine Romer included the following chart in her 1992 paper:

Note the fluctuations after Roosevelt assumed office. First we see a dramatic increase, up to 12 percent above normal, then a sharp fall. That happened in the beginning of Roosevelt’s second term, when he unwisely listened to that era’s austerians. When GDP fell, he resumed monetary expansion, as the chart shows. What happened to inflation?

Again, very low inflation, which never exceeded four percent.

When WWII broke out the Roosevelt administration, understandably, boosted spending on the military. The spending stoked manufacturing production, which led to massive employment increases, including, for the first time, a major rise in female workers. What happened to inflation, given the military stimulus?

There was a confounding variable in all this. In August of 1941 Roosevelt created the Office of Price Administration, which had broad authority to control prices. Its effects were most acutely realized between 1942 and 1945.  The OPA was disbanded in 1947. The high inflation that year no doubt reflected pent-up demand and industrial overhang. By 1949, the inflation rate reached negative numbers. Between the end of the Korean War and 1968 inflation never exceeded four percent.

Now, I hasten to add that in periods of “full” employment and high capacity utilization, increasing the money supply or a rise in government spending can be inflationary. Neither have boosted prices or wages since 2008 because the unemployment rate is high and capacity utilization is low.

Krugman and others argue that the economy is up against a zero lower bound. As such, expanding the money supply or fiscal stimulus will not cause prices and wages to rise—ceteris paribus. So far, he’s been right and others, like the economist, have been wrong.

Have a nice weekend.
