A nice primer on Libor

July 5, 2012 by davald

I posted briefly on the Barclays scandal involving the manipulation of Libor. Matthew Yglesias provides a quick overview of Libor and the implications of unlawful arbitrage. He adds:

But precisely because the Libor benchmark is so widely used, knowledge of where Libor is going can be helpful in a huge range of financial activities. And yet the very same banks whose interbank lending activities determine where Libor is going also have trading desks who can profit from knowing where Libor is going. The scandal is that banks—most of all Barclays—were taking advantage of this arbitrage opportunity and screwing around with Libor in order to benefit other aspects of the business.

Health care in America: a bit of history

June 30, 2012 by davald

Something I didn’t know but often wondered about: Why did the U.S. chart a much different course on health care than most other industrial nations? Why, specifically, do we have a bloated private insurance sector, extremely high medical costs, so many without insurance, and serious problems of coverage for those who do?

Writing for the New York Times, Cornell economist Robert Frank observes that we need to go back to WWII to find the answer. In both the preparation for and prosecution of the war the federal government froze wages and prices. Since there was such a great need for factory workers to produce war materiel, and given the freeze on wages, how could firms acquire sufficient labor? While the government would not allow wage competition it said nothing about benefits. Enter health insurance. Frank:

The United States probably would have adopted one of those models had it not been for historical accidents that led to widespread adoption of employer-provided plans in the 1940s. To control costs of World War II mobilization, regulators capped growth of private-sector wages, making it hard for employers to hire desperately needed workers.

But because many fringe benefits weren’t capped, employers spied a loophole: they could offer additional benefits, like health insurance. Its cost was deductible as a business expense, and in 1943 the Internal Revenue Service ruled that its value was not taxable as employee income. By 1953, employer health plans covered 63 percent of workers, versus only 9 percent in 1940.

Today, of course, the whole issue of health care has become a political football, heavily tarnished by irresponsible propaganda (is there any other kind?). Universal health insurance is socialist! Well, that’s an old epithet, used successfully by the American Medical Association when Harry Truman proposed a Canadian style of government-guaranteed health insurance. Americans, as a rule, become apoplectic at the mere mention of socialism, without bothering to ask why other, perfectly respectable countries, have viable and, in some cases, dominant socialist political parties.

Our level of discourse sucks, because we choose to be willfully ignorant and pathologically myopic. We yell and scream at one another, playing cynical games as if nothing serious were at stake.

Meanwhile, millions suffer unnecessarily in service to “freedom.”

Blessed are the poor?

June 30, 2012 by davald

Not in GOP-dominated states. Several Republican governors announced that they would not expand Medicaid, despite the federal government’s paying all of the costs over the next few years. Under the Affordable Care Act states would gradually assume more of the Medicaid costs, up to a maximum of 10 percent by 2020. Yet, even that is too much for our modern-day conservative.

A.C.A. provides subsidies to those making up to four times the poverty level to fund health care insurance premiums. But those making less than $23,050 for a family of four would be covered by Medicaid.

However, in its decision on Thursday the Supreme Court, while validating the individual mandate, allowed states to opt out of expanding Medicaid. As written, A.C.A. would have permitted the federal government to withhold all Medicaid subsidies unless states expanded Medicaid. Republicans have cheered this part of the ruling, with New Jersey Governor Christie calling it “a ray of sunshine in an otherwise cloudy day,” according to the New York Times.

Regulatory capture

June 29, 2012 by davald

Economist Simon Johnson argues that the Federal Reserve has a conflict-of-interest problem. In particular, Jaimie Dimon, the CEO of JP Morgan Chase is a director of the New York bank of the FED. And get this, he was on the board during the negotiations chronicled in Too Big to Fail, which I wrote about here. Timothy Geithner, then-chair of the New York Fed, answered to Dimon while Dimon’s firm was receiving billions of dollars of “capital infusion” to stabilize financial markets. Johnson writes:

Over the last several weeks on this blog, I have expressed a broad set of concerns about governance arrangements at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. I have made the specific case for Jamie Dimon, the chief executive of JPMorgan Chase, to step down from the New York Fed’s board because of the large, unexpected losses in his bank’s London proprietary trading operation — and the fact that these activities and their disclosure are now under investigation by the Fed.

JP Morgan Chase lost as much as $9 billion on a London trading operation. Yet Dimon, who called the operation “stupid,” has bus-loads of lobbyists pressuring Congress to cut away the regulations and enforcements in the Dodd-Frank bill.

Audacious, to be sure.

A new manifesto

June 29, 2012 by davald

Paul Krugman co-authored “a manifesto for economic sense.” He urges fellow economists and kindred travelers to sign it, which you can do here.

The manifesto debunks the conventional wisdom spouted by Very Serious People, including many of the so-called “fresh water” school of economic thinking, whose adherents preach markets über alles, the folly of government intervention, and, by all means, slash federal spending to reduce the debt. From the manifesto:

As a result of their mistaken ideas, many Western policy-makers are inflicting massive suffering on their peoples. But the ideas they espouse about how to handle recessions were rejected by nearly all economists after the disasters of the 1930s, and for the following forty years or so the West enjoyed an unparalleled period of economic stability and low unemployment. It is tragic that in recent years the old ideas have again taken root. But we can no longer accept a situation where mistaken fears of higher interest rates weigh more highly with policy-makers than the horrors of mass unemployment.

You’ll note in the long list of signers the Snohomish Observer. Care to join me?

Speaking of markets

June 27, 2012 by davald

Matt Taibbi, in his inimitable style, tells us that major financial institutions behave(d) like Mafia dons in defrauding municipalities. Basically, just about any entity that sold municipal bonds was robbed through collusive actions to rig interest rates.

Someday, it will go down in history as the first trial of the modern American mafia. Of course, you won’t hear the recent financial corruption case, United States of America v. Carollo, Goldberg and Grimm, called anything like that. If you heard about it at all, you’re probably either in the municipal bond business or married to an antitrust lawyer. Even then, all you probably heard was that a threesome of bit players on Wall Street got convicted of obscure antitrust violations in one of the most inscrutable, jargon-packed legal snoozefests since the government’s massive case against Microsoft in the Nineties – not exactly the thrilling courtroom drama offered by the famed trials of old-school mobsters like Al Capone or Anthony “Tony Ducks” Corallo.

Today, the New York Times reports that the British financial firm Barclays settled a claim brought by U.S. and London regulators. The Times:

Barclays has agreed to pay more than $450 million to resolve accusations that it attempted to manipulate key interest rates, the first settlement in a sprawling global investigation involving many of the world’s biggest banks.

The British bank struck a deal with regulators in Washington and London, as well as the Justice Department. The settlement is seen as the first in a series of potential cases against other major financial firms.

Last night I watched the HBO dramatized documentary Too Big to Fail. Wall Street’s major financial institutions, as we know, bet wrong, losing trillions of dollars in booked assets. In 2008 we learned that long-established brokerages were hemorrhaging money as their sophisticated financial instruments began to unravel. There is a brief scene in which the ingredients of the looming financial meltdown are explained. Quite the primer.

Essentially, Wall Street and London, for the most part, discovered a way to extract billions of dollars by packaging mortgage-backed securities then selling them to unwitting investors all over the globe. But like a garbage incinerator in constant need of greater quantities of fuel, Wall Street demanded more and more mortgages to bundle with other financial assets to sell for profits. So, the qualifications to buy a home were relaxed. Instead of needing a high credit score and 20-percent downpayment, a much lower credit score and no cash would get you into a house. These were known as sub-prime mortgages, typically with variable rates. The initial rate may have been affordable, but the resets were not. To hedge against the unthinkable—declining home values—Wall Street turned to the insurance giant A.I.G. to cover against losses. When the resets kicked in and people could no longer pay the mortgages, the banks foreclosed. Foreclosures precipitated a sharp decline in home values. Contagion set in. A.I.G. had insufficient deposits to cover the failed mortgage-backed securities and their derivatives. Whoops. A.I.G. had trillions of dollars in potential claims across the globe and nowhere near the money to pay them.

There is plenty of drama in this HBO production, none of it contrived. As Treasury Secretary Hank Paulson, Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke, and New York Fed chair, Timothy Geithner, gradually realized the enormity of the problem, desperation set in, along with countless sleepless nights. They first tried to persuade the tottering financial firms to fix their own mess by buying out Lehman Brothers, whose CEO, Dick Fuld, went from cocky to terrified in a matter of days if not hours. Citi, Goldman Sachs, et al. refused the deal. Paulson, Bernanke, and Geithner had only one option left: persuade Congress to rescue the banks, an action they all loathed. But it was absolutely necessary to immediately inject billions of dollars into these companies to avoid global catastrophe.

This may strike some as hyperbolic. Global catastrophe? Really? But the facts as revealed in Too Big to Fail and elsewhere strongly support the contention. The world’s economic system was at the edge of a very high cliff.

Recall the scene from It’s a Wonderful Life when Jimmy Stewart’s character rushes into the lobby of the Building and Loan to assuage the fears of his depositors. They were there to withdraw their money. Paulson et al. envisaged a global run on the banks as depositors lost confidence in the financial institutions.

Of course, and as the production tells us in the textual epilogue, the bailed-out financial firms gave the finger to the Rest of Us. The CEOs of Goldman etc. quickly resumed their greedy ways, realizing record-breaking compensation packages. If my memory serves, just ten financial institutions now control 77 percent of Wall Street’s wealth. They have become so large since the near-crash that they truly are too big to fail.

Meanwhile, the Wall Street CEOs have legions of lobbyists prowling the corridors and offices of Congress in a concerted effort to water down if not altogether eliminate provision of the Dodd-Frank legislation designed to thwart the worst abuses of the housing bubble and near financial meltdown. Unfortunately for the Rest of Us, elections have become so expensive that our elected officials feel they must turn to Wall Street for the requisite campaign donations.

Yes, Virginia. Our government is corrupt.
