Times’ tension

November 13, 2012 by davald

We should not be shocked to learn that two writers for the New York Times, David Brooks and Paul Krugman, test each other’s patience, with the latter thinly veiling his contempt for the former’s opinions. This morning we are treated to a rejoinder of sorts to Krugman’s recent post, wherein he counseled Obama to essentially tell the obstructionist Republicans to go f**k yourself. In specific terms, the economist warned against giving in to the Grand Bargain devotees, those, like Brooks, who believe that success can come only through statesmanlike negotiations. Wasn’t that the failed strategy of Obama’s first term? And what did he get for all that “bipartisanship”? Brooks:

Some on the left are suggesting that he adopt a strategy of confrontation and conquest. The president should use the advantages of victory to crush the spirit of the Republican House majority, they say. Reject the Grand Bargain approach. Instead, take the country over the so-called fiscal cliff. Blame it on the Republicans who are unwilling to even raise taxes on the rich. Wait until they fold, and then you will have your way.

The first thing to say about this strategy is that it is irresponsible. The recovery is fragile. Europe may crater. China is ill. Business is pulling back at the mere anticipation of a fiscal cliff. It’s reckless to think you can manufacture an economic crisis for political leverage and then control the cascading results.

Here’s what Krugman advised:

So President Obama has to make a decision, almost immediately, about how to deal with continuing Republican obstruction. How far should he go in accommodating the G.O.P.’s demands?

My answer is, not far at all. Mr. Obama should hang tough, declaring himself willing, if necessary, to hold his ground even at the cost of letting his opponents inflict damage on a still-shaky economy. And this is definitely no time to negotiate a “grand bargain” on the budget that snatches defeat from the jaws of victory. Krugman reckons that making a deal with the devil is worse than no deal at all. Krugman:

So what should he do? Just say no, and go over the cliff if necessary.

To what effect? Unlike the debt-celing crisis, which really would have been disastrous had Congress not come to its senses, Krugman avers that “nothing very bad will happen to the economy if agreement isn’t reached until a few weeks or even a few months into 2013. So there’s time to bargain.”

Brooks, at the vanguard of the Very Serious, returns to his favorite whipping boys as he pitches dealmaking: minimizing the value of taxing the rich and reforming “entitlements.” Brooks says, “higher tax rates on the rich…wouldn’t even produce enough revenue to cover a tenth of the deficit.” Thus, we should proceed with screwing the Rest of Us again and again.

As if on cue, Krugman produces a chart on his blog this morning that illustrates the consequences of raising the eligibility age for Social Security and Medicare for those in the bottom half.

;

Brooks, who is surely in the top fifty percent of income earners, wants to see that eligibility age increased even further, to the detriment of half the population. As for taxing the rich, we might take another look at the impact of Bush’s policies, including tax cuts, versus Obama’s contributions to the deficits.

;

Cutting taxes during Bush’s terms represented 35 percent of the $5 trillion hit on the federal budget for the 2002-2009 period. Meanwhile, Bush went to war on a credit card.

We can put these numbers in a different perspective (source).

But Very Serious People seem to be uncomfortable with these figures, however presented. They insist, with Romney, that too many of us prefer to live on the dole, mooching from the self-reliant job creators. Thus, any and all efforts to send the proper message to the freeloaders are not only welcomed but deemed salutary. Tough love, you know.

The Republicans, ladies and gentlemen, care not a whit about the Rest of Us. They’ve been quite clear about their attitudes. Too clear for even David Brooks to ignore. Yet, he continues to advocate on their behalf, insisting that entitlement reform remain at the top of the national agenda.

One can hope that Obama pays more attention to Krugman than Brooks. But we shall see.

Repeating history

November 12, 2012 by davald

After FDR’s first re-election the president listened to what passed as Very Serious People of the mid to late 30s. They counseled fiscal austerity, warning that the rising federal deficit would deliver unbelievable horrors. So, a dutiful FDR sought to reduce the budget and, therefore, spending, only to observe what John Maynard Keynes and others had predicted: more depression economics (source for graph below: NIPA tables, 1.1.5).

 

In his column this morning Paul Krugman issues a warning of his own. Adopting the policies of modern-day Very Serious People will likely contract the economy—similar to what happened during the initial years of FDR’s second term. As we can see from the chart below, the economy is still quite fragile.

 

Yet, rumors swirl that Obama will replace Timothy Geithner with Erskine Bowles, and if any name evokes seriousness, it is Bowles’s. Krugman:

But in case there’s any reality to it, let’s recall his record. Mr. Bowles, like others in the deficit-scold community, has indulged in scare tactics, warning of an imminent fiscal crisis that keeps not coming. Meanwhile, the report he co-wrote was supposed to be focused on deficit reduction — yet, true to form, it called for lower rather than higher tax rates, and as a “guiding principle” no less. Appointing him, or anyone like him, would be both a bad idea and a slap in the face to the people who returned President Obama to office.

I’m sure that if Obama doesn’t read his daily Krugman someone on his staff does. They would do well to heed his advice. After all, he’s been spot on with his analysis during these troubled times.

Return on investment [u]

November 8, 2012 by davald

I happened to catch an episode of Rick Steves’s travel program. He was in Denmark, showing us the sights and sounds and people. One comment stood out, at least for me. The Danes, Steves suggested, willingly pay high taxes, but, and this is the interesting part, they demand a return on their investment. So far, that calculus seems to work, and Danish people enjoy splendid public services, including universal health care and transportation.

This got me thinking. We Americans generally loathe taxes, and we believe that ours are too high. Never mind that they are among the lowest of the industrial democracies. With few exceptions, we record our antipathy via the ballot box, as Washingtonians did again, passing the latest Tim Eyman super-majority tax requirement for Olympia. Otherwise, we vote Republican.

Could contempt for taxes be a consequence of poor return? When we look about us we see bridges and highways in disrepair. Our school buildings are falling apart. We have no well-functioning passenger trains to speak of. Our cities are generally dirty, because we don’t hire people to clean them. And everyone knows that our public education system doesn’t work; provide your own check list of dissatisfaction, whether it be the teachers’ fault or crowded classrooms or misguided curricula.

In our heads we may judge tax revenues adequate, since they’re already too high. So when considering the lousy state of public services, we instinctively resist giving the bastards any more money, which will surely be wasted or otherwise spent foolishly.

Consider this possibility. Imagine that there is a critical threshold of taxing, below which we get only mediocrity combined with persistent loathing of taxes, and above which we begin to realize societal value, the kind of value expected by the Scandinavians but rarely experienced here in America. How high could we go? (source of data below)

Emanuel Saez, French-born but now a professor of economics at Berkeley, wrote a provocative paper with his longtime collaborator, Thomas Piketty, arguing that marginal tax rates could be raised significantly (up to 80 percent) without adversely affecting economic growth.

The authors believe that extreme inequality is problematic in itself, thus warranting greater wealth distribution via a more progressive tax code. But for our purposes here, the extra revenues derived from higher tax rates would go to schools, transportation, health care, and other public services that the private sector is unable or unwilling to provide.

Of course, there is no way that Congress would even attempt to increase tax rates, and they’d surely run for the hills at the first mention of Scandinavia. Thus, we are consigned to mediocrity.

But at least we know that it’s possible to have a much better public sector and that citizens are evidently willing to pay for it. Just not in America—yet. Wait a minute, we used to pay much higher taxes and had good schools and roads. Ah, those were the days.

Insular punditocracy

November 2, 2012 by davald

As a rule I find nothing useful in anything David Brooks writes, except for the possibility that he may represent a certain point of view that remans terribly obscure to me and perhaps to you of which I should be aware. I could add, in the most left-handed sort of way, that his words provide more than adequate grist for others’ mills. He writes. Detractors counter—big time.

I have no idea how to get inside the mind of Brooks (or anyone else’s, for that matter). But I often wonder how he keeps coming back with what I and many important people judge hogwash. He will not be deterred.

And that’s a problem for anyone claiming intellectual gravitas, as in Very Serious People. How does one keep at it column by column, if as soon as your “insights” hit the Internet, an avalanche of counterpoise buries everything you’ve fashioned? How does one face the mirror each morning with the knowledge that people with far greater smarts think you’re a blooming idiot?

If one writes in a style suffused with self-importance and expects to be taken seriously, at what point does one begin to realize that you’re saying the wrong things? At what point do you reevaluate your cherished biases? Most important, when does one change his or her mind?

Masochistic readers of Brooks know that he steadfastly adheres to a set of beliefs about public policy. The short list includes:

  • rein in entitlements
  • cut the federal deficit and debt
  • and to the nation’s highest office holders—always think “grand”

The professor who writes under the handle “Gin and Tacos” expresses complete befuddlement at one of Brooks’s latest ramblings.

Everyone line up and kick him around for a while. This column might contain, even given Brooks’ lengthy career of logic molestation, the most ass-backwards “logic” I’ve ever seen. Just try to make sense of it. I dare you. Apparently, Mitt Romney is the better choice because he believes in nothing concretely, and that will allow him to accomplish “bigger” things with…the House Republicans, or moderates, or the Bloc Quebecois, or what in the hell is David Brooks talking about. And that, my friends, is “The Upside of Opportunism.”

Economist Dean Baker can be relied on to offer his quick reactions to Brooks’s meanderings. His latest rejoinder includes:

If you get through the excess verbiage in his column, the main point is that President Obama hasn’t moved to cut Social Security and Medicare in his first term. This is what Brooks means when he says:

“get our long-term entitlement burdens under control, get our political system working, shift government resources from the affluent elderly to struggling young families and future growth,” and by his later call for “sacrifice.”

Of course Brooks doesn’t really mean “affluent” elderly. We know that Brooks and his political allies question whether even people earning above $250,000 a year are affluent when it comes to tax cuts. If the cuts in Social Security and Medicare were restricted to this group then we would barely need to change the projections for these programs. There are so few seniors with incomes above this cutoff that whether or not they get Medicare and Social Security makes almost no difference to the financial health of these programs.

I suspect the New York Times pays Brooks a handsome salary in addition to providing him with a prestigious platform from which to spout his views. I also suspect that, despite his addled rhetoric, he receives encouragement from like-minded readers. (I could view the comment section, I suppose, to confirm or deny.)

Yes, all of this goes with the territory of opinion writing, of which I inhabit, though in much smaller measure. While I try to include charts and links to support my statements, I am decidedly biased, the sources of which would take several psychiatric sessions to discover.

But let’s take just one of Brooks’s favored assertions: that “entitlements” must be cut. Why? In order to reduce the deficit. Why is it important to reduce the deficit? Well, he doesn’t really say, other than to suggest that Very Serious People believe it so. Will deficits retard economic growth? Will they discourage private investment? Do deficits cause unemployment? Again, he doesn’t say.

Suppose we grant him the proposition. What happens if the government were to cut entitlements (read Social Security and Medicare) in the near term? We could call such reductions “austerity.” And how is such a strategy working elsewhere? Not very well. Austerity  under these depressed economic conditions actually impedes economic growth, as anyone with only a cursory understanding of Keynesian macroeconomics would appreciate.

Would Brooks also argue that the people receiving the entitlements don’t deserve them? Scrooge-like, is he jumping into bed with Mitt Romney in tacitly labeling recipients “moochers”? Well, I paid into Social Security. I’ve also paid into Medicare. I expect, as a quid pro quo, to benefit from these programs—and I am hardly among the “affluent” elderly. Besides, why should a nation as wealthy as ours deny senior citizens a decent retirement, relatively free of health care cost worries? Should such security belong only to the self-reliant rich in the form of private purchases?

We all engage in confirmation bias; we select evidence and fellow judgments that bolster our views. Brooks is likely no exception. Outside his published world he may find studies and opinions that give him aid and comfort. In this modern era of instant and overwhelming information one could easily happen upon supportive narratives, even with an academic provenance. Yet how can or should we judge the evidence? To whom or what might we appeal?

In the end, Brooks probably fancies himself somewhere in the political middle. Indeed, he defines himself by what he is not. He is not far right. He is certainly not far left. This middle, as we can readily appreciate, is huge, allowing much room to maneuver, to proclaim one’s earnestness by distance. I am not Tea Party. I am not socialist. So, I am serious. No need for facts or logic.

But, with Gin and Tacos, what the hell are you talking about?

Tax rates and the economy

November 1, 2012 by davald

Readers of this blog may recall several posts on tax rates and economic growth. Republicans have long argued, without evidence, that lower tax rates stimulate economic growth. Not so.

Of course, you should not take my word for it. Appeal to a higher authority, say The Congressional Research Service, a non-partisan adjunct to Congress. You can read its until-now-suppressed report here. But consider its conclusions:

The top income tax rates have changed considerably since the end of World War II. Throughout the late-1940s and 1950s, the top marginal tax rate was typically above 90%; today it is 35%. Additionally, the top capital gains tax rate was 25% in the 1950s and 1960s, 35% in the 1970s; today it is 15%. The average tax rate faced by the top 0.01% of taxpayers was above 40% until the mid-1980s; today it is below 25%. Tax rates affecting taxpayers at the top of the income distribution are currently at their lowest levels since the end of the second World War.

The results of the analysis suggest that changes over the past 65 years in the top marginal tax rate and the top capital gains tax rate do not appear correlated with economic growth. The reduction in the top tax rates appears to be uncorrelated with saving, investment, and productivity growth. The top tax rates appear to have little or no relation to the size of the economic pie.

However, the top tax rate reductions appear to be associated with the increasing concentration of income at the top of the income distribution. As measured by IRS data, the share of income accruing to the top 0.1% of U.S. families increased from 4.2% in 1945 to 12.3% by 2007 before falling to 9.2% due to the 2007-2009 recession. At the same time, the average tax rate paid by the top 0.1% fell from over 50% in 1945 to about 25% in 2009. Tax policy could have a relation to how the economic pie is sliced—lower top tax rates may be associated with greater income disparities.

Imagine that. The fundamental policy prescription of the GOP—ever lower taxes—doesn’t spur economic development. The only thing lower taxes on the rich accomplish is greater wealth accumulation at the top. The Republicans didn’t like the conclusion, so they suppressed the report.

America’s dystopian future

October 30, 2012 by davald

That’s the clear initial takeaway from a new book by Chrystia Freeland. She’s writing about Plutocrats, with the dismal subtitle: “The Rise of the New Global Super Rich and the Fall of Everyone Else.”

The author recently appeared on Bill Moyers’s show with Matt Taibbi. Though she acknowledged that the 50s and 60s were good for most Americans, with incomes rising for an expanding middle class, Freeland dismisses talk of returning to those “relative halcyon days,” when labor unions meant something and workers could count on a job paying family wages.

I may have more to say about the book in subsequent posts, but for now I will emphasize a couple of points Freeland makes. First, the new super rich are indeed global, having multiple homes in disparate places and transacting business in the splendid offices and luxurious hotels of Dubai, Hong Kong, New York, London, and increasingly in the BRIC nations (Brazil, Russia, India, and China). She tells us that these über wealthy have more in common with each other, no matter their provenance, than they do with their neighbors, using the term very loosely. While they may express nostalgia and perhaps even an emotional attachment to their native countries, the super rich are in the business of exploitation, regardless of geography or cultural barriers. Their obsessive goal is to make money, lots of it. The second point has to do with Americans, both as workers and consumers.

Steve Jobs famously remarked to President Obama that the manufacturing and assembling of Apple products “aren’t coming back.” I would add: if they were ever here to begin with. The reason is much simpler than the explanation proffered by Jobs: workers in  Asia and more recently Brazil and India have the requisite skills but charge only a small fraction of U.S. labor costs. For example, the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics estimates that China employs about 100 million in “manufacturing,” and the average hourly wage is $1.86.

If we allow ourselves to think outside our American box, we might appreciate the benefits of increasing globalization. The developing nations are getting richer, though it may be at our expense. Consider this excerpt from Freeland’s book:

“There are going to be one billion consumers joining the middle class in Asia. I think for us to reduce unemployment, exports are going to be a key way to do it,” [GE’s Jeffrey] Immelt told me. “It’s this country’s only destiny just because most of the consumers are some place other than here.”

Export what? GE is a major outsourcer of jobs. Indeed, most legacy industrial firms domiciled in the U.S. have exported production to other countries, where labor is much, much cheaper. Also, we’re not doing so well in our balance of exports and imports.

But capitalist economies, both global and domestic, depend on people buying things. And despite severe obstacles we Americans strive to do out part (source: BLS).

 

 

How can we continue to buy if our wages remain stagnant? Well, we borrow.

 

Isn’t massive debt how we got into our current mess? Indeed.

Moreover, our perceived need to pay down our debts following the collapse of the housing bubble impeded economic recovery. We weren’t spending and we weren’t saving. We were deleveraging.

These three components—debt, income, and buying—and how they interact pose problems. We cannot continue to finance purchases through debt, and our savings rate sucks.

 

Since Reagan assumed office, Americans have saved less and less of their wages, dropping below two percent in 2005. There are no signs that U.S. wages for the Rest of Us will increase any time soon, if ever, so to keep buying we’ll borrow more and save less over time.

The biggest problem of the three is income. We don’t have enough of it. That’s the Rest of Us, I mean.

Berkeley’s Emmanuel Saez estimates that 93 percent of the income gains during the meager recovery have gone to the upper one percent.

 

Well, that top one percent includes the plutocrats Freeland discusses. They’ve got most of the money, and insofar as we borrow to finance spending, we owe them our dollars too.

Yet, even the plutocrats realize that ultimately there must be buyers for their goods and services. They can’t simply trade amongst themselves, as if I’ll do your laundry if you do mine. GE’s Immelt suggests that those buyers will not be Americans. Rather, they will be Chinese, or Brazilian, or Indian, or even African.

Whoever buys needs income. Since Americans, for the most part, aren’t getting any more, those foreign (to us) buyers will need higher wages. I suspect that’s happening. Our incomes will remain stagnant or decline while those of the Chinese et al. will grow.

Globalization without government fetters will tend to even out wages across the planet. As always, those who desire work will have to bring something to the table, whether it be skills, brains, the right wage, or, more likely, some combination of all three.

And speaking of brains, here’s another quote from Freeland’s book:

“It is no longer about brain drain, or even brain gain,” Dr. Wang [Huiyao, founder and president of Beijing’s Center for China and Globalization] said. “It is about global brain circulation.”

Meanwhile, the plutocrats will exploit the combinations. They don’t care about geography. Increasingly, that means that they don’t care about the American as either worker or consumer. They need sufficient productive capacity and demand, and they will find it wherever.

Welcome to dystopia.

Screwing the neighborhood

October 29, 2012 by davald

I know, most of us don’t think that our local real estate agent is the quintessential villain. They just sell houses, right?

But I don’t mean to vilify the woman who just sold you a brand new home. She spent time shepherding you around suburbia in search of that “perfect” house in the “perfect” neighborhood within a short drive of the “perfect” school. Chances are she earned her commission and gained a satisfied customer.

I’m talking about all of the real estate agents put together, along with the mortgage brokers, banks, and city council members. But I repeat myself.

My family spent almost three decades in a city fashioned willy-nilly by what I’ll call ‘the real estate industry.’ It was a small town when we arrived, with fewer than 5,000 official residents. Today it is the second largest city in Snohomish County, with a population over 60,000.

Marysville, Wash., is the poster child for how not to allow the purveyors of dirt and sticks to run amok. It is as if the industry concocted a chunky goo of politics-cum-real-estate and simply spread it over expanding acres of soil once home to strawberry fields. No building exceeds two stories.

The placement of civic structures makes no sense, with the library way over here, the city hall (converted Allstate Insurance office) up the street, schools scattered near and far, and a post office much too small to serve the burgeoning population.

Amidst the goo are cars, made absolutely necessary by the patterns of development. Forget about walking anywhere.

Consider the above rant a preamble to a story running in this morning’s New York Times. It’s about the mortgage interest deduction.

As it happens, very few of us can benefit from this curiously popular tax contrivance. As the Times tells us:

According to Joseph Rosenberg, a research associate at the Urban-Brookings Tax Policy Center, only about 30 percent of taxpayers itemize, rather than take the standard deduction. And the majority of these itemizers are upper-middle and upper-income households.

Within that privileged category, the people who tend to derive the greatest dollar benefit from the mortgage interest deduction are households earning $100,000 to $500,000 a year.

“About two-thirds of the total benefit go to that group in the 80th through the 99th income percentiles,” Mr. Rosenberg said.

Once again the top income earners have succeeded in depriving the Rest of Us of the revenues we need to actually build a better society. And you can guess who is vigorously defending this unfair tax advantage. The real estate industry, of course. The Times:

Real estate and building industry groups have loudly condemned proposals by both presidential campaigns to shrink the mortgage interest deduction. Central to their arguments is the long-hallowed deduction’s value to the middle class. But a closer look at who benefits suggests that this perception, though prevalent, is not accurate.

First of all, we don’t have much of a middle class any more. Those in the bottom 90 percent make an average of less than $30,000 a year (Saez data).

(We should note, however, that incomes for the Rest of Us were steadily climbing from the end of WWII until—you guessed it—the Great Divergence. There was a slight uptick during the latter Clinton years.)

Now here are some interesting tidbits. The deduction enjoys broad support, even though 70 percent of Americans don’t benefit from it. I’ll suggest the Archie Bunker syndrome: he didn’t want his taxes raised because, he imagined, some day he might be rich. The Times:

Interestingly, despite its concentrated effect, various surveys have shown broad public support for the mortgage deduction. This is possibly because, in part, “lots of people who don’t itemize today may aspire to be itemizers in the future,” said Jed Kolko, the chief economist for Trulia, a real estate listing Web site.

I urge readers to appreciate the inertia effect of the rich preserving their legislative fruits. The Times:

“Policies that give concentrated benefits to a small number of people often survive better,” Mr. Kolko said, “because the benefit is great enough to make them resist changes in it. They have an incentive to organize to protect that benefit.”

Inequality is good for you. Not. [u]

October 26, 2012 by davald

Okay, that’s not the explicit message the rich convey, but it’s close. Without our plutocrats the Rest of Us would be in even worse shape. You buy that, don’t you?

There should be no question about America’s inequality. It’s high and getting higher. Nor should there be any question that the extremely wealthy, the top 0.01 percent of households, extract the bulk of income from the economy. Let’s revisit some data (from Emmanuel Saez).

But despite the wealth gap, the Rest of Us haven’t fared so well. We see this clearly in the following chart:

 

Notice that flat line at the bottom. That’s the Rest of Us. And, yes, the average income of the top 0.01 percent surpassed $7 $19 million before the Great Recession. In 2010 it fell back to a piddly $5.9 $16.3 million. How can they possibly manage? Oh, and these numbers do not include capital gains, which comprise most of the super rich’s income.

Keep in mind that these charts reflect inflation-adjusted dollars. Going back to 1917, the earliest data year, the income of the bottom 90 percent has hardly budged.

Nobel Prize-winning economist Joseph Stiglitz tells New York Times readers that inequality actually stunts economic growth. He also reminds us that social and economic mobility in the U.S. is the lowest of all advanced industrial democracies. Then Prof. Stigltiz sets out to debunk five essential myths promulgated by Romney, the Republicans as a whole, and the capitalist class. All of the following are false:

  1. America is a land of opportunity.
  2. Trickle-down economics works.
  3. The rich are “job creators.”
  4. The costs of reducing inequality are so great that “we would be killing the goose that lays the golden egg” should we try to raise the incomes of the bottom 90 percent and lower them at the top.
  5. Because markets are efficient and self-regulating the government should not meddle in the economy.

In a recent Harper’s essay (subscription required) Jeffrey Madrick emphasizes the importance of raising taxes, especially on the wealthy. Americans pay about 26 percent of their income on taxes of all forms. The OECD average is 38 percent. Yet higher tax rates do not impede growth. Madrick:

…the high-tax economies grow about as fast as ours does, sometimes faster. Prosperous Denmark, Norway, and Sweden have tax rates well above 40 percent.

Suppose Americans were to pay the OECD average. Madrick says that over 10 years tax revenues would increase by $16 to $17 trillion. The Budget Control Act, which, if allowed to trigger, would reduce the deficit by only $1.5 trillion over the same period.

Now here’s a statement that’s sure to rankle the Randians. “Better government, not weaker government, is the path to prosperity.” The whacky Tea Partiers would prefer no government, except for a bloated defense and a larger criminal justice system. But if we are to make the investments necessary to restore the economy and, I dare say, “compete” with other countries—or at least improve our trade balance—then our governments need more dollars. Here’s Madrick:

Politicians and ideologues are playing a cruel game by keeping serious tax increases off the table, but it is especially hypocritical to do so in the name of fiscal responsibility. America’s budget problem is a revenue problem, not a spending problem. The current national conversation about tax hikes is a fine example of political deference to the rich and powerful. It is not good economics.

So, ladies and gentlemen, our first order of business is to raise taxes. The rich will do just fine, by the way, and the Rest of Us will benefit from a solvent Social Security system, better transportation, greener energy (Madrick recommends a carbon tax), and world-class schools.

Unless, of course, you really enjoy misery.
