Irrational choice

December 10, 2011 by davald

You may share my bewilderment over so many of our fellow citizens choosing political candidates whose benefactors and ideologies betray positions and interests antithetical to those of the Rest of Us. No? Then feel free to skip this.

If one has been paying attention to the media and blogosphere since the collapse of the housing bubble and the emergent Great Recession one could not escape the sharp divide in rhetoric, diagnosis, and prescription. While consensus exists that the economy is not like it used to be, none exists beyond that trivial observation.

I have titled this blog “Irrational choice.” By that I mean deciding against one’s own interests, as opposed to a more literal connotation of unreasonable or illogical. Those who vote against their perceived self-interest no doubt possess reason and can wade through a syllogism. I might suggest that what’s going on is more subtle and yet perverse.

In his book Thinking, Fast and Slow, psychologist Daniel Kahneman tells us that most of what passes for cognition is instinctive and unconscious. He calls this “System 1.” Deliberation, inference, conscious mental behavior involves “System 2.” These do not stand for physical locations within our brain, but rather distinct processes. System 1 thinking (“fast”) can be affected by an infinite number of what Kahneman calls “priming effects.” (He didn’t coin the term.) The problem for our species is that System 1 dominates, even to the point that we “see” the world through prior influences and, especially, associations. Why? Kahneman suggests that System 1 mental behavior is easy, requiring less energy than System 2. Besides, it keeps us out of danger, since System 2 can’t react to warning signs quickly enough.

System 2 tends to be lazy and readily defers to System 1. It requires concentration, calories, and selectivity. We’ve got to block out stimuli in order to focus our attention. Kahneman gives the example of trying to calculate in one’s head the product of two numbers, 17 and 24. Unless we’re another “Rainman,” we’re forced to drop everything else to perform the mental arithmetic. System 2 would prefer (I’m being metaphorical here) to take the easy train, just because it is so much more intensive than System 1. It really doesn’t want to overrule our unconscious biases.

I’ll venture out onto a long intellectual limb here: the Republican mind operates almost exclusively in the System 1 realm; the Democratic mind avails itself of System 2 more frequently. Stepping out a bit further, Republicans make more irrational choices than Democrats.

Now, I’m not the first person to draw such an inference. Following the GOP landslide in 1994, Berkeley cognitive scientist George Lakoff was so anguished by voters’ electoral preferences that he decided to write a book. Published two years later, it bore the title Moral Politics. It initially carried the subtitle What Conservatives Know That Liberals Don’t. He replaced this in the second edition with How Liberals and Conservatives Think. (Thomas Frank, now the editor of Harper’s magazine, was also befuddled by the conservative victories, which required so many to vote against their interests. He wrote a book in response, What’s the Matter with Kansas?)

Lakoff set about to describe two broad categories of metaphors to distinguish between the conservative and the liberal, or progressive. The former falls under the strict father morality; the latter under nurturant parent.

The strict father is all about self-discipline, self-reliance, and tough love—I’m doing this or that to you, child, for your own good. It will make you stronger. Good behavior is rewarded; bad behavior is punished. If you fail, chances are it’s your own damn fault.

One also sees this constellation within the conservative Christian religion, at least as practiced in the U.S. There is a definite hierarchy. God, the father, is at the top. All must show obeisance to him, or else incur his wrath. The bible is a set of rules that must be followed.

Likewise, in the family unit the father/husband is the authoritative figure; everyone else defers to him, including the “submissive” wife. In turn, the father/husband is expected to provide for the family. Failure to do so, regardless of circumstances, is a sign of moral weakness.

If you’ve bought into this so far, you can appreciate that the conservative tends to view government as harmful, if not pernicious. The government, especially as provider of public services, contributes to moral weakness by creating dependencies, which lead to sloth and irresponsibility.

Quite opposite, the liberal is the product of nurturant parent morality. The child will be raised in a loving, caring, compassionate environment. Mistakes or misbehaviors are not signs of moral failing but part of normal development. Husband and wife are co-equals. Both are responsible for the child’s well-being.

If the liberal is religious, his or her god will be fair, just, sympathetic, forgiving. There is an emphasis on the New Covenant as described in the bible, rather than the Ten Commandments. The Sermon on the Mount looms large. Disagreements should be resolved by negotiation rather than fisticuffs. Peace is preferable to war.

Government, in the liberal mind, should provide help to those who are victims of circumstances, often outside their control. Government is about safety nets and collective insurance and less about punishment of wrongdoing. Taxes are judged necessary to provide important services, and tax rates should be progressive, so that those who have more pay higher rates.

Lakoff, like Kahneman, opines that most of our thinking is unconscious. We react instinctively, and we’re easily influenced by metaphors, as Lakoff argues. Indeed, that’s how we think—metaphorically. Lakoff discusses certain terms and phrases in our vocabulary and their service as metaphors in evaluating our experiences. For example, we associate ‘white’ with ‘good’ and ‘pure’ and ‘black’ with ‘bad’ and ‘dirty.’ ‘Taller’ is better than ‘shorter.’ Thus when someone says that he “looks up to someone” he is doing two things: elevating the admired individual while subordinating himself.

Both Ladoff and Kahneman attach much importance to associations, which are as strong as they are pervasive. The broad metaphorical categories described above yield relevant associations that affect how we view the world around us. Since System 1 is always “on” during our waking moments, we have a strong propensity to make snap judgments about nearly everything. (As Malcolm Gladwell put it, we decide in a “blink.”)

What I find perverse about all this is that the mental associations and the priming effect actually determine what we see—even after our judgments have been falsified by objective proof. Worse, there is some research that suggests that our prejudices are reinforced by the counterfactual evidence.

Consider the topic of global warming. Those who deny the science and its conclusions will do so in even stronger tones when confronted by more scientific evidence confirming that global warming is real and largely human-caused. That is to say, we cannot appeal to the denier’s reason, since his System 1 mode blocks out his System 2. The prejudices intensify.

Now let’s return to behaviors that undermine one’s self-interest. In the economic realm, most people belong to the 99-percenters. They have virtually nothing in common with the One-percenters, whether it’s accumulated wealth or the company kept. Most Republicans belong to the larger group. Yet, the Republican Party, as evinced by policies emanating from the House, not only ignore the interests of the 99-percenters; they eviscerate them. The GOP prefers to coddle the very wealthy, and stick it to everyone else.

That attitude becomes clearer with each proposal issuing from the White House. Most recently, the Republican-dominated House is holding hostage Obama’s proposals: extended unemployment benefits, the payroll tax cut, and about $20 billion in funding for the new health care law. The New York Times:

House Republicans moved Friday toward a showdown with President Obama over the Keystone XL oil pipeline, which they included in their bill to extend tax relief for 160 million American workers through 2012.

Republicans said the pipeline would create jobs and had support from dozens of Democrats in Congress. And they welcomed the opportunity to split two Democratic constituencies: labor union members who stand to benefit from work on the pipeline and environmentalists, who oppose the project.

The House Republican bill, scheduled for a vote next week on the House floor, would deal with three urgent issues: extending a payroll tax cut scheduled to expire next month; continuing jobless benefits for the long-term unemployed; and averting a 27 percent cut in Medicare payments to doctors that would otherwise occur on Jan. 1.

The bill, unveiled Friday, would also block certain air pollution rules and eliminate more than $20 billion of spending planned under Mr. Obama’s new health care law.

The House Republicans, and all current GOP presidential candidates, favor policies that benefit extremely wealthy citizens at the expense of the Rest of Us, including most conservatives. So why do so many Republicans support policies adverse to their own interests?

It may have everything to do with Lakoff’s strict father morality, which is dominated by System 1 cognition. Financial success, the conservatives hold, deserves praise and honor rather than opprobrium. The rich should be worshiped rather than vilified. They have earned their money through discipline, self-reliance, and dint of hard work—or so the myth goes.

Thus, in the conservative mind, the Occupy Wall Street crowd are infidels; they would seek to subtract from the vast fortunes of the wealthy. By this System 1-thinking, extracting more taxes out of the rich violates the mores of the strict father metaphor. Likewise, using tax dollars to fund “entitlements” is equally pernicious to the conservative mind, since government benefits reward the weak and, therefore, the morally degenerate.

Irrational, to be sure, but nevertheless understandable within the above-described context. It would follow, then, that no attempt to demonstrate the folly of undermining one’s self-interests can succeed. More likely, such efforts only harden the resolve.

The liberal Rest of Us waste precious time and resources engaging the conservative in argument. There is a battle going on, and it’s time to forcibly press the nurturant parent. Here’s Lakoff:

Conservatives have, to a significant extent, come together on the question of their central values. Progressives [and liberals] must also unite, if they are to have a chance of holding their own against the conservative juggernaut. I think this issue to bring progressives together should be this most central of all issues—raising children to become responsible, empathetic adults.

Earn money then spend it

December 7, 2011 by davald

Most practicing economists would agree with this statement: the economy suffers from reduced aggregate demand. If we, all together, just bought more things, stores would hire, manufacturers would hire, the transportation sector would hire, then, eventually, the construction industry, severely hurt by the Great Recession, would hire.

One problem: we don’t have any money.

I don’t mean that in a universal sense, because over 80 percent of us who want to work are still holding down jobs, which means we’re still making money. Collectively, our incomes have fallen in real terms, though. On the other hand, the corporate heads and Wall Street bankers are doing fabulously well, of course.

It would seem that if recovery depends on us spending more money, then we’ll need some mechanism to acquire that money, normally through working at a job. So, jobs=paychecks=spending=economic recovery.

Now I want to introduce income inequality and its effects on the above. As you know, the gap between the rich and the Rest of Us has grown wider, obscenely so.

Economists like to talk about “marginal propensity to consume.” A fancy term, to be sure, but there is some sense behind it.

It is usually defined as the change in spending divided by the change in income. Simply put, the more we make the more we spend.

However, the value of the quotient varies inversely to wealth. That is, the richer we are, the less we’ll spend of additional income. If I already make a million dollars a year and I get another hundred, let’s say, I’ll spend less of the hundred than someone who earns only $10,000 a year who receives the same hundred.

If you’ve followed me thus far, you can appreciate that economic recovery depends on the Rest of Us getting our hands on more money. Giving more money to the rich, as the Republicans want to do, always, yields less spending and more savings than if the Rest of Us got the dough.

That means that Robin Hood was right. We need to get that money from the rich then distribute it to the Rest of Us.

Problem solved. All in favor, raise your hands.

Buy low, sell high

December 7, 2011 by davald

Any investor’s dream. Dean Baker tells us how such dreams come true for the One-percenters on Wall Street.

Perhaps the most important issue is the Fed’s claim that it did not lend at a below market rate to banks, thereby effectively giving them a subsidy. In fact, it is almost definitional that the rate did provide a subsidy.

No one forced the banks to borrow from the Fed. If they had better options, they would have borrowed elsewhere. Instead the Fed made large amounts of money available to banks at a time when liquidity carried an enormous premium. Simple, straightforward, and compelling account of systemic corruption at the highest level.

Government spending

December 7, 2011 by davald

We hear a lot these days about government spending, that it’s too high, and must be substantially reduced if the economy is to recover. Is it true that spending is up?

The Bureau of Economic Analysis publishes a lot of data, including breakdowns of gross domestic product (GDP) and its components. They’re called ‘NIPA tables,’ which stands for National Income and Product Account. Here’s the annual change in federal spending since the second half of 2007. It jumped by almost eight percent between the fourth quarter of 2007 and the 1st quarter of 2008, before falling by over four percent in the fourth quarter of 2008. It rose modestly for the most part until the first quarter of 2010. It’s been down and up since.

 

So what about state and local government spending. Has that risen or declined?

In only four quarters has state and local government spending risen from the previous year. Otherwise, the trend is down. We’ve certainly seen this in Washington state, where the governor and legislature are attempting to cut another $2 billion from the budget.

Washington legislatures have been hamstrung by a Tim Eyman initiative, which requires super majorities on both Olympia houses to raise taxes. Not so in New York state.

Yesterday, Governor Andrew Cuomo and Albany reached an agreement to raise taxes on the wealthy, the One-percenters. But as you read beyond the headlines you discover that the new tax replaces an expiring surcharge, which had generated $4 billion in annual revenues for the state. The new tax raises only $1.9 billion, so that state coffers will take a hit to the tune of $2.1 billion a year. Because we live in a political wonderland, everyone in Albany declared victory over the bipartisan measure.

A few posts ago I broke down Washington state revenues and expenditures. A plurality of revenues comes from the federal government, while about three-fourths of spending goes to public education and social services. Thus, there is a connection between the two Washingtons. If Congress cuts more, our state will likely receive fewer federal dollars.

Economist Robert Hall has written a paper (pdf) about fiscal stimulus, which is also the title. (This comes via Krugman.) He includes this chart, based on the NIPA data:

Many on the right argue that there is no such thing as a multiplier effect and that, therefore, stimulus doesn’t work. But Hall has studied the numbers. During normal economic activity there is a one-to-one relationship between federal spending and output. However, during recessionary periods like ours the multiplier is around two; for every dollar the government spends, two dollars in economic activity are generated.

In times of extreme recession, such as 2009, there is widespread agreement that the infra- structure multiplier is higher–perhaps twice its normal value.

However, the stimulus money is running dry as states continue to contract their budgets. This combination does not bode well for the economy. Add to this the fact that businesses are hoarding their dollars rather than investing, we can certainly appreciate the challenges the Rest of Us face; the One-percenters are doing even better than before, and they thank us each day with stepped up lobbying and campaign contributions to those who will preserve the status quo.

Hall concludes that without the stimulus dollars the economy would be in even worse shape. Since the Great Recession, GDP has fallen about 8.2 percent below the trend line. Without the stimulus, the number would have been 10.2 percent.

But it’s tough to argue counterfactuals, especially in the current political climate. The numbers are bleak, Obama is president. Therefore, it’s his fault. Obama would have a difficult time arguing to voters that things could have been worse. That’s hardly a winning strategy.

Better to just say, “Throw the bum out!” Yep, that’s the ticket.

Can you say “President Gingrich”? I just choked trying to.

We are all “emerging economies”

December 7, 2011 by davald

I watched most of the New York Times‘ live streaming of a conversation moderated by Tom Friedman. It featured economists Carmen Reinhart and Paul Krugman, in addition to journalist Joe Nocera, also of the Times.

Among the topics were Europe, debt, housing, and what to do. None of the guests offered much optimism.

I was struck by some of Reinhart’s comments, who, along with fellow economist Kenneth Rogoff, has studied recessions and recoveries, including causes for each. Here’s a characterization that particularly caught my attention. She said that the things happening now to the U.S. and most European nations were the stuff of “emerging economies.” Reinhart added, and I’m paraphrasing, “We’re all emerging economies now.” I inferred from this remark that we nations are saddled with debt, low growth rates, troubled currencies, and high unemployment—in the manner of emerging economies.

What we don’t have, and countries like Argentina experienced, is runaway inflation. Indeed, Paul Krugman believes that the “emerging economies” would benefit from higher inflation over an extended period of years. In the Eurozone, he said, Germany, especially should be inflating with respect to peripheral countries like Greece, Italy, and Portugal, since deflation in the periphery would be disastrous. He added that a few folks at Princeton, including himself, had warned that we were at risk of duplicating Japan’s experience. (He named his Princeton colleagues, then seemed to stumble with the last. He was making a facetious reference to Ben Bernanke, now running the Federal Reserve.) Krugman said that he owed Japan an apology, since the U.S. situation is much worse.

Joe Nocera was asked about the Occupy Wall Street movement. He replied that he had deliberately refrained from writing about it, because, as he warned the audience, his viewpoint would likely rankle. Nocera contrasted the Tea Party with OWS. The former, he said, worked to jettison more conventional Republicans from office and replace them with rabid right-wingers. As we’ve discovered, the current crop of GOP presidential candidates must at least rhetorically bow to the wishes of the Tea Party, or else. The OWS, on the other hand, took over a few parks and provided us with a few slogans. Unlike the Tea Party, the OWS, according to Nocera, is not about to change the system through the ballot box.

Krugman stepped in, suggesting that he had a more positive take on OWS. To wit, the topic of conversation has changed from a heavy emphasis on austerity and budget cutting to the gross inequalities of U.S. society. He cited Pres. Obama’s speech in Kansas earlier in the day wherein he spoke of sharp divide between the wealthy and middle class.

As to policy prescriptions to fix the mess, Krugman, of course, championed both monetary and fiscal stimulus, asserting that it wasn’t until the massive war-related spending that the Great Recession ended. Reinhart was less sympathetic with this recommendation, although I didn’t catch why. No one on the panel, however, believed that Congress would actually do something to stimulate an economic recovery.

Alas.

I should add that there was a bit of gallows humor from Krugman. He spoke of “President Gingrich” nominating Ron Paul to be his Secretary of Treasury. I think this caused Friedman to eek.

Overcoming the intuitive

December 2, 2011 by davald

We are mired in debt. That’s certainly the headline, and it’s true. We’re not alone; nearly all of Europe is over-leveraged. Debt, of course, is not the only problem. Despite some relatively good news today on the unemployment rate, millions of Americans have lost their jobs, their homes, their small savings, and are now wondering what to do, having exhausted their government benefits. Adding to the misery, the New York Times reported yesterday that only seven percent of the unemployed who eventually found work landed jobs that paid the same or better than their previous jobs.

What to do?

I don’t think that I’m venturing too far out on a limb to suggest a common expectation: that those in a position to effect change are observing the same universe. That doesn’t seem to be the case.

In the Eurozone the principal players, notably Germany’s Ms. Merkel, have demanded austerity from the peripheral states. Reduce government spending while paying down debt. England’s Mr. Cameron has imposed the same austere measures on Great Britain.

Here at home, Republicans have rallied round the same austerity flag. They want to reduce America’s debt through government cuts alone; they refuse to budge on their no-new-taxes pledge.

The proffered reason for this tighten-your-belt strategy is two-fold: (1) raising taxes, while never a good idea in the Republican mind, would be disastrous for this weak economy; and (2) spending cuts will keep inflation low and restore “confidence” for  Wall Street and CEOs. As a result of the latter, businesses will expand, hire more workers, and banks will start lending them money.

But suppose that this recipe would actually make matters worse, much worse. Suppose that the solution to our problems lies in part on taking on even more public debt.

That’s the argument submitted by a number of prominent economists, including Mr. Krugman, Mr. Stiglitz, Mr. DeLong, and Ms. Romer. In his column this morning Mr. Krugman writes:

How did things go so wrong? The answer you hear all the time is that the euro crisis was caused by fiscal irresponsibility. Turn on your TV and you’re very likely to find some pundit declaring that if America doesn’t slash spending we’ll end up like Greece. Greeeeeece!

But the truth is nearly the opposite. Although Europe’s leaders continue to insist that the problem is too much spending in debtor nations, the real problem is too little spending in Europe as a whole. And their efforts to fix matters by demanding ever harsher austerity have played a major role in making the situation worse.

With much evidence, the underlying assumption of Krugman et al. is that economies are suffering from too little demand, a point I’ve made on these pages. Since neither consumers (who are income-constrained) nor businesses (who won’t expand their enterprises if too few people will buy their output) can boost demand on their own, the government becomes the last resort, as J.M. Keynes argued.

How can the government accomplish this? In two major ways: it can print more money and it can spend more money, thus increasing public debt. These are anathema to the Republicans; and since they control the House, there’s virtually no chance that expansionary policies will be effected.

It does sound counterintuitive that the solution to our economic woes lies on the path of increasing debt. But that’s what the doctor has ordered.

However, this is judged a temporary fix. In the long run, we should reduce debt, not by cutting government budgets but by raising revenues via higher taxes. After all, we’ve had much, much higher tax rates in the past. Indeed, the US lived in prosperous times, with ever increasing GDP, when the marginal tax rate was around 90 percent. Today it’s at 35 percent.

Without a significant change in the ideological complexion of Congress, the prescription will remain unfilled.

A philosopher doing economics

November 30, 2011 by davald

I share one thing in common with Matthew Yglesias, now at Slate: we both studied philosophy in college, although he got his degree from Harvard and I received mine from California State University East Bay, formerly Hayward—I also earned a bachelor’s degree from Berkeley in history, which qualifies me for not all that much. At any rate, Yglesias has obviously acquired a lot of economic knowledge since leaving Cambridge, and he dares to proffer analysis and prognostication alongside those of Krugman, DeLong, Stieglitz—all heavyweights in their professions. I find his comments insightful and informative, products of deep thinking, as is the philosopher’s wont.

This post is no exception. He’s talking about the risk of Eurozone contagion inflicting significant harm on the U.S. economy.

But a different kind of analysis suggests that the United States could face catastrophe if the Eurozone tanks.

This terrifying possibility is suggested in a Nov. 7 lecture by Princeton professor Hyun Song Shi, “Global Banking Glut and Loan Risk Premium” (PDF). The starting point for his analysis is the fact—well-known to financial practitioners, unknown to the public, and perennially rediscovered by the economics profession—that a very large share of the world’s dollars are held in non-American accounts. Indeed, for several years in the late aughts the total dollar assets of non-American banks actually exceeded the total assets of the U.S. commercial banking system and even today the ratio is close to 1:1.

These foreign dollars—mostly held by European-headquartered global conglomerates—are not isolated from the American economy. Just as U.S. firms and households deposit money in American banks and take loans from the banks, European global banks intermediate between savers and spenders of dollars. A 2010 Bank of International Settlements survey (PDF) revealed that as of 2009, 161 foreign banks were operating 226 branches in the United States that raised more than $1 trillion in wholesale funding, largely through money markets. Dollars raised in the United States tend to ultimately work their way back to the United States (which, after all, is where you can use dollars to buy things) through the shadow banking system. European banks aren’t the only ones in this game, but they are the largest player. The upshot is that decisions made in Europe about how much leverage to take on play almost as big a role in determining American credit conditions as do decisions made in the United States [my emphasis].

The plausibility of this argument is underscored by what happened following the collapse of the U.S. housing bubble and the near meltdown of Wall Street banks. Our contagion spread across the pond, engulfing Europe big time. As the financial sector has come to dominate the U.S. economy, so, too, Europe and Asia. The dominoes are truly global. The precariousness of our current predicament is manifest almost daily in stock volatility; the least sign of negativity sends shares plummeting.

It works in reverse, as well. Today the Federal Reserve and foreign central banks announced a cooperative effort to east the debt crisis. As a result, the stock market “soared.” As I write the Dow is up 3.6%. Under current circumstances, that qualifies as “soaring.”

Yet, we’ve seen this movie before—again and again. What everyone hopes for—again and again—is for sustainability, preferably in a positive direction. Instead of a single-day stock movement, the financial sector and, by default, the Rest of Us want days turning to weeks becoming months of upward economic growth. That will require the Rest of Us to spend money that we don’t really have, since we’re trying to pay down our debt or boosting our savings.

This ain’t over. Tomorrow could yield a bout of bad news, sending stocks downward. As I said, the situation is precarious at best. But you didn’t need a philosopher to tell you that.

It’s “ludicrous”

November 29, 2011 by davald

This interview pays dividends to the reader. So, read it, if you’re a reader and you like dividends.

A few tantalizing excerpts:

There are clear-cut things that you do if you’re in a liquidity trap. A liquidity trap is simply defined as when the private sector is in a deleveraging mode, or a de-risking mode, or an increasing savings mode all of which you can also call deleveraging phenomena because of enduring negative animal spirits caused by legacy issues associated with bubbles. In that scenario, the animal spirits of the private sector are not going to be revived by a reduction in interest rates because there is no demand. It’s not the price of credit driving the deleveraging. It’s “I took on too much debt during the bubble. I have negative equity in my home. I don’t care what the price of credit is, I already have too much outstanding. I am paying down credit!” That can be entirely rational for an individual household. It can be rational for an individual firm. It can be rational for an individual country. However, in the aggregate, it begets the paradox of thrift. What is rational at the microlevel is irrational for the community, or at the macro level, and I’m amazed that this is not assumed as a given description of what we’ve got going on right now. The paradox of thrift and the liquidity trap are fellow travelers that are functionally intertwined.

I’ve touched on these topics here and here and here. Another excerpt from the McCulley interview:

You mean that cutting federal spending in a liquidity trap, like we’re in, is absolutely counterproductive?

Yes, it’s ludicrous and I don’t use that word too often.

And, finally, this excerpt:

There obviously are a lot of inconsistencies that we have to deal with in a democratic society. But what really puzzles me is how the concept of public investment is being perceived as an oxymoron. That’s just wrong. The notion that if we just would quit subsidizing idleness, that the unemployed would go to work, is another thing that is just ludicrous. I don’t know a lot of people who want to be subsidized in idleness. Nor do I know a lot of people who want to subsidize it. But there are just no jobs out there. There aren’t jobs because we had a bubble in housing. We went from 2 million housing starts to half a million housing starts. The notion that you could monetize equity in your home with a second mortgage is an oxymoron. Nonetheless, we had a housing sector bubble and everything that goes with it. Actually, if housing starts were our only problem, that wouldn’t be a big deal. But the house became the magic genie that made up for the fact that we’ve had stagnant real wages in our country for a long time and then the genie died.
