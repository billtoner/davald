Retirement planning

December 28, 2011 by davald

I suppose that I should have put scare quotes around ‘planning,’ since few of us have the acumen or the resources to do it properly. But we all get old and at some point too old to work, even if we need the income or actually like our jobs.

I’m approaching the magic age of official retirement; next year my wife and I turn 65. We’ll also celebrate our 44th wedding anniversary and, if the stars are in alignment, her retirement from public school teaching. My term of office has three years remaining, and, if my health doesn’t deteriorate, I’ll likely run for re-election.

So, how does one go about retiring? Nearly every other life phase is relatively simple; more often than not it’s encountered by accident. Retirement is different and vastly more cumbersome. Besides, the big question looms large: Will we have enough income to support ourselves at all, let alone in the style with which we’re accustomed?

During the past few weeks we’ve been studying online materials, acquiring the necessary forms, and consulting with school and union staffers—in my wife’s case. We’ve toted up the projected incomes from her pension, Social Security, my salary, and, later, my rather modest public pension. We think we’re going to be okay. In this we are fortunate.

The Pew Research Center has issued a report on the widening gap in net worth between 65-and-older households and those headed by individuals under the age of 35. It’s always been lopsided in favor of the elderly, about 10-1 in 1984. It’s gotten much worse of late. The ratio is now 46. What accounts for this disparity? It’s got almost everything to do with home ownership, despite the housing bubble collapse.

However, subtract the value of the home and at least half of the elderly are left with only their Social Security benefits. Pew Research:

If it had not been for home equity, the median net worth of older American households in 2009 would have been 33% lower than that of older households in 1984, instead of 42% higher.

We read much these days about the extravagant wealth and lifestyles of the rich and occasionally famous. But the Rest of Us exist in modest fashion. Take a look at this graphic from the Pew report.

Lumping everyone together yields a median net worth of $71,635. Those under 35, many of whom face years of college debt, have only $3,662—assets minus debts. Of course, most people in this age group do not own their homes, while nearly 80 percent of those 65 and older do, and their mortgages probably retired.

Economist Dean Baker:

In short, most of the country is looking at a situation where they are desperate for work or fearful about losing their job. Older workers are looking at a retirement where they are not far above the poverty level, even after spending a life working in middle class jobs.

Earlier in this post I remarked that my wife and I will likely be all right as we enter our “golden years.” I must say, though, that this wouldn’t be the case were it not for Social Security.

Unfortunately, conservatives and worse obsess over “reforming entitlements.” That’s thinly veiled code for reducing the benefits of Medicare and Social Security, despite their vital importance to most of the elderly. The Republicans shower contempt on anything public, including, of course, “government” in all its forms.

Against this right-wing tide Matthew Yglesias counters that Social Security is much too small. He argues that we should not have to “invest” in a myriad of private schemes or pay financial consultants through the nose. He writes:

But the best way to “save” for retirement is to live in a country with a properly functioning Social Security system. The way a system like this works is that a certain share of national income is set aside to support the living standards of elderly people. That share is collected as taxes from working people and paid out as benefits to retired people. The benefits received are proportional to the taxes you paid when working, so incentives to maximize earnings are preserved. This way everyone “saves” (i.e., reduces consumption below income) while working in exchange for a payoff that’s properly calibrated to overall long-term economic growth without needing to act as an “investor.”

Imagine that. Dispense with all the various pensions, public and private, eliminate individual retirement accounts and 401(k)s. Transfer all the money involved in these various instruments, including marketing, to one big Social Security system. No need to fear retirement, as the elderly would receive sufficient defined benefits to live comfortably.

The One-percenters can still “invest” all they want and live lavishly—or lose it all. They would contribute to Social Security as they do now, and the contributions would be capped, just as they are currently. I’d even let them take their SS benefits upon retirement just like the Rest of Us.

As for Medicare, imagine this.

No more private insurance companies. No more marketing. No more administration. No more caps. No more deductibles. No more co-pays. Instead, we’ll have a national health system in which every single person receives a card entitling them to proper health care, including prescription drugs. Need treatment? Show your card. Need a prescription filled? Show your card. Need long-term care? Show your card. The provider need not know if you’re rich or poor. Besides, the provider has the confidence that his or her bills will be paid.

Once again, if you’re rich and insist on lavish health care consistent with your lavish lifestyle, go ahead and buy supplemental coverage from your favorite broker. The Rest of Us won’t mind, since we’re covered.

Rising inequality [u]

December 27, 2011 by davald

New York Times columnist David Brooks tends to write in broad generalities (a penchant he shares with fellow columnist Thomas Friedman) coated with a thick layer of moralism. Today’s column is more of the same, which is titled “Midlife Crisis Economics.”

He correctly notes that the United States and other industrial democracies are much different societies than they were a half-century ago. Brooks cites rising inequality as one factor marking the difference.

Inequality is growing in nearly every developed country. According to a report from the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development, over the past 30 years, inequality in Sweden, Germany, Israel, Finland and New Zealand has grown as fast or faster than inequality in the United States, even though these countries have very different welfare systems.

As we see Canada, Finland, and the Netherlands have experienced only a moderate rise in inequality since the mid-1970s (data from OECD). It may help to consider more inequality statistics. The higher the index the greater the inequality.

The United States is the most unequal society in the group. Indeed, very few countries (e.g., Mexico and Brazil) have higher Gini indices. Even before the Great Divergence in the mid-70s, the U.S. was much less egalitarian than the Nordic countries and Canada.

So, what accounts for the growing gap between the Haves and the Have-nots? The OECD suggests (pdf) several plausible explanations:

  • globalization
  • technological innovation
  • policy choices, including regulations

OECD dismisses the first.

This report finds that neither rising trade integration nor financial openness had a significant impact on either wage inequality or employment trends within the OECD countries.

As for technology and capital flows, the OECD writes:

The study also shows, however, that increased financial flows and technological change had an impact on inequality. Growing outward FDI [foreign direct investment] was associated with increases in wage dispersion, albeit only in the upper half of the wage distribution, while technological progress contributed to the increase in overall wage dispersion, chiefly in the upper half of the distribution.

I am not surprised that the OECD concluded that education-derived skills explain most of the wage gap.

The growth in average educational attainment thus appears to have been the single most important factor contributing not only to reduced wage dispersion among workers but also to higher employment rates. On the basis of these results, the evolution of earnings inequality across OECD countries over the past few decades could be viewed mainly as the difference between the demand for and supply of skills or, as neatly summarised by Tinbergen (1975), the outcome of a “race between education and technology”.

It also occurred to me that there may have been a decline in tax revenues, which, in turn, may have reduced wealth transfers from the Haves to the Have-nots. Let’s look at tax rates (percentage of total worker compensation) of selected countries. They’ve declined for the countries selected, especially in Sweden.

The OECD publishes two sets of data on the Gini index of multiple countries. One pertains to inequality before taxes and government transfers; the other after taxes are collected and spent on public services benefiting the less wealthy. I’ve created a chart that shows the difference in the two as a percentage change and over time. The higher the percentage change the greater the wealth transfer.

There was not much change over time for Canada and the US. However, after rising from the mid-70s to the mid-90s, the Nordic countries’ saw a decrease in the difference between the before and after (taxes and transfers).

Yet, whatever the causes of income inequality the OECD authors conclude that governments must enact policies to reduce the gap between the Haves and the Have-nots.

Rising income inequality creates economic, social and political challenges. It can stifle upward social mobility, making it harder for talented and hard-working people to get the rewards they deserve. Intergenerational earnings mobility is low in countries with high inequality such as Italy, the United Kingdom, and the United States, and much higher in the Nordic countries, where income is distributed more evenly (OECD, 2008). The resulting inequality of opportunity will inevitably impact economic performance as a whole, even if the relationship is not straightforward. Inequality also raises political challenges because it breeds social resentment and generates political instability. It can also fuel populist, protectionist, and anti-globalisation sentiments. People will no longer support open trade and free markets if they feel that they are losing out while a small group of winners is getting richer and richer.

Those countries with high inequality, including the United States, experience low “intergenerational earnings mobility” and reduced economic performance. Moreover, public support for public institutions wanes most in unequal societies. We’ll note that David Brooks wrote: “According to a Gallup survey, 64 percent of Americans polled said they believed that big government is the biggest threat to the country.”

What should be done?

The OECD:

…policies that invest in the human capital of the workforce are key. Over the past two decades, the trend to higher educational attainment has been one of the most important elements in counteracting the underlying increase in earnings inequality in the long run. Policies that promote the up-skilling of the workforce are therefore key factors for reversing the trend towards further growth in inequality.

Human capital policies comprise two main strands. First, better job-related training and education for the low-skilled (on-the-job training) would help to boost their productivity potential and future earnings. This requires measures to ensure that training markets perform better, as well as ensuring sufficient incentives for both workers and firms to invest more in on-the-job training (OECD, 2006). To compensate for mobility (staff turnover), corporate tax policies that encourage employers to make additional investments in the human capital of their employees are warranted (e.g. deduction of training expenses as business costs).

The second strand is equal access to formal education over working life. Access to tertiary education is important for improving the prospects and living standards of lower-skilled people and giving individuals the opportunity to acquire the skills needed in the labour market. Educational or learning accounts can be a means to help achieve this objective (OECD, 2005), but tax incentives need to be designed in such a way that they do not disproportionally benefit higher-wage earners in high marginal tax rates.

Alas, a huge segment of our country, including almost all Republicans, are loath to adopt such policies. Indeed, the GOP in both houses is determined to increase inequality. As for accessing tertiary education (college), good luck with that. It’s expensive, with less and less government aid. Thus, the average college graduate faces over $25,000 in accumulated debt.

I wonder if I could have afforded a college education today. When I attended Berkeley in the 60s there was no tuition—just small “incidental fees.” When I entered as a freshman an academic semester cost me $60 in fees plus books and living expenses. When I graduated four years later the fees had risen to $100 a quarter. (Berkeley switched from the semester to the quarter system during my tenure.) Today it will cost $13,200 per year for tuition and fees (still a bargain, I suppose). The university estimates that total costs will exceed $30,000. That’s $120,000 for four years.

But if college is evidently so important to avoid poverty, why does a student have to become a pauper in the process? Shouldn’t the state invest in human capital, thereby facilitating an inexpensive tertiary education?

I have an idea. Take billions from the Pentagon and transfer the dollars to universities to make education free.

Pass it on.

A perspective on economic growth

December 22, 2011 by davald

As a follow up to my prior post on what will likely be only a temporary blip of economic good news, I take you to the words of economist Dean Baker, via brief background.

In the linked New York Times article of the previous post economists were quoted as saying that short-term economic growth could be 3.7 percent, but that more likely the GDP increase will fall to less than two percent by mid year. Now to Baker:

It is worth noting that even at a 3.7 percent annual growth rate it will take us until almost 2017 to get back to the economy’s potential GDP. According to the Congressional Budget Office, the economy is operating at about 6 percent below its potential level of output. With a potential annual growth rate of 2.5 percent, 3.7 percent growth GDP growth reduces this gap by 1.2 percentage points a year. That means it will take roughly five years of growth at this rate to close the gap.

The message: hone your survival skills; this will take a while. And if the Republicans attain majorities in both houses and secure the Oval Office, strive hard to become a One-percenter. Otherwise, you’ll be eating dog food while shivering in the dark.

We’ll save lives, but will it stand?

December 22, 2011 by davald

E.P.A. head Lisa Jackson announced yesterday that electric-generating plants will have to substantially reduce mercury emissions and other toxins. The Times:

The new rule, unless blocked by Congress or the courts, will be the first time the federal government has enforced limits on mercury, arsenic, acid gases and other poisonous and carcinogenic chemicals emitted by the burning of fossil fuels.

Mercury is bad stuff. It kills thousands of people a year, although you won’t find ‘mercury’ on the toe tag. That it took 20 years to finally get the regulation in place attests to the strength of financial interests vis-a-vis clear scientific evidence that mercury is not good for your health.

The biggest source of mercury, however, is coal combustion. About half of the electricity produced in the U.S. comes from coal-burning plants. Although the new regulation will not force closure of the plants it will require utilities and independent power producers to install scrubbers and other devices to prevent the mercury from entering the atmosphere. Here’s Wikipedia:

Mercury is a highly toxic element commonly found in coal and municipal waste. Wet scrubbers are only effective for removal of soluble mercury species, such as oxidized mercury, Hg2+. Mercury vapor in its elemental form, Hg0, is insoluble in the scrubber slurry and not removed. Therefore, additional process of Hg0conversion is required to complete mercury capture. Usually addition of the halogens to the flue gas are used for this purpose. The type of coal burned as well as the presence of a selective catalytic reduction unit both affect the ratio of elemental to oxidized mercury in the flue gas and thus the degree to which the mercury is removed.

Burning coal, a relatively cheap way to generate electricity, will now get more expensive, though not so much as to render the plants economically inviable. Nevertheless, there are more than 600 coal plants in the United States and both their owners and endusers wield bipartisan political influence. So, it remains to be seen whether or not E.P.A.’s new regulation will survive.

Of course, for the good of the planet and its numerous species all coal plants should be terminated. It’s going to get too damn hot if they’re not.

A small, temporary blip

December 22, 2011 by davald

There has been a bit of good news on the economic front. Housing starts are up, albeit mostly in the multi-family sector. Claims for unemployment benefits dropped. The stock market experienced a small rally.

Don’t get too cocky. The long-term prospects for economic recovery have not improved. The New York Times:

But next year, Washington is increasing some taxes and reducing spending as temporary measures enacted during the worst of the recession expire. That will damp growth by a percentage point or more next year, forecasters say. Provisions like a tax write-off to help businesses pay for equipment are winding down or ending.

Most worrying is the prospect that Congress will drop aid for the long-term jobless and allow payroll taxes to rise to 6.2 percent from the current level of 4.2 percent, amounting to a $1,000 tax increase on the average wage earner. Macroeconomic Advisers, a prominent forecaster, estimates that the expiration of the two provisions could cost the economy 400,000 jobs and cut growth by half a percentage point next year.

While it’s getting easier to blame the Republicans for the above, their deliberate dampening of the economy improves their chances for electoral victories, including a return to the White House. If there is no significant economic recovery in the weeks before next November’s general elections, the GOP stands to gain—even though the party is responsible for the continued misery.

More on Hungary

December 20, 2011 by davald

Paul Krugman’s colleague followed up on her guest post. She answers the question I could only guess at. Why did Hungarians go to the right?

Hungary was saddled with communist-era debt that was the largest per capita of any of the former Soviet satellite states. It has not been able to get its books in order since. The prior Socialist government, elected in 2002 and reelected in 2006, was mired in scandal and became hugely unpopular. But then economics made things worse.

Hungary had a housing bubble, fueled by mortgages that Hungarians took out in euros and Swiss francs. The world economic crisis, combined with economic mismanagement of the prior government, resulted in a sharp fall in the forint (Hungary’s national currency). As a result, Hungarians’ mortgage payments suddenly doubled and even tripled.

Fortunately, the U.S. has its own currency. So, despite even worse numbers, we Americans haven’t yet seen the need to install fascists.

The Brandeis ratio

December 19, 2011 by davald

Neither had I—heard of it. It’s featured in a New York Times op-ed this morning.

Quoting Supreme Court justice Louis Brandeis, the authors:

“We may have democracy, or we may have wealth concentrated in the hands of a few, but we cannot have both.”

The ratio, per the authors, is the average income of the One-percenters divided by the average middle-class income. Before the Great Divergence, roughly associated with Ronald Reagan’s terms, the ratio was 12.5. As of 2006, the last year of data, the ratio had skyrocketed to 36.

The authors, both university professors, Yale and Berkeley, propose a one-time law by Congress. (Already I’m squirming.) Each year the IRS would calculate the Brandeis ratio of the previous 12 months. If the number exceeded 36, the One-percenters would be charged a marginal tax on incomes in excess of the “poorest” in the fractile, that is, incomes above $330,000 a year.

There’s a Rawlsian* element in here, too. The law would allow for income increases of the middle class, and as long as the ratio remained at or below 36, the marginal tax would not kick in.

The authors:

Part of our goal is to change the way politicians speak about income equality. Framing the income of the wealthy in relation to the median income will help us all keep in mind the relative success of the middle class. Our grandparents would be shocked to learn that the average income of the 1-percent club has skyrocketed to more than 30 times the median income — just as we will be shocked if 20 years from now 1-percenters make 80 times the median, which is where we will be if inequality continues to grow at the current rate unabated.

 

As much as I might applaud this idea, I’m sure that you would agree with me that our current caustic politics precludes any meaningful considerations of our tax structure. The Republican-controlled House prefers that the Great Divergence become even greater. The GOP in both houses wants to lower taxes on the very wealthy, arguing—against reason and facts—that putting more money in the hands of the extremely privileged will create jobs.

In a previous post I drew attention to the conclusion of the International Monetary Fund, via New York Times columnist Charles Blow, that countries marked by significant income inequality were less able to grow their economies. If so, then further enriching the One-percenters will impede, not promote, economic growth.

Why do we tolerate such disparity?

December 17, 2011 by davald

A recent U.S. Census report reveals that half of all Americans are either poor or near poor, according to official income thresholds. Think about that statistic for a moment. On average one out of every two people you encounter makes less than 200 per cent of the poverty level, which is about $45,000 for a family of four.

In his column for the New York Times this morning Charles M. Blow writes:

Our growing income inequality is a fact. So is the possibility that it could prove economically disastrous.

An April report from the International Monetary Fund found that growing income inequality has a negative effect on economic expansion. The report said that long periods of high growth, which were called “growth spells,” were “much more likely to end in countries with less equal income distributions. The effect is large.” It continued: “Inequality seemed to make a big difference almost no matter what other variables were in the model or exactly how we defined a ‘growth spell.’ ”

Our income inequality could jeopardize our recovery.

Yet another Gallup report issued Friday found that most Americans now say that the fact that some people in the U.S. are rich and others are poor does not represent a problem but is an acceptable part of our economic system.

If denial is a river, it runs through doomed societies.

Perhaps we were always an ignorant nation. But we seem to be willfully so over the last few decades, probably corresponding with the Great Divergence. Here are some more startling statistics, these from CBS News:

The majority of low-income families — 62 percent — spent more than one-third of their earnings on housing, surpassing a common guideline for what is considered affordable. By some census surveys, child-care costs consume close to another one-fifth.

Paychecks for low-income families are shrinking. The inflation-adjusted average earnings for the bottom 20 percent of families have fallen from $16,788 in 1979 to just under $15,000, and earnings for the next 20 percent have remained flat at $37,000. In contrast, higher-income brackets had significant wage growth since 1979, with earnings for the top 5 percent of families climbing 64 percent to more than $313,000. [By the way, Mr. Blow evidently has his facts wrong on what happened to the bottom 20 percent since 1979. He wrote: “But it rose just 18 percent for the bottom 20 percent.” CBS says that inflation-adjusted incomes in this quintile fell by nearly 11 percent over the period.]

About that Gallup poll.

More than half of the respondents, which surely includes both the poor and near-poor, said that it was either not important or only somewhat important to reduce inequality. WTF? And get this: As Mr. Blow writes, realizing the most popular strategy—growing the economy—can’t occur under conditions of severe inequality.

I’d go further. Expanding GDP requires income gap reduction. The Rest of Us spend our money on goods and services. If we have higher real incomes more money circulates in the economy to hire and produce.

Have we simply become inured to the growing gap along with the misery suffered by nearly half the population? Have so many people accepted as inevitable the workings of our capitalist system, as Mr. Blow opines?

This dismissal of financial duress would explain the ho-hum treatment given the Occupy crowds. We’ve got a lot of poverty. So what?

Take a look at the following chart (from Wikipedia), which shows selected countries’ Gini index over time.

The U.S. is neck and neck with China for third place behind Brazil and Mexico. If trends continue, China and the U.S. will overtake Mexico for second place. Come on, Americans. You can make it to the top. If you do, you’ll be recognized as the most unequal country on the planet.

You’ll notice, by the way, that just after the war, the United States was a more equal society, with a Gini index below 40. Since the 1970s, however, the Great Divergence began with an ever widening gap between the rich and the poor.

A tale of two countries

December 16, 2011 by davald

Ireland, once the Celtic Tiger, is in the midst of economic contraction, occasioned by its government’s austerity measures. At least that’s the view of some, including Paul Krugman. In response to my previous post on the subject, a reader offered these words and a link to a website:

I am interested in economics and read your post. I do not think this is the whole story. Perhaps this can help in your analysis of Ireland’s economy. http://mises.org/daily/3405 Also, ever studied the depression of 1920-21? It is a good case study. Thanks for the post. Pursue truth no matter where it takes you.

Okay, I’m flattered that anyone reads my blog, and the comment forced me to look at the link and to review OECD data. (I’ll investigate the 1920-21 “depression” later.) The link is to the Ludwig von Mises Institute, which is dedicated to “advancing the scholarship of liberty in the tradition of the Austrian School.” The Austrian School seems to have its origins in the works of Frederick Hayek, an Austrian economist who won the Nobel Prize in 1974. Hayek wrote The Road to Serfdom, in which he warned that tyranny (both fascism and communism) was the inevitable product of central government planning. Hayek was obviously no Keynesian.

The link to the Mises site delivers this sweeping polemic:

The answer to Ireland’s economic woes is not further government intervention in the economy, but a return to economic liberalism, small government, and sound monetary policy. Ireland needs to look closely at Austrian economic theory and take a step back from the dissolving, failed Keynesian model. Ireland needs to return to a firm base: it must eliminate, or at the very least curtail, its leviathan public sector; it must bring inflation under control through responsible monetary and fiscal policy; and it must cut down government economic controls in order to bring lasting stability to its economy. The only sound plan can be to cut public spending, eliminate social-welfare programs, and cease propping up failing sectors of the market.

My immediate reaction to these words was to seek a comparison with a country with high taxes and extensive social-welfare programs. Since I’ve been writing a bit about Finland’s education transformation, why not Finland, which is essentially a social democracy?

I then consulted the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD). It has data galore on most, if not all, countries. So, let’s get comparing.

We see that the Irish have fared better than the Finns; however, Finland is gaining ground. (Numbers are in current US dollars, purchasing power parity.)

As a social democracy we should not be surprised that Finland’s citizens pay high taxes. I’d expect them to be higher than Ireland’s. They are:

Indeed, the average Irish worker pays lower taxes on his income than the Finnish worker. What about government revenues and expenditures?

Interesting. The Irish government was running a modest surplus before the Great Recession; Finland also had a surplus, a bit larger than Ireland’s. Finland’s government spent more on public services than Ireland’s until the recession. Last year Ireland’s government spent almost 70 percent of GDP, although GDP had declined—about 10% from its peak in 2007 to 2010. (A smaller denominator with stable expenditure levels, the numerator, would increase the ratio.)

Finally, let’s take a look at unemployment. Which economy is doing better by its workers?

As for labor compensation, Finland’s workers are doing much better of late.

What might these numbers suggest? In particular, if Ireland’s problems are the result of too much government, too high a tax rate, and too much debt, how should we explain Finland’s superior position, since Finland has higher taxes and its government spends more money on public services—at least prior to the Great Recession. In 2007, the last year of data collected by the OECD, total social expenditures were about 25 percent of GDP, while Ireland’s were only 16 percent. Does the latter earn the description “leviathan public sector”? If the size of the public sector is Ireland’s problem why isn’t it Finland’s?

A wee bit of the Irish

December 16, 2011 by davald

Until the Great Recession, Ireland was an economic jewel, lovingly called “the Celtic Tiger.” From Wikipedia:

From 1995 to 2000 GDP rate growth ranged between 7.8 and 11.5%. The rate then slowed to between 4.4 to 6.5% from 2001 to 2007. During that period the Irish GDP rose dramatically to equal then eventually surpass that of all but one state in Western Europe.

The Irish are well educated and labor is “flexible,” a euphemism for low wages. Thus, many technology firms, including Apple, established facilities there. Construction boomed in the early aughts, real estate prices soared, rainbows ubiquitous.

In 2008 the economy tanked, as it did across the western industrialized world (with few exceptions). The Irish government responded with sharp budget cuts, unemployment rose, bankruptcies increased. Not a few observers claimed that Ireland’s responses warranted praise. Austerity was precisely the correct approach. Some went so far as to claim that such frugality would lead to economic growth and greater employment.

Via Paul Krugman this morning we are treated with a blog site called “The Irish Economy.” Contrary to the more optimistic judgments, it seems that Ireland is not doing all that well. From the blog:

I especially liked a commenter’s take:

I’m shocked. Contractionary economic policy is contractionary.

Any chance we can increase gov’t spending now and start hiring loads of people and building the infrastructure we need? Or must the beatings of the poor and middle class continue until they finally get pissed off enough to start lopping off heads?
