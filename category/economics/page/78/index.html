Debunking classical economics

December 15, 2011 by davald

Daniel Kahneman, a professor of psychology, won a recent Nobel Prize—in economics! How’s that? Based on his years of research, in close collaboration with other psychologists, Kahneman has successfully demonstrated that we really don’t think the way we, well, think we do. Indeed, we are so heavily influenced by the most trivial of external factors that our very judgments and behaviors are compromised. We might say that our normal mental states are nothing but a constellation of illusions.

The physicist Freeman Dyson reviews Kahneman’s latest book, Thinking, Fast and Slow. He writes:

A typical example of a Kahneman experiment is the coffee mug experiment, designed to measure a form of bias that he calls the “endowment effect.” The endowment effect is our tendency to value an object more highly when we own it than when someone else owns it. Coffee mugs are intended to be useful as well as elegant, so that people who own them become personally attached to them. A simple version of the experiment has two groups of people, sellers and buyers, picked at random from a population of students. Each seller is given a mug and invited to sell it to a buyer. The buyers are given nothing and are invited to use their own money to buy a mug from a seller. The average prices offered in a typical experiment were: sellers $7.12, buyers $2.87. Because the price gap was so large, few mugs were actually sold.

The experiment convincingly demolished the central dogma of classical economics. The central dogma says that in a free market, buyers and sellers will agree on a price that both sides regard as fair. The dogma is true for professional traders trading stocks in a stock market. It is untrue for nonprofessional buyers and sellers because of the endowment effect. Trading that should be profitable to both sides does not occur, because most people do not think like traders.

The Republicans are all about financial responsibility

December 15, 2011 by davald

No? Well, you’re right. They’re not. Certainly the record of Republican administrations belies the conservatives’ claim to fiscal austerity.

Writing for the Huffington Post Geoffrey Stone does us the favor of comparing the last several presidencies. The biggest budget buster of them all is the sainted Ronald Reagan, who swallowed the Laffer Curve, hook, line, and sinker. Reagan believed that sharply reducing taxes would spur economic activity thereby boosting tax revenues. It didn’t happen. Stone:

The tax reform plan that was designed to stimulate business and produce a substantial budget surplus instead drove the United States deeper into debt and triggered a recession. By mid-1982 unemployment stood at 9.7%. Businesses failed right and left, and Reagan’s approval rating plunged to 35%. Instead of reducing the federal deficit, Reagan managed to do the impossible: he tripled the cumulative national debt of the United States in only eight years. Reaganomics did not work.

The above graphic is included in Stone’s article. It plainly shows that both Carter and especially Clinton were exemplars of frugality compared with their GOP counterparts. If we were to count the post-administration effects of Bush II’s tax cuts, the last column would be much higher. We might recall that Bush II converted an inherited $7 trillion budget surplus into a $7 trillion dollar deficit, which greatly exacerbated our present economic misery.

Worried about Social Security?

December 15, 2011 by davald

Before my very mirror I have become an old fart. That means that within a few months I’m eligible for Medicare, which I won’t take just yet, since I’m covered at work. My wife and I expect to begin taking Social Security benefits the following year, when we both turn 66.

Since I was self-employed for over a decade, I’ve paid a bucketload of dollars to SS; I had to contribute as both “employer” and “employee.” So, I’m not too shy about taking this money. Nor is my wife, who worked her tail off for 25 years teaching Marysville children.

Yet, as we reach these “golden years,” we have become more sensitive to the chatter about the program’s viability. In particular, will there be any money in the federal till when we decide to take our share?

The answer, from an actuarial perspective, is a decided yes. Social Security is healthy, and even without a few minor tweaks, will not exhaust its fund for decades, by which time the Mr. and Mrs. will be occupying urns.

The New York Times has included a valuable chart with its article today on threats to SS. If you take a look you’ll note that revenues exceed expenditures, and they have done so since the mid-80s.

But, as always, Republicans have never cared for “entitlements,” even those paid for by workers. They’d love to let their benefactors siphon off public dollars for private gain. Why, they want to privatize Social Security, and we know how that’s working in health care, where we pay more and get less than our European counterparts.

As you know Obama would like to extend the payroll tax cut, which has added another $800 a year to my wallet. Those tax revenues go into the Social Security fund. The Republicans see this decision point as yet another opportunity for brinkmanship, to force concessions from Democrats on even unrelated matters. The Times:

Citing the Bush-era tax cuts that were supposed to end in 2010, they [a group of liberal senators and representatives] expressed fear that the payroll tax cut would become permanent as politicians shied from letting them lapse. Mindful of some Republicans’ goal of privatizing Social Security, the Democrats suggested that tinkering with the payroll tax “may be used as the first step in a larger battle to fundamentally dismantle Social Security.”

Robert Reischauer, is one of two public trustees for Social Security. He told the Times:

“The nightmare that I have is that when it comes time to raise the tax back up to 6.2 percent, conservatives are going to propose that these two percentage points of payroll tax be devoted to individual accounts.” Mr. Reischauer said. “That will precipitate a huge fight and could change Social Security in a fundamental way.”

The “individual accounts” is a feature of privatization. Should the Republicans succeed in their effort, we might as well say goodbye to the program and hello to more wealth extraction by the One-percenters.

Oh how I long for an Eisenhower Republican these days. The current GOP is a haven for loonies, who have managed to buffalo the voters and gain control of the levers of government. Historian Thomas Frank calls them “the wrecking crew.” Should they achieve a majority of both houses and the White House, very soon we won’t recognize this country. We’ll certainly prefer to be elsewhere.

Meanwhile, back to the Great Depression

December 15, 2011 by davald

As I’ve written several times here, there’s still significant debate over the cause and resolution of the Great Depression. While there seems to be wide agreement among economists who have studied this period that the massive military spending during WWII finally brought economic recovery, consensus is illusive on what occurred before Japan bombed Pearl Harbor.

I’m going to skip a discussion of possible causes of the Great Depression to focus on government responses once Roosevelt assumed office. In particular I want to briefly sketch the role of monetary policy.

Christine Romer, economics professor at Berkeley and former head of Obama’s Council of Economic Advisors, is widely touted as an expert on the Great Depression. She concluded (pdf) that monetary expansion, rather than Roosevelt’s New Deal stimuli, grew the economy in FDR’s first term.

Between 1933 and 1937 real GNP in the United States grew at an average rate of over 8 percent per year; between 1938 and 1941 it grew over 10 percent per year. These rates of growth are spectacular, even for an economy pulling out of a severe depression…

I argue that the rapid rates of growth of real output in the mid- and late 1930s were largely due to conventional aggregate- demand stimulus, primarily in the form of monetary expansion [my emphasis].

Not so, argues Joseph Stiglitz in a Vanity Fair essay:

But it was not until government spending soared in preparation for global war that America started to emerge from the Depression. It is important to grasp this simple truth: it was government spending—a Keynesian stimulus, not any correction of monetary policy or any revival of the banking system—that brought about recovery.

Looking at this next chart suggests that Romer was correct about rising GDP (data from Bureau of Economic Analysis, NIPA table 1.1.5).

It’s interesting to note that the economy contracted between 1936 and 1938. Roosevelt was being warned about rising debt, so he pulled back on his public spending and raised taxes. To his credit, he saw this as a mistake and resumed his previous strategies.

Romer analyzed the effect of New Deal fiscal spending. She found that it had little to no impact on growth. Rather it was a dramatic increase in money supply that did the trick.

What about today? We’ve had a tripling of the money supply since the start of the Great Recession, yet the economy remains flat. If monetary expansion worked in the 30s why isn’t it working today?

Here is where Paul Krugman et al. enter the picture. They diagnosed a liquidity trap, which, by definition, means that increasing the money supply will have no measurable effect on the economy. Krugman:

Early on in this crisis I and quite a few other economists — but not enough! — declared that we had entered a classic liquidity trap. This is a situation in which even a zero short-term interest rate isn’t low enough to restore full employment; it is, if you think through the logic, a situation in which desired saving, or more accurately the savings people would make if we were at full employment, exceed desired investment even at a zero interest rate.

So, unless there is a spontaneous recovery, highly dubious, the only remedy available is massive, and I mean massive, federal spending, as Stiglitz and Krugman advocate. Since Congress would never go there, we should prepare ourselves for a very long period of economic stagnation.

I do hope that I am wrong.

A confounding letter

December 14, 2011 by davald

A few posts ago I wrote about “irrational choice“—people voting for candidates and issues antithetical to their interests. I cited the work of George Lakoff, whose book Moral Politics attempted to make sense out of irrational choice. He suggested that non-rich conservatives vote against their economic interests because of a particular moral paradigm, one Lakoff called ‘strict father morality.’

Under this paradigm rich people are deemed to have gotten so through the diligent application of their abilities. Thus, they deserve every penny of their substantial portfolios.

Well, we seem to have a “strict father” in Marysville, who took the time to pen a rather nasty letter to the Herald in defense of the One-percenters. He writes:

Fifty years ago this country had the largest, most productive middle class in the world; until politicians started thieving money from the assiduous to give away to the motivationally deficient for their votes. When you rob Peter to pay Paul, you can always count on Paul’s support!

The writer has not been paying attention. He clearly hasn’t read my blog. If he had, he’d know that he’s got the cause and effect exactly backwards.

Fifty years ago the middle class was thriving, experiencing rising prosperity and job security. The marginal tax rate was as much as 90 percent. Income disparity was much lower. Unions were still a counterforce to Capital. These relative halcyon days disappeared into the Great Divergence, when marginal tax rates plummeted and blue-collar jobs evaporated. Meanwhile, the financial sector (presumably “the assiduous”) grew exponentially, along with the incomes of the One-percenters.

The decline in federal revenues occasioned by reduced tax rates on the wealthy delivered mounting debt. Then along came the housing bubble, a product of ever more exotic financial instruments designed to extract wealth from the Rest of Us. It could not last, of course. The bubble burst, plunging us into the Great Recession.

The chart below shows accumulated debt since 1950.

The “assiduous” were quickly bailed out with taxes paid by the “motivationally deficient,” another term, I guess, for the Rest of Us. Wall Street has rebounded handsomely while the unemployment rate remains stubbornly high, people lose their homes, and real incomes decline.

The rich have so much money that they don’t know what to do with it, other than hoard it. They won’t invest their stockpiles of cash on productive activities because aggregate demand has fallen. Why expand production if too few people can buy the output?

I have an idea. Increase the taxes on “the assiduous” to bring in more revenue for the federal government. In turn, since the private sector isn’t spending, the government can invest the money in tangible assets, putting people back to work.

Contrary to John Boehner’s claim, the rich aren’t putting people to work. If lower taxes on the wealthy were the answer, we’d be swimming in jobs.

By the way, I seriously doubt that the letter-writer is a One-percenter. If he were, he’d certainly not be living in Marysville.

I like it

December 14, 2011 by davald

The Everett Herald‘s editors have broached an interesting alternative for the soon-to-be-shuttered Kimberly-Clark plant. They suggest converting it to produce hemp-based paper products. The hemp could be grown in Washington state.

Why do we cling to the notion that paper products must come from trees? At least two sustainable alternatives exist: Hemp and bamboo.

Industrial hemp, the blue-collar cousin to marijuana that has no psychoactive properties, has been grown for at least 12,000 years for fiber (textiles and paper) and food. (Thomas Jefferson drafted the Declaration of Independence on hemp paper, according to the North American Industrial Hemp Council.) It was grown commercially in the U.S. until the 1950s.

If it’s good enough for Tommy Jefferson, it’s good enough for the Rest of Us. Better to keep the plant running then to shut it down and cast several hundred workers to the street.

Tell a good story

December 12, 2011 by davald

Judy Lightfoot tells us about Elizabeth Warren, who is running against incumbent Scott Brown to represent Massachusetts in the Senate. Warren is offering a simple, truth-filled narrative about the Great Divergence. Lightfoot:

Warren told the story, which is becoming familiar around the country, in Seattle Friday night before a crowd of people with $40 tickets to a joint fundraiser with Sen. Maria Cantwell at the Paramount Theatre. The story begins in the Great Depression, Warren said, “a dark point in America’s history,” a colossal bust in the boom-and-bust cycle that had caused panics, bank crashes, and the destruction of ordinary American lives and livelihoods every 15 years or so since the 1790s.

But then “America said we can do better,” Warren said. We instituted FDIC insurance, banking rules, and the SEC to enforce those rules. We also “invested in ourselves and built America’s middle class” by putting money into education K-through-grad school (“for every dollar we put in, we got five dollars back in taxes”) plus into infrastructure like highways, transit, a power grid, and “research, research, research.” Thus “our country got richer, and families got richer right along with us. It worked for more than half a century.”

Sadly, the story goes, about 30 years ago we lost our way. A deregulated financial industry and unfair taxation have “staggered America’s middle class,” said Warren. “G.E., a profitable company with worldwide operations, pays zero taxes.” At the same time, we starve the infrastructure that our millionaires depended on for growing their enterprises, “and we tell young people ‘You’re going to have to take on debt to get an education.’ ”

Pass it on.

Not my father’s economy

December 12, 2011 by davald

I was born in 1947. I’ve been on the planet long enough to experience what now must be considered America’s glory days, years filled with rising prosperity, the realization of the American Dream, and financial wellbeing, as most workers were employed by large corporations promising job security and adequate pensions. This period began after WWII and lasted until the mid- to late-70s. In the 80s, conditions for the Rest of Us changed dramatically, and not for the better.

Consider this graph (I found savings data back to 1959, so that’s where the series begin.):

Not only were households spending more money they were also saving more. (Savings rate is percentage of disposable income.) About the time Ronald Reagan assumed office the trends began a downward trajectory. If the Rest of Us started putting fewer dollars in the bank and also reducing our spending, that can mean only one thing: our incomes were falling. Well, not for everyone (data from Emmanuel Saez).

 

Paralleling the Great Divergence was a major transformation of the economy. While we used to making things, increasingly the money began flowing out of manufacturing and into the financial sector, which really doesn’t make anything.

America’s middle class was strongly associated with blue-collar employment, which required skills but not necessarily college. A young man could land a job with General Motors, for example, and expect to stay there until retirement. He’d receive a continuously rising wage, medical and dental benefits, then a defined-benefit retirement sufficient to support a modest lifestyle for his remaining years. Moreover, he made enough money to buy a house, a car, appliances, and raise a family. His wife didn’t have to work. (I have essentially described my family’s existence, although my father worked at Standard Oil rather than General Motors.)

Granted, this Norman-Rockwell-America was not without its deep fissures. Women often felt unfulfilled if not also oppressed, their talents underutilized in the home; people of color were largely excluded; and suburban life was gradually eroding the environment. But for the predominantly white population things were mostly good and always looking better.

I used to think that the assassination of JFK signaled America’s loss of innocence, shattering Rockwell’s mythology. In retrospect, change was already afoot before that fateful Dallas afternoon, as these charts suggest.

The Sixties were an inchoate reaction to something going terribly wrong in our society. We were fighting a nasty, deadly war in the jungles of Southeast Asia, still engaging the communists around the globe, watching the murders of MLK and RJK on television, and witnessing the emergence of Black political consciousness—what had been suppressed for generations finally erupted in marches and massive demonstrations.

Meanwhile, the rich were sucking more and more wealth out of the economy as they purchased a compliant Congress to eliminate regulations and put the screws to the Rest of Us. We weren’t paying all that much attention.

The Great Divergence cannot long endure. Too much concentration of wealth combined with unchecked greed threatens everyone, including the One-percenters. Prof. Krugman opines that we’re in a depression, with the entire global financial sector behaving like a house of cards.

There must be an alternative, maybe an economy “as if people mattered.” Our current one is not working for the Rest of Us.

Poor students test poorly

December 12, 2011 by davald

Stop the presses on that one. It should be the right headline about education and its reform. But we never see it. Why is that? I’d point the finger at the usual suspects.

First of all there appears to be widespread denial that the U.S. is home to deep poverty. In a nation as rich as ours, how could there be so many poor? Since we’re such a great and wonderful country the solution to the conundrum is to deny the facts, a rampant American tradition. We see this attitude aplenty in the political debate over global warming. The fix is to deny.

Second, armed with willful ignorance, those who detest government and all things public fight efforts to reduce poverty. Meanwhile they blame the poor for being so.

Third, far better to condemn teachers and their unions for declining test scores and low graduation rates than to commit the necessary resources to eradicating poverty, the most significant factor in cognitive development and academic performance. Here it helps to emphasize studies that cite the correlation between teacher quality and student learning, while ignoring the vast body of evidence on the role of poverty.

In a New York Times op-ed this morning, Duke professor Helen Ladd and former Times education correspondent Edward Fiske write:

 NO one seriously disputes the fact that students from disadvantaged households perform less well in school, on average, than their peers from more advantaged backgrounds. But rather than confront this fact of life head-on, our policy makers mistakenly continue to reason that, since they cannot change the backgrounds of students, they should focus on things they can control.

Instead, we’re inundated with a new crop of buzz words: accountability, privatization, race to the top, no excuses, or G.W. Bush’s phrase, “The soft bigotry of low expectations.” Better to squeeze the education establishment in the hope of yielding an improved product. The authors:

Data from the National Assessment of Educational Progress show that more than 40 percent of the variation in average reading scores and 46 percent of the variation in average math scores across states is associated with variation in child poverty rates.

International research tells the same story. Results of the 2009 reading tests conducted by the Program for International Student Assessment show that, among 15-year-olds in the United States and the 13 countries whose students outperformed ours, students with lower economic and social status had far lower test scores than their more advantaged counterparts within every country. Can anyone credibly believe that the mediocre overall performance of American students on international tests is unrelated to the fact that one-fifth of American children live in poverty?

Finland, whose students outperform their counterparts from other countries on international tests, adopted a sharply different approach to education. The political leaders and policy experts acknowledged the importance of socioeconomic factors and pedagogy.

Samuel Abrams, writing for the New Republic, tells us:

Finland’s schools weren’t always so successful. In the 1960s, they were middling at best. In 1971, a government commission concluded that, poor as the nation was in natural resources, it had to modernize its economy and could only do so by first improving its schools. To that end, the government agreed to reduce class size, boost teacher pay, and require that, by 1979, all teachers complete a rigorous master’s program.

Today, teaching is such a desirable profession that only one in ten applicants to the country’s eight master’s programs in education is accepted. In the United States, on the other hand, college graduates may become teachers without earning a master’s. What’s more, Finnish teachers earn very competitive salaries: High school teachers with 15 years of experience make 102 percent of what their fellow university graduates do. In the United States, by contrast, they earn just 65 percent.

Instead of greenhouse-gas rhetoric, Finland’s political leaders actually did something to realize the importance of education, to which we can only genuflect. For example, as the Times‘ article reports, “schools provide food and free health care for students. Developmental needs are addressed early. Counseling services are abundant.”

Nor have Finnish schools abandoned subjects beyond the “core” areas of math and literature, the obsessive focus of U.S. schools. (Oh, and we do like our sports programs, and woe to any administrator who dares to drop them to concentrate on the academic curriculum. Cut anything but football and basketball, scream the parents.) The New Republic:

And students in grades one through nine spend from four to eleven periods each week taking classes in art, music, cooking, carpentry, metalwork, and textiles. These classes provide natural venues for learning math and science, nurture critical cooperative skills, and implicitly cultivate respect for people who make their living working with their hands.

Work with your hands? How pedestrian, Americans retort. We here respect only the rich, who make their fortunes through inheritance or Wall Street.

The U.S. is in the midst of test-mania, since we need to hold teachers and principals “accountable.” No wonder the focus is on the core subjects. Notwithstanding denials to the contrary, schools are all about “teaching to the test.” And why not? If you’re going to predicate my career on my pupils’ test scores, then damn right I’ll concentrate on preparing them for the all important test.

Wait, you say that Finnish schools don’t have their version of No Child Left Behind and Washington state’s Measurement of Student Performance (MSP)? TNR:

But perhaps most striking on the list of what makes Finland’s school system unique is that the country has deliberately rejected the prevailing standardization movement. While nations around the world introduced heavy standardized testing regimes in the 1990s, the Finnish National Board of Education concluded that such tests would consume too much instructional time, cost too much to construct, proctor, and grade, and generate undue stress. The Finnish answer to standardized tests has been to give exams to small but statistically significant samples of students and to trust teachers—so much so that the National Board of Education closed its inspectorate in 1991. Teachers in Finland design their own courses, using a national curriculum as a guide, not a blueprint, and spend about 80 percent as much time leading classes as their U.S. counterparts do, so that they have sufficient opportunity to plan lessons and collaborate with colleagues. The only point at which all Finnish students take standardized exams is as high school seniors if they wish to go to university.

Tests “consume too much instructional time”?  Let teachers “design their own courses”?

Back to the issue of poverty, from which we can draw huge disparities between Finland and the United States. The CIA publishes its World Factbook, and includes the Gini index of each nation. The Gini index, or coefficient, measures the distribution of income. A perfectly equal distribution would be 0; if all the money went to the top, the score would be 100. Finland’s Gini index (2009) was 26.9. The US was 45 (2007). As to poverty rates, Finland’s is less than four percent (pdf); the US is over 15 percent.

Yet, Republicans, especially, reject any international comparisons. It’s as if the U.S. were the only country on the planet. We certainly couldn’t learn anything from Finland, or Sweden, or Norway, and so on. After all, they’re mostly “socialists,” and we don’t cotton to them animals.

We’d prefer to do things our way, thank you very much. And our way is to pretty much screw the Rest of Us so that the rich can be even more so. Who woulda thunk it that God bestows his blessings exclusively on Wall Street and corporate board rooms?

We’re in a depression

December 12, 2011 by davald

That’s the conclusion of Nobel laureate Paul Krugman in his post this morning. Europe is in even worse shape.

First of all, the crisis of the euro is killing the European dream. The shared currency, which was supposed to bind nations together, has instead created an atmosphere of bitter acrimony.

Specifically, demands for ever-harsher austerity, with no offsetting effort to foster growth, have done double damage. They have failed as economic policy, worsening unemployment without restoring confidence; a Europe-wide recession now looks likely even if the immediate threat of financial crisis is contained.

Krugman chides those who readily dismiss America’s problems because they’re not quite as bad as those of present-day Europe or the Great Depression. There is small comfort in such comparisons, with 25 million Americans jobless or underemployed, a collapsed housing market wrought with continuing foreclosures, and no recovery in sight. Global financial contagion remains imminent.

A dismal science, indeed.
