Are taxes a form of austerity?

June 13, 2013 by davald

Matthew Yglesias writes:

In fact the general trend over the past several years among people on the left to start using “austerity” as a meaningless signifier for “policies I don’t like” has had an unfortunate negative impact on the quality of discourse. When state governments cut back social services in the face of falling tax revenue and rising employee health care and pension costs, that is a kind of austerity. But the available alternative of raising taxes is also a form of austerity.

I have been using ‘austerity’ to refer to ‘cutbacks in government spending,’ especially acute among the states, which typically have to balance their budgets. In the face of recession-triggered reductions in revenues, they have chosen to reduce expenditures on public services rather than raise taxes, a politically difficult choice.

But I have to wonder how raising taxes is necessarily a form of austerity. Yglesias seems to be suggesting that austerity can imply economic contraction. If so, then the answer depends.

If governments raise taxes on the wealthy, say the top one percent, would that in itself retard economic growth? It could if the dollars the rich pay in taxes were otherwise spent on productive activities. Yet the rich spend a disproportionately lower percentage of their income than the Rest of Us, who can use every penny we make. Here’s Joseph Stiglitz, writing for Vanity Fair:

The relationship is straightforward and ironclad: as more money becomes concentrated at the top, aggregate demand goes into a decline. Unless something else happens by way of intervention, total demand in the economy will be less than what the economy is capable of supplying—and that means that there will be growing unemployment, which will dampen demand even further. In the 1990s that “something else” was the tech bubble. In the first dec­ade of the 21st century, it was the housing bubble. Today, the only recourse, amid deep recession, is government spending—which is exactly what those at the top are now hoping to curb.

Also, we know that during the 50s and 60s the marginal tax rate at the federal level was much, much higher than it is today. Nevertheless, the economy grew at a far faster clip than it does currently when tax rates are low.

So, I would argue that increasing taxes on the wealthy, either by raising the marginal rate or by instituting Robert Frank’s consumption tax, could actually spur economic growth by stopping the flow of dollars from the spending bottom to the hoarding top. Here’a s chart I’ve used before from the Federal Reserve showing the Gini index and the velocity of money, the latter decreasing over time with increased wealth concentration.

 

During the relative halcyon days of the post-war era, the federal government balanced its budgets, collecting sufficient revenues to meet expenses, which continued to grow. With the fall in tax rates on both personal and corporate income, revenues have declined but federal spending has not, leaving us with a series of deficits and higher debt.

Another coincidence

June 13, 2013 by davald

Readers of this blog know that I spend quite a bit of time on what I call ‘the Great Divergence,’ a period beginning in the mid-1970s and continuing to the present. It has been marked by growing inequality, certainly, with the rich becoming richer and the poor poorer and more plentiful. A number of other metrics correlate with the decline of the Rest of Us and the ascendancy of the monied interests. These include, for example, the increasing financialization of the economy, the declining share of labor, diminished GDP from year to year, and a steadily falling labor participation rate among male workers. We can add another.

Bloomberg News reports that Americans’ dissatisfaction with Congress has reached another new low. The most recent Gallop Poll reveals that only ten percent of us are “satisfied” with federal lawmakers. The rating reached its highest level in 1973, at 42 percent.

Hmmm.

Economics matters

June 12, 2013 by davald

A few professional economists don’t like what they see in the mirror. They decry the sorry state of their discipline, whose practitioners predict badly as they offer erroneous advice to those in a position to make a difference.

So why should economics, of all the academic pursuits, currently summon our attention? After all, we don’t seem to mind that philosophers disagree sharply on some rather profound concepts, from the nature of knowledge to how we should behave. Admit it, you probably haven’t read a philosopher recently and feel that you haven’t missed much. Even scientists, those presumably in pursuit of verifiable truths about the world, differ on significant topics, including global warming, and yet we give them a pass, if we pay attention at all.

I suggest that economics matters now more than ever because many of us are concerned about broad and deep unemployment amidst wasted lives, and, more to the point, we believe that the government can play a vital role in ameliorating the situation. No academic field comes to mind in addressing these problems.

But economists themselves do us no favor when they fail to reach consensus on both description and prescription. They cannot agree on what ails us, nor do they present a unified front on what to do. Still others decline to proffer corrective policies at all.

Part of the reason, I suspect, takes us back to the post-war decades wherein Keynesianism held sway. I should clarify. Economists both in and out of government believed that the government could fine-tune the economy to smooth out what were assumed to be natural business cycles. If GDP seemed to be trending down, then open up the fiscal spigots. When inflation reared its ugly head, raise the cost of money. For a while the minor adjustments to the accelerator and brake appeared to work.

However, Berkeley economist Christine Romer concluded (pdf) that such government interventions, both monetary and fiscal, created a juggernaut of leap-frogging expectations between prices and wages, culminating in the stagflation period of Jimmy Carter’s latter years in office and the beginning of Reagan’s term. The Federal Reserve, led by Paul Volcker, sharply increased the federal funds rate, which quickly dampened inflation but at the cost of higher unemployment.

 

Following the grand Volcker intervention, the idea that the economy could be tamed by the government became anathema. Indeed, the less the government interfered in markets the better. Economic actors, guided by their own preferences and rational expectations, should be free to choose.

This attitude remains dominant, even following the collapse of the housing bubble and the major disruption of Wall Street, whose exuberantly wrong-headed bets necessitated the mother of all subsidies. Policy makers both here and in Europe stubbornly cling to the false dichotomy between public and private, insisting that the former’s role in the latter is perniciously counterproductive. Moreover, federal governments should never spend more than they take in and when they do all the world’s problems ensue.

In Europe, especially, fiscal consolidation reigns as the antidote to the spendthrift ways that ushered in the Great Recession. This is no time for easy money or public programs to put people back to work. Tighten your belts.

Yet, fiscal austerity hasn’t worked. It has failed to restore investor confidence or spur economic activity, as its proponents promised. Instead, it has had the predictable effect of contracting the economy, consigning millions of people to undeserving and unwanted idleness.

We might excuse political leaders for tightening their governments’ belts. After all, most are not professional economists. But they never lack for economists who provide them with intellectual cover, and it is those economists who deserve opprobrium.

Oxford economist Simon Wren-Lewis writes in his blog today:

I am not disdainful of those in 2010 who thought austerity was necessary because either a debt crisis was around the corner, or economic recovery had been assured, and have subsequently done what Keynes suggested should be done when the evidence becomes clearer. I think they were wrong back then, and said so, but it was an understandable mistake, and even the best economists make mistakes. But I’m afraid to continue in 2013 to advocate a course of action which anyone can see is doing immense harm to so many people is just inexcusable.

We are left, then, with a profession that came to disdain the teachings of John Maynard Keynes, though many surely misunderstood him, while adopting a “free-to-choose” orthodoxy championed by Milton Friedman and his saltwater acolytes. This is unfortunate for the Rest of Us.

The transformation from Nixon’s “we are all Keynesians now” to free-market apologists has coincided with the gradual eroding of income of the bottom 90 percent, rapidly escalating private debt, collapsing public infrastructure and services, and the increasing financialization of the economy, even as economic activity stagnates.

Cynics would suggest that money tends to have its way. With the financial sector siphoning off an ever-increasing share of total wealth, a resurgent stock market, and extremely low inflation, one might conclude that things are as they should be—meaning the Rest of Us just don’t count.

Hyperventilating Krugman

June 7, 2013 by davald

He writes:

But the lack of accountability, for ideas and people, is truly remarkable in a time of massive policy failure.

Prof. Krugman is exasperated at times, having to repeat his critiques of government actions, or lack thereof. He’s also terribly frustrated that the same people continue to trumpet the same warnings year after year, yet their predictions never materialize. He believes that such people need to revisit their models, or whatever frameworks they’re using, to appreciate why they have been wrong. But they clearly ignore his advice as they keep up their pontifications.

I share Krugman’s angst, though he’d probably stop short of that noun; he’s just too busy. Yet, even as he admits, it gets old writing the same things with no apparent effect on those running the show, notably the people in Washington, D.C., and the capitals of Europe.

So, despite miserable economic news, the European Central Bank sees no reason to change its slow-as-you-go monetary policy. There is still the lingering fear that the economy could heat up and—horrors—prices and wages will sharply rise.

With Republicans effectively stymying fiscal responses to our own problems of massive and persistent unemployment, we are guaranteed more of the same for years on end. The Bureau of Labor Statistics surmised that employment levels will not return to pre-recession norms until 2018.

Still waiting for the revolution

June 6, 2013 by davald

How bad do things have to get before the people start saying no en masse? Economic problems are most acute in Europe, where even Germany, the presumed success story, boasts growth of only 0.3 percent. Hooray. Meanwhile, the unemployment rates in France, Spain, and elsewhere are well above double digits, with no signs of relief.

Europe is hardly a stranger to revolt. We know what the French did to their monarchy when told to eat cake. The entire continent took to the streets in 1848. Western democracies seemed to have had enough a hundred years later, when protests were as common as flies in August. Yet, lassitude in the face of mediocrity has become the norm.

The Times:

Nearly six years after the financial crisis in the United States spread across the Atlantic, plunging Europe into recession and, in some places, desperate depression, “good” is relative. Economic figures that would be considered disastrous elsewhere are being held up by many politicians and policy makers as really not so bad at all — the first tender shoots of a recovery that is out there somewhere.

The wealthy are back to making lots of money, despite the dismal economic news. Perhaps the Rest of Us are truly superfluous. If that is to change, we’re going to have to make it so.

The editors of Monthly Review are encouraged, citing Occupy Wall Street, the pushback by Wisconsin public sector workers, and the resistance of Chicago school teachers to Rahm Emanuel’s dictates. They write:

One thing that these three rebellions had in common is the growing awareness that economic and political power in the United States is firmly in the hands of a tiny minority of fantastically wealthy individuals whose avarice knows no bounds. These titans of finance want to eviscerate working men and women, making them as insecure as possible and totally dependent on the dog-eat-dog logic of the marketplace, while at the same time converting any and all aspects of life into opportunities for capital accumulation.

I seem to recall someone urging: “Workers of the world unite! You have nothing to lose but your chains.”

Stark relief

June 6, 2013 by davald

The New York Times editors expose the inequities between and within states. Wisconsin, led by its stubbornly conservative firebrand Scott Walker, has refused to extend Medicaid, apparently a thumb-to-nose gesture at the Affordable Care Act, if not also to Obama himself. Meanwhile, next door, Minnesota lawmakers voted just the opposite, realizing that their state will save money via Medicaid.

In their opinion the editors write:

What’s really going on, of course, is that state Republicans have made poor people the victims of their ideological resistance to President Obama and his health care law.

That infection has spread to 25 states, mostly with Republican leaders, that have said no to the Medicaid expansion or are resisting it. As Robert Pear of The Times recently reported, more than half of all people without health insurance live in states that are not planning to expand coverage. Many of those states already do a terrible job of covering the poor and have no interest in changing their ways, no matter the financial incentive.

Readers of these pages know that I despise Republicans and their ideology, though I remain baffled by why so many subscribe to the idiocy, mendacity, and mean spiritedness that marks if not also governs the GOP. Or, maybe that’s the point. Our nation is home to bastards who care about their lily-white selves and not a whit for the fortunes of others, whom they are all too anxious to throw under the bus then run over again and again.

So, while I share the editors’ sentiment, I doubt their optimism:

The one virtue of a patchwork is that better examples are not far away. When residents begin to realize the grass is much greener on the other side of the state line, budget-slashing lawmakers will be under pressure to either change their ways or change jobs.

Why work?

June 4, 2013 by davald

David Brooks waxes philosophical today. He rejects a utilitarian ethic in favor of the Kantian notion that we are proper ends in ourselves and not just means to achieve a purpose. Okay. I get that there are deontologists and consequentialists and a host of other moral positions. Which is better or sounder or whatever is a subject of never-ending debate, truly.

But then Brooks has to slip in this sentence:

Taking a job just to make money, on the other hand, is probably going to be corrosive, even if you use the money for charity rather than sports cars.

To the millions of unemployed out there this sound particularly insensitive, don’t you think?

Return on investment

June 4, 2013 by davald

On occasion I visit the financial pages of Sunday’s newspapers. Nothing personal, as I have no direct ownership of stocks and bonds. But, as we were told a few years ago, follow the money. That’s where the action is—and the power and the glory, forever and ever, Amen.

If you perform the same cursory glance you’ll eventually come across data on “interest” and “yields,” the kinds of things that attract the watchful eye of people with money. What you’ll discover is that neither metric is very high these days. Indeed, both are somewhat south of unity.

When people with money do the math, they may come away depressed, both emotionally and financially. A thousand dollars in a certificate of deposit pays out a couple of bucks at the end of the year. Not very rich. So, where can a wealthy person go?

Increasingly, investors are returning to real estate. And it’s not difficult to understand why. Housing prices are escalating by almost ten percent a year, and they are growing fastest in areas that took the brunt of the Great Recession. Nathaniel Popper of the New York Times writes:

While these investors have not touched many healthy real estate markets, they are among the biggest buyers in struggling areas of the country where housing prices have been increasing the fastest. Those gains, in turn, have been at the leading edge of rising home prices nationwide.

I’m intrigued by that last sentence. Investors looking for higher returns on their money can influence the price of commodities and assets by making initial purchases of those items. As potential buyers of those goods see values appreciate, they are induced to buy sooner rather than later and pay more rather than less—for fear of being priced out of the market should they sit on the sidelines.

Those of us untrained in the lexicon of finance call such things ‘bubbles.’ Moreover, a bubble can form around any object, including, of course, a house. A few centuries ago the world went apeshit over Dutch tulip bulbs. We know that the markets for gold and silver and other precious metals have been continually manipulated. Remember the Pet Rock? More recently, of course, the global economy fell to its knees when the housing bubble collapsed in late 2007.

Money begets money, and it hates to be idle. Impatience breeds speculation that creates its own returns. The problem for the Rest of Us: individually, we don’t have any money, or not enough of it to make a difference. Thus, we cannot artificially create wealth. More often than not, we become pawns in speculative schemes and ultimately their victims.

I suppose we could collectively withhold our dollars and refuse to join the frenzy. But that is tantamount to shooting ourselves in the foot, since our buying stuff, including houses, fuels most of the economy. In turn, that economy provides the jobs and, therefore, the income to pay the rent and put food on the table. If we strike, we suffer.

Crazy system.

Get your jobs here!

June 3, 2013 by davald

Houston, it seems, spawns more new jobs than any other city in the U.S. It earned that status through a combination of laissez-faire zoning, flat, cheap land, and a “business-friendly” government, as in low taxes and few regulations. But would you want to live there?

To be outdoors in Houston in August is like breathing soup or living in the anal cavity of a giant mammal.

That’s ginandtacos at the keyboard, telling us like it is. We should be terribly afraid of our local governments trying to ape Houston:

This is supposed to be the template for other cities to follow – be more like the growing, sprawling megaplexes of the Sun Belt. Pay particular attention to Texas and the Southwest. Turn your city into a gargantuan strip mall, hand out money to oil companies (and defense contractors; good lord does Texas love it some defense contractors), actively reject the idea of urban planning as socialist, and tell everyone who lives there to fend for themselves. We have seen the future, and it sucks.
