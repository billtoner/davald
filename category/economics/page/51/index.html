Human capital

August 25, 2012 by davald

Capital, as we know, belongs to a privileged few, the Haves and Have-mores—otherwise known as the One-percenters. The Rest of Us scramble and struggle to make ends meet, while being forced to constantly look up to rich men’s butts. But, please, we don’t have to like the emanating shit.

Charles Blow, writing for the New York Times, tells us that “America is in trouble.” He’s thinking about the future, and at present course, that future belongs to China and India. Why? Because those nations (and others, especially central and northern European) are investing heavily in their children’s education and well-being, while America is content to let most of our young people do without. The privileged few made it, then pulled up the ladder of success, relegating the Have-nots to lives of misery and hopelessness. That we long ago surrendered to this perverse order and now embrace it evinces a Stockholm Syndrome second to none.

So I return to education. Blow cites the results of a recent study (pdf) that compares and contrasts investments made by different countries in their children’s present and future. America, as I said above and elsewhere, dropped the ball, which it had only loosely carried for generations. We shortchange our children and thus their and, collectively, our future, whereas China, India, and the Nordic countries, in particular, understand that enhancing human capital makes all the difference—across numerous fronts, including international competitiveness.

Here’s an excerpt from that study that strikes a familiar note with readers:

A consequence of unequal education funding is that the least experienced and prepared teachers receive the lowest pay and teach the most disadvantaged students. And the teaching corps itself is weak. America’s teachers have strong academic credentials, yet with no improvement on national or international student assessment in decades it’s clear that many teachers struggle to help their students learn.

This may be due, in part to the fact that high school students who choose to enter undergraduate programs for education have SAT scores on average in the bottom third of all students tested. That stands in sharp contrast to nations with impressive student results, such as Finland, which successfully recruit teachers from their top high school graduates.

The report reaffirms upfront the importance of teacher quality in properly reforming America’s education system. Despite the emphasis of No Child Left Behind on mandating that only “highly qualified” teachers teach, there has been no significant increase in students’ academic performance. We might conclude, correctly, I believe, that we still don’t get it when it comes to selecting then preparing our nation’s best and brightest educators. I submit, based on the Finnish experience, that focusing on this crucial ingredient and acting accordingly will enhance human capital.

Sadly, not only do we fail at delivering the best teachers to our public school classrooms, we deprive our children of those social and economic benefits enjoyed by their counterparts in Finland, Sweden, et al. Worse, America is trending toward increased privatization of education, marked by charter schools and class-based segregation—all in the name of competition and choice. The report:

Economists studying the root causes of economic growth increasingly stress that investments in early childhood development and learning are especially critical for the human capital development needed in a strong, competitive economy. That’s because prenatal care and experiences from birth through early childhood affect children’s physical and brain development—and therefore the cognitive, social, and emotional development that will carry them through their lives.

Though we bang our “We’re Number One” drum loudly and repeatedly, with no evident reason, we ignore a glaring societal defect: too many of us are poor. Nor do we seem at all concerned about diminishing poverty. Once poor in America you’ll likely stay that way; and the same holds if you’re born rich. How do we stack up against other countries on the inconvenient poverty metric (OECD, 2005 data)?

All of the above countries, including the U.S., of course, have market economies, whose outcomes relegate substantial portions of their populations to poverty. That’s before taxes and transfers. The Nordic countries, especially, re-distribute national wealth to their “losers,” in the form of economic security, education, and social welfare benefits. We, on the other hand, take less from the rich, the “job creators” (not), therefore depriving everyone else of a decent life.

John Lennon imagined a better world. In America, that’s all we can do—evidently.

Confounding economics

August 24, 2012 by davald

I make no claims whatsoever to a firm grasp of economics, having taken only one class in it too many years ago to remember. I do hang around with an economist, and I religiously pore over the musings of Paul Krugman, Matthew Yglesias (a philosopher by training who writes about money stuff), and Brad DeLong, a professor at my alma mater. So I have just enough knowledge of the discipline to utter my share of non sequiturs and nonsense. Yet, I try to adhere to what passes for economic truth, relying heavily on data gleaned mostly from the St. Louis Fed (FRED), OECD, and any other source I can lay my eyes on.

A week ago I acquired a used copy of Krugman’s textbook on economics, co-written by his wife, Robin Wells. As I expected, the pages are filled with charts and graphs, along with a spattering of cartoons and epigrams to spice up the content. Here I wish to focus on one item, written as if it’s gospel to all economists. Krugman and Wells tell us (pg. 258):

The most basic point to understand about savings and investment spending is that they are always equal. This is not a theory; it’s a fact of accounting…

Before I get into the accounting piece, I present you with this recent report from the Federal Reserve via Bloomberg News:

As deposits increased 3.3 percent to $8.88 trillion in the two months ended July 31, business lending rose 0.7 percent to $7.11 trillion, Federal Reserve data show. The record gap of $1.77 trillion has expanded 15 percent since May, the biggest similar-period gain since July, 2010. Banks have already bought $136.4 billion in Treasury and government agency debt this year, more than double the $62.6 billion in all of 2011, pushing their holdings to an all-time high of $1.84 trillion.

What I don’t understand—at first glance and perhaps forever—is how the equivalence cited by Krugman and Wells obtains when deposits exceed lending. If deposits are a form of savings, and I should think that they are, and if lending facilitates investments, which they surely do, then what am I missing, since there is a growing gap between the two?

The economists offer a definition by deduction.

  1. Total income = total spending
  2. Total income = consumption spending + savings
  3. Total spending = consumption spending + investment spending
  4. Consumption spending + savings = consumption spending + investment spending; therefore,
  5. Savings = investment spending

Let’s take the first premise, total income equals total spending. I suspect that economists have different definitions than I have. For example, I go about my days believing that I have a choice between socking some dollars into savings or spending them. I do not view them as equivalent.

The economist, however, may say that savings is nothing more than deferred spending, either by me or someone else. If I should die with money still in the bank, my successors will have it to spend on the iPhone 12. (I’m optimistic.) Or, the economist may assume that whatever is deposited in my savings account is available for current or future investment. After all, isn’t that what banks do? They lend money drawn from my and others’ savings accounts.

But isn’t it possible, as the Bloomberg News story suggests, that banks will not lend the money on deposit and instead use it to buy up Treasuries or each other? Would the economist reply that those purchased Treasuries translate into investments? I’m guessing the answer is yes, but I don’t know why.

 

As we can see, deposits have risen sharply since before the Great Recession ended. I want to call these deposits “savings.” Do Krugman and Wells prefer another term, say, “investment spending”? Calling Lewis Carroll. My savings equals your investment spending? I detect an oxymoron in the latter expression. Or, again, I’m an ignorant layperson, unschooled in academic vocabulary.

What about lending?

 

Loans are moving upwards, after a sharp decline during the recession. But they’re still far below the level of deposits shown in the previous chart.

As for consumer loans, we have this chart:

 

According to Bloomberg, total bank lending is about $7.1 trillion. Total deposits are $8.9 trillion, for a difference of $1.8 trillion. Where is the extra money? Or is that not a proper question from an economist?

The article goes on to suggest that banks and private firms are reluctant to invest. Why is that? Bloomberg:

“There’s all sorts of good long-term developments that are occurring on household balance sheets, but you sense the Fed would like them to be not quite as thrifty and instead put a little more money to work,” said Jim Vogel, head of agency-debt research at FTN Financial inMemphis, Tennessee. “But that’s not going to happen without salary incomes rising.”

That explains the gap between deposits and lending, said Jeffrey Caughron, a partner at Baker Group LP in Oklahoma City who advises community banks on more than $30 billion of investments.

“It’s a function of inherently weak demand for loans and that relates to inherently weak demand in the economy,” he said. “Consumers, households, businesses: they’re paying down debt, they’re saving money, they’re not borrowing. They don’t have an appetite.”

Banks are awash in money (savings? investment spending?). Yet they are loath to lend, which means(?) that there is insufficient investing going on in the economy. We are certainly saving, as the following chart shows.

 

But unless we start spending, rather than saving more, then the Rest of Us suffer under a mediocre economy. Wait, you say? You can’t spend or save because you don’t have enough money?

Apropos of a previous post, your income deficiency is your own damn fault. You need to get rid of the hammock then set about adopting different values, the same kind that propels the One-percent.

I still don’t understand the equivalence, though. I suspect that my confusion lies in definitions. The logician in me suspects a tautology. If I’m right, then the equivalence fails to advance my knowledge. Sometime in my life I learned that Venus was both the ‘morning star’ and the ‘evening star.’ But the equivalence taught me nothing about the planet itself.

But he is deadly serious about cutting taxes on the rich and slashing aid to the poor, very much in line with [Ayn] Rand’s worship of the successful and contempt for “moochers.”

The big punch bowl

August 23, 2012 by davald

GOP presidential candidate, Mitt Romney, unveiled his “energy independence” plan, which, as one might guess, is simply “drill, baby, drill.” He would essentially streamline permitting processes for new oil exploration and broadly expand available drilling sites, including off-shore. Despite Obama’s enabling a rapid increase in domestic oil exploration and production, Romney said today, as quoted by the New York Times:

“The net net of all this, as you can see [from a chart that no one could see], is by 2020, we’re able to produce somewhere between 28 million barrels per day of oil and we won’t need to buy any oil from the Middle East or Venezuela or anywhere else where we don’t want to,” Mr. Romney said. [By the way, the New York Times reports that the U.S. produces 15 billion barrels of oil a day. The CIA estimates the total to be just under 10 million barrels a day.)

The petroleum market is truly global. I liken it to a giant punch bowl, with many spigots pouring oil in and many more straws drawing it out. The price of each output unit is determined the old-fashioned way: by supply and demand.

So, what would really happen if the U.S. increased domestic supply?

  • It would have negligible impacts on global aggregate supply and thus scant effect on market prices.
  • Oil is traded on a global scale. Exxon, for example, seeks the best deal on wholesale quantities from wherever then sells at retail based on a highest price the market will bear. If Exxon finds a better wholesale price elsewhere, it will likely keep domestic supplies in reserve until it makes economic sense to use them.*
  • If market prices rise, so do the revenues of the oil companies. Since revenue equals price times quantity, the greater the supply of oil the greater the revenues—at any given price.
  • Further environmental despoliation.
  • Increased greenhouse gases.
  • Undermining of conservation acquisition and renewable resource development.

Let’s look at a few charts. First, we’ll place U.S. production within the global context.

 

 

In percentage terms, here’s what it looks like:

 

 

What about consumption? The U.S. consumes 21 percent of the world total. (Note: Our population represents only 4.4 percent of the planet’s human inhabitants.)

 

Now let’s compare production with consumption.

Based on 2010 data the U.S. imports nearly 50 percent of the oil it consumes. From whom do we get our oil? (By the way, the data used in this post via the CIA World Factbook.)

In addition to illustrating the sources of our oil, the above chart reminds us that there is a vast global petroleum market. Since we must import so much of the oil we use, we spend an inordinate amount of our tax dollars on defending shipping lanes and both acquiring and protecting foreign supplies of petroleum (e.g., Iraq, Kuwait).

On a sour note, I conclude that neither Romney’s nor Obama’s energy strategies will make us energy independent or prevent a global warming catastrophe. Such is the state of politics in America.

The y-axis is in millions of dollars. In 2010, the debt reached $2.7 trillion, the largest in history. Conservatives insist that the annual deficits and cumulative debt result from excessive government spending—alone. They completely ignore federal receipts, which are mostly in the form of taxes.

Here we see that in Reagan’s first term receipts began trailing spending, which did not subside, despite Reagan’s railing against big government; it only got bigger under his reign. Then under Bush II the parity started to unravel. Spending continued apace, but receipts really lagged behind. There are a few reasons for the latter: the Bush tax cuts, two wars, and a torpid economy. The Great Recession obviously exacerbated the trend.

Conservatives argue several points about the federal government. They say that they believe in limiting it. But whenever they’re in power, they expand it. However, their most fervent opinions focus on taxation. Their own self-interest demands that they push for ever lower taxes. Moreover, they argue, lower taxes spurs economic growth. Well, history proves them wrong.

The mother of all moral hazards

August 20, 2012 by davald

While I suspect that the following chart means something, I can only speculate for the moment. Here goes:

If we add up the total value of all U.S. residential and commercial property we get $4.3 trillion dollars, as of the first quarter of this year (Federal Reserve data). Last year the value of all U.S. based financial derivatives was $244 trillion, or about 57 times more than buildings and land (source—pdf).

Now it’s true that the value of anything is what people are willing to pay for it. For years we collectively bid up the price of housing, so that by 2007 home values were way off the charts. The bubble popped, with ruinous consequences.

But what about financial instruments, particularly derivatives, which, by definition, are based on some underlying asset, though it’s difficult, if not impossible, to discover just what that might be? We know that as the housing bubble expanded, so did the value of such derivatives, many based on the assumed value of real estate. When the bubble collapsed, so did the value of mortgage-backed securities, including associated derivatives.

The cynic in me suggests that there’s something not quite right about the increasing financialization of the economy. In a very literal sense, it’s not real, since, again, values are so intangible. Moreover, the value of the derivatives greatly exceeds the aggregate GDP of all the world’s economies (source). How can that be?

The derivative market strikes me as one giant house of cards that could very easily crumble, perhaps with only the slightest of what economists call “shocks.” What sustains it against so many potential disruptions, not the least of which is the loss of confidence in any number of institutions, precipitated by who knows what?

Now we enter the realm of too big to fail. However dodgy this derivatives market, we must acknowledge that it’s huge and represents a sizable chunk of investments by you, me, and thousands of pension funds, both public and private. With so much at stake, there is a strong incentive to keep the financial cards upright. And this is where governments come in.

When Lehman Brothers was on the brink of bankruptcy, America’s top financial advisors, including Ben Bernanke and Henry Paulson, first tried to pass it off. They firmly believed that markets self-correct without government intervention, and that the loss of Lehman posed no significant threat to the overall economy. Over several intense days, however, they eventually realized that the house of cards was ready to collapse along with the housing bubble. What would be the consequences? In divining the answer they grew horrified. Nothing short of a massive infusion of capital in troubled banks would prevent a global financial meltdown—thus TARP.

But this is perverse. Aside from enabling the mother of all moral hazards, the Rest of Us are serving as the insurers of a giant Ponzi scheme. And we know that such schemes, like garbage incinerators, demand ever-larger inputs. It’s as if we’ve signed a Faustian bargain but somebody forged our signatures, and if we don’t pay up when things go south, instant catastrophe.

However pernicious and absurd, the devils in this scheme, otherwise known as the oligarchs, have purchased the best Congress money can buy. This arrangement all but guarantees that forever forward, it’s heads they win and tails we lose.

Worse for the Rest of Us, both political parties are completely enthrall to Wall Street, for a very simple reason: that’s where the money is. If you want to get elected or stay in office, you learn quickly not to ruffle the oligarchs’ feathers. Should you even whisper that something’s askew with the financial sector and its control of the economy, you’re committing political suicide.

Sorry, ladies and gentlemen. I don’t have a quick solution to this mess, which emerged over time, beginning with the Great Divergence under Reagan. Actually, I’m rather pessimistic, since the incentives to prop up the financial cards greatly outweigh political opposition, which is paltry at best.

Just hold on as best you can.

Russell’s wisdom: why we fight

August 19, 2012 by davald

One essential primer is Bertrand Russell’s The Problems of Philosophy. Therein he provides a concise introduction to issues over which philosophers have long struggled. And if you have any familiarity with philosophy as an academic pursuit, you know that philosophers develop only tentative or provisional answers—such is the nature of the discipline. But if you’re patient, you’ll eventually uncover some kernels of wisdom, and Russell’s writings contain many.

For example, consider these words from the aforementioned book:

Material possessions can be taken by force and enjoyed by the robber. Spiritual possessions cannot be taken in this way. You may kill an artist or a thinker, but you cannot acquire his art or his thought. You may put a man to death because he loves his fellow-men, but you will not by so doing acquire the love which made his happiness. Force is impotent in such matters; it is only as regards material goods that it is effective. For this reason the men who believe in force are the men whose thoughts and desires are preoccupied with material goods.

Thus the conservative mind, since Republicans as a rule are obsessed with “material possessions.” What they have, they fear will be taken. What they lack, they strive to obtain—by almost any means. If they should rule, they will employ the nation’s considerable armaments to plunder the wealth of others. As for defending America’s fortunes, mostly owned by the privileged few, they will spare no expense.

They will not stop at the military or the police state. Their fear-cum-greed affects every fabric of our well-being.

In the hotly contested issue of educational reform, conservatives believe that students should learn those skills necessary to succeed in the acquisition of wealth or to tend to the care and feeding of the oligarchy. Critical thinking, the kind that would ultimately question status quo political arrangements, must be eliminated, since it poses a threat to the powerful. After all, the masses outnumber them.

Investing in the arts and music, except for the products of dead painters and composers, wastes dollars that should be earmarked for exploitation. Public dollars must never go toward self-expression or human development.*

On economic issues, merit determines all, and the rich possess a monopoly, since they made the rules and insist on punishing transgressors. At your workplace, you count for nothing, save for what you contribute to the wealth of your corporate benefactors. Drop any notion of unionism; Labor is evil, because it frustrates Capital’s unrelenting greed. Meanwhile, you are expected to work more hours and extract less from your effort.

 

 

Finally, the Rest of Us must stop worrying and learn to love:

  • decrepit school buildings
  • collapsing roads
  • urban ghettoes
  • ugly strip malls
  • pollution
  • global warming
  • no philosophy, no art, no music, no beauty (these are reserved for your masters, who are happy to pay dearly for them)
  • expensive and ineffective health care
  • mind-numbing TV reality shows
  • perpetual war
  • shit.

But take heart, your taxes are low.

Playing with fire or a coming apartheid?

August 15, 2012 by davald

How long can they keep them down on the farm? Republicans, apparently turning a blind-eye to changing demographics, have launched a coordinated assault on the poor, mostly, and people of color, in particular. Their vehicle, other than America’s draconian criminal justice system, is voter ID. If you don’t have a “proper” one, you cannot vote. As a disproportionately high number of now-minority groups lack the requisite identification, they are instantly disenfranchised in those states passing this latest voter-suppression tool.

Today Judge Robert Simpson upheld a Republican-passed bill in Pennsylvania’s legislature. He declined to grant an injunction sought by the law’s potential victims. If citizens wish to vote this November they must show a government-issued photo identification.

Revealing the party’s true colors, Pennsylvania’s house leader, Michael Turzai, predicted that the law’s passage would guarantee the state for Mitt Romney. The poor and people of color—mostly the same—are not expected to vote Republican in the upcoming or any election. Do you think?

The New York Times editors weighed in on the law and the court decision.

The real reasons for voter ID laws are quite clear. The desire to dampen the Democratic vote after 2006, and particularly in the wake of President Obama’s election, prompted six states to decide, virtually simultaneously, to pass voter ID laws. Their stated reason — combating voter fraud — is easily dismissed, since there are virtually no documented cases of impersonation fraud that could be reduced with an ID card. Mr. Turzai was simply indiscreet; most Republicans know better than to speak the truth out loud.

We live in truly Machiavellian times. The Republicans, composed principally of better-heeled whites, no doubt initiated the surreptitious investigations of ACORN, which had hired mostly poor people to register citizens to vote. It was discovered that many of the signatures were bogus, often explicitly so, with names like “Mickey Mouse,” for example. Conservatives cried “Voter fraud!” Hardly. As the Times editors point out, “there have been no prosecutions or even investigations of in-person voter fraud in Pennsylvania.” Never mind. The message stuck, and large numbers of Americans believe that voter fraud (e.g., voting under an alias or more than once) is rampant.

Anxious to prevent people from voting Democratic, the Republican Party introduced legislation in several state houses with the same requirement: a government-issued identification. The editors tell us:

The requirement will have a disparate impact on minorities, who tend to lack government IDs at a higher rate than the general population and tend to vote Democratic.

History does not stand still, nor do Hispanics. Their percentage of the U.S. population continues to grow.

The U.S. Census Bureau today [May 26, 2011] released a 2010 Census brief on the nation’s Hispanic population, which shows the Hispanic population increased by 15.2 million between 2000 and 2010 and accounted for more than half of the total U.S. population increase of 27.3 million. Between 2000 and 2010, the Hispanic population grew by 43 percent, or four times the nation’s 9.7 percent growth rate.

 

In less than 40 years minorities (i.e., non-whites) are expected to become the majority in the United States. Ten national elections to go. Republicans may certainly recognize the trend, and my guess is that they’re scared to death of so many brown and black people holding office and making laws. I suspect that they’re mounting a pre-emptive assault in the short term in hopes of delegitimizing the future majority; people of color may have the numbers, but, if the Republicans have their way, they won’t have the votes to repel the conservative agenda.

For the Republicans to prevail over the long term they must establish de jure apartheid, leaping beyond the current de facto. A white minority would control a colored majority. There are precedents, of course:

  • U.S. colonists over indigenous Americans
  • White southerners dominating majority black populations—all enslaved
  • White Afrikaners over black African natives
  • Spanish, Portuguese, and Dutch conquering and subjugating populations of black, brown, and red peoples

Should the economy continue to sputter indefinitely I see whiteness becoming as salient as black or brown in how we divide ourselves. That is, even nominal Democrats, increasingly fearful of their economic futures, may switch parties to join the Republicans to more overtly suppress people of color. We’ve begun to see formerly liberal states like Wisconsin and Pennsylvania transform into Republican strongholds, whereupon their legislatures and governors have moved swiftly to de-unionize public employees and restrict voter turnout. Could other states follow?

I’ve written previously about how dismal economies produce political extremism. We’re seeing it in Europe. It certainly happened there before WWII, during the Great Depression. If too many people remain out of work or see their wages stagnate or lose public benefits, will they be more inclined to support an FDR or a Paul Ryan?

Is it possible that the conservative strategists see the writing on the demographic wall and have already set in motion a serious of events and initiatives that, one, ensure that the economy continues to sputter, and, two, further thwart the potential electoral outcomes of a new colored majority?

If you think this is preposterous on its face, I call your attention to the decades-long strategy of the GOP to reverse liberal fortunes, beginning in earnest under Nixon. And it’s all about money, lots of it. Those that have it started investing in conservative “think tanks,” political action committees, electoral races, and lobbying. (Much of this has been chronicled by Dean Baker, Chris Hedges, Corey Robin, Joseph Stiglitz, John Cassidy, and Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson.) That is to say, the rise of the modern-day conservative movement didn’t happen by accident.

The point here is that conservatives have amply demonstrated their commitment to the long haul. They succeeded when so many had written them off.

I would not bet against them. They have the money, the power to manipulate outcomes in their favor, and the resolve to stay the course.

Meanwhile, liberal Democrats are clueless. And this may be the scariest point of all.
