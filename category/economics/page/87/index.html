Entitlement reform

September 12, 2011 by davald

The New York Times’ token conservative pundit, Ross Douthat, posts an interesting opinion today. Perhaps correctly, he posits that Obama’s bid for a second term amounts to a plea for a “do-over.”

Douthat reminds us of congressional testimony given by Alice Rivlin, Clinton’s budget director. She warned Congress within days of Obama’s assuming office of three matters:

  1. Obama’s stimulus package was ill-designed.
  2. That the administration should refrain from making optimistic predictions about the stimulus’ impacts.
  3. Obama should be concerned with deficits, in particular, constraining entitlement spending.

I agree with the first two. But I disagree with the third.

The assumption is that our fiscal problems result from excessive federal outlays on three programs: Social Security, Medicare, and Medicaid. I hasten to mention that these “entitlements” are paltry in comparison to those enjoyed by our European counterparts. That said, let’s move on to the numbers.

I suggest that what ails us is diminishing receipts rather than spending, although it’s obvious that our health care costs are extravagantly higher than those of any western industrial society. We should expect that the federal programs that pay those rising costs will also grow. (Chart below based on BEA data.)

If one focuses only on federal outlays for the three social programs and, now, extension of unemployment benefits (blue line), then, yes, it’s clear that these are rising. They’ve even risen over time as a percentage of GDP (red line). But let’s also look at the green line, which tracks receipts (tax revenues) as a percentage of GDP.

As I suggested in a previous post, with the Great Divergence came first a leveling of tax revenues then a sharp reduction with Bush II’s tax cuts. Even if social spending had held constant, its ratio to receipts would have continued to climb.

But there is another matter that should be considered. Because the recession has taken such a massive toll on so many Americans, government relief is even more necessary. Unemployment benefits, certainly, have contributed to a rise in social spending. The abnormally high and prolonged unemployment rate also increases pathologies in the form of depression, anti-social behaviors, and disease—all increasing demands on government programs like Medicaid. Also, people who have lost jobs as they approach retirement age, choose to take Social Security benefits sooner rather than later, because they need the money.*

I am disappointed that in our country we lack political parties that are primarily concerned with human welfare. Instead, both the Democrats and Republicans are beholden to the super rich, increasingly the source of campaign contributions.

Yet, at the risk of going all Frank Capra, I submit that we have become divided as a nation between those who give a rat’s ass about people and those who couldn’t care less. Our elections, it appears, reflect an immoral majority. Ugly.

* Over the weekend I overheard an elderly gentleman, who looked younger than I, talking about how he had lost his job, his retirement, and his house because of the recession.

Some numbers

September 12, 2011 by davald

The chart above, based on data from the Bureau of Economic Analysis, provides an historical perspective of three trends:

  1. private sector wages and salaries (blue line);
  2. government wages and salaries (red line); and
  3. the savings rate as a percentage of disposable income (green line).

We see that the lowest savings rate (-1.7%) occurred in 1933; it was highest in 1944, the year before WWII ended. After the war the savings rate steadily increased, hitting a post-war maximum in 1982 of 10.6%. On average, people were saving a whopping 26 percent of their income. The rate then fell steadily and sharply until 2005, when it reached a low of 1.5%. Last year the rate rose to 5.3%, still below the post-war average of 7.2%.

Now let’s look at per capita income in real dollars.

The blue line reflects the steady rise in real per capita income (chained 2005 dollars). On average, Americans got much richer than they were in 1929, but with a three-percent drop from 2008 to 2009. The largest annual decrease occurred between 1931 and 1932, when real disposable income fell 14 percent. The largest increase, of 14%, happened between 1940 to 1941.

I hasten to emphasize that these are average numbers. We know that a relative few enjoy huge incomes while the Rest of Us manage to survive.

This chart, based on data from Berkeley economist Emmanuel Saez, shows in real dollars the income gains made between 1929 to 2005. The top 1/100th of one percent saw their incomes soar. The next line down, in purple, shows the top 5%. The bottom 90 percent (the Rest of Us) barely saw their real incomes rise since the start of the Great Depression vis-a-vis those in the upper brackets. (To be sure, real incomes of the Rest of Us moved upwards from $12,800 in 1929 to $37,872 in 2005. That’s a percentage increase of almost 200%. But that top one-tenth of one percent realized an increase of 500%. That same fractile’s income reached a peak in 2000—when it was nearly 600 percent higher than 1929 figures.)

I should point out that for several decades running, the 30s through the 60s, the incomes of the bottom 90% rose steadily then—surprise, surprise—began falling when Richard Nixon assumed office. They remained stagnant through Reagan’s terms, rising a bit during Clinton’s administration. Here’s the chart (from Saez data):

We’ll soon see that the 70s began the Great Divergence. The incomes of the top 10% followed a pattern similar to the bottom 90%, as the next chart shows.

Of course, the starting point is higher than that of the bottom 90%. But incomes rose in this group even during the Clinton terms.

What becomes obvious from the next chart is that the already super rich became even more so.

As for that narrow sliver of privilege, the top 1/100th of the top one percent, well, just take a look.

 

One conclusion from all this is that the country began changing dramatically with the arrival of Nixon. The Rest of Us had seen gradually improving lives while the fabulously wealthy merely stayed that way—until Nixon. The fortunes of the top 1/100th of the top one percent escalated rapidly during the Reagan and Clinton years.

Follow the money. That’s certainly what our two political parties do.

Why the US is not like Greece, or Spain, or Portugal or…

September 10, 2011 by davald

We often hear from certain policy makers that the US could suffer the same fate as Greece, which is to say, risk default. Such remarks peaked immediately following S&P’s downgrade of American debt.

The fear is misplaced. The reason is quite simple: the US has just one currency, its own. Spain, Greece, et al., however, belong to the Eurozone; they are sovereign, yes, but not when it comes to currency. (Investors certainly understood this, as they flocked to US Treasury bonds, sending their yields lower and lower.)

In his blog Paul Krugman links to a paper by Paul De Grauwe. I’ve just skimmed it to derive an interesting takeaway.

De Grauwe contrasts the UK with Spain. The former does not belong to the Eurozone; the latter does. The UK has a significantly larger debt as a percentage of GDP than does Spain. Yet, Spain is in financial trouble while the UK is not (yet). De Grauwe argues that the difference has everything to do with sovereign currency, which the UK has but Spain lacks. (The chart below is from the paper.)

When entering a monetary union, member-­‐countries change the nature of their sovereign debt in a fundamental way, i.e. they cease to have control over the currency in which their debt is issued. As a result, financial markets can force these countries’ sovereigns into default. In this sense member countries of a monetary union are downgraded to the status of emerging economies.

De Grauwe contends that the monetary union not only jeopardizes the economies of Greece, Spain, and others. It also renders itself “fragile,” which suggests inherent instability.

Krugman goes further. He believes that Europe is in a real mess, which adversely impacts the US.

So, that’s why they call economics ‘the dismal science.’

How high’s your spending, Mama?

September 9, 2011 by davald

It seems as if Americans as a whole may be inching back to their usual habits, if only for a short while. The Bureau of Economic Analysis released this chart over the last few days. Let’s take a look:

Notice the July figures. We’re spending more but making less. That’s American. But not American enough.

Real consumer spending has jumped only 0.5 percent. It has to be much, much higher to spur economic recovery. Yet, American wages actually fell on an annualized basis for the month of July, after seeing very modest increases in the preceding three months. And I do mean modest. Spending more than you make is hardly sustainable in the long run.

However, we should not commit the fallacy of composition. What may be good for the individual doesn’t always apply to the whole. The federal government, in particular, should be spending massive amounts of dollars, according to Krugman et al., far more than it takes in.

Yep, that means even higher deficits in the short run. When the economy sufficiently recovers (I hope it’s when and not if), taxes should be raised and federal spending curbed. I’d start with the Pentagon.

And one more thing. If our per capita medical costs were in line with Europe’s, we’d likely be running a government surplus.

Feisty

September 9, 2011 by davald

The longshoremen temporarily blocked a train and closed ports. They’re angry, and it’s about time.

Meanwhile, back in the other Washington, President Obama delivered his long-awaited speech on “jobs.” David Brooks, writing in the New York Times, called him “feisty.”

The mainstream economic view is that we should combine near-term stimulus with long-range austerity. Up until now, the political system has been unable to perform this two-stage approach. Republicans won’t touch spending, and Democrats won’t touch entitlement reform.

The president clearly wants to give it a final shot. His tone on Thursday was feisty and will please Democrats. But the substance was heterodox and worth pursuing. In this moment of peril, the country needs an insurance policy against the double dip.

I do share one thing in common with Brooks: neither one of us is properly schooled in economics, but that doesn’t deter us from talking about it. We quickly part company, however, on the topic of “entitlement reform,” among a long list of other topics.

Only the ignorant would suggest that Americans as a whole are coddled by generous entitlements. We lack universal health care coverage. We lack retirement security. And now, for certain, we lack jobs that pay livable wages. The ones who are being coddled, as Warren Buffett pointed out, are the very rich.

But David Brooks won’t hear of taxing the wealthy.

If he [Obama] comes up with his own deficit proposal that pays for his programs with some serious entitlement reforms (and not merely with some boilerplate “let’s tax the rich” plan), then Republicans would be wise to work with him to make his growth ideas more effective.

Oh, and I think that David Brooks is coddled, too. He sure acts like it.

Speaking of governance…

September 8, 2011 by davald

I wrote an op-ed for the Everett Herald a while back, part of the paper’s Viewpoints series. With some false modesty I had suggested that local governments, including school boards, should be structured and run like the Snohomish County Public Utility District. The PUD has three elected board members, lots of autonomous authority, and one direct report, the general manager. Governance is simple, minimal, direct, and accountable.

Many people have noticed that there’s a lot of work that needs to be done around here, from building and maintaining transportation systems and public schools to ensuring that we have a clean environment and a healthy, sustainable economy. That’s a tall order, to be sure, but necessary and, I would argue, doable.

The last part is tricky, however. How do we accomplish the necessary? There are resources. There are ideas. Yet, we seem to be collectively unable to make things work.

I believe it has everything to do with governance. And so does architect and former Seattle city council member Peter Steinbrueck.

The solution, he suggests, is the “coordination and consolidation of local power into a new regional form of governance.” He writes:

 I’d suggest a Congress of Puget Sound, consisting of democratically elected representatives of municipalities, as a strong, common voice for the region while preserving local independence at the municipal level.

Rather than be divided through prolonged infighting and turf wars, why not create an institutional mechanism  for intra-local priority-setting and inter-local decision-making that can transform the metropolitan areas such as the  Puget Sound basin into much stronger strategic regions?

Okay. How’s this going to happen? Who takes the first step?

As I was saying…

Few believe that the Republicans will endorse any major initiative that Obama may propose tomorrow night. Indeed, Mitch McConnell said as much. The GOP rightly concludes that their chances of improving their numbers, including the cherished one in the Oval Office, depend inversely on the economy’s health. As the unemployment rate remains stubbornly high and as marginalized individuals go off the rails, next November’s electoral prospects loom large for Republicans. Never mind that they are chiefly responsible for the mess.

And I think they know that. It even occurred to me that Boehner, and McConnell, and Cantor actually understand Keynesian economics, or at least that point about massive government spending being the only way to recovery under these extraordinarily dismal conditions. In this cynical, albeit speculative, interpretation of conservative groupthink among congressional leaders, Boehner et al. will do all that they can to deny the American people what they so desperately need—which are mostly jobs and financial security.

Mike Lofgren used to drink this vile Kool-Aid, but he evidently lost his taste, having “left the cult.” From his former insider’s perspective, Lofgren lists three principal tenets of today’s GOP:

  1. The GOP cares solely and exclusively about its rich contributors.
  2. They worship at the altar of Mars.
  3. Give me that old time religion.

The first requires no explanation or elaboration. This has always described the Republican Party.

The second, of course, warrants clarification. Lofgren is talking about the Roman god of war. Republicans, he says, have never met a country they wouldn’t invade.

However, I think the worshiping here is bipartisan. The Democrats have had their share of presidential warmongers, among them: Wilson, Truman, Kennedy, Johnson, Carter, Clinton, and now Obama. Our own Henry M. Jackson is at the top of the congressional list. The Pentagon loves both political parties.

As to the last point, it’s become clear that the Republican Party needs the Christian fundamentalists. They are the most feral, loyal, and boisterous Americans. Moreover, along with the Tea Party, they are a most gullible collection—the GOP, with the able assistance of Fox News, Rush Limbaugh, and Matt Drudge, can tell these people anything and they’ll believe it. Global warming is a hoax. Old people don’t need Social Security or Medicare. The unemployed would prefer not to have a job. People of color are dumber than whites. Obama is a socialist. Unions are responsible for the recession. The list goes on.

How to make things worse

September 7, 2011 by davald

The Republicans, joined by far too many Democrats, seem quite willing and evidently able to throw the economy under a bus for political gain. I seriously doubt that they will support any initiative proposed by the president in his upcoming address to Congress.

But I also suspect that the Republicans, and far too many Democrats, truly believe that cutting government spending will lead to economic recovery. It’s an article of faith rather than reason.

We live in extraordinary times, which require extraordinary measures. Indeed, the necessary solution is completely antithetical to the dominant calls for fiscal austerity. No need to take my word for it.

A group of prominent economists—led by Paul Krugman but including Lawrence Summers, Christine Romer, Brad DeLong, Dean Baker, and fellow Nobel laureate Joseph Stiglitz—argue that present circumstances, which are marked by a Keynesian liquidity trap and depressed aggregate demand, require huge fiscal stimulus, far beyond what was tried by ARRA. They also argue that fiscal austerity will make matters much worse.

Now comes the United Nations with a new report (pdf) echoing the recommendations of Krugman et al. and warning of the dangers induced by spending constraints. Among them: a protracted recession with abnormally high unemployment rates on a global scale. The authors write:

After many years of calls for a reduced role of the State in economic management, many governments in both developed and emerging market economies launched large stabilization packages to restore aggregate demand, and intervened in the rescue of the financial sector. Before the crisis, expansionary fiscal policies were often considered ineffective, on the grounds that any increase in the public sector deficit would be compensated by a concomitant downward adjustment in private expenditure. But as the impact of monetary policy was limited during the crisis, the orthodox concern with balanced budgets or short-term fiscal targets came to be ignored, and governments were again viewed as “buyers and borrowers of last resort”.

However, recent developments in fiscal and monetary policy in many economies, and the recommendations of major international institutions such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), suggest that recognition of the need for fiscal stimulus during the crisis has not been followed up by a more profound rethinking of the principles of macroeconomic policy. In 2011, many governments have again reversed their policy orientation from one of fiscal expansion to fiscal tightening, and others are planning to do so. This is of particular concern since, in most developed economies that were severely hit by the financial crisis, the private sector has not yet completed the deleveraging process whereby non-financial agents try to reduce their indebtedness and banks try to restore their capital ratios. In such a debt-deflation process, even if monetary easing and low interest rates were to be continued, they could not be expected to have a major stimulating effect.

Those who support fiscal tightening argue that it is indispensable for restoring the confidence of financial markets, which is perceived as key to economic recovery. This is despite the almost universal recognition that the crisis was the result of financial market failure in the first place. It suggests that little has been learned about placing too much confidence in the judgement of financial market participants, including rating agencies, concerning the macroeconomic situation and the appropriateness of macroeconomic policies. In light of the irresponsible behaviour of many private financial market actors in the run-up to the crisis, and costly government intervention to prevent the collapse of the financial system, it is surprising that a large segment of public opinion and many policymakers are once again putting their trust in those same institutions to judge what constitutes correct macroeconomic management and sound public finances.

The report faults the burgeoning financial sector for causing much of the problems we face. It is therefore no surprise that much of the authors’ focus is on reforming that sector, which, they suggest, often falls prey to “herd behavior,” described ably by economist Hyman Minsky in his book Stabilizing an Unstable Economy.* The authors:

The fatal flaw in the functioning of financial markets lies in the fact that the most profitable activities are often derived from herd behaviour (i.e. following the trend for some time and disinvesting just before the rest of the crowd does). Acting against the majority, even if justified by accurate information about fundamentals, may result in large losses. Thus, whenever market participants “discover” that price trends in different markets provide an opportunity for “dynamic arbitrage” (i.e. investment in the probability of a continuation of the existing trend), and all bet on the same outcome, such as rising prices of real estate, equities or other assets, since the same information is available to all market participants, there is a strong tendency for herd behaviour. As a result, the herd acquires the market power to move those prices in the desired direction.

This is why prices in financial and “financialized” markets sometimes tend to overshoot, which gives rise to wrong prices for extended periods of time. As herding dominates the scene, no single participant questions whether the underlying information is correct or can be rationally related to events and developments in the real economy. This phenomenon has been observed not only in securities markets and markets for financial derivatives, but also in currency and commodity futures markets. Thus financial markets themselves have created most of the “fat tail” risks that have led to their collapse in financial crises. Uncertainty about the appropriate values of bank assets during such bubbles can become so high that no capital requirement or liquidity buffer can absorb the subsequent shock, so that governments have to step in with rescue packages.

Financial institutions are able to borrow money at essentially zero interest (Treasury bonds, for example) to buy foreign currencies with higher yields. Such arbitrage, the authors contend, makes a few people rich without contributing to productive economic activity. They write:

As the problem of mispricing is a systemic feature of financial markets, regulation should focus on the system, rather than on behaviour inside the system, with a view to ensuring that the system as a whole better serves real productive investment and growth in the real economy.

The report recommends recreating the separation between deposit banks and investment firms, which, in the U.S., were allowed to conjoin with repeal of the Glass-Steagall Act. The risks of speculation should be borne by investors and not backstopped by federal governments, as was the case in the United States.

As for fiscal stimulus, the authors write:

As current budget deficits and the stock of public debt have risen sharply in several countries, there is a widespread perception that the space for continued fiscal stimulus is already – or will soon be – exhausted, especially in developed countries. There is also a perception that in a number of countries debt ratios have reached, or are approaching, a level beyond which fiscal solvency is at risk.

However, fiscal space is a largely endogenous variable. A proactive fiscal policy will affect the fiscal balance by altering the macroeconomic situation through its impact on private sector incomes and the taxes perceived from those incomes. From a dynamic macroeconomic perspective, an appropriate expansionary fiscal policy can boost demand when private demand has been paralysed due to uncertainty about future income prospects and an unwillingness or inability on the part of private consumers and investors to incur debt.

In such a situation, a restrictive fiscal policy aimed at budget consolidation or reducing the public debt is unlikely to succeed, because a national economy does not function in the same way as an individual firm or household. The latter may be able to increase savings by cutting back spending because such a cutback does not affect its revenues. However, fiscal retrenchment, owing to its negative impact on aggregate demand and the tax base, will lead to lower fiscal revenues and therefore hamper fiscal consolidation. Since current expenditure can be difficult to adjust (because it is composed mainly of wages and entitlement programmes), fiscal retrenchment usually entails large cuts in public investment. This reduction in growth-promoting public expenditure may lead to a fall in the present value of future government revenues that is larger than the fiscal savings obtained by the retrenchment. The outcome could be an improvement in the immediate cash flow of the government, but with negative consequences for long-term fiscal and debt sustainability. Moreover, making balanced budgets or low public debt an end in itself can be detrimental to achieving other goals of economic policy, namely high employment and socially acceptable income distribution. [my italics]

Alas, I see little hope that our policy makers, including the president, will read, much less adopt the recommendations of this report. Thus, if the authors are correct, most of the world economy will descend further into a rather awful spiral, characterized by high unemployment and increasing revenue-constrained public debt.

Family-wage jobs

September 6, 2011 by davald

In his new book, End-of-Loser Liberalism: Making Markets Progressive, economist Dean Baker writes:

A conscious goal of trade agreements like the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) is to make it as easy as possible for U.S. manufacturers to relocate their operations to Mexico and other developing countries. This has the effect of putting U.S. manufacturing workers in direct competition with low-paid workers in the developing world. The predicted and actual result of this policy is to eliminate jobs in manufacturing in the U.S. and to put downward pressure on the wages of the workers still employed in the sector.

The association is clear, if not also the causality. Consider the following chart, which represents the difference between U.S. exports (positive) and imports to the U.S. (negative)—also known as the current account balance:

The North American Free Trade Agreement, or NAFTA, became effective January 1, 1994. Almost immediately, the U.S. current account balance fell sharply.
