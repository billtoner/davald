Do facts matter?

August 29, 2011 by davald

I’ve often thought it a liberal conceit that one need only present facts to prevail in arguments. Joe believes x, but is told that the truth is y. Therefore, Joe abandons x and embraces y. Unfortunately, that’s not the way people decide issues. Like George Bush’s infamous quote, it’s more a matter of “the gut.”

In his column this morning Paul Krugman scares us. He merely recounts some sobering poll results that show Republicans believing the darndest things. Among the statistics:

According to Public Policy Polling, only 21 percent of Republican voters in Iowa believe in global warming (and only 35 percent believe in evolution).

Republicans, as a rule, also believe that Obama is responsible for most of what ails us. Now, I have my truck with the president, as I’ve revealed here on numerous occasions, but I know—don’t I?—that Bush spent more and took a federal surplus into the toilet.

The folks at Truthout.org are being helpful. They provided me, and you, with three charts that show my view of the world is more accurate than the Republicans’.

Whose administration spent more federal dollars?

What about the deficit?

And as for jobs?

Before I get carried away with these comparisons, I don’t think it’s appropriate to either credit or blame a president for all events that happen on his watch. However, it is appropriate to take issue with those who practice “the blame game,” especially if they’re obviously unfamiliar with the facts—or, more likely, choose to ignore them.

It would be comforting, nonetheless, to know that Americans, by and large, decide on whom to vote for by consulting the more obvious truths. For example, it would be silly to elect one of the Republican candidates because one thought that the GOP was better at creating jobs. The last chart, certainly, should disabuse one of that notion.

Also, if you believed that innovation in science and technology would help spur economic growth you really shouldn’t vote for Michelle Bachman, who said that hurricanes were signs from God. Nor should you select Rick Perry, who dismisses both evolution and global warming.

That said, Perry, the dimmer version of G.W., is now leading all other GOP candidates by double digits. Who are these people, the Republicans?

Krugman concludes his column thusly:

Now, we don’t know who will win next year’s presidential election. But the odds are that one of these years the world’s greatest nation will find itself ruled by a party that is aggressively anti-science, indeed anti-knowledge. And, in a time of severe challenges — environmental, economic, and more — that’s a terrifying prospect.

But, hey, Americans elected G.W. not once but twice. Well, that’s what the Supreme Court said, anyway, in the first go around.

In times of economic gloom, too many people lose their heads. Unfortunately, there’s always a sufficiently large pool of professional candidates who are willing to exploit the madding crowd. After all, they’re one of them.

More on cities

August 26, 2011 by davald

James McCusker, who writes a weekly column for the Everett Herald, devoted today’s entry to “urbanization costs have taken a heavy toll,” the heading for his piece. Therein, McCusker, an economist living in Bothell, writes:

Ordinary people recognized that simply adding population didn’t translate into economic growth. Instead, most often it would translate into higher taxes.

The underlying cause of higher taxes also relates to something that the American people understood a lot better than their politicians: It costs more to live in the city.

This begs the question: Do increasingly denser urban centers yield lower or higher economic growth? I’d pose a related question: Are cities more or less efficient than either suburban or rural environs?

The September special issue of Scientific American is all about cities, most of it positive and optimistic. Physicists Luis Bettencourt and Geoffrey West authored one of the articles, entitled “Bigger Cities Do More with Less.” They write:

The numbers show that urban dwellers produce more inventions and create more opportunities for economic growth. Often large cities are also the greenest places on the planet because people living in denser habitats typically have smaller energy footprints, require less infrastructure and consume less of the world’s resources per capita. Compared with suburban or rural areas, cities do more with less. And the bigger cities get, the more productive and efficient they tend to become.

The authors concede that housing prices are higher in cities than elsewhere. But the overall benefits exceed aggregate costs.

In a city with high rents, only activities that add substantial value can be prof- itable. These economic pressures push urbanites to come up with new forms of organizations, products and services that carry more value added. In turn, higher profitability, excellence and choice tend to attract more talent to the city, pushing rents higher still, fueling the need to find yet more productive activities. This feedback mechanism, in a nutshell, is the principal reason cities accelerate innovation, while diversifying and intensifying social and economic activity.

Data suggest that a doubling of a city’s population increases economic output by 15 percent than if the same population were split in two. Size does matter.

Likewise, the larger and denser a city the lower the per capita environmental footprint. The reasons are fairly straightforward. Owning and driving a car in New York City is a burden rather than a necessity, as it is elsewhere. People walk or use public transportation or get purchased items delivered; and living in apartments requires less energy to heat and cool vis-a-vis a suburban house, where all walls are exposed to the elements.

Also, as a city’s population increases so do wages. However, we should not draw the conclusion that merely living in a city translates into a bigger paycheck. Rather, cities are more productive and innovative, thereby attracting more capable citizens, those with more education and, therefore, greater earning ability. (On a personal note, my wife and I could not afford to buy a house on Seattle’s Capitol Hill, but our lawyer daughter and her lawyer husband can and did.)

I wish to address a few gratuitous comments of McCusker. He writes:

The higher costs of urban societies are aggravated by the fact that most of these costs are the domain of government, a traditionally inefficient organization.

I don’t pretend to understand what McCusker means by this. Are rents and housing prices, the biggest component of urban living’s costs, the “domain of government.” Wouldn’t real estate value be the product of supply and demand rather than municipal government? The obvious attractiveness of Seattle combined with geographical constraints lead to higher rents and housing prices than in, say, Marysville, which has few amenities, little commercial activity, and automobile-dependent densities.

McCusker also writes:

Costs and productivity limitations are the reasons why we can’t buy way out of the fix we are in. To get out we must shift resources away from federal, state and local government and into manufacturing, mining, agriculture and petroleum industries so that we can regain some productivity growth.

The recessionary economy is doing its part to reduce the size of governments across the board, along with a ridiculous aversion to taxes. State, county, city, and school district governments have been slashing budgets right and left. Why? They’re losing revenues.

And how might “we” transfer resources to the private sector? Americans are among the least taxed citizens in the industrial world. If the money isn’t going to governments, where is it being spent? Well, McCusker has apparently failed to notice that nobody is spending money, which is the source of the economic problem. Consumers aren’t consuming because they’re either out of work or their wages have stagnated or they’ve lost the value in their homes, which had enabled continued buying during the housing bubble’s expansion. Firms aren’t spending money to expand their business because they have no confidence that people can or will buy their goods.

McCusker also fails to appreciate that labor productivity is up in almost every sector. Corporations are getting more and more output per worker, which helps explain why they’re not hiring. That said, it’s true that growth is mediocre and far below capacity in each of the industries he mentions. Again, that can be explained by a multitude of reasons, including a concerted effort to shift manufacturing off shore.

And what shall we mine? I suspect that McCusker is thinking of oil, coal, and natural gas. He evidently hasn’t noticed that the U.S. reached its peak oil production decades ago (it costs too much to extract relative to retail price). Nor has he noticed that we’re in the midst of a natural gas glut, with prices steadily falling. Further, McCusker appears to dismiss global warming either as science or threat. There’s one thing the planet needs less of and that’s more carbon-rich resources.

Finally, McCusker tosses out a non sequitur. He writes:

We will also need a new economic model—not a Keynesian hand-me-down…

What does Keynes have to do with anything he’s been discussing? Moreover, how could he with a straight face assert that “we” are living under a “Keynesian hand-me-down” economic model? No one in the White House, no Republican, and only a handful of Democrats espouse Keynesian doctrine. That’s too bad, since Keynes’s model explains our current economic problems, one defined by constrained aggregate demand and pervasive uncertainty (see Keynes’s chapter 12).

I infer from McCusker’s “hand-me-down” expression that he believes that all of Keynes can be summed up in government giveaways. My guess is that McCusker criticized the stimulus package, because it was both Keynesian and “hand-me-down.” It was neither. Real Keynsians understood that the economy needed a huge infusion of dollars, at least three times as much as the Recovery Act, which was inadequate in size and form—too much of it was tax cuts and not enough in public infrastructure.

So, does McCusker think that the economy will spontaneously recover? If so, what will be the trigger? What event or events will restore confidence and induce spending and hiring?

I’m listening, Mr. McCusker.

Want to save money? Raise the Medicare eligibility age.

August 25, 2011 by davald

Not.

The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities (CBPP) published a report on this very proposal, which we hear from the mouths of Republicans and Democrats alike. Why, we’re living longer, right? So we’ll simply push access to Medicare a few birthdays later. We’ll start at, say, two, then gradually up it to…what? A slippery slope, I suggest.

To be sure, the federal government could save a nickel or two, although that’s dubious, as I’ll discuss briefly below. But costs will surely shift to vulnerable citizens, those who are in poor health and without private medical coverage. They’ll have to wait a few more years to receive Medicare benefits. The CBPP writes:

The principal study of the effects of raising the Medicare eligibility age, by the Kaiser Family Foundation, estimates that its increased state and private-sector costs would be twice as large as the net federal savings.  If the proposal were fully in effect in 2014, Kaiser estimates, it would generate $5.7 billion in net federal savings but $11.4 billion in higher health care costs to individuals, employers, and states.

Let’s think a bit more on this.

Suppose you’ve been working at a blue-collar job, one that has required lots of physical labor. You finally retire at age 65. However, the thirty years on the job have taken their toll on your body. You’ve got a bad hip that requires replacement. Perhaps you’ve developed a heart condition. Whatever, you don’t get Medicare. You’ll have to wait until you’re 67 or maybe even older. Well, you don’t have any money for the necessary medical treatments. So you put off getting them. The months and years pass and you’re only getting worse. Now your condition has deteriorated to the extent that corrective procedures will be far more involved and much more costly than they would have been when you retired. Now you’re eligible for Medicare. You’ve become, because of the longer wait period, a greater financial liability. Your claims against Medicare are much higher because of the delays in getting treatment.

One of the compelling arguments for universal health care, or even “Medicare for all,” is that people can and will seek medical attention after initial symptoms. No need to put off seeing a doctor, because you don’t avoid out-of-pocket expenses. You’re covered. Period. Well, early prevention and treatment are less expensive in the main than letting conditions go untreated.

Consider a diabetic. If the person can’t afford regular physical examinations, his problem gets worse. He may have been able to effectively counter the diabetes through diet and exercise, provided he had an early diagnosis. Now that he’s forced to wait, because he lacks health insurance or cash, the diabetes requires insulin or, god forbid, amputation of an extremity. He’s much worse off, an invalid, and taxpayers get a much higher bill.

The rational approach to medical care is to cover everyone on an equal basis, from pre-birth to death. Receiving medical care should be a right rather than a privilege. It would certainly be cheaper for all of us.

It’s been a while since I visited Glenn Greenwald’s website, which I used to consult every day. Can’t tell you why, save for a personal need for relief from his passionate critiques of government misdeeds. But I happened to be browsing just a moment ago. Hey, what’s Glenn up to?

Former Bush VP Dick Cheney has written a memoir. You’ve seen the headlines wherein he not only declines to express regrets for water boarding, torture, mass imprisonments without charges, domestic surveillance—he seems to be rather proud of his behavior.

Greenwald reminds us of just what a bastard Cheney was and how he’s now able to amass a small fortune with the book and associated tour, he’s being resurrected as a very serious person people should listen to. Greenwald:

Less than three years ago, Dick Cheney was presiding over policies that left hundreds of thousands of innocent people dead from a war of aggression, constructed a worldwide torture regime, and spied on thousands of Americans without the warrants required by law, all of which resulted in his leaving office as one of the most reviled political figures in decades. But thanks to the decision to block all legal investigations into his chronic criminality, those matters have been relegated to mere pedestrian partisan disputes, and Cheney is thus now preparing to be feted — and further enriched — as a Wise and Serious Statesman with the release of his memoirs this week: one in which he proudly boasts (yet again) of the very crimes for which he was immunized.

While the Obama administration has refused to revisit prior bad acts, saying that it and the nation must “move forward,” Cheney is now free to spout and prance. Not so for a victim of Cheney’s crimes, who has been barred from receiving money for a book he wrote chronicling his mistreatment at Guantanamo. He committed no crime and was not charged. But what the heck, off to prison anyway. Greenwald:

The U.S. Government loves to demand that other countries hold their political leaders accountable for serious crimes, dispensing lectures on the imperatives of the rule of law. Numerous states bar ordinary convicts from profiting from their crimes with books. David Hicks, an Australian citizen imprisoned without charges for six years at Cheney’s Guantanamo, just had $10,000 seized by the Australian government in revenue from his book about his time in that prison camp on the ground that he is barred from profiting from his uncharged, unproven crimes.

Makes you want to stand tall and salute, doesn’t it?

Breaking the arc of history

August 7, 2011 by davald

We are in the summer of our discontents. Those of us, that is, who are either intellectually struggling with the mess we’re in, how we got here, and, most important, how we might extricate ourselves—or are physically and emotionally struggling with how to put food on our table, a roof over our heads, and a job to give us income and dignity. If any time cried out for a strong, significant leader it is now, with a sputtering, jobless economy, housing foreclosures, declining real wages, two costly wars, and a revanchist, greedy Wall Street.

But we seem to have elected the wrong person to the White House. Instead of a Roosevelt we got a, well, Great Capitulator or, in Krugman’s words, “President Pushover.” Whereas Teddy Roosevelt took on the powerful trusts and his cousin FDR welcomed the concerted hatred of the monied interests, Barack Obama gleefully takes their cash while throwing the rest of us elegant rhetoric that we can neither eat nor spend.

Many of us have taken to wonder who is this guy Obama. Were we misled? Is he well-meaning but woefully inadequate to the task? Does he know how to do only one thing well—speak nice words? We’re told that he’s bright by those closer to him. But I’ve seen no signs of wisdom, no indications that he is in any sense walking among us or feeling our pain. For me he remains aloof, disconnected, bewildered, overmatched.

The more charitable among us cling to the belief that Obama always meant well and that his words were substantive. Yet in his brief reign he’s somehow lost his way.

One such person is Emory University professor Drew Westen. In several pages of the New York Times, Prof. Westen ponders what might have been.

He was there for the inauguration of Barack Obama—with his eight-year-old daughter. He says that even then, as he listened to Obama’s words, “he had a feeling of unease,” as if he were witnessing a harbinger of darker days to come. Westen would have preferred a different narrative on that august January. Something like this:

“I know you’re scared and angry. Many of you have lost your jobs, your homes, your hope. This was a disaster, but it was not a natural disaster. It was made by Wall Street gamblers who speculated with your lives and futures. It was made by conservative extremists who told us that if we just eliminated regulations and rewarded greed and recklessness, it would all work out. But it didn’t work out. And it didn’t work out 80 years ago, when the same people sold our grandparents the same bill of goods, with the same results. But we learned something from our grandparents about how to fix it, and we will draw on their wisdom. We will restore business confidence the old-fashioned way: by putting money back in the pockets of working Americans by putting them back to work, and by restoring integrity to our financial markets and demanding it of those who want to run them. I can’t promise that we won’t make mistakes along the way. But I can promise you that they will be honest mistakes, and that your government has your back again.”

Of course, that’s not what Westen or any of us heard. Yes, we got elegance, nearly a given when Obama speaks formally. But absolutely no leadership, no hint that this new president understood the challenges that lay ahead or what he could and should do to address the huge problems facing this nation.

Obama talked then and later before Congress of a three-point agenda: health care reform, the environment, and energy. He only talked, leaving the “details” to a legislature that would eventually be stymied by a recalcitrant brand of “young guns,” dedicated to ensuring that Obama served but one term and dismantling both government and the public sector. As a result of his evident hands-off approach, the crazies seized the opportunity to undermine his entire agenda, demonizing him in the process.

Westen reminds us of what FDR did:

In similar circumstances, Franklin D. Roosevelt offered Americans a promise to use the power of his office to make their lives better and to keep trying until he got it right. Beginning in his first inaugural address, and in the fireside chats that followed, he explained how the crash had happened, and he minced no words about those who had caused it. He promised to do something no president had done before: to use the resources of the United States to put Americans directly to work, building the infrastructure we still rely on today. He swore to keep the people who had caused the crisis out of the halls of power, and he made good on that promise. In a 1936 speech at Madison Square Garden, he thundered, “Never before in all our history have these forces been so united against one candidate as they stand today. They are unanimous in their hate for me — and I welcome their hatred.”

Obama doesn’t welcome. He cowers and capitulates—or so it seems.

Westen is as confused by Obama as the rest of us. He writes:

Like most Americans, at this point, I have no idea what Barack Obama — and by extension the party he leads — believes on virtually any issue. The president tells us he prefers a “balanced” approach to deficit reduction, one that weds “revenue enhancements” (a weak way of describing popular taxes on the rich and big corporations that are evading them) with “entitlement cuts” (an equally poor choice of words that implies that people who’ve worked their whole lives are looking for handouts). But the law he just signed includes only the cuts. This pattern of presenting inconsistent positions with no apparent recognition of their incoherence is another hallmark of this president’s storytelling. He announces in a speech on energy and climate change that we need to expand offshore oil drilling and coal production — two methods of obtaining fuels that contribute to the extreme weather Americans are now seeing. He supports a health care law that will use Medicaid to insure about 15 million more Americans and then endorses a budget plan that, through cuts to state budgets, will most likely decimate Medicaid and other essential programs for children, senior citizens and people who are vulnerable by virtue of disabilities or an economy that is getting weaker by the day. He gives a major speech on immigration reform after deporting a million immigrants in two years, breaking up families at a pace George W. Bush could never rival in all his years as president.

With respect to divining the real Obama, I find myself sympathetic to Westen’s somber speculation.

A somewhat less charitable explanation is that we are a nation that is being held hostage not just by an extremist Republican Party but also by a president who either does not know what he believes or is willing to take whatever position he thinks will lead to his re-election. Perhaps those of us who were so enthralled with the magnificent story he told in “Dreams From My Father” appended a chapter at the end that wasn’t there — the chapter in which he resolves his identity and comes to know who he is and what he believes in.

Or perhaps, like so many politicians who come to Washington, he has already been consciously or unconsciously corrupted by a system that tests the souls even of people of tremendous integrity, by forcing them to dial for dollars — in the case of the modern presidency, for hundreds of millions of dollars. When he wants to be, the president is a brilliant and moving speaker, but his stories virtually always lack one element: the villain who caused the problem, who is always left out, described in impersonal terms, or described in passive voice, as if the cause of others’ misery has no agency and hence no culpability. Whether that reflects his aversion to conflict, an aversion to conflict with potential campaign donors that today cripples both parties’ ability to govern and threatens our democracy, or both, is unclear.

Aside from the increasing chances of another recession, I fear this outcome in 2012, aided and abetted by an inept president unwilling to confront evil and rally the rest of us in the manner of a Roosevelt: a total “shellacking” by the feral Republicans who then assume control of everything. That will be hell on earth.

Lessons from the 50s

August 4, 2011 by davald

The stock markets around the world tanked today, with the Dow Jones shedding over 500 points, the biggest one-day drop since 2008, reports the NY Times. As to why, the consensus seems to be: spiraling problems in the Eurozone and the US economy’s continued sputtering. Few commenters site the growing American debt, but that hasn’t stopped the Republicans from pushing a constitutional amendment to force balanced budgets.

My weekly lunch companions believe that there is nothing that can be done and that things will get darker before they go completely black. Nevertheless, people and policies are being blamed for both allowing the decline and failing to take corrective action.

For the GOP, Target Number One is Barack Obama, who turned 50 today, but looks as if he’s approaching his golden years already—the toll taken by those who sit in the Oval Office. Though hardly a Republican and only a nominal Democrat, I, too, fault Obama, as I’ve done in numerous posts. It’s been said that one shouldn’t negotiate with terrorists, as doing so only bolsters them by bestowing an undeserved status. Insofar as the Republicans are willing to throw the economy and the rest of us under a bus, I’d treat them as terrorists. Obama consistently seeks compromise with these crazy folks expecting, somehow, a different outcome. But I’m afraid that he’s only emboldened them. How far right would they go? It’s almost as if Obama is anxious to discover the answer.

I’ve heard it said that the problems we now face are the culmination of three decades’ worth of attempting to dampen the swings of inevitable business cycles. Such meddling has made matters worse, although I don’t pretend to know how.

Former advisor to Obama, Christine Romer, has made this point in a 2007 paper (pdf). She writes:

…the revolution of the 1960s was a revolution in economic ideas. The model that policymakers used to understand the economy changed in dramatic ways. This led them to make radically different policy recommendations.

The resulting policy outcomes had two effects on the economy. One was to generate inflation and short-run instability. The second was to end the historical norm of long-run budget balance. The 1960s introduced Americans to the phenomenon of persistent peacetime deficits.

In my previous post, I suggested that the federal government spend more money on those things the private sector either can’t or won’t. We can think of transportation, education, and social welfare. But, and it’s a big but, I also recommended that tax-revenues rise in step. Here’s Romer:

Another manifestation of the 1950s belief in fiscal prudence is the prevalence of “spending-driven” tax changes.

She cites three examples: the Korean War under Truman, and both Social Security and the federal highway program, under Eishenhower. In each of these cases, Congress raised taxes to pay for these government outlays.

The 50s gave way to the irresponsible decades of the 60s and up to the present, a period marked by greater deficit spending and a rising national debt. I included the following chart in a previous post. It shows the marginal tax rate against GDP annual changes.

We can see that the economy was growing in a steady fashion in the 50s, 60s, and 70s. We’ll also note that the marginal tax rate was above 90 percent until the Kennedy administration pushed for a significant reduction, because it believed that the economy needed stimulation. Reagan proposed even more cuts in the tax rate, and Congress obliged, dropping the rate down to 30 percent, until G.H.W. Bush neglected to read his own lips. During the Clinton era, the tax rate rose to 40 percent, only to drop down to 35 percent under Bush II. The economy contracted during this prolonged tax-reduction period. Coincidence? Causally related?

At any rate, the gap between spending and revenues increased at the same time. The chart below underscores Romer’s point.

Actually, the divergence between outlays and receipts began under Nixon and were greatest under Reagan/Bush I. We’ll also note that the deficit was not caused primarily by increased spending, which began to decline under Reagan. The following chart shows federal outlays as a percentage of GDP:

Here’s another chart, this one showing nominal outlays and receipts since 1950. The same pattern emerges. From 1950 to the mid-1970s the budget was balanced.

That growing gap looks like this on an annual budget level.

But the deficits accumulate.

What strikes me about these graphs is that spending wasn’t the problem; lack of tax revenue was. But addressing spending for a moment, surely our private health care system coupled with our propensity to engage in wars must be addressed. Unlike Truman and Eisenhowever, the federal government since Reagan did not “pay as you go.” It chose to reduce taxes on the rich and borrow the rest.

Welcome to the Great Recession redux. The problems are much worse than most think. Not only is the economy in a mess, but we neglected our public infrastructure for far too long—and we have no money to fix bridges, build trains, clean the air, or educate our children. A real tragedy.

Moreover, the time to seek a balanced budget is not now, not with the economy in such dire straits. The time would have been during Clinton’s last years or whenever the economy recovers. When’s that going to happen?

Hang on, folks.

Barfing on Monday

August 1, 2011 by davald

Congressional leaders say they’ve struck a deal to raise the debt ceiling. Unfortunately for the rest of us, the agreement calls for major cuts in safety-net programs while hardly touching the revenue side of the federal budget. The rich will continue to get richer; the poor, poorer. Just as the selfish Republicans demand.

“Our” guy in the White House once again demonstrated conviction and leadership. Yes, I am being sarcastic.

Professor Krugman’s reaction here. He writes:

For the deal itself, given the available information, is a disaster, and not just for President Obama and his party. It will damage an already depressed economy; it will probably make America’s long-run deficit problem worse, not better; and most important, by demonstrating that raw extortion works and carries no political cost, it will take America a long way down the road to banana-republic status.

Economist Dean Baker briefly explains the significance of the deal here. He writes:

The government is projected to spend $7.8 trillion on the military over the next decade. If this area is largely protected, then most of the cuts would likely come from the $6.7 trillion of spending on the domestic discretionary portion of the budget. This is the portion that includes spending on infrastructure, education, research, and other areas that are considered investment.

Berkeley’s Brad DeLong writes:

It is a loss for the American people: it is not the way that they want to reduce the long-term deficit. It is certainly not a win for bipartisan compromise.

And it is not a win for the economy.

UPDATE 2 (Aug. 1, 2011):

Elizabeth Drew’s piece in the upcoming NY Review of Books reveals the behind-the-scenes machinations and emerging political philosophy of Obama. She writes:

The speech Obama gave on April 13 marked his conversion to fiscal centrism; to being the fiscally responsible Democrat. In that speech he stated that he wanted to reduce the debt by $4 trillion—thus aligning himself with the Republicans—but also asked for revenues to partly offset that reduction. This all fits with another development in the Obama White House. According to another close observer, David Plouffe, the manager of Obama’s 2008 presidential campaign, who officially joined the White House staff in January 2011, has taken over. “Everything is about the reelect,” this observer says—”where the President goes, what he does.”

Plouffe’s advice to the President defines not just Obama’s policies but also his behavior. Plouffe tells the President, according to this observer, that the target group wants him to seem the most reasonable man in the room. Plouffe is the conceptualizer, and Bill Daley, the chief of staff who shares Plouffe’s political outlook, makes things happen; Gene Sperling, the director of economic policy, and Tom Donilon, the national security adviser, are smart men but they come out of politics rather than academia or deep experience in their respective fields. Once Austan Goolsbee, chairman of the Council of Economic Advisers, departs later this summer, all of the President’s original economic advisers will be gone. Partly this is because the President’s emphasis on budget cutting didn’t leave them very much to do. One White House émigré told me, “It’s not a place that welcomes ideas.”

Deeply troubling, to be sure, but should have been expected. Obama, after all, is first and foremost a politician. Forget all that inspiring campaign rhetoric. Those were easy words to gain your vote. Once in office, however, Obama is jilting his suitors, one cut at a time.

The president’s lawyers

July 26, 2011 by davald

I found a note of irony regarding President Obama’s use of legal counsel. It seems that with respect to engaging US troops and military apparatus in Libya, Obama rejected his lawyers’ advice. Yet, when on the verge of a possible economic meltdown as a result of government default, Obama takes cover behind his lawyers’ skirts and trousers. After all, former president Clinton urged Obama to invoke the 14th Amendment to unilaterally raise the debt ceiling, but Obama claimed that his lawyers wouldn’t let him.

So, the scorecard reads: off to another war, yes; avert a government default, no. Who’s playing politics here?

In his blog this morning, Paul Krugman writes:

I hope and pray that Obama’s lawyers discover that the 14th amendment solution is valid after all. Because otherwise we’ll be looking at very dire things, even if the markets stay calm.

Valid, smalid. If it’s convenient, take the lawyers’ counsel—or not.
