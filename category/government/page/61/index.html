Urban green

July 15, 2012 by davald

I’ve been to New York City once. That was back in 1992 during the Democratic National Convention, which was held at Madison Square Garden. My daughter, then 15, seconded Jerry Brown’s nomination for president, several hours before prime time, as I recall. During that week I had occasion to walk, walk, and walk some more.

While my daughter was ensconced with my wife in a NYU dorm room for the week, my son and I had opportunities to explore Manhattan. We spent the bulk of our time in Central Park, but occasionally ventured southward into the bowels of skyscraper-city. What struck me most about the park was the greenery. Here amidst the concrete was a splendid oasis of water and trees and ballfields and amphitheaters and a zoo. We saw it all and enjoyed every moment.

In most cities, however, green is not to be found amidst the gray and asphalt. I’ve mentioned previously that my most recent residence sits in downtown Everett, which, save for the trees that align the streets, is devoid of green open space. There is no city park, no central location for relief.

Frank Bruni, the splendid writer for the New York Times, devotes this piece to the verdant renaissance of New York City, which has been his home for the better part of a generation. The municipal administration, via deliberate policy, has transformed urban real estate into beautiful natural refuges and visual pleasures. Bruni:

This city looks nothing — nothing — like it did just a decade and a half ago. It’s a place of newly gorgeous waterfront promenades, of trees, tall grasses and blooming flowers on patches of land and peninsulas of concrete and even stretches of rail tracks that were blighted or blank before. It’s a lush retort to the pessimism of this era, verdant proof that growth remains possible, at least with the requisite will and the right strategies.

If NYC can lead the way will Everett and other cities follow?

One quibble:

One of the principal legacies of his long mayoralty will be a city that, in certain charmed spots on certain charmed days, can feel as relaxed and breezy and kissed by nature as one of those ecologically vain enclaves of the Pacific Northwest.

“Ecologically vain”? Hey, we’re doing our best to destroy that image. Just give us some time.

More on taxes

July 14, 2012 by davald

The Tax Policy Center has released a study of Mr. Romney’s tax proposal. Ezra Klein, writing for the Washington Post, provides a chart comparing tax rates between Obama and Romney. Here ’tis:

Ladies and gentlemen, please understand that Mr. Romney intends to lower taxes even more on the wealthy, who already enjoy the lowest tax rates in over 80 years, and raise taxes on the poorest fifth of Americans. Nevertheless, you may like that he would toss most of us a bone, those in the next few quintiles. Here’s a chart based on Tax Policy Center data showing mean, or average, income by quintile (2009):

Those of us who have even a passing acquaintance with the Occupy Wall Street movement know about the One-percenters and the 99-percenters. The former have most of the money; the latter not so much.

On these pages I have often mentioned Emmanuel Saez, a professor of economics at UC Berkeley. He studies income inequality, and has become quite the expert on the topic. I will go out on a very short limb here to predict that he will eventually win the Nobel prize in economics. Like Paul Krugman before him, he’s already won the John Bates Clark Medal, awarded to the top economist under the age of 40. That was in 2009. Two years ago he received a MacArthur Foundation award. Saez spends a lot of his time poring over income data from multiple sources.

I want to show you a couple of charts based on his work. The first shows median income (2010 dollars) of what Saez calls ‘fractiles.’

That first column to the left shows the average income of the bottom 90 percent. In 2010 dollars it was only $30K. More on this segment in a bit. You’ll see that the average income rises as we move up the “fractiles.” Those in the bottom portion of the top 10 percent made an average of $126 thousand in 2010. The next four percent, up to the top one percent, made an average of $205.5K.

Let’s pause to think about this for a moment. We’re already nudging the top one percent of household incomes (figures include capital gains income, by the way) and yet we’re still a long way from the millionaires. Indeed, as we can see as we move right along the chart, even the top .04 percent don’t average a million dollars in income. It’s only when we inch next to the top .01 percent that we see average incomes above a million, and here they’re $2.8 million.

Before I slice these fractiles even smaller, a word about that bottom 90 percent. Here’s a disturbing chart, again based on Saez data:

Yep. The average income for the bottom 90 percent in real dollars has remained essentially flat all the way back to 1965, the year I graduated high school. You may find this interesting, or not: the highest average income for this group, which is most of us, after all, was a bit over $34,683 in 2000, the last year of Clinton’s presidency. Just before the economy dropped into the toilet in 2007, the income level for this class had edged back up to $34K. It’s fallen by 12.5 percent since. (I don’t have 2011 or 2012 data.)

As for the very rich, things are much, much different.

The top 10 percent are still making modest salaries, certainly as compared with professional athletes, for example. Even the top five percent average just (?) $150K. The One-percenters pulled down $352K on average. When we skip to the top 0.01 percent, the figure skyrockets—all the way up to an average income of $7.9 million.

How big is that, you might ask? Well, it’s about 265 times the average income of the bottom 90 percent!

Back to taxes before we end.

Romney proposes to reduce the taxes of the very wealthy. The top 0.1 percent would see a reduction of 8.7 percent in federal taxes. Let’s suppose that, like Romney, these very rich people pay an effective tax rate of 15 percent. If you’re fortunate enough to be in that bracket, you would have made roughly $1.5 million before taxes. At 15 percent, your tax liability would be $225,000. Should Romney’s proposal prevail, then you would save about $20K.

Remember, the average income of the bottom 90 percent is a bit less than $30,000. Thus, Romney would give the top 0.1 percent “relief” equal to two-thirds the income of 90 percent of American workers.

If we dare jump up to the 0.01 percent, and I doubt that anyone reading this is in that bracket, we’d find that they pay an average federal tax of $1.2 million at a 15 percent effective rate. Should they receive the same 8.7 percent reduction in taxes, they’d save more than $100 thousand, or over three times the average salary of the bottom 90 percent.

So, I found it most disturbing that in the paper this morning a recent poll showed a majority favoring an extension of the Bush tax cuts even on the very wealthy! Here’s McClatchy News:

Young voters ages 18-29 favored tax cuts for everyone by a margin of 69-29, the largest margin of any age group.

Latinos favored tax cuts for all incomes by 62 percent to 36 percent. Whites supported tax cuts for every income by 50 percent to 44 percent. African-Americans split, 48 percent for limiting the tax cuts to incomes below $250,000 and 47 percent for extending them to all incomes.

And those making less than $50,000 supported tax cuts for all incomes by 53 percent to 41 percent.

Huh? A couple of final comments.

Do our youth have their collective ass stuck up their smartphones? Or is this the clearest sign of the massive failure of our public education system? Perhaps both.

Last time I checked, the rich are predominantly white, by choice, I should imagine. There aren’t too many Latinos and African Americans in the upper strata. Yet the former favored giving the rich even more money by a whopping margin. Blacks, as it turns out, were the least supportive of the racial groups in further coddling the wealthy.

Only in America.

Economic conversation

July 13, 2012 by davald

Most Thursdays you’ll find me at a local restaurant for lunch. Opposite me sits an economist and a lawyer. No, this is not the start of a joke. While our topics are wide-ranging, from evolution and genetics to politics and philosophy, the bulk of the give-and-take involves the economy. And why not? The economy is front and center for obvious reasons.

Yesterday we got around to discussing two questions:

  1. Should the government have rescued the banks?
  2. What can the government do to stimulate the economy and, if it can, should it? Our answers varied.

The economist, first off, believes that the government should not have intervened following the collapses of the housing bubble and Lehman Brothers. He offered two reasons: First, the banks under duress, those principally responsible for the housing bubble, should not have been bailed out; they took risks, so they—and they alone—should have suffered the consequences. Second, the economy would likely have shrunk without a bailout, and the downturn may have been more pronounced, but it may have recovered more quickly.

I responded by referencing the HBO movie Too Big to Fail. I previously wrote about it here—and from that post:

There is plenty of drama in this HBO production, none of it contrived. As Treasury Secretary Hank Paulson, Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke, and New York Fed chair, Timothy Geithner, gradually realized the enormity of the problem, desperation set in, along with countless sleepless nights. They first tried to persuade the tottering financial firms to fix their own mess by buying out Lehman Brothers, whose CEO, Dick Fuld, went from cocky to terrified in a matter of days if not hours. Citi, Goldman Sachs, et al. refused the deal. Paulson, Bernanke, and Geithner had only one option left: persuade Congress to rescue the banks, an action they all loathed. But it was absolutely necessary to immediately inject billions of dollars into these companies to avoid global catastrophe.

We might gain some sense of the scale of the global challenge by looking at the value of outstanding derivatives. According to Wikipedia, that figure stood at $220 trillion at the end of 2004. By the end of 2007, it had reached almost $600 trillion. Think about that for a moment. The U.S. GDP is around $15 trillion. GDP for the entire world totals $62 trillion. So, the value of derivatives is ten times as much as global GDP! Paulson et al. eventually realized that they were on the edge of a financial cliff. If these financial institutions, especially A.I.G., which had insured many of the derivatives, including those built on mortgage-based securities, declared bankruptcy, as they surely would have without T.A.R.P., the planet’s economies collapse.

Of course, I’m not privy to the negotiations or the numbers dramatized in Too Big to Fail. I do recall Princeton economist Paul Krugman appearing on PBS’s News Hour during the early days of the crisis. He did not like the idea of bailing out the banks but thought it absolutely necessary to avert a complete global meltdown. No doubt he had in mind the value of those derivatives.

Returning to my previous post, I wrote:

Recall the scene from It’s a Wonderful Life when Jimmy Stewart’s character rushes into the lobby of the Building and Loan to assuage the fears of his depositors. They were there to withdraw their money. Paulson et al. envisaged a global run on the banks as depositors lost confidence in the financial institutions.

We can imagine a counterfactual, that the governments of the U.S. and Europe stood on the sideline, refusing to give in to moral hazard—heads the banks win, tails we lose. Suppose this attitude had prevailed in the 1930s.

Following the stock market crash of 1929, the world’s economies suddenly plummeted. Roosevelt expanded the monetary base. While that helped, the economy was still a long way off from full recovery. It took the mother of all fiscal stimuli—World War II—to finally push the economy into robust recovery. The chart below, from Christine Romer’s 1992 paper on the post-crash economy, illustrates the extraordinary plunge in real GDP. The pre-war gains, Romer argues, were mostly the result of monetary expansion.

The U.S. and other economies are many times larger today in both nominal and real dollars. There was much more to lose in 2008 than in the 1930s.

Here, then, I rely on those with greater insight and understanding of the enormity of the challenge presented by the twin collapses. If the value of derivatives played a role in concentrating concern, then I cannot fault the logic of Paulson et al. Something had to be done and done very quickly. A bailout, however distasteful, was deemed necessary by those in a position to know the stakes involved.

What about the role of government in stimulating the economy? The economist, again, argues against such a role. Why? Because he believes the effects are only temporary and, moreover, will lead to inflation.

In Europe, the peripheral economies continue to feel the effects of the Great Recession. There has been little fiscal stimulus. Rather, led by Germany, the core nations are demanding the opposite—austerity—in exchange for monetary rescue. It’s not working. Europe is likely to fall into another recession. Meanwhile, unemployment rates, especially in Spain, Greece, and Italy, remain stubbornly high. Among Spanish youth, the figure is 50 percent.

As for the United States, the Federal Reserve’s tripling of the monetary basis has neither increased inflation nor raised interest rates. Nor has it had much effect on the economy. Firms continue to hoard cash rather than invest in productive activities. Krugman et al. cite a decline in aggregate demand: people lack the money to spend on goods and services—at least in sufficient amounts to spur hiring and expansion.

As I mentioned above, we do seem to have an historical record of government stimulus working, namely the huge military buildup, beginning in 1939-40 (the U.S. had already stepped up defense spending to aid the British and other eventual allies in their initial challenges from the axis powers).

We can see this blip in the following chart:

The economist argued that any stimulus must be permanent rather than temporary. Why? Because individuals and firms will adjust to the increased government spending by assuming that taxes will have to be raised to pay for the higher expenditures.

But, again, we see in the above chart that federal spending fell as sharply and as quickly as it rose in the WWII era. Spending increased again in the early 50s to fund the Korean War. (We’ll also note that despite the “guns and butter” programs of LBJ, federal spending as a percentage of GDP fell during his administration. Indeed, since the end of the Korean War, spending-vs-GDP has fallen almost every year, which calls into question the GOP claims of runaway government spending.)

In his blog yesterday Paul Krugman reminds us of John Maynard Keynes’s diagnosis of the 1930s economies. Here’s Krugman:

Consider the extended version of the “magneto trouble” metaphor I use in my recent book. Keynes argued that the Great Depression could be thought of as a failure in the car’s electrical system; so let’s think of it as a situation where your car won’t run because it has a dead battery — that is, you could get it running again with a fairly trivial and easy intervention: just buy a new battery, which costs only a tiny fraction of the expense of a new car.

Following the crash of 1929 the national unemployment rate ranged from 15 to 25 percent. It fell to 4.6 percent in 1942 and 1.2 percent in 1944, a year before WWII’s end. It remained below seven percent until 1974 (Bureau of Labor Statistics).

Did the U.S. raise taxes after the fact to pay for the war-time stimulus? Here’s a chart showing the marginal tax rate from 1929 to 1974:

In 1944 and 1945, the marginal tax rate was 94 percent. It fell in 1946 through 1949, reaching a low of 82.1 percent in 1948 and 1949. In 1950, Congress bumped it up to 91 percent, where it remained until 1964, when it fell to 77 percent. Despite the Vietnam War and his Great Society domestic programs, LBJ’s Congress reduced the marginal rate to 7o percent from 1965 to 1967.

We can conclude that the marginal tax rate was not increased in the post-war era following military Keynesianism. If “rational” economic players modified their financial decision in anticipation of higher tax rates, they did so in error.

If the history of the post-Great Depression era can serve as our guide, then, yes, a massive infusion of federal dollars will right the economic ship. But should the government do so?

The economist first began with a brief example. Suppose that a high school baseball phenom is drafted. He signs then begins his career in the minor leagues. We’ll assume that he advances a couple of levels. But when he reaches AAA his skills prove inadequate to the challenge. The major league club that drafted and signed him let’s him go. Now what? His only job has been playing baseball.

Is the government responsible for putting this man to work in something else? If we answer no, then why should the government assume responsibility for the 12 million or so who are out of work and want to? If not responsible for the one, then not responsible for the many.

I didn’t get a chance to respond to this before time ran out. But had I the opportunity I might have argued as follows.

We can assume that a single individual losing a job, though a hardship certainly to that person, can hardly be measured in terms of total economic output. If ten people lose their jobs, same thing. We can go on, adding first a hundred, then a thousand. Still there is no significant impact to the economy, though these jobless personally suffer.

At the end of 2011 there were about 109.3 million people in the private labor force. Another 22 million worked in government at all levels. That’s over 130 million men and women in jobs. If a million people are given their pink slips, then the unemployment rate is less than one percent. Even as many as five million unemployed is about four percent, which happens to be a level widely considered to reflect full employment.

In June of this year over 13 million Americans were out of the work force. Yes, most of these people are going to be miserable. But I would suggest, and others have too, that this many people out of work for extended periods is also bad for the Rest of Us. They are not making any money, save for time-limited unemployment benefits or whatever savings they may be able to draw from. So, they’re not paying taxes. Worse, they’re not spending money as they use to. And that’s a real problem.

It’s a problem because when people don’t spend money, as I mentioned above, firms don’t hire and capitalists don’t invest in productive activities. Why expand operations if there aren’t enough people to buy the goods and services?

Thus, the government and the economy suffer. The former from diminished tax receipts; the latter from lower-than-desired growth.

Did either the recent monetary expansion or Obama’s stimulus package boost inflation, as the economist warned? Let’s look at a chart from the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

The stimulus package was passed in 2009, though forty percent was in the form of tax cuts, not the best way to induce spending. The annual change in both GDP and CPI fell into negative territory from 2008 to 2009. Also, keep in mind that we’ve got a $15 trillion-a-year economy and that A.R.R.A. was about $800 billion, give or take, or just five percent of GDP. Inflation, as we see above, moved upwards, peaking at just over three percent in 2011. Since last year the inflation rate has fallen to 1.4 percent, a very low number, indeed, by historical standards.

I should end with another quick point about the 1930s in the context of inflation. Christine Romer included the following chart in her 1992 paper:

Note the fluctuations after Roosevelt assumed office. First we see a dramatic increase, up to 12 percent above normal, then a sharp fall. That happened in the beginning of Roosevelt’s second term, when he unwisely listened to that era’s austerians. When GDP fell, he resumed monetary expansion, as the chart shows. What happened to inflation?

Again, very low inflation, which never exceeded four percent.

When WWII broke out the Roosevelt administration, understandably, boosted spending on the military. The spending stoked manufacturing production, which led to massive employment increases, including, for the first time, a major rise in female workers. What happened to inflation, given the military stimulus?

There was a confounding variable in all this. In August of 1941 Roosevelt created the Office of Price Administration, which had broad authority to control prices. Its effects were most acutely realized between 1942 and 1945.  The OPA was disbanded in 1947. The high inflation that year no doubt reflected pent-up demand and industrial overhang. By 1949, the inflation rate reached negative numbers. Between the end of the Korean War and 1968 inflation never exceeded four percent.

Now, I hasten to add that in periods of “full” employment and high capacity utilization, increasing the money supply or a rise in government spending can be inflationary. Neither have boosted prices or wages since 2008 because the unemployment rate is high and capacity utilization is low.

Krugman and others argue that the economy is up against a zero lower bound. As such, expanding the money supply or fiscal stimulus will not cause prices and wages to rise—ceteris paribus. So far, he’s been right and others, like the economist, have been wrong.

Have a nice weekend.

Suppose we could vote FOR

July 12, 2012 by davald

Our electoral system makes it nearly impossible for a third party to win an election. In winner-take-all contests, simple game theory favors two parties only. It’s much easier for third parties to gain a foothold in a proportional representation system. The history of U.S. elections bears this out.

In the upcoming presidential election we have a choice between two, and only, two viable candidates. Some of us will enthusiastically cast ballots for one or the other. Others will hold their nose and vote the lesser of two presumed evils. Others still, and an increasing percentage of the electorate, will cast no ballots at all—in effect, saying “none of the above.”

This process stinks, but I don’t see it changing any time soon, if ever, despite the fact that our system is unique among the western democracies, all of which have parliaments and most of which enjoy proportional representation.

So, once again, a third party candidate emerges for the November ballots. Her name is Jill Stein, a physician by education and training. She’s running on the Green Party ticket. And she stands absolutely no chance of winning the White House. Nevertheless, I like her.

The New York Times gives her some coverage today. At the moment, she is on 21 state ballots and expects to secure a place on 22 more by this November. I like what she says. The Times:

Dr. Stein says she emphasizes issues like ecological sustainability, racial and gender equality, and economic justice. The centerpiece of her platform is a so-called Green New Deal, a twist on the Roosevelt-era programs intended to stimulate job growth and the depressed economy. It could be paid for by ending the presence of American troops in Iraq and Afghanistan, the campaign says, and by eliminating waste in the health care system. Beyond that, Dr. Stein favors a progressive income tax that would raise rates on the wealthy.

I find nothing wrong in her suggestions. I may vote FOR her this November, provided she’s on Washington state’s ballot. Typically I vote AGAINST candidates, always Republicans. But I’m so fed up with Obama and his cult of feckless bipartisanship, I may toss simple game theory to the wind.

Sigh.

Social harmony

July 11, 2012 by davald

Academy Award winner Milos Forman, writing for the New York Times, chastens those Americans who dare brand Obama a “socialist.” As the caption reads: “not even close.”

Forman knows a particular brand of socialism, having lived in Czechoslovakia under Soviet-style socialism. Citizens’ every move was surveilled. Speech itself was controlled. He tells this story from his experience.

My sister-in-law’s father, Jan Kunasek, lived in Czechoslovakia all his life. He was a middle-class man who ran a tiny inn in a tiny village. One winter night in 1972, during a blizzard, a man, soaked to the bone, awakened him at 2 in the morning. The man looked destitute and, while asking for shelter, couldn’t stop cursing the Communists. Taking pity, the elderly Mr. Kunasek put him up for the night.

A couple of hours later, Mr. Kunasek was awakened again, this time by three plainclothes policemen. He was arrested, accused of sheltering a terrorist and sentenced to several years of hard labor in uranium mines. The state seized his property. When he was finally released, ill and penniless, he died within a few weeks. Years later we learned that the night visitor had been working for the police. According to the Communists, Mr. Kunasek was a class enemy and deserved to be punished.

This is the brand of socialism conservatives decry, and rightfully so. However, when those on the American right use the term ‘socialism,’ Forman shudders:
It offends me, and cheapens the experience of millions who lived, and continue to live, under brutal forms of socialism.

There are many shades of socialism, including democratic socialism, viably present in most European countries. Americans, Forman pleads, would do well by realizing the distinction between “Marxist-Leninist totalitarianism” and “Western European-style socialism, and its government provision of social insurance and health care.”

Joseph McCarthy enjoyed a brief career by besmirching Americans for being “communists.” Now that the Soviet Union is gone, the right’s vocabulary has substituted socialist, hoping for the same effect. Among the madding crowd, unfortunately, labels work, however false.

Forman wishes for a different conversation. He ends his post thusly:

What we need is not to strive for a perfect social justice — which never existed and never will — but for social harmony. Harmony in music is, by its nature, exhilarating and soothing. In an orchestra, the different players and instruments perform together, in support of an overall melody.

Today, our democracy, a miraculous gathering of diverse players, desperately needs such unity. If all participants play fair and strive for the common good, we can achieve a harmony that eluded the doctrinaire socialist projects. But if just one section, or even one player, is out of tune, the music will disintegrate into cacophony.

I am not asking Mr. Obama and the Republican leaders to stop playing instruments of their choosing. All I am asking is that every player keep in mind the noble melody of our country. Otherwise the noisy dissonance might become loud enough to wake another Marx, or even worse.

One candidate for worse:

A week after an extremist right-wing party gained an electoral foothold in Greece’s Parliament earlier this summer, 50 of its members riding motorbikes and armed with heavy wooden poles roared through Nikaia, a gritty suburb west of here, to telegraph their new power.

Political polarization marinated in a dismal economy mixed with austerity measures breeds radicalization. We certainly see it in Europe. Our version, fortunately, is neither as strident nor as yet violent. However, the tendency to scapegoat our own is as strong as ever.

Design trumps the environment

July 10, 2012 by davald

In designing its new Retina-display notebook Apple made it well-nigh impossible to recycle its parts following the product’s end of life. The company then took the extraordinary action to withdraw its products from the Electronic Product Environmental Assessment Tool (EPEAT), which is funded by EPA. Apple prefers design to disassembly.

Almost immediately the City of San Francisco announced that it would no longer purchase Apple products, since the municipality requires that all computer electronics be EPEAT-certified. Other government agencies with similar buying guidelines will surely follow.

I must say that I’m disappointed in Apple. I should think that it would be possible, if not also feasible, to design gadgets that meet aesthetic, engineering, and usability standards and also comply with EPEAT guidelines. Where’s Sir Jony Ive?

Until the release of the MacBook Pro with Retina display Apple seemed intent on becoming more environmentally conscious, touting, for example, its use of renewable energy to power new data centers. The company has a nice web page illustrating “the story behind Apple’s environmental footprint. Its product-recycling page is still up; Apple has partnered with PowerOn to recycle most of its products, including notebooks. Washington state has its own e-cycle program. From the state’s website:

What can I recycle for free?

  • televisions
  • computers
  • computer monitors
  • portable or laptop computers including “tablet computers”
  • e-readers (also called e-book readers)

I have no idea how this decision by Apple will play out. If governments at all levels ban the company’s products, will Apple’s sales take a big hit? If the private sector follows, then what? Will environmentally conscious consumers stop buying Macs and iPads?

Although Steve Jobs was all about making “insanely” great products, he was also interested in making money, although he was quick to add that the former determined the latter. Should companies and governments and consumers start abandoning its products for environmental reasons will Apple change its tune, either compromising on design or figuring out a way to wed green with wow? We’ll see.

A brief postscript: Since this change in policy followed the release of the Retina-display MacBook Pro, will we see future Apple products putting design over the environment? After all, the company withdrew all its products from EPEAT, even though the existing lines now comply, with the exception of the new MacBook Pro.

Who caused the debt?

July 10, 2012 by davald

Paul Krugman comments on what he calls a “very peculiar column” by the Washington Post‘s Robert Samuelson. If nothing else, Samuelson is as provocative as he is wrong. He writes:

Wondering why government can’t restart the sluggish economy? Well, one reason is that we are still paying the price for the greatest blunder in domestic policy since World War II. This occurred a half-century ago and helps explain today’s policy paralysis. The history — largely unrecognized — is worth recalling.

Shamelessly he then misreads history, perhaps clouded by ideological lenses. Samuelson says that the “blunder” on Keynesian economists in the Kennedy administration. Samuelson:

Kennedy’s economists, fashioning themselves as heirs to John Maynard Keynes(1883-1946), shattered this consensus [that budgets should be balanced]. They contended that deficits weren’t immoral and could be manipulated to boost economic performance. This destroyed the intellectual and moral props for balanced budgets.

Samuelson goes on to acknowledge that the economy is sputtering with too many people out of work. What to be done? Does Krugman have the answer? Samuelson:

Only one group of economists has a coherent response: Keynesians. Led by New York Times columnist Paul Krugman, they argue that the deficits haven’t been large enough. If consumers and businesses aren’t spending enough to revive the economy, government must substitute. Its support would be temporary until more jobs and profits strengthened private spending. Sounds convincing.

But it collides with the 1960s’ legacy. Running routine deficits meant that the federal debt (all past annual deficits) was already high before the crisis: 41 percent of the economy, or gross domestic product (GDP), in 2008. Huge deficits have now raised that to about 70 percent of GDP; Krugman-like proposals would increase debt further. It would approach the 90 percent of GDP that economists Kenneth Rogoff of Harvard and Carmen Reinhart of the Peterson Institute have found is associated with higher interest rates and slower economic growth.

Again, Samuelson completely ignores the Reagan period, with its higher spending and lower receipts, occasioned by sharp reductions in tax rates. The legacy is not of the 60s; it’s of the 80s.

Krugman says that Samuelson focused on the wrong period. If what he says is true about “moral props for balanced budgets,” he should have been looking at the Reagan years. Let’s take a look.

Gosh, those Kennedy Keynesians sure went crazy. Wait. The debt as a percentage of GDP actually fell during the 50s, 60s, and early 70s? Yep. Then Reagan took office. That’s when things really got out of hand.

Ironically, Reagan was no rhetorical friend of big government. Indeed, he denounced it. Yet, belying his words, the federal debt climbed rapidly during his administration.

And the debt:

But what about the huge deficits under Bush and Obama? First Bush:

Bush cut taxes, and therefore federal receipts, but spending (outlays) continued apace. Since spending exceeded revenues, each year after his inaugural saw budget deficits.

Now for Obama:

The recovery, if you like, is meager. The unemployment rate is stuck above eight percent. There are still too many people out of work for each job opening. Keynesians would argue that the federal government should extend benefits to the unemployed because those dollars will be spent, almost in their entirety and quickly upon receipts. If we are still a compassionate nation, we would want to extend a collective hand to the down and out. The government has done this. Let’s look at federal outlays for unemployment benefits (Bureau of Economic Analysis).

In 201o the government spent about $140 billion on helping the unemployed. Before the Great Recession the level was about $35 billion. That’s a jump of 300 percent.

One thing that both Samuelson and Krugman agree on: the economy would be in much better shape following the recession had both the government and private sector not been in so much debt before the housing bubble collapsed. If the Krugman prescription is the right one, that the government should fiscally stimulate the economy, the mountain of debt makes this a difficult, if not impossible, sell. Samuelson:

Now, imagine that the country had adhered to its balanced-budget tradition before the crisis. Some deficits would have remained, but the cumulative debt would have been much lower: plausibly between 10 percent and 20 percent of GDP. There would have been more room for expansion. Balancing the budget might even have forced Congress to face the costs of an aging society.

The blunder of the Sixties has had a long afterlife. Economic policy is trapped between weak demand and the fears of too much debt. Yesterday’s Keynesians undercut today’s Keynesians. “In the long run we are all dead,” Keynes said. But others are alive — and suffer from bad decisions made decades ago.

But, Mr. Samuelson, it was not the blunder of the Sixties. It was Reagan’s blunder, pure and simple. Lowering tax rates turns out to be bad for the economy and certainly for the Rest of Us.

Cutting taxes

July 10, 2012 by davald

The New York Times has an interesting, if not also premature, article on two states. Democratically controlled Maryland decided to raise taxes on the rich to sustain funding of public services, including education. Kansas, dominated by Republicans, and very conservative ones at that, elected to lower taxes. The argument: Kansas was losing business to lower-taxed states. Which policy succeeds remains to be seen.

The article points out that Ohio, which cut aid to public schools and social services, announced that it has a $239 million surplus. Will those dollars be used to restore services? No. They will be placed in a reserve account.

But we do have a live experiment on our hands. With the Bush II-era tax cuts, the United States has among the lowest taxes in the industrial world, and both the marginal and corporate tax rate at near historic lows.

The recession took a lesser GDP toll on Maryland than on Kansas, but a much larger hit on employment. But it’s difficult to argue that Kansas’s tax rates were adversely impacting its economy, since the economy was growing steadily prior to the Great Recession.

Moreover, despite higher federal tax rates in the 50s and 60s, the U.S. economy was growing. Since tax rates were lowered, the economy has not fared so well. And lower tax receipts have led to annual budget deficits and, therefore, accumulated federal debt.

The post-war period is marked by parallel spending and receipts, with budget surpluses the norm. Beginning in Nixon’s second term, we see a decline in receipts and an increase in spending. That gap grew under Ronald Regan: spending was much higher than revenues. Only during Clinton’s second term do we see the reverse. Then all hell broke loose under Bush II. Spending did not fall (the wars, you know), and receipts fell dramatically. The surplus inherited by G.W. Bush was quickly squandered.

The lesson: Don’t lower taxes to spur economic development. It doesn’t work. Besides, you create increasing government debt. Yet, Mitt Romney, according to Joe Biden, is “Bush on steroids.”

The Great Divergence: Are we surplus?

July 10, 2012 by davald

Comes now Thomas Edsall, writing for the New York Times, to address the headline: “The Hallowing Out.” The middle class is shrinking, while the poor and rich proliferate. Is this phenomenon a result of “natural” forces or the effect of deliberate policies, whether or not their outcomes were intentional? Edsall:

The issue of the disappearing middle is not new, but credible economists have added a more threatening twist to the argument: the possibility that a well-functioning, efficient modern market economy, driven by exponential growth in the rate of technological innovation, can simultaneously produce economic growth and eliminate millions of middle-class jobs.

While I question the premise of “efficient modern market,” since there’s little evidence that it exists in reality—more likely the figment of ideology—if “the rate of technological innovation” drives more of us into superfluity, we’ve got problems. Whatever arrangements obtain, it’s clear that a small slice of the population continues to do exceedingly well while the Rest of Us stagnate or fall further behind. Edsall includes this chart:

The faint, dotted line represents all after-tax profits. After plunging during the Great Recession, profits have risen even more sharply and attained a much higher level than at any time since January of 1995. GDP has risen only slightly, while the employment-to-population ratio has plummeted. In other words, the chart depicts the suggested narrative that this new economy, however efficient, doesn’t require as many workers.

Edsall quotes an MIT researcher, Andrew McAfee:

As computers race ahead, acquiring more and more skills in pattern matching, communication, perception, and so on, I expect that this decoupling will continue, and maybe even accelerate.

The decoupling is another word for labor disenfranchisement. If you can’t master technological skills or know how to marshall technology for productive outcomes, then this economy doesn’t need you.

Let’s step back a bit from this thread to ask ourselves some fundamental questions. I’ll begin with this: For whom or what is an economy for?

Some might think this an odd question, perhaps because many if not most of us assume that the economy just is. It has no moral purpose, no conscience, no sense of self. In the textbooks we might find something to the effect that ‘economy’ is descriptive: millions of people buying and selling goods and services for which millions of people are needed to produce. It is not, strictly speaking, for anyone or any thing.

If so, the questions of morality or human development must be raised and answered outside economic textbooks. This may be the proper stage for philosophers.

I’ll jump ahead in this inquiry by assuming that philosophers emerge from their deliberations with an answer, one that suggests that the economy’s outcomes are immoral in that, say, there’s too much money at the top and not enough at the bottom. Whatever their answer, I’m more interested at the  moment in another question: If the economy’s outcomes fail some morality test, what can and should be done to produce better results, perhaps along some Rawlsian distribution?

For those who believe that the economy is what it is and that it’s unfortunate, but inevitable, that the rich get richer and the poor more so, attempts to engineer the economy to produce different outcomes are deemed foolish. On the opposite side, those who reject such inevitability, the answers can be found in history.

One would be naive to suppose that the rich and powerful have simply stood on the sidelines over the last several decades while enjoying their inevitable desserts. Actually, they have been busy, as numerous accounts have chronicled (e.g., Stiglitz, Cassidy, Baker). They have been busy in Congress, persuading its members to cut the marginal tax rates, reduce financial regulation, and stifle labor.

In turn, these legislative actions have yielded a different economy than the one that existed during the relative halcyon days of the 50s and 60s. This different economy has disproportionately benefited the few at the expense of the Rest of Us. Wealth has become more concentrated, and with that concentration of wealth comes concentration of political power, which is used to increase concentrations—and so on. This new economy, dominated by the financial sector and, perhaps, technology, manages to produce record profits for some while rendering the Rest of Us surplus.

The evidence, then, indicates that the economy can be changed. The challenge for the Rest of Us is to somehow persuade Congress and state legislatures to modify the economy to produce outcomes that benefit the many at the expense of the few. Don’t worry. They can afford it.

Again, history provides guidance. During those relative halcyon days, a byproduct of the Great Compression during the late 30s to the end of WWII, marginal tax rates were higher as were union participation rates. Corporate taxes were also higher. The country experienced economic growth and low inflation. Moreover, and much to the point, America’s Gini index was much lower than it is today: the gap between the rich and poor was narrower.

To those who stress the inevitability of perverse outcomes I say “poppycock.” Things can be different. Things should be different. Will they be different? That’s the challenge for the Rest of Us.

The proper targets

July 9, 2012 by davald

Americans should be angry, since there is so much to be angry about. Where to begin?

Our transportation infrastructures are crumbling. Our public school buildings are decrepit. Real wages have stagnated. Too many of us lack jobs that we desperately need. Politicians long ago stopped listening to the Rest of Us, hoping that we can be ignored as they seek campaign donations from their wealthy benefactors.

And speaking of wealth, Americans must surely be in the dark about the enormity of the Great Divergence. Worse, we may be in the midst of our own “Stockholm syndrome,” as labor organizer Stephen Lerner suggested to Bill Moyers.

I think many of us at least have spent our life sort of waiting for the great leader to come and, you know, come and save us. And I actually am not waiting. I don’t think that there’s going to be somebody in Washington that’s going to emerge and do that. I think instead we have to look at where are the battles that we can have that we can both win but also become symbolic and exciting that inspire and move people. Because the labor movement’s suffering a version of the Stockholm Syndrome. That we’ve been held captive by capital for so long, we’re so used to losing, that we almost identify with our oppressor. And that part of what has to happen here is brilliant strategies and tactics, but there’s another piece which is that we just have to be willing to say slowly dying is worse than having a really big fight and trying to win [my link].

Have the Rest of Us embraced our captors? If not, we’ve surely given them a pass.

On his blog over the weekend Paul Krugman includes a table from Thomas Piketty, at the University of Paris, and Emmanuel Saez, the Berkeley economist who’s become an expert on America’s income inequality. Here’s the table:

Look closely at the 1960 column and note how tax rates increased sharply with income. The top .01 percent paid an average of 71.4 percent of their income in 1960. But by 2004, their tax rate had been cut by more than half, to 34.7 percent. Meanwhile, tax rates increased for the full population and rose for all income groups below 99 percent, save for those within the second lowest quintile (20-40), who saw their rates drop.

But the table is only part of the picture. Not only are the very rich paying a much lower percentage of their incomes in federal taxes, their incomes have skyrocketed. I’ve shown the following chart several times (based on Piketty/Saez data).

The chart shows real income in 2005 dollars. The bottom 90 percent remains flat. Even the top 10 and 5 percent barely register an uptick. Those in the top one percent have seen their incomes rise modestly. It’s only when we look at the top .01 percent that we gain an appreciation for the Great Divergence.

In their interview with Bill Moyers, Bill Fletcher, Jr., and Stephen Lerner express sympathy for the populist fervor of the right, otherwise known as the Tea Party. But the object of their anger is surely misplaced. Nevertheless, strongly aided and abetted by the very rich (e.g., the Koch brothers), they’ve succeeded in transforming the Republican Party into a frenzied mass of the starkly mad. Fletcher, in particular, argues that throwing out facts, and there are many, in an effort to persuade voters and other fellow citizens hasn’t worked. What’s needed, he says, is a story that somehow resonates.

Lerner, who is less pessimistic about labor’s chances, offers several examples of small victories. He notes the successful janitors’ strike and the new California law restricting bank foreclosures—the result of telling stories of real people to Sacramento legislators. Lerner, for one, believes that politicians can be shamed into action, as he told the story of a woman whose house was being foreclosed by Bank of America as she was accepting her son’s purple heart.

Is the winning story about particular individuals and their plights rather than a broad, historical narrative about the rich stealing from the Rest of Us, replete with charts and figures? Do the new books by Krugman and fellow Nobel laureate Joseph Stiglitz—about inequality, the dismal economy, and what to do about it—count for nothing in the struggle? Are their words dismissed or ignored, despite the authors’ curricula vitae?

Or is the more compelling narrative just a simple connection between the man in the Oval Office and lousy economic news? No need to establish causation; the audience will surely connect the dots. That appears to be Mitt Romney’s campaign in a nutshell.

And speaking of Mitt, Paul Krugman’s column contrasts the candidate’s father with the son. To wit, both made a lot of money, but their differences overwhelm that commonality. George, the father, earned his fortune the old-fashioned way. He made products people wanted to buy. Mitt, on the other hand, extracted his wealth through “financial engineering,” as Krugman put it. George produced a dozen years of tax returns during his bid for the presidency. He paid nearly three times the rate in federal taxes as his son, who proudly announced that he pays not a penny more  to the government than absolutely necessary. Mitt hides his wealth and never mentions his Swiss bank accounts or his use of offshore tax havens.

F. Scott Fitzgerald wrote that the rich are different. Hardly a bombshell. But today’s rich are even more different than they were in the Great Gatsby’s time; they want it all, the money along with secrecy. They may engage in conspicuous consumption, perhaps to elicit our veneration. However, they don’t want the Rest of Us to know just how wealthy they are and to whom they contribute their political dollars.

On this final note, I urge you to watch and listen to Bill Moyers’s on-air essay about the coddled rich, the “cowardly lion.” Moyers may have aged. He may appear conventional. His words, though, ignite. (Note: You may get a 404 error. If so, click on “use original player” in the lower left of the video frame. If that still doesn’t work, you can read the full transcript.) By the way, if the septuagenerian is outraged, why not the Rest of Us?
