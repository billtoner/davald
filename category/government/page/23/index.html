Always a darker side

October 29, 2013 by davald

Angela Merkel is not happy. Nor are her European counterparts as they learned that the U.S. has been monitoring and capturing their electronic transmissions, including cellphone calls, for over a decade. What possessed them to believe that their communications, however ordinary, were private? This is the U.S., after all, and we do as we damn well please. Meanwhile, we ordinary Americans present a collective shrug, since we’ve come to embrace the Internet, especially social media that allow us to share our most mundane activities. But at what cost? Who cares?

Here’s a line from a piece (subscription may be required) in the New York Review of Books, written by Sue Halpern:

But while we were having fun, we happily and willingly helped to create the greatest surveillance system ever imagined, a web whose strings give governments and businesses countless threads to pull, which makes us…puppets.

The education pie

October 21, 2013 by davald

I’ve learned to cringe upon reading or hearing the word ‘reform.’ Whether applied to “the economy” or, especially, public education, I know that those who advocate for reform are up to no good. Regarding the economy, advocates preach “structural reform,” which is tantamount to reducing wages and the impact of unions.

As for education, I’ve got a cynical Venn diagram.

A quick read of Diane Ravitch’s latest book, Reign of Error, provides a workable description of each category. Tea Party types and libertarians, whose populations overlap, have it in their heads that government is simply the problem regardless of context. The true believers (e.g., Michelle Rhee and Wendy Kopp) assert that they are absolutely right to push testing and its implications on every facet of education, including teacher evaluations. Then there are the corporate moochers.

Mitt Romney condemned nearly half the population as “moochers,” those who depend on government programs and are never likely to vote Republican. However detestable his insult, he completely ignored the biggest moochers of all, those in the corporate sector who see government as a means to extract dollars from the Rest of Us.

Consider public education. According to the New America Foundation, a bit over $300 billion was spent on K-12 during the 2010 fiscal year. Here’s the breakdown.

 

That’s a lot of dough that’s currently going to union teachers, school maintenance, books, and supplies. Wouldn’t it be nice, say the reform advocates, to divert much if not all of those government dollars to corporate salaries and stockholders?

Of course, that’s not really how they say it. Rather, the rhetoric is multi-pronged and includes the following propaganda:

  • unions, whomever they represent, are evil
  • teachers are mostly bad and must be fired
  • the best tool to measure academic performance and evaluate teachers is a set of standardized tests
  • in turn, those tests are to be based on Common Core Standards, adopted by nearly all the states as a condition for receiving federal funding
  • charter schools promise the best solution
  • ultimately, all schools should be run by private entities receiving government funding

The school reform movement has many sponsors, including Bill Gates, Michael Bloomberg, the ubiquitous Koch brothers, the Walton family, and several right-wing foundations. In America, as we know, money talks. So, the more money you have, the bigger the talk.

Oh, and I should not leave out Arne Duncan and Barack Obama, who have jumped into bed with the aforementioned moneyed interests to undermine public schools as I used to know them. No Child Left Behind is an abomination. Race to the Top is worse, much worse. After all, a race connotes competition and winners and losers, assuring that many, if not most, students will be left behind. Obama doesn’t get irony, I guess.

There’s profit to be made from public education budgets. Children, be damned.

So, when you hear “reform,” be at least suspicious of the motivations behind the term. Keep in mind, as well, that you will never, ever hear a word from the reformers about poverty, the most reliable correlate of low test scores. If we as a nation are truly concerned about boosting academic performance for all students, raise living conditions.

Dem banks

October 21, 2013 by davald

Last month I wrote a bit about the proliferating banks in downtown Everett.

One thing downtown Everett (Wash.) doesn’t need: another bank. Yet, over the last couple of months the city will have added two, People’s Bank and Homestreet Bank. My problem with this is that money goes into these businesses but, judging by the state of downtown, stays there; it is not invested in improving the built and cultural environments.

It seems that I’m not alone in my misgivings about banks choking off commerce in urban cores. Yesterday the Seattle Times ran a piece on revitalizing Rainier Square, home of the giant pencil. (I’m dating myself: I moved to Puget Sound before the tower was built. For a while it was the happening center of retail activity, replete with a grand piano in the lobby with an open invitation to sit at the keyboard.) I was struck by these words in the article:

What the block’s corners definitely don’t need, Outcalt and Johnson [consultants] said, is more banks, even if they’re the ones that can afford the leases.

Downtown Seattle has nine key retail blocks, and banks occupy too many corners, they said. “Those folks aren’t traffic generators,” Outcalt said. “They’re not enhancing retail vitality.”

Imagine that. Banks don’t generate pedestrian traffic and fail to enhance “retail vitality.” Duh.

As anyone who has read Jane Jacobs can attest, a city succeeds in proportion to the number of people and windows on the streets.    Banks just won’t do it. Yet, Everett’s political and business leaders, such as they are, seem intent on preventing such success. Indeed, they give every indication of being completely ignorant of the ingredients necessary to develop and sustain vibrant cityscapes.

I give you one example of an Everett bank prominently located in what could be part of a vital urban core.

You get the picture.

There is much at stake, we’re told. A catastrophe? Disaster? Economic collapse? The loonies will have none of this. Remember. it’s all just a game.

Dubious debt declaimers

October 10, 2013 by davald

The Republicans would have us believe that they really care about government deficits and debts. Indeed, they are quite willing to throw the world economy under the bus to exact their pound of fiscal flesh from Obama, whom they generally despise. They further claim that the answer to the alleged debt “crisis” is to cut government spending. Period, since they are loath to raise taxes or otherwise boost federal revenues.

But the irony in all this, even if we assume that the Republicans’ concerns are genuine, is that curtailing government spending under current conditions (e.g., historically low inflation and interest rates—a liquidity trap) retards economic growth. Here economist Dean Baker weighs in:

All of these papers showed evidence that the causation overwhelmingly went from slow growth to debt rather than in the other direction. In other words, countries that were growing slowly tended to accumulate lots of debt, rather than the other way around.

We may justifiably surmise that the GOP’s obsession with debt is a fraud. They simply don’t like government, though they raise and spend millions of dollars to be part of it.

Republican suicide?

October 10, 2013 by davald

The current issue of Rolling Stone delves into the history of right-wing radicalism and the increasing stranglehold on the GOP enjoyed by those who relish the opportunity to expose their lunacy. Sean Wilentz provides the historical perspective; Tim Dickinson reports on the breakdown of conventions in the House, with the roguish right thumbing its collective nose at the party’s “leaders.”

Dickinson’s piece features “a confederacy of dunces,” a group of Tea Party true-believers who utter complete inanities. Unfortunately for the Rest of Us, far too many citizens applaud their rhetoric with unbridled enthusiasm. Worse, the crazies have taken center stage and are not about to exit anytime soon.

Consider these exhibits:

Steve King (R-Iowa) on Mexican immigrants:

For every one who’s a valedictorian, there’s another 100 out there who weigh 130 pounds, and they’ve got calves the size of cantaloupes because they’re hauling 75 pounds of marijuana across the desert.

Paul Broun (R-Georgia), who sits on the House science committee and is a physician by training:

…all that stuff I was taught about evolution, embryology, Big Bang theory…is lies straight from the pit of hell.

Michele Bachman (R-Minnesota) on global warming:

…is all voodoo, nonsense, hokum, a hoax.

Dickinson believes that the Republican Party is engaging in open warfare. On one side are the traditional conservatives; on the other, the loonies. The title of Dickinson’s article: “Inside the Suicide Machine.”

Sean Wilentz’s essay ends on a sobering note:

For the foreseeable future, though, the prolonged death throes of the Republican Party will lead from crisis to crisis. Certainly, there is little chance that the Republicans, even if they fail to get their way, will learn any lessons in moderation and self-control that might calm their destructive, subversive fury. For now, that spirit of subversive fury defines the Republican Party.

So the acceleration of radicalism and the political crises will continue. Even Mitch McConnell – a notoriously conservative partisan, the party boss behind the obstructionist Senate filibusters and a man often openly contemptuous of President Obama – is the target of a primary challenge from the right in his 2014 re-election campaign. Sadly, frighteningly, after the 2011 debt-limit deal was struck, McConnell observed that “it set the template for the future,” and threatened that soon “we’ll be doing it all over.” And so, all too soon, we will, in a reprise that ought to alarm Americans across the political spectrum: the Constitution unheeded and endangered, the nation’s history blithely ignored, and the security of the American people put severely at risk by an extremist political faction.

The new G.O.P.

October 9, 2013 by davald

We hear a lot about clouds these days, mostly associated with computing. But there’s another variant. They’re called ‘word clouds,’ and Thomas Edsall links to a couple of doozies in his column this morning.

Edsall cites the work of Stan Greenberg, a pollster for the Democratic Party, who is focused on learning about the Republican Party “from the inside.” Greenberg divides the GOP into four broad categories: evangelical, observant, Tea Party, and moderates. Their respective percentages: 30, 17, 22, and 25.

The first word cloud demonstrates with remarkable clarity the fear and loathing of Republicans about all things Obama, including the color of his skin and the birthplace of his father. I copy it below.

 

Notice the words that are in the largest type. It occurs to me that if we subtracted a few of the above from the cluster, we could be describing G.W. Bush, at least from my perspective.

The second of Greenberg’s word clouds includes feelings and attitudes about the Republican Party by its own members, based on six focus groups.

 

What I find ironic is that I share many of these descriptors—but about the country, not the GOP. That is, I’m very much worried about inequality, the high level of poverty, the governments’ fiscal issues, climate change, and the dominance of monied interests in just about everything. I’m also scared that failure to raise the debt ceiling, along with the refusal to pass a continuing budget resolution, could send us into another deep recession, or worse, with dire consequences for the Rest of Us, including those of us who now receive Social Security checks.

So, how is it going to end? I’m not hopeful.
