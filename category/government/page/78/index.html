Federal spending

February 14, 2012 by davald

When our economy grows slightly, or not at all, people suffer. Firms layoff employees, reducing income and the ability to buy things. Add to this the problems with housing foreclosures and the decline in property values—which prevents using equity as an ATM—and we’ve got a lousy situation.

Fortunately there are programs in place—however inadequate—that do provide some assistance. Unemployment benefits, food stamps, Medicaid, energy assistance, and job training all help, though not enough.

We should not be surprised, then, that government spending on such programs has risen since the collapse of the housing bubble.* A lot of people are hurting.

Yet, from the right we hear that the government spending is out of control, adding more and more to the federal debt. The latter part is true; the first part is false. Spending on safety-net programs is up because there is more need for them.

As for the rising debt, more of that can be explained by diminishing revenues. If incomes on the Rest of Us decline or remain stagnant, there are fewer tax dollars reaching the Treasury. Meanwhile, the plunge in tax rates on well-to-do incomes, rather than creating jobs is simply causing more debt. In 2010 revenues reached their lowest level since 1972. (data from CBO)

The dark black line in the above graph shows revenues declining since 2007, falling from 18.5 percent of GDP to 15.1 percent in 2010. Spending on safety-net programs (e.g., Social Security and Medicare) rose sharply from 2007 to 2009, a jump of over 42 percent.

No matter, the Republicans would like to give even more money to the rich, always on the pretext that the wealthy are “job creators.” Never mind facts to the contrary. If lowering taxes were the answer, everyone would have a job by now.

Money and values

February 14, 2012 by davald

We’re stuck with chicken-egg dichotomies with no simple resolutions. Indeed, debate springs eternal. One now in currency is money vs. values. Are poor people poor because they lack money or are they poor because they lack the requisite values?

My simple brain registers a tautology: one is poor precisely because one lacks money, by definition. So why don’t the poor have money?

I think it fair to say that most of us would prefer to have a job, especially one that bestowed a sense of self-worth along with an adequate wage. If we were in a full-employment economy in which everyone who wanted to work could land a job, then those who chose idleness may be chastised for their irresponsibility. But we’re a very long way from such an economy; there are at least four potential willing applicants for each job vacancy, more than double the pre-recession rate.

New York Times columnist David Brooks, who prefers broad, overarching moral narratives to facts and figures, seems determined to put values before money. In his latest effort he takes a swipe at fellow columnist Paul Krugman, who reverses the order. Brooks:

Liberal economists haven’t silenced conservatives, but they have completely eclipsed liberal sociologists and liberal psychologists. Even noneconomist commentators reduce the rich texture of how disadvantage is actually lived to a crude materialism that has little to do with reality.

Oh, and I’m sure Brooks knows full well “how disadvantage is actually lived.” Not.

So Krugman retorts in a blog post today, first quoting Brooks:

I don’t care how many factory jobs have been lost, it still doesn’t make sense to drop out of high school.

Krugman turns this argument on its head:

I don’t care how little manual work Americans engage in these days, or how available fast food has become, it still doesn’t make sense not to stay at your ideal weight through diet and exercise.

Why are so many of us fat, even when we know it’s not in our best interests? Similarly, why do so many teenagers drop out of high school, even though it’s not in their best interests to do so? Krugman suggests that the answer in both cases is that we’re “human”; we don’t always behave rationally and are vulnerable to all sorts of outside influences or innate tendencies, such as cravings for French fries and potato chips.

Brooks offers this sweeping critique:

In the half-century between 1962 and the present, America has become more prosperous, peaceful and fair, but the social fabric has deteriorated. Social trust has plummeted. Society has segmented. The share of Americans born out of wedlock is now at 40 percent and rising.

All true, to be sure. Is the reason for these metrics an erosion of personal morality? Let me suggest a better argument, and it has much to do with the Great Divergence. (data from Emmanuel Saez, UC Berkeley)

The accumulation of vast wealth by a privileged few leads to its perpetuation and acceleration via tax-code modifications and legislation that favors the already wealthy. We’ll note that it wasn’t always this way. Something changed in the mid-70s. How and why have been chronicled by several writers, including the authors of Winner-Take-All Politics (Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson) and Dean Baker, who wrote The End of Loser Liberalism: Making Markets Progressive. Here’s the opening paragraph of the latter:

Money does not fall up. Yet the United States has experienced a massive upward redistribution of income over the last three decades, leaving the bulk of the workforce with little to show from the economic growth since 1980. This upward redistribution was not the result of the natural workings of the market. Rather, it was the result of deliberate policy, most of which had the support of the leadership of both the Republican and Democratic parties.

The theme of these authors, and no doubt many others, is that we have become a very stratified society not by the natural working of free markets or declining mores but by deliberate schemes to make money “fall up.” And as researcher Richard Wilkinson concludes, economically disparate societies are bad for our physical and psychological health—the rich as well as the poor.

The social fabric has been rent because the very wealthy have taken all the toys, consigning the Rest of Us to drawing lines in the sandbox. The rich have become so economically and thus politically powerful that they have and live in a different society with different rules. They have far more than they need leaving the Rest of Us needy. And, damn it, they’re not about to share.

One last thing, this on the percentage of children born “out of wedlock,” a quaint term that has replaced ‘illegitimacy.’ Brooks evidently hasn’t noticed that the U.S. is hardly alone in this statistic.

The Nordic countries have higher rates of out-of-wedlock births than America’s. Yet, and I offer this as a refutation of Brooks, these countries have greater social cohesion than we have.

First of all, they have much lower inequality metrics. More important, perhaps, they have far stronger safety nets (what Republicans, especially, deride as “entitlements”), and actually greater social and economic mobility—it’s easier for a person from a lower stratum to enter higher ones. (Wikipedia data for chart below; the lower the coefficient the more equal the society)

This may seem trivial, but economic inequality is bad for the social fabric. Too much money at the top—and keeping it there—creates huge scarcities for the Rest of Us, which leads to all those nasty “moral” problems over which Brooks obsesses.

Making cars—again

February 13, 2012 by davald

You may have seen the Chrysler Super Bowl ad, the one featuring Clint Eastwood’s voice telling us that it was “half-time in America.” It seems that Detroit is making a comeback, thanks mostly to federal bailouts orchestrated by Pres. Obama. Cue the GOP boos.

I had an uncomfortable feeling while watching this slick video. Is that the only thing America can make these days—cars and trucks and SUVs? Whether or not true, the planet asks for fewer automobiles, regardless of who manufactures them. Surely there are better things to do.

We’ve got huge problems, and they’re getting worse. Millions still out of work, with scant hope of reclaiming their previous jobs. We lack what every other industrial democracy has—universal health care. People are losing their homes, often through no fault of their own. We face mounting public and private debt. We’ve got precious few surplus dollars to stick in the bank. Comfortable retirement may be the new American Dream, with so many of us baby boomers reaching 65 with collapsed 401(k)s. Rising Medicare costs show no signs of abating. The gap between the very rich and the Rest of Us is greater than at any time since the Great Depression. Our schools need urgent repair, as do our roads and bridges. We’ve done nothing to cure our addiction to oil and coal, so greenhouse gases continue to accumulate, promising wide-scale devastation by the end of the century. Most of us will be long gone by then, but our grandchildren will not thank us for the mess we’re now brewing.

I’ve begun reading Ronald Susskind’s Confidence Men: Wall Street, Washington, and the Education of a President. The year is 2007. Obama is still a senator from Illinois, but already in the midst of the presidential campaign. Polls show that he trails Hillary Clinton by 21 points. He’s set aside a couple of hours in a tight schedule to confer with his economic advisory team, led by Austan Goolsbee. That morning Obama had delivered a speech on Iran, quoting JFK’s line, “Let us never negotiate out of fear, but let us never fear to negotiate.” It did not go well. So Obama was hoping for a domestic issue that could rally the faithful and overcome his deficit with Clinton.

Already there were signs of a fragile housing market; yet Wall Street had bet heavily on the mortgage-based collateralized debt obligations. One of his advisors suggested that things could begin unraveling, although Ben Bernanke had estimated that only $100 billion was at stake should the housing bubble collapse. Not one of your finer moments, Ben.

Obama took a swig from his water bottle and sat up, ramrod straight. “Okay, in year two of my administration, when the housing bubble finally bursts, I come to you guys as my economic advisers and say, ‘What do we do!’ Well, what do we do?”*

Prescient, certainly. As for the advisors’ plan?

Everyone in the room was concerned about the ongoing decline in work traditionally done by men, and that would only exacerbate when the bubble collapsed, kicking hundreds of thousands of construction workers to the curb. A few talked about renewable energy. Obama could address global warming while putting people into green-collar jobs. How many? Two million tops, came the reply. What else?

The health care sector was expanding, adding more jobs each day. But Obama said that was mostly “women’s work,” a phrase that took everyone aback. Obama:

“Look, these are guys,” he said. “A lot of them see health care, being nurse’s aides, as women’s work. They need to do something that fits with how they define themselves as men.”

Then Alan Kreuger, a Princeton economist, blurted out: “Infrastructure. Rebuilding infrastructure.”

Obama liked it. Susskind:

And there it was: the mind of a man who hoped to be president, showing how it bent toward integration; coolly fitting disparate, competing analyses into a coherent whole and then seasoning this with a dollop of trenchant human insight. And just like that, a policy to repair the nation’s infrastructure was born. The federal government, in partnership with the private sector, would call upon the underemployed men of America to rebuild the country, and in doing so restore their pride.

Ah, but we know how this story ends. The Republicans dropped a giant turd into Obama’s punchbowl.

So, we build cars, while the nation’s infrastructure further rots. The bastards.

Masochism and cognitive dissonance

February 12, 2012 by davald

Many people say they are angry because the government is wasting money and giving money to people who do not deserve it. But more than that, they say they want to reduce the role of government in their own lives. They are frustrated that they need help, feel guilty for taking it and resent the government for providing it. They say they want less help for themselves; less help in caring for relatives; less assistance when they reach old age.

— New York Times

This from a splendid New York Times piece on those who decry government and its safety nets while taking advantage of them. It’s as if such people have their left hand open to receive the subsidy and their right fist clenched, poised to strike the purveyor.

The conservative, as we learned in previous posts (here and here, for example), believes in self-reliance, discipline, and strong morality. It follows that those who succeed in life deserve what they’ve earned, and governments should not reduce their wealth via confiscatory taxes. Those doing worse are also deserving of their fate; they are judged as failures, unworthy of “handouts.” Insofar as government compensates the losers from the winners’ share, government itself is evil.

Ideological purity trumps individual circumstance, even if you happen to be a conservative “loser.” Those who take taxpayer money therefore “resent” the government for playing Robin Hood on their behalf.

The Times has a nice chart showing where federal dollars go.

Since the 60s, spending has increased sharply, from around a thousand dollars per person to over $7,000 today—in real dollars. But the distribution has changed. Again, the Times shows us graphically.

Over the last thirty years the share of federal benefits going to the bottom 20 percent has fallen from 54% to 36%, while every other quintile has seen its benefit share rise. The official poverty rate has risen from 9.8 percent before the Great Recession to 11.7 percent in 2010, an increase of 20 percent. However, by international standards, the U.S. poverty rate before taxes and transfers was 26% in 2005 (last year of data) and still 17% after taxes and transfers, the highest rate amongst the wealthier nations. The above chart can help explain why we have so many poor.

The Times tells us that many, if not most people, would suffer much more without government programs like Medicare, Social Security and the earned income tax credit. Yet, and this I find strange, the conservatives interviewed were of one mind: reduce benefits rather than increase revenues. They believe their taxes are already too high; increases are out of the question. Stranger still, the interviewees seem ignorant of one glaring fact: U.S. tax rates are lower than those of any other industrial democracy. (data from OECD)

Americans, as a rule, lack a clear understanding of the facts, whether they be scientific or political. I doubt that those interviewed by the Times appreciated the following:

Almost half of all Americans lived in households that received government benefits in 2010, according to the Census Bureau. The share climbed from 37.7 percent in 1998 to 44.5 percent in 2006, before the recession, to 48.5 percent in 2010.

When asked what might happen if, say, Social Security and Medicare were no longer available, or substantially diminished, the respondents realized that they or certainly their aging parents might be on the street, as one person put it. Nevertheless, don’t raise taxes to sustain the programs, however niggardly they are by international comparisons.

I don’t get it.

Social output gaps

February 10, 2012 by davald

Some economists have rightly focused on the gap in GDP from the pre-recession trend to the present. I calculated that the economy lost about $16 trillion in gross domestic product, or about $1.3 trillion a year since the collapses of the housing bubble and Lehman Brothers.

There is another important gap, which has drawn much attention since the release of Charles Murray‘s new book, Coming Apart: The State of White America, 1960-2010. Therein Murray described the income gap between the rich and the poor as “more a symptom than a cause.” New York Times columnist David Brooks quickly glommed onto Murray’s central thesis, that a breakdown in moral virtues prevents the lower classes from landing and maintaining adequate-wage jobs, the foundation for a stable family life.

Which is it, virtue before money, or the other way around? Consensus is lacking, as this New York Times article reveals.

One reason for the growing gap in achievement, researchers say, could be that wealthy parents invest more time and money than ever before in their children (in weekend sports, ballet, music lessons, math tutors, and in overall involvement in their children’s schools), while lower-income families, which are now more likely than ever to be headed by a single parent, are increasingly stretched for time and resources. This has been particularly true as more parents try to position their children for college, which has become ever more essential for success in today’s economy.

A study by Sabino Kornrich, a researcher at the Center for Advanced Studies at the Juan March Institute in Madrid, and Frank F. Furstenberg, scheduled to appear in the journal Demography this year, found that in 1972, Americans at the upper end of the income spectrum were spending five times as much per child as low-income families. By 2007 that gap had grown to nine to one; spending by upper-income families more than doubled, while spending by low-income families grew by 20 percent.

“The pattern of privileged families today is intensive cultivation,” said Dr. Furstenberg, a professor of sociology at the University of Pennsylvania.

…

There are no easy answers, in part because the problem is so complex, said Douglas J. Besharov, a fellow at the Atlantic Council. Blaming the problem on the richest of the rich ignores an equally important driver, he said: two-earner household wealth, which has lifted the upper middle class ever further from less educated Americans, who tend to be single parents.

The problem is a puzzle, he said. “No one has the slightest idea what will work. The cupboard is bare.”

Paul Krugman falls clearly on the money before virtue side of the argument. If morals were so important to family cohesion, Krugman asks, then why the “plunge in teenage pregnancies among all racial groups since 1990 or the 60 percent decline in violent crime since the mid-90s. Could it be that traditional families aren’t as crucial to social cohesion as advertised?”

The evidence, Krugman asserts, suggests that job opportunities for poor and under-educated males have fallen because women work and their wages are closing in on men’s. Krugman:

Most of the numbers you see about income trends in America focus on households rather than individuals, which makes sense for some purposes. But when you see a modest rise in incomes for the lower tiers of the income distribution, you have to realize that all — yes, all — of this rise comes from the women, both because more women are in the paid labor force and because women’s wages aren’t as much below male wages as they used to be.

For lower-education working men, however, it has been all negative. Adjusted for inflation, entry-level wages of male high school graduates have fallen 23 percent since 1973. Meanwhile, employment benefits have collapsed. In 1980, 65 percent of recent high-school graduates working in the private sector had health benefits, but, by 2009, that was down to 29 percent.

Men without family-wage jobs are less likely to get married and raise families. Moreover, working women rely less and less on men for income, since they have their own. Thus, marriage rates have fallen for all classes, but especially among those without a college education.

Krugman concludes:

So we should reject the attempt to divert the national conversation away from soaring inequality toward the alleged moral failings of those Americans being left behind. Traditional values aren’t as crucial as social conservatives would have you believe — and, in any case, the social changes taking place in America’s working class are overwhelmingly the consequence of sharply rising inequality, not its cause.

While I tend to concur with Krugman, that money precedes virtue in social and economic achievement, it does not follow that becoming a more egalitarian society will evaporate individual differences. We discussed this in a previous post. Humans are not “infinitely malleable,” so changing conditions or social relations—as in putting all of us on a more level playing field—will not eliminate either competition for scarce resources or what seems to be the natural tendency to stratify and form hierarchies.

But I beg to differ with Mr. Besharov that “no one has the slightest idea what will work.” As followers of this blog can attest, we do have answers, just not here. If it is our goal as a nation to reduce poverty, educate the masses, and otherwise provide the requisite foundations for a well-functioning society—then we can certainly learn a thing or two from the Nordic experience, if not also from France and Germany.

These countries have lower unemployment rates, lower poverty rates, and better-educated populations. Health care is deemed a right rather than a privilege, as it is in the U.S. People in Sweden, Norway, et al. don’t have to worry as much about their retirements or the vagaries of life. Ample social safety nets are in place, paid for by higher taxes than we Americans pay.

We say that we don’t know what will or will not work because of our national myopia. There is only the U.S. and nothing else matters. Tragic.

Taxes

February 9, 2012 by davald

I’ve just computed my tax obligation for last year. My effective rate is 15 percent, about the same as Mitt Romney’s. I won’t tell you my income, but you won’t be surprised to learn that it’s far less than Romney’s speaking fees, which he said were “not very much”—only $326,000 last year. I could live on that.

Romney may have been as helpful as the Occupy Wall Street movement in drawing into sharp relief the huge disparities between the very rich and the Rest of Us. The former face much lower tax rates since JFK began reducing them. They fell lower and lower until Clinton bumped them up a bit. Lackeys for the One-percenters have been deployed to change the story about the coddled wealthy. The new narrative has the rich paying, in absolute dollars, the highest percentage of total Treasury receipts. So stop whining, you people.

By way of Jonathan Chait we discover that one such lackey is Veronique de Rugy, whose title is “senior research fellow of the Mercatus Center at George Mason University.” Quick quiz: Can you guess who sits on the board of the Mercatus Center? Would you believe Charles Koch? Yes, that Charles Koch, the one who, with his brother, gives enormous sums of money to lackeys like Ms. de Rugy. She writes:

Contrary to common belief, the United States already has a more progressive tax system than do the most industrialized democracies worldwide.

Huh?

Ms. de Rugy bases this remark on a simple, but misleading fact: America’s rich pay a higher share of total taxes than everyone else.

Then Chait dares respond with the obvious:

They may be paying a higher share of the taxes because they have a higher share of the income to begin with. Which, in fact, they do.

But, as Chait goes on to argue, a progressive tax structure is all about the rates and not about absolute dollars. Let’s look at rates of selected countries, using data from OECD. (The rates include all taxes on income, including state and local.)

You’ll notice that every country listed has a progressive tax rate. As one moves up from the income ladder one pays a higher percentage of taxes. (By the way, the legend at the bottom of the chart refers to percentage of the average gross wage expressed in the nation’s currency.)

Of course, this chart doesn’t begin to tell us the whole picture, since in the U.S., especially, the One-percenters earn far more than 167% of the average wage, which, in America, was $43,040 in 2010. The threshold for entrance into the One-percenters is about $350,000, or eight times as much as the average wage.

The above pie chart shows the share of total 2010 income made by each quintile, from the lowest (fifth) to the highest (first). The top quintile extracted over half of all income.

As regular visitors to this site know, the U.S. has the most unequal society in the OECD, save for Mexico. If one were to assume a flat tax on all income, the rich would contribute more dollars to the Treasury than the poor. It’s simple arithmetic: rate x income = tax paid. If the rate increases up the income ladder, than the rich will pay even more.

The notion that the U.S. has a more progressive tax structure because the wealthier contribute more tax dollars than the Rest of Us is rather silly, don’t you think? Of course they do, because they’re richer.

But before we go, let’s compare the total tax rate as a percentage of average labor cost. (OECD data)

Our taxes, here in the U.S., don’t fund good schools, don’t keep millions of us out of poverty, don’t build satisfactory transportation systems, don’t provide adequate pensions, and don’t cover health care costs. If you want those, and you should, then either figure out a way to get rich in a hurry or move to a Nordic country, although either France or Germany will do. Or, you can throw the bums out, and you know who I’m talking about.

Black cloud still looms

February 9, 2012 by davald

The OECD economies still lag behind their potential. Their output gaps remain negative. (OECD data)

The black line represents Greece, which is clearly in trouble, having suffered a precipitous drop in GDP. But the other countries’ economies, including those of Germany and the U.S., have a ways to go before they realize their potential. (The numbers reflect deviations from full output.)

Germany, considered the only really strong and stable economy in Europe, is not yet out of the woods. Writing for the New York Times, the former chief economist of Deutsche Bank, paints a pessimistic picture of Germany’s fiscal situation. Current numbers are deceptive. “Though Germany is undeniably Europe’s strongest economy,” Norbert Walter says, “its outlook is not nearly as rosy as people imagine.”

Is now a time to rejoice? Is Germany the star performer, the role model to be emulated? Or is the start of 2012 just a snapshot, showing a series of lucky coincidences that should not be misread as structural strengths? With some qualifications, I believe it is the latter.

The Greek problem festers, threatening to drag down the rest of the Eurozone. Walter recommends that German chancellor Merkel should “lead the way toward a strong fiscal union.”

That goal has been advocated by many, both here and abroad. That is, Europe needs the equivalent of our Federal Reserve. The status quo cannot long endure, in which Germany, especially, is forced to prop up Greece, in particular, but also the other peripheral nations, including Italy and Spain.

Another Eurozone recession, as some have predicted, will impact those American states relying on exports to Germany, France, et al. Washington is among those most at risk. Krugman, while concurring that a European recession is likely, nevertheless believes that its impacts will take only a small hit on the U.S. economy. But even if Washington’s exports to Europe represent a seemingly small 2.8 percent of our GDP, that’s still about $9 billion—and nearly half of the state’s education budget.

Spreading the wealth around

February 8, 2012 by davald

When Obama said those words, or something quite similar, to Joe the Plumber, conservatives cried “socialist!” But not really, especially after we consider other countries.

In a recent post I advocated for a more Nordic-like economy-cum-government. I had in mind winners compensating losers under a market-based system. Americans, however, seem to tolerate poverty and misery on an unprecedented scale.

Paul Krugman includes a chart in one of his slide presentations. It compares poverty rates of selected countries before and after taxes and transfers.

We see that the U.S. ranks dead last, after taking into account taxes and transfers (from the richer to the poorer). There we are, with 17 percent of the population in poverty—after taxes and transfers.

The Nordic countries (Norway, Finland, Sweden, and Denmark) all have much, much lower poverty rates, from five percent to a high of seven percent (Finland). Yet, their poverty rates before taxes and transfers are not all that different from the U.S.’s. What can we glean from these data?

The numbers suggest that market-based economies, which exist in all these countries, sort their populations about the same, given that pre-tax-and-transfer poverty rates fall within a fairly narrow range; the U.S. appears to be at or near the mean. (I’m ignoring here the extreme growth of the One-percenters in the U.S., which really skews the data.)

That the Nordic countries’ poverty rates fall dramatically after taxes and transfers suggests that their governments, and presumably their citizens, deliberately strive to mitigate the “natural order” determined by market economies. Not so in the U.S.

As I’ve mentioned before, the one moral virtue that really sets off the conservative from the liberal is compassion, or empathy. Liberals have it in spades; conservatives not so much. It would seem that the Nordic populations are even more empathetic than liberal Americans, given their long-standing support for wealth transfers.

Oh, wait. We have wealth transfers, too—but in an opposite direction.

A Darwinian Left

February 8, 2012 by davald

That’s the title of one of philosopher Peter Singer’s books. It seems apropos of my posts on conservatism and liberalism, for Singer, like Jonathan Haidt and George Lakoff, believes that the right has something to teach the left.

In discussing Haidt’s remarks to Bill Moyers I mentioned that the University of Virginia social psychologist asserts that conservatives have a more accurate view of human nature. Maybe, maybe not. Lakoff disagrees, but Singer falls in with Haidt.

Singer, before making his mark as a prominent ethicist at Princeton, was a student of Marx’s philosophy, though he certainly avoids the label ‘Marxist.’ He seems more comfortable in the world of moral philosophy, specifically his version of utilitarianism, marinated with socialist compassion.

In the book, A Darwinian Left, Singer writes, first quoting Marx from his Theses on Feuerbach (VI):

“…the human essence is no abstraction inherent in each single individual. In its reality it is the ensemble of the social relations.”

It follows from this belief that if you can totally change the “ensemble of social relations,” you can totally change human nature.

Singer suggests that this point forms the core of Marx and most of the traditional left’s political philosophy. But, Singer says, “The Left needs a new paradigm.”

And what might that be?

I may not do justice to Singer here, but, with Haidt, he asserts that human nature is such that individuals will fall into hierarchical arrangements, no matter the circumstances. It is a consequence of Darwinian evolution. Thus, Marx got it wrong, says Singer, for believing that egalitarian societies will emerge from a rearrangement of the “ensemble of social relations.”

The new paradigm for the left, then, begins with a rejection of Marx’s core principal and an acknowledgement that humans eventually form stratified groups, with superiors and subordinates arrayed on one basis or another. In U.S. society, the dominant organizing principle is money. But human societies have always been sorted by color, that most salient of characteristics. We can add to this religion and no doubt a host of other categories that tend to distinguish one group of individuals from another.

In Chapter 5 of his book Singer sets out what a new Left would reject and what it would embrace. I’ll mention a few of each.

  • The Left should reject the notion that humans are essentially good (contra Rousseau) and infinitely malleable (contra Marx).
  • The Left should reject the belief that humans can live in a state of indefinite harmony without war or famine. There will always be conflict between and among us.
  • The Left should not assume that all differences in social or economic status are the result of deliberate discrimination. Some of us, by our inherited abilities (Darwin) will rise to the top (of whatever), some will fall to the bottom, and the rest in the middle.

Meanwhile, the Left should:

  • develop a better understanding of human nature;
  • not infer, as conservatives do, that what is “natural” is therefore morally “right”;
  • appreciate that individuals will compete for fame and fortune in a condition of scarcity, and economics is all about the distribution of scare goods;
  • understand that notwithstanding the innate tendency to compete, individuals will prefer cooperation if they perceive the likelihood of mutually beneficial outcomes; the Left should push for structures and conditions that promote cooperation and “channel competition into socially desirable ends.”

In my previous discussion of Haidt I failed to draw attention to one category that sharply distinguishes the liberal from the conservative. Liberals score highest on the category “care and compassion,” far higher than conservatives, who tend to rank the same in each of the six categories. (You’ll need to go to Moyers website to see the breakdown.)

Singer wants us to embrace our empathetic tendencies, or at least that’s his prescription for a new “Darwinian Left.” But we should always keep in mind that egalitarian utopias are only figments of imagination; our human nature prevents them from forming or certainly enduring.

Nor should we ignore the fact that cooperation is at least as natural as competition. The challenge is to build a society in which the winners compensate the losers through cooperative structures and mechanisms. As Singer puts it, “We need to think about how to set up conditions in which cooperation survives.”

I take from this, though I found no reference to this by Singer, that a more Nordic-like society gives greater expression to the new Left paradigm than our existing winner-take-all system. For whatever historical reasons, and I’m ill-equipped to name them, Sweden, Finland, et al. have more egalitarian societies than the U.S., which is near the top of the inequality scale. Moreover, Nordic citizens also have a stronger regard for government than Americans do, and they evidently view their respective governments as an embodiment and facilitator of cooperation. But these Nordic countries also have market economies, which entails competition between and among private firms. The combination of competition and cooperation is manifested in strong social welfare societies that mitigate the extremes of wealth and poverty.

I think we can—and should—follow Singer’s advice. However, we don’t have to reinvent the wheel. Precedents already exist in northern Europe.

As Europe goes so goes Washington state?

February 6, 2012 by davald

European leaders are trying, and failing, to snatch an austerian victory from Eurozone defeat. Today it was announced that Greece will cut 15,000 government jobs in a bid to dramatically cut its federal spending, a condition imposed by core Eurozone leaders.

Paul Krugman asserts that the strategy “is not going to work.” Slashing government spending along with wage-paying government jobs will exacerbate the revenue problem, since there will be fewer workers paying taxes, which the Greeks were never very good at collecting anyway. Lower revenues will add to the deficit, all other things being equal. In turn, the core leaders will demand more belt tightening, and so on.

Europe, including its core countries, is far from escaping recessionary woods. The Eurozone could suffer negative growth, and significantly so, should its austerity measures fail, as Krugman predicts.

Suppose that happens. How exposed is Washington state? Here’s a graphic prepared by Chase, using U.S. Department of Commerce and Wells Fargo Securities data (via Krugman).

Notice those states with the “highest risk.” Yep. There’s Washington, with 2.8 percent of its GDP in the form of exports to European countries. My guess is that Boeing and Microsoft are in there, perhaps along with agricultural products, including wine.

Washington’s 2011 GDP was $313 billion, according to this report (pdf). Its European exports, then, would be about $8.9 billion. If Europe falls into another recession, as is widely speculated, wages and consumer spending will decline, leaving American products on the shelves.

Should Europeans sharply curtail their purchases of Washington stuff, our companies will produce less, leading to layoffs or wage reductions or both. That means fewer revenues for Olympia, resulting in further budget cuts.

More dismal economics, I’m afraid. Unless, of course, you’re Mitt Romney, should he emerge as Obama’s challenger, which gets more likely with each primary result.
