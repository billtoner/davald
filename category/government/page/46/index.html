Collectivism

January 22, 2013 by davald

David Brooks, as is his wont, criticizes Obama’s second inaugural address for ignoring “decentralizing genius,” which he chooses to symbolize by ‘Wall Street,’ ‘State Street,’ ‘Menlo Park,’ and ‘Silicon Valley.’ He contrasts the U.S. and Europe, the latter having “invested in national welfare states, American localities [having] invested in human capital.” As is my wont, I continue to wonder about Brooks’s grasp of reality.

There is perhaps no place on earth that both clings to “democracy” and “rugged individualism” to the measure of America. We have manifestly preferred a more Hobbesian dystopia of nasty, brutish, short-lived lives to the utopian dreams of social and economic security in which individuals maximize their potential within a state that values personal dignity.

After all, we love our guns, the more the merrier, and have little compunction about putting people to death, even if their ignominious fates were determined erroneously. Instead of demonstrating sympathy and compassion for the poor and near-poor we dismiss them as “moochers,” condemning their lack of appropriate attitudes, the alleged modus operandi of the privileged and the decentralized genius.

Americans lack basic necessities, guaranteed health care, and the ability and conditions to improve their existence. Once the model of social mobility, the United States has ossified into rigid economic strata, as it blocks the poor from becoming rich, and vice versa. College now means debt rather than enlightenment and self-fulfilment.

Meanwhile, besotted with manufactured myths, we have permitted the rise and aggrandizement of the super-wealthy, whose fortunes belittle others and deprive the Rest of Us of our due. Indeed, we believe that we’re undeserving of anything beyond our morning porridge. They are geniuses, one and all. Don’t disparage them. Embrace them. Worship them. If you possibly can, emulate them.

As for Europe, having established social welfare states, its citizens are free to realize their potential. Several of its countries enjoy higher economic growth and greater opportunity for personal advancement than do Americans. They do not brazenly kill one another, and they deny their civil authorities the license to execute comrades for their crimes, however heinous. Their societies are more egalitarian; education is typically free.

Europe’s nations are not utopias on earth. But their denizens seem to have understood that an obsession with ducats demeans rather than ennobles. They understand and appreciate that life is full of vagaries that are best addressed by a collective response than Randian self-reliance. So Europeans pool risks, making it difficult to become either insanely rich or desperately poor.

Obama hinted in his speech that we have more than our fair share of decentralized geniuses, many of whom threw us under the bus last time round. Now its finally time for America to build a better society, one that would make Dr. King proud.

We’ll see.

Rising debt. So what?

January 18, 2013 by davald

James McCusker, who writes for the Everett Herald, generally lauds those Paul Krugman calls ‘Very Serious People,’ policy makers and pundits who advocate entitlement reform and fiscal balance, regardless of economic conditions or inflicted pain. In today’s column, McCusker opines:

It’s tough, apparently, for some to recognize that spending-related debt is a problem that neither more debt nor higher taxes can solve in the long run.

Paul Krugman disagrees:

The budget deficit isn’t our biggest problem, by a long shot. Furthermore, it’s a problem that is already, to a large degree, solved. The medium-term budget outlook isn’t great, but it’s not terrible either — and the long-term outlook gets much more attention than it should.

On the other hand, the federal debt-to-GDP ratio continues to rise. (All chart data from FRED.)

The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, or CBPP, whom Krugman cites in his own column this morning, offers its own views on debt:

The debt ratio — the debt held by the public as a share of GDP — should rise only during hard times or major emergencies and should fall during good times.  That allows the ratio to remain stable over the business cycle as a whole and enables the government to combat recessions through tax cuts and spending increases (and to address human misery during bad times).

The debt ratio cannot rise forever.  If it did, that would shrink the amount of national saving available for private investment, ultimately impairing productivity growth and, in turn, living standards.  Alternatively, if foreign capital offsets the shortfall in domestic capital, the profits from that investment will go abroad; either way, U.S. living standards would suffer.  In a crisis, international credit markets might refuse to lend to the U.S. public or private sectors at a reasonable price.  These problems would occur if the debt ratio became too high.  While no one knows what “too high” means for the United States, a debt ratio that rises in both good times and bad will become increasingly problematic.

Are we in bad times? I suppose that reasonable people can disagree, but the numbers suggest that the answer is yes, with a lingering high unemployment rate, sputtering economic growth, and no real signs that we will escape from the doldrums any time soon. So, with Krugman, perhaps we should be adding more debt now.

On the other hand, even during relatively good times we let debt rise, mostly because we’ve allowed tax rates to fall, thereby reducing federal receipts. A couple of unfunded wars don’t help, either.

Besides, we don’t seem to have a consensus understanding of what “too high” is. Japan’s debt-to-GDP ratio is much higher than ours.

Japan’s prime minister took rather bold action recently, committing to more government spending in an effort to stimulate a stagnant economy. He is deliberately adding to his country’s already high debt.

With a debt-to-GDP ratio more than twice ours, could Japan face the challenges described by CBPP? Will foreign lenders stop buying up Japanese debt? Will the country’s standard of living fall? The prime minister appears to believe that inaction will be worse.

As we’ve seen, Japan’s real GDP has stagnated. The country relies heavily on exports, which have fallen from their pre-recession trend. (Numbers in billions of chained 2005 yen.)

Exchange rates alone can make a different. If the Japanese currency rises against the U.S. dollar, Japan will export fewer goods and services to America. Let’s take a look.

We’ll note that Japan was able to increase its exports despite a rising currency exchange rate—until the Great Recession. Now its exports have declined while—or because—the yen has risen against the U.S. dollar.

Both Ben Bernanke and Paul Krugman wrote papers on Japan’s struggling economy during the 1990s, when GDP shrunk. They urged that the government target a higher inflation rate. Why? To lower the value of the yen and thereby boost exports. Krugman continues to recommend that Japan adopt policies that will increase domestic demand (fiscal stimulus) and raise inflation. He does so here.

Where are the lessons for the U.S.? Japan may be one. It has demonstrated that high government debt alone does not lead to the problems that Very Serious People warn against. Also, the prime minister’s recent moves may show us a way out of our own economic swamp. Krugman thinks as much.

America’s massive spending on WWII correlated with robust economic recovery. We can see that in the chart below:

The biggest gain in GDP occurred during the initial war years of 1941 through 1943. Federal spending increased by 176 percent in 1941 and by over 200 percent in the following year.

It’s pretty obvious that the Great Depression and World War II were marked by extreme volatility. After the war both spending and GDP changes assumed narrower variances. But the GDP numbers should give us pause, just as they apparently did in Japan. The trend is downward, which translates into those troublesome effects that generally translate as misery, pain, and suffering.

Against McCusker, I’d say spend, gov, spend, and perdition for the Very Serious People. After all, they have no problem consigning the Rest of Us to the same condition.

Should we ever get out of this mess, then, and only then, should be strive for fiscal balance. If history be our guide, that will assuredly require much higher taxes on the rich.

Tax fairness

January 17, 2013 by davald

Americans pay a lower average tax rate than their counterparts in other rich democracies.

This fact does not prevent us from whining about high taxes. Nor do we seem to be all that bothered that our tax system is decidedly regressive, both with respect to other countries and between our individual states; Washington state’s is the most or nearly the most regressive in the nation.

Amidst all the blather about entitlement reform are calls to raise the retirement age for Social Security and Medicare while reducing benefits. Writing for the New York Times Thomas Edsall reminds us: “For 35 percent of elderly white beneficiaries, for 42 percent of Asian-Americans, for 49 percent of blacks, and for 55 percent of Hispanics, Social Security represents 90 percent or more of total income.” By all means, kick these people onto the streets in the name of fiscal responsibility.

But Edsall also reports that a rather simple adjustment would solve the projected budget problem. Simply remove the cap on the payroll tax, which is now set at $113,700 per year.

He links to this article in Forbes. The writer, Igor Greenwald, compares the effects of the recent fiscal-cliff deal on two fictional workers. One is self-employed, which means that she must pay both the employee and employer contributions of the payroll tax. Her income is $50,000 a year, and she will now pay $5,726 in Social Security taxes in 2013. The other worker makes $200,000, a salary he receives from his firm, which pays half of the payroll tax. He will send the government $7,049 this year in Social Security taxes. Despite making four times as much, he pays just 23 percent more in taxes. Hardly fair.

According to a congressional study Greenwald cites, if the cap were removed Social Security would be solvent for the next 75 years. That would make the system fairer and financially secure.

Congress, of course, is not listening to the Rest of Us.

Encapsulating our predicament

January 16, 2013 by davald

Paul Krugman appeared on Bill Moyers’s program over the weekend. You can read the transcript or view the video here. I’ll offer a few excerpts with commentary.

BILL MOYERS: Yeah, you write that we are in a depression that is essentially gratuitous. We don’t need to be suffering so much pain and destroying so many lives.

PAUL KRUGMAN: Gratuitous in the sense that there’s nothing, the only obstacles to putting people to work, to having those lives restored, to producing hundreds of billions, probably 900 billion a year or so of extra valuable stuff in our economy, is in our minds.

If I could somehow convince the members of Congress and the usual suspects that deficit spending, for the time being, is okay, and that what we really need is a big job creation program. And let’s worry about the deficit after we’ve had a solid recovery, it would all be over. It would be no problem at all, which is what, that’s the lesson of 1940, 1941.

During the interview Krugman said that under normal conditions any variance in GDP or interest rates or inflation could be corrected by “two aspirins,” essentially monetary policies enacted by the Federal Reserve. But, and this is a key point in Krugman’s view of the world, we are not living under normal conditions. Indeed, he says that we’re in a depression, similar to the one the country experienced during the 30s. Thus, we will need “heavy duty prescription medicine” to cure the patient, using his metaphor.

Too many “Very Serious People” predicted runaway inflation after the Fed tripled the monetary base. It didn’t happen. Too many VSPs predicted that investors would lose confidence in a U.S. with rising debt, either demanding higher interest rates or taking their money elsewhere. Neither occurred. Inflation remains low, and in real terms, interest rates are stuck below zero. For Krugman, the failure of the predictions should have caused VSPs to recant or reform their opinions. They didn’t, as if facts don’t matter.

However, it’s been extremely difficult to persuade government leaders to adopt policies that were effective in response to the Great Depression and are desperately needed during our “lesser depression”—Krugman’s characterization. Those policies consist of federal spending in massive doses. Krugman:

Right now it’s easy [as to where and how much to spend the money] because right now we can do it very quickly simply by restoring the spending cuts that have already happened. If you gave me unlimited carte blanche in terms of spending, I would want to go beyond that. I’d want to talk about and pretty straightforward things, even so. We have you know, fix the sewer lines. I mean, we have, we have a lot of, a lot of basic infrastructure needs that are worth doing in any case.

But right now you can get a quick boost just by rehiring those school teachers and filling those potholes. We are something like $300 billion a year short of the spending that we should be undertaking just for the normal business of government. And that extra $300 billion a year would be a really big deal for the economy if we could do it right now.

It would be nice if those who lost their public-sector jobs found work in the private economy. But we know that once they were laid off, they joined the ranks of the unemployed for the most part. Without their former wages the jobless spend less, which exacerbates depression economics. Here’s Moyers doing his best at encapsulation:

… I tried to condense to one sentence the message and argument of your book. And I wrote down, “The answer is simple. Increase spending and boost consumption because the fundamental problem at the root of this crisis is a lack of demand.”

This is strange, though eerily similar to the U.K. situation, where PM David Cameron made the same argument in defense of his government’s austerity measures. However, interest rates there remained low, suggesting that investors had not lost confidence, but the U.K. is in the midst of a “double-dip recession.”

Up, down, or just staying the same

January 15, 2013 by davald

I routinely check the stock markets, though I have no direct investments in equities. I do so because, rightly or wrongly, I view the Wall Street trajectories as a gross indicator of economic health. Intuitively I sense that if the Dow Jones and S&P are moving upward at a steady pace, things are generally good. If they’re trending downward, people are feeling less hopeful about the economy.

John Maynard Keynes offered his opinion in The General Theory:

Even outside the field of finance, Americans are apt to be unduly interested in discovering what average opinion believes average opinion to be; and this national weakness finds its nemesis in the stock market. It is rare, one is told, for an American to invest, as many Englishmen still do, ‘for income’; and he will not readily purchase an investment except in the hope of capital appreciation. This is only another way of saying that, when he purchases an investment, the American is attaching his hopes, not so much to its prospective yield, as to a favourable change in the conventional basis of valuation, i.e. that he is, in the above sense, a speculator.

The American is “attaching his hopes” to a belief that others will increase the value of his holdings. If more people buy a particular stock, the price will rise, since shares are finite. The trick is to “get in” when the price is low then wait until it rises before cashing out.

A friend of mine recently purchased about 80 shares of Apple stock. As I recall he bought when the price was around $550 a share. Quickly he “made” several thousand dollars, as the value appreciated. But timing is everything. This morning Apple is trading at around $490.

Let’s look at the Dow Jones Industrial Average since 1950.

We’ll note that in the post-War decades before Ronald Reagan assumed office, the stock market barely moved, either up or down. Then things changed, dramatically so. Stock prices began to rise every year in exponential fashion. They did so especially during Clinton’s last term.

Part of the reason for the change must surely be the amount of economic output going to capital and the declining fortunes of labor. With so much money consolidated in the hands of a few, where to put it? The rich, as a rule, don’t particularly care to put their dollars in mattresses. They want their cash to grow. So they invest, with the hope, really, that value appreciates.

Yet, investing in the stock market is not for the faint-hearted. If we plot the annual change in the Dow we get a chart like this:

That red line is the linear trend, which is about nine percent. If one has money to invest and can be patient, buying stocks eventually rewards the purchaser over time.

Now back to the stock market as a gross economic indicator. The economy continues to sputter; and since Reagan, annual growth rates have steadily declined. Here’s a chart comparing the changes in DJIA and real GDP.

While the market fluctuates markedly, real GDP not so much. However, and as I’ve noted previously, the trend in GDP is downward. Indeed, sputtering economies are the new norm.

Not much there to get excited about. And if my intuitions are correct, then I am not surprised that stock markets are also sputtering. The reigning pessimism is manifest in both economic output and Wall Street metrics.

My biggest concern is that the numbers will stay modest for a very long time. Should that occur, millions will continue to suffer for lack of work and thus income.

Yet the rich continue to do well, and since they’ve accumulated so much wealth they have acquired the political power to match. So they are not likely to advocate policies that will improve matters for the Rest of Us. Indeed, many of them have demonstrated an open hostility to “the moochers,” otherwise known as “the 47 percent.” At the very least, we’re irrelevant.

This is tragic, not only because so many continue to suffer, but also because of Americans’ profound ignorance. Consider this one simple statistic. Ninety percent of U.S. households have incomes below $100,000 a year. Yep. If your family draws down a bit more than that, you’re in the top 10 percent. Now that doesn’t seem like a whole lot of money to me. It does not bespeak tropical vacations or second homes along beaches. That kind of money is not usually associated with a Mercedes in the garage or fancy meals each week. We’re talking the basics and perhaps something left over for rainy days.

I suppose, since the Rest of Us outnumber the rich, that a more representative government could be had, one that worked for rather than against our interests. I’m not holding my breath.

Building less

January 14, 2013 by davald

Fewer people employed in construction. No surprise there, given the collapse of the housing bubble and the severe overhang of both stock and debt. We would also expect spending to be down in this sector as well.

Consider this excerpt from a recent study (pdf) on infrastructure spending:

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The goal of this report is to understand the short- and long-term effects of public infrastructure spending on the U.S. economy, as well as to contribute new suggestions towards alternative financing of future road construction.

Estimated Short-Run Effects

  •  In the short-run, a dollar spent on infrastructure construction produces roughly double the initial spending in ultimate economic output.
  •  The biggest effects of infrastructure spending occur in the manufacturing and business services sectors.
  • In better economic times, spending on infrastructure construction generates a larger return. Yet even in a recession, the overall effects of initial spending still double output as they ripple through the economy.

Estimated Long-Run Effects

  • Over a twenty-year period, generalized ‘public investment’ generates an accumulated $3.21 of economic activity per $1.00 spent.
  •  Over twenty years, investing $1.00 in highways and streets returns approximately $0.35 in tax revenue to federal and state/local governments, of which $0.23 specifically accrues at the federal level.
  • Over twenty years, investing $1.00 in sewer systems and water infrastructure returns a full $2.03 in tax revenue to federal and state/local governments, of which $1.35 specifically accrues at the federal level.

Despite the need for and desirability of spending on infrastructure, Republicans resist any and all fiscal stimulus. They say, in their defense, that they just don’t like government debt. Nor do they care much for government at all, save for the jobs they hold within it.

But look at the above numbers. Every dollar the government spends on building roads or schools, for example, adds two dollars to GDP. A stronger economy yields higher government revenues, thereby reducing deficits. Since Washington can borrow money at essentially zero cost to fund infrastructure improvements, we seem to have a no-brainer.

All of which suggests that Republicans lack more than a brain.

UK: a “natural” experiment

January 11, 2013 by davald

Britain’s political leaders assumed office with the stated intention of reining in debt. So they slashed federal funding. In so doing, they promised, the economy would actually grow. They relied on a firm belief that what causes output to expand is “confidence.” If capitalists were assured that the government would keep its fiscal house in order, then they would buy bonds and invest in productive pursuits.

Well, Cameron’s Tory regime definitely cut spending. But the promise of prosperity remains a very empty one.

Writing for the London Review of Books, John Lanchester calls the Cameron-Osborne policies “a failure.” (Subscription may be required to read article online.)

Saying ‘I told you so’ is supposed to be near unbeatable fun, so it’s disappointing to report that, in the case of the government’s handling of the British economy, speaking for myself, no fun is being had. As George Osborne’s autumn statement made clear, the scale and speed and completeness with which things are going wrong are numbing. The Tories went into the 2010 election with a manifesto commitment to reduce the structural deficit – the amount by which the government’s spending in any given year exceeds its income, excluding temporary effects from the downturn. The first point in their economic policy read as follows: ‘We will safeguard Britain’s credit rating with a credible plan to eliminate a large part of the structural deficit over a Parliament.’ How? It’s on the next page: ‘We will cut government spending to bring the deficit down and restore stability.’

I showed this chart previously. It compares real GDP of the UK and the U.S. since the Great Recession, or roughly the end of 2007. Lanchester cites the Office of Budget Responsibility’s figures, which show that the UK economy will contract again this year.

Lanchester discusses “the multiplier.” If the government spends a dollar, does the economy grow by that same amount, or does it either contract or expand by a greater amount? Likewise, if the government cuts spending, is there a one-to-one correspondence in GDP? The International Monetary Fund recently concluded that government austerity measures, in the UK especially, have a negative multiplier above unity. That is, for every dollar the government cuts spending, there is more than a dollar contraction of GDP.

In a previous post I included a link to a rough transcript prepared by Berkeley economist Brad DeLong of a four-member panel discussion. One of the members, economist Valerie Ramey, argued that government spending had a multiplier of less than one. Therefore, she concludes, fiscal stimulus would actually harm the economy. But if this were true, then how to explain what’s going on in the UK? Why hasn’t austerity done what its proponents promised? Could it be that the IMF is right and Ramey is wrong?

We can appreciate that the answer is important. If Ramey is correct, then the government really can’t do anything to stimulate the economy, contra John M. Keynes. If she is wrong and the multiplier is above unity, then the federal government can improve economic output. Or, as we’re seeing in Britain, contracting federal expenditures can actually retard GDP.

Lanchester provides an interesting discussion of a related topic, the velocity of money. Here’s how he tells it:

Imagine for a moment that you come across an unexpected ten pounds. After making a mental note not to spend it all at once, you go out and spend it all at once, on, say, two pairs of woolly socks. The person from the sock shop then takes your tenner and spends it on wine, and the wine merchant spends it on tickets to see The Bitter Tears of Petra von Kant, and the owner of the cinema spends it on chocolate, and the sweet-shop owner spends it on a bus ticket, and the owner of the bus company deposits it in the bank. That initial ten pounds has been spent six times, and has generated £60 of economic activity. In a sense, no one is any better off; and yet, that movement of money makes everyone better off. To put it another way, that first tenner has contributed £60 to Britain’s GDP. Seen in this way, GDP can be thought of as a measure not so much of size – how much money we have, how much money the economy contains – but of velocity. It measures the movement of money through and around the economy; it measures activity. If you had taken the same ten quid when it was first given to you and simply paid it into your bank account, the net position could be argued to be the same – except that the only contribution to GDP is that initial gift of £10, and if this behaviour were replicated across the whole economy, then the whole economy would grind to a halt. And that, broadly speaking, is what is happening right now. People are sitting on that first tenner.

I have suggested elsewhere that inequality reduces the velocity of money. The lower classes, in economic terms, tend to spend most, if not all, of what they make. The rich buy what they need and want and still have a surplus, which they park somewhere and otherwise withhold from circulation.

Here’s a chat that I’ve used before. It shows the Gini index and the velocity of money. As we see, as the country got poorer it spread less of its money around.

 

So, we could increase economic activity by reducing inequality. We could help reduce inequality by putting more people to work in better-paying jobs. Since the private sector isn’t doing very well on this front, we must turn to the government. We’ve got a lot of things that need fixing around here.

Taibbi reveals the lies

January 10, 2013 by davald

The inimitable Matt Taibbi chronicles the systematic lying and secretiveness of the government’s bailout of the financial industry. In his estimation, we’ve been duped, and the huge problems of that sector have only gotten worse as a result. He writes:

To listen to the bankers and their allies in Washington tell it, you’d think the bailout was the best thing to hit the American economy since the invention of the assembly line. Not only did it prevent another Great Depression, we’ve been told, but the money has all been paid back, and the government even made a profit. No harm, no foul – right?

Wrong.

It was all a lie – one of the biggest and most elaborate falsehoods ever sold to the American people.

Read it, if you dare.
