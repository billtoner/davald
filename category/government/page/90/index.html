Urban ugly

July 18, 2011 by davald

The German philosopher and psychologist Erich Fromm in his book Escape From Freedom posited two sorts of freedom: freedom from and freedom to. Of the former we might talk about protection or avoidance, as in freedom from tyranny. Of the latter we could mention speech or travel or any behaviors intended to fulfill one’s aspirations.

As I recall from reading the aforementioned book many years ago, Fromm was attempting to explain the German people’s attitudes that allowed for the rise of Nazism. Why Hitler? Fromm suggested that the Germans were nostalgic for the days of Bismarck, who had unified the German-speaking states, ruling with “blood and iron.” Following Germany’s defeat in WWI and the imposition of war reparations by the victors, Germans, Fromm believed, suffered humiliation and, perhaps, too much freedom. He likened Bismarck’s Germany to the womb, a metaphor for comfort and protection. The tradeoff was limited personal freedom. The establishment of parliamentary democracy (the Weimar Republic) assumed broad freedom to choose. The German people weren’t quite ready for it. Then along came a new Bismarck in the form of Adolf Hitler, permitting, if not also demanding, an escape from freedom.

Our constitutional democracy emphasizes freedom to, as even a cursory reading of the First and Second Amendments confirm. We believe that freedom to speak in whatever form is sacrosanct. Indeed, the Supreme Court has broadened that freedom of expression to include corporate campaign spending and selling violent video games to minors.

As for urban ugly the virtually unlimited right to free expression encompasses business operations, subject to vague zoning laws. So, in downtown Everett, where I currently reside, I get this just around the corner:

Aside from the death-inducing effects of tobacco, the storefront visually assaults the observer. We have in this instance the two forms of freedom discussed by Fromm: the proprietor has the freedom to place this ugly shop in proximity to my residence; yet I have been denied my freedom from having to experience this abomination on my daily walks.

In short, the wholesale embrace of the first freedom interferes with the second. The city has clearly given deference to the proprietor at my expense. My options are to endure or move, which are my freedoms, I suppose.

Quoth the curmudgeon.

We’d like everything for free, thank you.

July 13, 2011 by davald

The NY Times‘ David Leonhardt pens a nice column revealing Americans’ insatiable desire for free lunches. We like all the good stuff, and will resist efforts to diminish them, but we’re sure as heck not going to pay for them. He writes:

Unfortunately, this nostalgic view depends on a misunderstanding of the budget. It imagines a budget in which the United States indefinitely has the world’s highest medical costs, its largest military, an aging population and, nonetheless, taxes that are among the world’s lowest. Economists have a name for that combination: a free lunch.

Members of Congress behave irresponsibly, reflecting their constituencies. They evidently believe that they can get away with such gimmicks as pledging not to raise taxes or even allow shameful subsidies to elapse. Why? Besides strict adherence to a loopy ideology I think the principal reason is that each of them are nearly anonymous to the whole. That is, being one of 435, as in the House, or even one of 100, as in the Senate, provides cover for the ridiculous. No single member, after all, is responsible to the whole.

But simple logic tells us that the aggregation of individual irresponsibility yields collective irresponsibility. I suggest that the only individual in D.C. who may genuinely feel some responsibility to the whole—as in country—is the president. Yet he is clearly hamstrung by the collective irresponsibility of the other branches. He can’t accomplish much of anything without the backing of Congress; and this Congress has no intention of acceding to presidential victories.

An obvious example of the irresponsibility is screaming bloody murder about the horrific federal debt (not really) while simultaneously refusing to raise taxes that would eliminate the debt. (Recall that the country was on a path to retire its debt, had the Clinton-era tax rates continued.)

How far will Obama go?

July 7, 2011 by davald

The news disconcerts. Obama, upon meeting with key leaders in both houses and a private tête-à-tête with Speaker Boehner, offers to modify Medicare and Social Security, although details are sketchy. The Times reports:

The president’s renewed efforts follow what knowledgeable officials said was an overture from Mr. Boehner, who met secretly with Mr. Obama last weekend, to consider as much as $1 trillion in unspecified new revenues as part of an overhaul of tax laws in exchange for an agreement that made substantial spending cuts, including in such social programs as Medicare and Medicaid and Social Security — programs that had been off the table.

Recently, Mr. Boehner proclaimed that tax cuts are “off the table.” He appears to be more stubborn than the president in adhering to his principles, such as they are.

As for increasing revenues, it seems that Republicans are open to eliminating the egregious subsidies to oil and gas companies and even tax breaks for “corporate jets.” But there have been no reports of agreements to let the Bush-era tax cuts expire without renewal.

A few Democrats grumble on the expectation that they may be asked to simply accept the negotiated deal emerging from the White House. The Times:

House Democrats have their own fears about the negotiations, which they expressed in an hourlong meeting Wednesday night with Treasury Secretary Timothy F. Geithner.

“Depending on what they decide to recommend, they may not have Democrats,” Senator Sheldon Whitehouse, a Rhode Island Democrat, said in an interview. “I think it is a risky thing for the White House to basically take the bet that we can be presented with something at the last minute and we will go for it.”

We’ve got to hand it to the Republicans. Despite being in the minority for the first two years of Obama’s presidency, they managed to thwart the more ambitious Democratic proposals and sharply modify others, including the stimulus package, comprised mostly of GOP-cherished tax cuts. Now that they control the House, their harebrained ideas ascend to prominence; they now set the agenda, because of the Great Capitulator.

How about this? Bernie Sanders for president. Pass it on.

Now’s the time

June 29, 2011 by davald

In an ailing economy should public agencies commence capital projects? I say yes.

I’ll mention a couple of guiding principles in support of my opinion. First, insofar as a public agency (e.g., school districts, public utilities) is expected to be around for decades it helps to take a long-term view of capital projects. Second, there is a silver lining in depressed economic times—both capital and labor are cheap.

The Snohomish County PUD, of which I am one of three elected commissioners, decided to accelerate capital projects, those that were on the list to be completed sooner or later. The board judged that land, labor, and money were as inexpensive as they might be in the foreseeable future. Under the two guiding principles, the commissioners gave its approval to advance the utility’s capital plan. In the long run, the PUD will save money—in the millions of dollars.

The Everett school board is wrestling with a couple of capital projects, including a new and much-needed administration building. The other is a resurfacing of two high school tracks.

The Everett School Board appears to be one step away from approving a new $23.3 million administration building, a project that has been talked about off and on for years.

The proposed 62,000-square-foot building is by far the biggest of three construction projects expected to be voted on at the school board’s next meeting on July 5.

I say now’s the time.

Paying to save

June 21, 2011 by davald

Last night I had the honor of presenting the Marysville School District with a check for some $82,000. The district had cooperated with the PUD to design a more energy-efficient high school. The utility offered incentives to the district for the installation of better lighting and other devices. As I said to the school board, doing so saves money and the environment.

But before I gave my remarks I sat in the audience listening to several people making their case to the directors to save programs and rescind the transfer of an evidently beloved high school teacher.

The teacher, it seemed, had been involuntarily transferred from one school to another. A half-dozen or more of his students, sharply dressed, attended on his behalf. One from their ranks spoke admirably of the teacher, giving the rest of us in the audience reason to question the wisdom of the district’s personnel decision. I gathered that the transfer was a matter of numbers and seniority.

And the pain on display over program cuts is all about numbers. The district’s finance director presented a slide showing declining revenues from the state. He pointed out that the district faced another budget shortfall, this one over $3 million. Since the district cannot simply impose taxes, fees, or rates at its discretion, the only way to close the gap is by cutting further.

The district has already been forced to slash music programs, job counselors, physical education, among other cuts. Teachers have received layoff notices. The state budget calls for a reduction in the salaries of teachers and principals. District employees will be asked to pay more out of pocket for health and welfare benefits.

So, I could not help prefacing my prepared remarks with a sympathetic statement: “I don’t envy your challenge. With so many deserving demands on ever-dwindling resources it’s a shame that the school board lacks the same authority as the PUD board.”

We all clamor for more and better education. But we straitjacket school districts, denying them the resources to fulfill our wishes. If learning is so damn important, and I certainly believe that it is, then why do we routinely underfund it?

Were school districts governed like PUDs, we’d have topnotch educational programs. I guarantee it.

I guess I’m not alone

June 14, 2011 by davald

I happened upon David Brooks’s column this morning, by accident, I assure you. I know that I should not be afraid of words, including his, but the guy has a real knack for turning me off.

Yet, today he touches on a familiar theme in these posts: the absolute mess we’re in. Brooks offers a few ideas, consisting of baskets, mind you. But let’s explore, nonetheless.

He tells us that he’s going to cover the 18-month election cycle under protest. Why? Neither party has any real solutions, he avers, to very serious problems. How serious?

This election is about how to avert national decline. All other issues flow from that anxiety.

The election is happening during a downturn in the economic cycle, but the core issue is the accumulation of deeper structural problems that this recession has exposed — unsustainable levels of debt, an inability to generate middle-class incomes, a dysfunctional political system, the steady growth of special-interest sinecures and the gradual loss of national vitality.

Point conceded, as these pages attest.

As for the GOP, Brooks writes:

The Republican growth agenda — tax cuts and nothing else — is stupefyingly boring, fiscally irresponsible and politically impossible.

As for the Democrats, Brooks is equally critical:

…they offer practically nothing. They acknowledge huge problems like wage stagnation and then offer… light rail! Solar panels! It was telling that the Democrats offered no budget this year, even though they are supposedly running the country. That’s because they too are trapped in a bygone era.

For new and refreshing ideas Brooks would have us adopt the ways and means of—Alexander Hamilton! How long has he been dead?

Brooks, of course, will never bother to see how other countries are surviving the Great Recession and, in some cases, even prospering. Norway, for example, is actually running a budget surplus; and Brooks truly believes that our public and private debt is somehow at the root of our problems. Okay, then what is Norway doing different from the US?

Norway, along with most of western Europe, also spends less on health care as both a percentage of GDP and per capita than does the US, and Brooks wants us to know how important it is to rein in health costs. But surely Brooks, who is unabashedly “pro-market,” would not wish to adopt the mostly public health care systems of our counterparts across the pond. So, is he really serious, then?

Americans enjoy the lowest tax burdens of OECD countries. How much lower would the Republicans go? Their rhetoric suggests that they’ve only just begun in slashing taxes.

Brooks wants lower corporate taxes, although he says nothing here about personal income tax rates. If he’s worried about debt, shouldn’t he consider the revenue side for some relief, or does he really align himself with the GOP?

What should we make of this excerpt?

There would be an entitlement reform package designed to redistribute money from health care and the elderly toward innovation and the young.

Republicans, and far too many Democrats, like to combine the terms ‘entitlement’ and ‘reform.’ Those of us who have worked our butts off for most of our lives toiling at some obscure job that has paid in real dollars no more today than four decades ago might be forgiven for having a sense of “entitlement.” If a rich man invests in a company that does well, he will surely feel entitled to a benefit, in the form of either a robust dividend or rising stock price. Hey, he risked his money, he’ll tell you, so he deserves the reward when things go well. (And if he’s a big enough investor who has bet wrong, he feels equally entitled to your tax dollars to compensate for his losses. You remember all that, don’t you?) But if I’m a struggling schmuck who has earned enough to pay a mortgage, put food on the table, and help sustain the economy by buying stuff, I’d like to think that in my waning years I won’t have to eat dog biscuits or sell my house to pay for medical bills.

Brooks, however, wants to shift dollars from us old farts and our health care to the young and innovative. In a decent society, we’d surely avoid the unseemly tradeoff: we’d do whatever it took to ensure that the elderly have financial security and our youth have plenty of opportunities to prosper.

Perhaps I’m asking too much. By what manner of thinking should I dare suggest we become a decent society in which citizens have every right to and expectation of entitlements? That’s so terribly un-American.

The whole planet is my backyard

June 13, 2011 by davald

When toxic chemicals—or too much of a good thing, like carbon dioxide—enter the atmosphere, geographical boundaries cease to have meaning. Thus, a coal plant in China has almost the same effect on us in Puget Sound as Centralia.

Ironically, while Olympia passes a bill to eventually close down our state’s sole coal plant for environmental reasons, Washington may soon become a transportation hub for shipments of Wyoming coal to China. We’re told that it’s all about jobs.

But would we consent to creating jobs for the purpose of burning babies? I think not. That’s going too far, we’d say. Yet, coal-burning emissions surely cause human deaths, although we’re unable to write the words on toe-tags, “Death by coal.”

At some point, if it’s not already too late, more of us will have to accede to the mantra: The whole planet is my backyard. For increasingly it makes little difference where the bad stuff happens; whatever is emitted has equal-opportunity consequences.

The Whatcom County Council will render its decision soon as to whether it should allow Bellingham to host a terminal for China-bound coal. That city’s mayor is against it. But the complexion of the council, a majority committed to jobs, bodes ill for the planet, in general, and the citizens of that fair city, in particular.

For a thoughtful and well-written viewpoint on the topic, I commend this piece appearing in the Seattle Times.

As proposed, the Burlington Northern Santa Fe railroad will be transporting 48 million metric tons of the black rock every year — all the way from Wyoming, through Seattle, Edmonds, Mukilteo, Everett, Marysville, Stanwood and Burlington, then straight through our city center to a harbor just north of here called Cherry Point. From there, it is bound for China.

If you lined up all those coal cars for a year from end to end they’d stretch from Seattle to Beijing!

Oh, and someone will surely die. Would you trade babies for jobs?

Score one for “the public”

June 9, 2011 by davald

The insufferable David Brooks penned a recent NY Times column in which he blasted public provision of health care. For Brooks, the private sector does and would do it better. He was startling wrong in this, as many have since pointed out.

Of all places, the Economist does a fair number on Brooks’s point, treating us to a vivid example of why health care should not be subject to the ways and whims of the market.

Just think about this for a minute. A medical technology company is going public to generate the money it needs to advertise its products to hospital directors and insurance-company reimbursement officers. This entails significant extra expenditures for marketing, the new stocks issued to fund the marketing will ultimately have to pay dividends, banks will have to be paid to supervise the IPO that was needed to generate the funds to finance the marketing campaign (presumably charging the industry-cartel standard 7%)…and all this will have to be paid for by driving up the price the company charges to deliver its technologies. But beyond the added expense, why would anyone think that a system in which marketing plays such a large role is likely to be more effective, to lead to better treatment, than the kind of process of expert review that governs grant awards at NIH or publishing decisions at peer-reviewed journals? Why do we think that a system in which ads for Claritin are all over the subways will generate better overall health results than one where a national review board determines whether Claritin delivers treatment outcomes for some populations sufficiently superior to justify its added expense over similar generics? What do we expect from a system in which, as ProPublica reports today, body imaging companies hire telemarketers to sell random people CT scans over the phone?

Repeatedly we are told that America’s longterm fiscal well-being depends in large part on our ability to control health care costs. We know that we get far less bang for the buck in medical treatment than our counterparts in Canada and Europe. Indeed, unless we manage to rein in those costs, which continue to escalate exponentially, we’re either going to have to raise taxes considerably (don’t hold your breath on that one), dramatically reduce payouts to drug companies, doctors, and hospitals, or witness a rising sea of red ink on the government’s balance sheet.

The obvious solution to this mounting and severe problem is to jettison private insurance companies, as most reasonable countries have done. Adopting a “Medicare for all” program would bring us closer to the systems enjoyed by Canadians and Europeans.

Yes, I suppose, there are tradeoffs. Here, in market-dominated America, you can buy all the health care you need or want—provided you have the means to pay for it. As for the rest of us, take out your credit card, mortgage your house, or declare bankruptcy.

But why should basic health care be marketed like shoes?—wonders the Economist. Why, indeed.
