Tax and spend

November 9, 2011 by davald

I’m looking at our state budget, in particular the audited results of 2010. From whom does the state derive its revenues? Where do they go?

Let’s glance at the revenues first.

 

Are you surprised to learn that the state’s biggest source of revenue is from the federal government? It dwarfs all other categories. The 2010 figures, over $12 billion, represent a huge jump from previous years, which were a bit over $8 billion each year between 2001 and 2007, before the Great Recession. Yep. The big jump is a result of the federal stimulus program, much scorned by Republicans. The state sales tax accounted for 19% of total revenues in 2010.

Now let’s see where the money went.

 

No surprise that the biggest chunk was for education. It should also not surprise us that human services were a close second. In 2007, expenditures were just over $11 billion. In 2010 they’d jumped to almost $14 billion.

We here much chatter about the size of government. So what were the costs of running it? Would you believe only 4 percent of total expenditures?

Yet, too many people talk about shrinking the size of government. In this past weekend’s Viewpoints, state senator Steve Hobbs, who evidently is proud to call himself a “moderate” Democrat, and Rep. Deb Eddy, write:

We are constitutionally required to balance our budget, so the reductions are not optional at this point. The cuts hit educational support and safety-net programs particularly hard, because so much of the budget is considered off limits, a characterization that we believe needs to be challenged.

In the meantime, to balance the budget with cuts, more programs would have to go on the chopping block, while attempting to protect the most critical services.

They argue for a three-part strategy to place the state government on a sustainable path. These are: reduce the size of government; reform government through re-prioritization; and then, and only then, boost revenues.

But what to cut, especially when over three-quarters of the budget pays for education and human services? If education is cut further, there will be long-term consequences, all bad. To slash human services when so many of our citizens are suffering from unemployment and high medical bills is not only immoral but obscenely so. Moreover, any reductions in government spending, regardless of where, can only exacerbate this recessionary economy, marked by constrained aggregate demand. As Keynes argued, one doesn’t practice austerity during recessions, unless, I would add, you’re a fool.

Prudence and macroeconomic logic require one of two things: increased revenue or greater debt. They are not mutually exclusive.

Unfortunately, raising revenues, if done improperly, can further drag down the economy. We Washingtonians had an opportunity to do it right a couple of years ago: imposing an income tax on the wealthy. Since they spend a lower percentage of income on goods and services than the Rest of Us, their tax dollars would not reduce overall demand, at least not on a dollar-for-dollar basis. The Rest of Us, however, spend almost everything we make. Not necessarily because we want to but because we must in order to survive.

As for incurring more debt, that may be constitutionally impermissible, even if it might be what Dr. Keynes would prescribe in the short-term. In general, he was appalled by government debt over the long-run, and believed that all sovereign entities should avoid it in almost every circumstance—save during extraordinary periods. (On the other hand, if we can legally increase our borrowing, now is a very good time to do so, since interest rates are so low.)

We are in one of those extraordinary periods. The demand for human services increases during economic recessions, as one would expect. As I said above, millions of people can’t find a job, millions more are underemployed, and millions more suffer from mounting debt occasioned by medical costs with no health care insurance. To whom do these people turn? To us, the government. Boeing’s not going to ameliorate their conditions. Nor is Microsoft, or Amazon, or Paccar.

And speaking of those companies, John Burbank wrote a spot-on essay in this morning’s Herald. He first makes the case that all of these companies and more benefit from public services provided by our tax dollars.

What if you are a business like Boeing, anxious to bring in the next generation of workers as the baby boomers retire? The community colleges, like Edmonds, are training workers for aerospace. The University of Washington graduates a pool of engineers from which you can pick and choose. Highway 526 provides part of the transportation infrastructure to get workers and airplane components to Paine Field. All those things are paid for with taxpayer dollars.

Yet, Boeing paid no taxes. Indeed, as Burbank points out, the company received a massive subsidy from Olympia. (I remember this well, when Boeing was threatening to close down its Puget Sound plants. Boeing leaders had the audacity to demand that they get their electricity free, or at a substantial discount. Other PUD customers would have been forced to make up the difference.) Here’s Burbank:

So you might think that Boeing pays an appropriate amount of taxes for the things from which it benefits, like K-12 education, community colleges, highways, and the state patrol — but sadly, you’d be mistaken. Boeing bullied the Legislature into a $3.2 billion tax rebate package instead, which might be OK, if Boeing actually made the new 787 in Washington. Instead, they have contracted out production all over the world. And now they are building a new 787 site in South Carolina. So our tax give-away has worked out pretty well for them … but not for us!

The aforementioned companies are making profits. Yet they’re not paying taxes, here or to the US Treasury. Worse, as Burbank tells us, “On a three-year profit of almost $10 billion, Boeing actually received $177.6 million from the federal government.”

Burbank concludes:

It is time to turn off the spigot of taxpayer money flowing into already overstuffed corporate pockets. We need it for education, health care and transportation. Those are the public services we expect government to provide. We all benefit from them, and all who are able should help pay for them.

Another Amen.

Dreaming vs. doing

November 8, 2011 by davald

You may share my despair in our country’s inability to go beyond dreams. We have evidently lost the capacity to actually do. I’m not talking small, necessarily, but grand and, forgive me, wonderful.

I am of two minds on small vs. big, however. It may have to do with process. For example, Amory Lovins talks about a lot of smalls making a big. I get that in terms of energy development and consumption. I’m also open to the idea that a relatively small germ of an idea (say a modest dream) can serve as the genesis for something much bigger and, perhaps, better.

On the other hand, there was a time in this country when we could and did do big—from start to finish, from conception to execution. I think of the New Deal projects, for example, of which the Grand Coulee dam was just one. It certainly wouldn’t be built today. Yet generations of regional citizens and businesses continue to reap its benefits. I was alive to witness the great federal highway construction, although I judge it to have done more harm than good. Oh, we’ve always excelled at war making, the bigger and more expensive the conflict the better.

But bear with me a moment to consider an alternative idea on how our everyday lives could be different, with the complete understanding that none of it can be realized in our current fiscal and political climate—if ever. After all, we’re not French. Besides, we’ve grown bitterly cynical.

I bring France into this, prompted by an essay appearing in the Atlantic and featured on Crosscut. It was written by Seattle attorney Charles Wolfe. I was immediately struck by this image:

It’s a photo of a tram running through the southern French city of Nice. The cars themselves are sleek, pure, and functional, with lots of windows. Note, too, the grass strips between the rails. Nice touch, pun intended. Reasonable minds may disagree, but I find this image pleasing and attractive, enough to make me want to live nearby, say in one of the adjacent apartment buildings. I want to be closer, not farther away.

The tram is one part of a very extensive transit plan. A Google translation tells me that the embedded video was shown to Nice’s Community Council, which approved its portion of the plan in 2009. As you’ll note, a full buildout of the transportation system will connect several French cities along the Mediterranean and inland, including Alsace-Lorraine. (I gather from the palm trees that these places have temperate climates.)

The video includes futuristic images of multiple subways and overhead trams. All is neat and tidy, as are the human figures. The goal is to have everything in place by the year 2030. Good luck with that.

Such projects are difficult to accomplish, even in France. As I said above, they’re probably impossible here in the US.

The principal proffered reason would surely be economic. We simply can’t afford it, more so now than in better times. Yet, I have my doubts that we’ll ever see major public works projects in my lifetime if not also my children’s, even if the economy recovers.

I would suggest that the real reason that we can no longer do is that we’ve lost faith in government and in our ability to imagine together. It’s all about the individual rather than the collective we. Voters deny government sufficient revenues to properly fund the public sector, including schools and transportation. The only governmental activity we support is war, of which we can never get enough.

We’ve become mean, brutish, and narrow-minded. No permission is granted to dream to envision to wonder, from our schools to the councils of government. Work for “the man” or don’t work at all.

The rich have siphoned off all the money, and they don’t need trams or busses or public schools. And we said nothing.

Tragic.

Skipping civilization

October 31, 2011 by davald

For most of my adult life I have tended to socialize with like-minded people. Not surprising, since that socializing was the byproduct of working on peace and justice issues, which attract few, if any, conservatives.

More recently, however, I have been in the company of individuals espousing viewpoints that I simply don’t share. Among these are support for capital punishment, which I strenuously oppose, and lower taxes on the wealthy. Yes, you read that correctly.

On the latter point the argument I heard can be summed up neatly: taxes should be imposed on the basis of need. Since the rich, almost by definition, need fewer public services, they should be taxed at a lower rate than the poor, who rely more heavily on government programs.

Neat, but nevertheless bizarre.

I admit to being taken aback by such thinking, probably because I’m just not used to it. How should the argument be countered? Wherein lie the defects?

Having spent a bit of time on this, I’ve come up with an analogy, influenced by Prof. Krugman: the U.S. government is one large insurance company. Each of us in this arrangement, whether rich or poor, is subject to risks outside our control. To hedge against individual catastrophe, we pool our risks. We do so by paying taxes, with the implicit understanding that the revenues gained will be used to mitigate the suffering incurred by others.

The arrangement presupposes commonality. At the simplest level, we are of one species, homo sapiens, who inhabit a particular chunk of real estate. Yet there is something more, I suspect: we are in a fundamental sense co-equals.

The equality has little to do with outcomes, which are extreme in the U.S. Rather, we share the myth of equal opportunity. With a bit of luck and a whole lot of pluck we can become the next Bill Gates or Warren Buffet.

In reading the Walter Isaacson biography I’m struck by how many fortuitous events conspired to yield the person of Steve Jobs: his being adopted into a home in which the father manifested an unusual concern for detail and aesthetics in his work; trekking up to Reed College, only to drop out so that he could audit classes he liked, one of which was on calligraphy and type; his accidental pairing with Steve Wozniak, whose talents complemented Jobs’s; his ouster from Apple, the company he established with Woz, which led to his developing the NeXT computer operating system that later served as the kernel for Mac OS X and buying Pixar from George Lucas; that the Apple board would agree to purchase NeXT and bring Jobs back to Apple; and so on.

As it happened, the innate character of Jobs enabled him to exploit these serendipitous events for personal and corporate gain. He died a very wealthy man, though not the richest in the cemetery. But we also know, as Steve Jobs knew, that things could have turned out very differently for him, including his becoming a starving artist. There might not have been an iPod, an iPhone, iTunes, the Mac, or the iPad.

All of us have read accounts of individuals whose lives did not turn out well at all. The Great Recession has produced millions of sad stories.

A few weeks ago I happened to overhear a middle-aged man in the grocery store tell a young clerk that he had lost his job, his pension, his wife, and his house. He said that he had a good, steady position that paid well. When the economy tanked, so did his life.

The man was now at the mercy of government programs, including Social Security and perhaps Welfare. The funding of the programs he desperately needed comes from all of us via taxes.

We cannot legislate sympathy. But upon some moment of reflection we should arrive at an appreciation for those whose fortunes have soured. It could happen to any of us.

My contrary friends presume that the incomes of the rich are justly deserved. Thus, taxation amounts to theft. The government has no legitimate claim to the money earned by its citizens. By extension, the poor have no claims against the rich.

In a recent essay Gar Alperovitz writes:

A half-century ago, in 1957, economist Robert Solow showed that nearly 90 percent of productivity growth in the first half of the 20th century alone, from 1909 to 1949, could only be attributed to technical change in the broadest sense. The supply of labor and capital – what workers and employers contribute – appeared almost incidental to this massive technological “residual.” (Solow received the Nobel Prize for this and related work in 1987.) Another leading economist, William Baumol, calculated that “nearly 90 percent … of current GDP [gross domestic product] was contributed by innovation carried out since 1870.”

The truly central and demanding question is obviously this: If most of what we have today is attributable to knowledge advances that we all inherit in common, why, specifically, should this gift of our collective history not more generously benefit all members of society? The top 1 percent of US households now receives far more income than the bottom 150 million Americans combined. The richest 1 percent of households owns nearly half of all investment assets (stocks and mutual funds, financial securities, business equity, trusts, nonhome real estate). A mere 400 individuals at the top have a combined net worth greater than the bottom 60 percent of the nation taken together. If America’s vast wealth is mainly a gift of our common past, how, specifically, can such disparities be justified?

All of us, not just Steve Jobs, are products of an endless string of fortuitous events beyond our control. Some will wind up doing well; others not so much. In no case do individuals deserve their fates.

A civilized society would have the winners compensating the losers via tax-funded government programs. Perhaps the problem here is that we’re not all that civilized. Oscar Wilde reckoned so:

America is the only country that went from barbarism to decadence without civilization in between.

Liquidity traps

October 10, 2011 by davald

I must admit to some confusion regarding diagnoses of and prescriptions for economic conditions. I am hardly alone, of course, since the economics profession as a whole is so sharply disagreeable.

If you read Paul Krugman’s blog, for example, you will be exposed to much talk of liquidity traps. He argues that we’re in one. If we are, then monetary policies won’t work. He writes:

By contrast, the model of an economy in a liquidity trap, in which big increases in the monetary base don’t matter, comes through just fine.

He includes this chart in a recent post:

But tripling the money supply has had no effect on GDP.

If you do a search of ‘liquidity trap’ you will see multiple references to John Maynard Keynes as the originator of the concept, although he did not use the term in his General Theory. Keynes focused on uncertainty in Chapter 12 of his opus. Investors have little if any confidence that people will buy products and services, so they hoard their money. Consumers may have even less confidence, since so many of them are out of work and those who are working have seen their incomes decline. Moreover, they are using what little discretionary dollars they have to pay down their considerable debts.

If we are indeed in a liquidity trap right now, then expanding the money supply will not help grow the economy. Indeed, that is the very definition of ‘liquidity trap.’

Yet there is considerable confusion, as I said above, about conditions and their cure. Much of the confusion involves the notion of liquidity trap. In particular, are there lessons to be learned from the Great Depression that can be applied to present circumstances?

Christine Romer wrote a paper in 1992 entitled “What ended the Great Depression?” She concluded:

Plausible estimates of the effects of fiscal and monetary changes indicate that nearly all the observed recovery of the U.S. economy prior to 1942 was due to monetary expansion. A huge gold inflow in the mid- and late 1930s swelled the money stock and stimulated the economy by lowering real interest rates and encouraging investment spending and purchases of durable goods.

If she is correct, then there was no liquidity trap in the 1930s, since a growing money supply spurred economic growth, albeit slowly and modestly. But insofar as economists and policy makers insist that there was a liquidity trap during the Great Depression then they will be loath to advocate monetary expansion, since, be definition, it wouldn’t do any good.

But it seems that the Federal Reserve does not believe that we’re in a trap, since they have tripled the money supply, no doubt expecting GDP growth.

Well, if we really are in a trap, as Krugman et al aver, then alternatives to monetary expansion will have to be investigated and applied. The Keynesians, and Krugman is a proud one, push for a massive fiscal stimulus—upwards of two trillion or more. Such government spending won’t crowd out private investments, the usual rejoinder to fiscal strategies, since it’s clear that the rich are simply hoarding their dollars. They do not lack for money; they’re just unwilling to invest it in productive enterprise.

Meanwhile, the Rest of Us struggle as we wait for the economists and policymakers to fix the mess. Speaking of confidence…

The obvious problem in the economy, including the low rate of start-ups that is troubling Samuelson, is a lack of demand. This should scare you

September 27, 2011 by davald

Health insurance premiums are soaring. If you’re lucky enough to have a job and your employer provides medical insurance, his or her annual cost has doubled in the last 10 years, from around $7,000 per annum to roughly $15,000 today. But hold on. That annual premium is expected to double again by the end of the next decade.

These frightening stats come by way of the Kaiser Family Foundation’s annual survey. (You can download the full report here.) The survey also found that employees are having to foot more of the bill. Couple that inconvenience with stagnant or falling wages, and we can appreciate that a worker’s take-home pay has declined, which also means that the worker is spending less on other things, which helps explain why the economy continues to sputter.

What I’ve never understood is why the business community and far too many of us oppose universal health care systems. They really do save money. And they relieve businesses of the complex and dreary task of managing employees’ insurance. For that matter, purveyors of medical care should also welcome universal health care; they wouldn’t have to fill out those endless forms for an unwieldy array of different insurers.

So, if a universal health care system is better than our private, market-based system, why don’t we have one? Simple. It’s called bribery, and it’s legal.

The job of members of Congress is to stay in office. To do so, they must raise and spend a boatload of dollars each election cycle. Who’s got the money? Corporations and rich individuals, many of whom are in the health insurance and delivery businesses. They buy Congress.

And they also fund huge propaganda campaigns to convince us that we have the best health care system in the world; anything else is just “socialism.” We believe this crap, which is why we re-elect Senator X.

End recessions

September 18, 2011 by davald

In his most recent column David Brooks avers:

The Democrats, besotted by the myth that the New Deal ended the Great Depression, have consistently overestimated their ability to turn the economy around.

If Brooks has in mind FDR’s fiscal strategy in the form of public works spending, then he can cite Christine Romer’s paper “Ending the Great Depression.” She concluded that fiscal policies had little effect on the recovery, although they obviously put people to work who would otherwise be on the streets.

However, if he believes that no federal policy during the New Deal helped end the GD, Romer would object—strenuously so. Indeed, the federal government can, and she would add should, intervene to stimulate recovery.

So, what did end the Great Depression? Romer concludes that a huge expansion in the money supply, facilitated by the U.S. Treasury, drove real GDP upwards. The mechanism was a large influx of “unsterilized” gold fleeing war-torn Europe, which in turn occasioned monetary expansion. Romer writes:

…the war may have aided the recovery after 1938by causingthe U.S. money supplyto grow rapidly. Thus, World War II may indeed have helped to end the Great Depression in the United States, but its expansionary benefits worked initially through monetary developments rather than through fiscal policy.

An important takeaway is that deep recessions place downward pressure on prices (deflation), causing a decline in economic output. (Wage decreases reduce aggregate demand.) The best, if not only, way out is through inflation, the result of monetary expansion. Again, Romer:

The very rapid growth of the money supply beginning in 1933 appears to have lowered real interest rates and stimulated investment spending just as a conven- tional model of the transmission mechanism would predict. The money supply grew rapidly in the mid- and late 1930s because of a huge unsterilized gold inflow to the United States. Although the later gold inflow was mainlydue to political developments in Europe, the largest inflow occurred immediately following the revaluation of gold mandated by the Roosevelt administration in 1934. Thus, the gold inflow was due partly to historical accident [the war] and partly to policy. The decision to let the gold inflow swell the U.S. money supply was also, at least in part, an independent policy choice. The Roosevelt administration chose not to sterilize the gold inflow because it hoped that an increase in the monetary gold stock would stimulate the depressed economy.

If Romer is correct—and who is David Brooks to doubt her?—then it would certainly seem that the federal government, through either the Federal Reserve or perhaps also the Treasury, has some tools to turn this damn thing around. The nice aspect to this approach is that it would not require bribing the bastards in Congress.

As for Brooks wanting to “reform” entitlements, you know where I stand.

You’re not making enough money

September 16, 2011 by davald

You may hear “experts” talk about structural unemployment, which is kind of code for inflexible wages, which, in turn, means that we’re making too much money. Gosh, we could employ everyone if we’d work for nothing.

But I have a different idea. The problem in America and elsewhere is that wages are too low. The steady gains made by workers after WWII suddenly stopped in the 70s, leading to the Great Divergence. Yes, the rich got richer and the poor got poorer. That’s a problem, and here’s why.

The Rest of Us spend most of the money we make. The rich, on the other hand, are too busy enhancing their portfolios to invest in productive enterprises. And they can only spend so much to meet their needs and satisfy their wants; the rest is surplus, and it’s mostly surplus. Contrary to Speaker Boehner’s b.s., the rich are not “job creators.” They’re money hoarders who don’t give a damn about the Rest of Us.

If the Rest of Us made more money we’d actually get the economy humming again. Also, we’d be paying more taxes, helping governments fund education, build transportation systems, clean the air and water, and so on.

Okay. How do we get more money?

Well, the rich have cornered the market on stealing it. We’ll have to rely on legitimate means.

We could, of course, simply shut down the economy with a massive withdrawal of labor. Not going to happen? I hear you.

The wonkheads, including Ben Bernanke and Paul Krugman, have written that the central bank can and should target a higher inflation rate. Today that target is around two percent. But because we’re in a liquidity trap, a higher inflation rate would lead to higher wages—and prices, too.

We could form and join unions, which should deliver higher wages. Moreover, higher union participation rates correlate with greater wealth equality. (Note: The higher the Gini index the greater the inequality.)

We could demand that Congress increase taxes on capital gains and otherwise deter non-productive uses of money. While they’re at it, the senators and representatives should restore marginal tax rates to the levels associated with the era of economic growth, namely the 50s and 60s. Huh? Higher tax rates correlate with a rising GDP? It seems so.

By the way, a growing economy yields higher wages. So whatever strategies designed to stimulate the economy should be employed. And, no, it’s not lower taxes and fewer regulations, pace Boehner. Indeed, it’s just the opposite.

The Great Divergence and the withering of empathy

September 13, 2011 by davald

We are, of course, entitled to our own opinions. Some might go further to argue that we’re equally entitled to our own facts, which belies ignorance of the the term ‘fact.’ However, it’s always been the case, and seemingly more pronounced in these times, that passions rule reason, as Hume suggested. In other words, what we may lack between our ears is more than offset by the quantity of our guts.

In response to my recent post on “So Why is America So Great?,” a reader offered these words:

Okay, best national defense in the history of earth.  That’s a plus, though it is a bloated bureaucracy.

This is obfuscation.  The issue is medical care, not health care.  There’s more MRI machines in Philadelphia than in all of Canada and England . . . combined.

The richest (owned houses, cars tv’s, cable, autos, electronics, health) and fattest “poor” in the history of humankind, and with a standard of living higher than the average (not “poor”) person in, say, Paris or Rome or Madrid.

The rich keep doing what made them rich and the poor (the few who remain such for as much as a decade) keep doing what made them poor.

My congratulations and admiration to the billionaires.  keep up the good work.

I really don’t know what to make of this. But it’s clear that the writer and I live on different moral planets. It also seems as if presidential candidate Ron Paul lives on the same planet as the commenter.

Last night emcee Wolf Blitzer asked the congressman, “Are you saying that society should just let him [a quite sick man near death who opted not to buy private health insurance] die?” Several members of the audience yelled out, “Yes!”

Despite having studied several years’ worth of philosophy, I would not expect to put forth an argument that would persuade Mr. Paul and the commenter to change their minds. Rather, I suspect that we either have empathy or we don’t. It’s not so much a matter of the gut as a function of two other factors: evolutionary biology and early childhood development.

I read the Wikipedia entry on empathic concern. Consider this excerpt from the article:

Human beings are strongly motivated to be connected to others.[4] In humans and higher mammals, an impulse to care for offspring is almost certainly genetically hard-wired, although modifiable by circumstance.

To be fair to Congressman Paul, in answering the question he recounted his professional medical practice and charitable works. He implied that he cares about others. But he also said something to the effect that freedom means assuming personal responsibility for your own welfare and not relying on “socialism.”

One common thread that runs through all of the Republican candidates for president is abhorrence for governmental programs, especially public social programs like Medicare, “Obamacare,” and even Social Security, although there seems to be some retrenchment from the latter after Texas governor Rick Perry called it a “lie” and “a Ponzi scheme.” And while Ron Paul is decidedly libertarian in his views, which places him a bit more out there than others in his cohort, he believes with his competitors that government should keep its hands off the private sector, which brings me back to my commenter and his praise of billionaires.

I happen to think that highly unequal societies—and the U.S. is at or near the top of this category—are unhealthy societies. There is considerable research to support this view. At the forefront of this research is Prof. Richard Wilkinson of the UK. With Kate Pickett, a professor of epidemiology, he has written his latest book, entitled The Spirit Level: Why Greater Equality Makes Societies Stronger. Here’s an excerpt from the dust jacket:

Further, more unequal societies are bad for everyone within them—the rich and middle class as well as the poor.

Interesting. As it happens, inequality is bad for your health, even Mr. Paul’s and Mr. Buffett’s.

Wilkinson and Pickett show that inequality tracks with the following syndromes:

  • obesity rates
  • morbidity rates
  • mortality rates
  • and a host of psychological diseases.

Consistent readers of my posts know that I focus on inequality. For example, I recently wrote about what I call the ‘Great Divergence.’ It began in the 7os and has continued unabated, enlarging the gap between the extremely rich and the Rest of Us. (Coincidentally, the Washington Post has begun a series on inequality, replete with charts and graphs.)

I am deeply disturbed by the brazen selfishness of the Tea Party, as evinced by members of the debate audience. Likewise, all of the Republican presidential hopefuls exhibit what I believe to be a perverse disregard for the wellbeing of others. We will recall that at the previous Republican debate some in the audience cheered governor Perry’s unseemly record of state executions.

Robert Reich, now teaching at my alma mater, talks about America being two societies. One of the very rich and the other populated by the Rest of Us. (He also wrote the foreword to the Wilkinson book.) The Great Divergence may have already reached a point of no return. The very rich and their odd supporters have become increasingly inured to the misfortune of others, no doubt for a very simple reason: they have no exposure to the Rest of Us. There is no overlapping of these disparate societies. Yes, we know of the rich. We see them on TV and in magazines. But they do not, perhaps cannot, know us.

And that’s a problem, because the rich have all the money, and money translates into political power, which is put to the further service of the rich, and so on. Yet, rather than inducing a revolution we get “admiration.”

I don’t get it.
