Neglect

December 9, 2012 by davald

The next time you hear someone proclaim America (is) first, consider the state of our nation’s public infrastructures. You will quickly disabuse yourself of sharing the hoary sentiment.

The New York Times editors provide some measure of how bad things are with the country’s roads, sewer systems, and other public works. We are behind trillions of dollars in making systems safe and reliable, including the electric grid.

Yet, the conservatives have been so successful in demonizing government and its obligations that the Obama administration and dozens of Democratic governors dare not present a realistic proposal to fix the nation. For example:

When the president asked recently for a modest $50 billion for transportation improvements in the “fiscal cliff” talks, Republicans literally laughed out loud. There will be no stimulus in any deal, said Representative Bill Shuster of Pennsylvania, the incoming chairman of the Transportation and Infrastructure Committee.

The amount, the editors said, would amount to only a “modest downpayment” on what’s really needed, more than $2 trillion just to restore public transportation to working order. Forget about high-speed rail or state-of-the art schools.

England proves Keynes right

December 7, 2012 by davald

John Cassidy exposes the UK’s horrible dalliance with austerity. It doesn’t work, just as John Maynard Keynes said some 80 years ago. Cassidy:

One of the frustrations of economics is that it is hard to carry out scientific experiments and prove things beyond reasonable doubt. But not in this case. Thanks to Osborne’s stubborn refusal to change course—“Turning back would be a disaster,” he told Parliament—what has been happening in Britain amounts to a “natural experiment” to test the efficacy of austerity economics. For the sixty-odd million inhabitants of the U.K., living through it hasn’t been a pleasant experience—no university institutional-review board would have allowed this kind of brutal human experimentation. But from a historical and scientific perspective, it is an invaluable case study.

The country’s chancellor of the Exchequer, in both announcing the austerity measures and stubbornly sticking to them, promised a far better outcome. According to Cassidy, “Osborne said that his austerity policies would cut his country’s budget deficit to zero within four years, enable Britain to begin relieving itself of its public debt, and generate healthy economic growth.”

Oh, and don’t listen to the Republicans on this side of the pond.

Crisis mongering

December 7, 2012 by davald

Not a day goes by that I don’t see a headline in the papers warning of “the debt crisis” or the dire repercussions of “the fiscal cliff.” As economists Dean Baker and Paul Krugman remind us, it’s one thing for us ordinary folks to be ignorant and quite another for media editors and fact-checkers to go AWOL.

Both link us to a Business Insider poll revealing the level of citizen misunderstanding regarding the fiscal cliff, with nearly half the respondents, a significant plurality, believing that if Congress fails to act before the appointed deadline, federal deficits and, therefore, debts, will rise.

Krugman takes the opportunity to deride the Washington Post for its blatant bias on fiscal policies, with the newspaper trumpeting austerity measures for some time now. He writes:

So what’s going on with the subhed? The answer is that the Very Serious People — and there’s nothing as VSP as the WaPo — have spent years crying Deficits! Debt! Danger!, and staff at the Post can’t wrap their minds around the fact that suddenly it’s a too-rapid fall in the deficit that has those very same People terrified.

It speaks to the state of confusion that all the deficit fearmongering has created. And if headline writers at a major newspaper can’t get it straight, how can you expect ordinary voters to get it?

Dean Baker, by the way, performs a much-needed public service by combing media headlines and articles in pursuit of the misleading and just plain wrong. His blog, Beat the Press, routinely exposes sloppiness and, in some cases, egregious errors committed by the Washington Post, in particular, and major newspapers, in general. He’s especially fond of picking on New York Times‘ columnist David Brooks for his fatuous pontification.

There is a larger problem, of course. Those of us who read rely on the media for our sources of information. If the media get it wrong, and we’ve failed to intellectually arm ourselves against falsehoods and misrepresentations, then we also get it wrong. Should too many of us live in ignorance, we’re likely to vote for Republicans. And that’s a very bad thing.

Pay me now or pay me later

December 6, 2012 by davald

The New Yorker‘s James Surowieki offers the Holland experience for a guide to disaster preparedness. Billions of dollars could be saved by favoring storm prevention over after-the-fact restoration. But that’s not the American way. He writes:

Politically speaking, it’s always easier to shell out money for a disaster that has already happened, with clearly identifiable victims, than to invest money in protecting against something that may or may not happen in the future.

Did you see that Obama is asking Congress for $50 billion for Sandy repairs?

Surowiecki adds that Americans are loath to adequately fund infrastructure in general. For whatever defect in our collective psyche, we prefer the erosion of bridges, roads, and schools to extracting a bit more from the rich.

Strange, we are.

Starve the recession

December 6, 2012 by davald

Or something like that.

Conservatives and too many Democrats who want to be taken seriously (by whom?) resist the temptation to spend more federal dollars on economic recovery. First, they don’t believe it works. Second, with so much debt, why create more of it?

This sounds plausible, doesn’t it? If we find ourselves spending more than we take in, we may take the necessary steps to curb our appetites, since it’s far more difficult to raise income than cut expenditures.

But what appears prudent for the individual consumer is likely counterproductive if too many of us behave stringently at once. As I’ve mentioned several time on this blog, decreasing aggregate demand under recessionary conditions actually makes matters worse: we don’t buy, so producers don’t sell, which means they don’t hire or lay off the people they already have, further eroding incomes and thus the capacity to spend. It really can become a vicious circle.

So, if you are at all familiar and comfortable with Keynesian economic thinking, you know that the federal government must step in. To do what? Spend money, even if it means adding to the deficit.

I’ve seen a few pieces recently that predict protracted economic doldrums. The gross domestic product will likely grow by a percentage point or just a bit more. That’s hardly enough to thin out the reserve army of the un- and underemployed.

And that’s a real problem going forward. There are millions of us who have been out of work for far too long. And when we’ve been sitting on the sidelines month after month employers pass us over to select new entrants into the workforce, if they hire at all.

Paul Krugman links us to a paper (pdf) published by the Boston Federal Reserve. Its authors conclude:

Considering all the evidence together, we conclude that the current outward shift of the Beveridge curve is likely being driven by something other than a mismatch between workers’ skills and the demands of available jobs.

The Beveridge curve is an observed correlation between job vacancies and unemployment. Here’s a chart from Wikidpedia:

As you can see, things get a bit out of whack in recent years, which is what the authors are talking about. They surmise:

One reason why the Beveridge curve relationship for the long-term unemployed has apparently shifted may be a change in the desirability of the long-term unemployed to employers.

That’s not good. Those who have been out of work the longest need income the most. And this does not bode well for future economic growth, which is why fiscal policies must be enacted soon.

Of course, the Republican answer to any problem is to cut taxes on the wealthy and screw the Rest of Us. Not what the economic doctor ordered.

Disparate views

December 5, 2012 by davald

Following the 1994 Republican sweep of Congress Berkeley linguist and cognitive scientist George Lakoff was baffled and bewildered. How could so many voters turn against their own political and economic interests to put so many conservatives in control? What was going on? So he set out to make some sense of the electoral tsunami.

Relying on metaphors, which, he believes, dominate our thinking about the world, Lakoff wrote a short book entitled Moral Politics. Therein he first confessed his profound disorientation following the November 1994 debacle. He then offered his observation that the two ends of our mainstream political spectrum talk past one another. Why is that? Lakoff surmised that each group operates on different sets of metaphors.

Grossly put, conservatives are imbued with a Strict Father metaphor; liberals, a Nurturant Parent. The former is all about the individual and the need to survive in a competitive world. Thus, the virtues of self-reliance, discipline, and fortitude are both praised and inculcated. Conservatives are more likely to be religious, tribal, and practice tough love amidst a rigid family hierarchy led by a strict father, of course. Governments frustrate this “natural order.”

On the other hand, liberals emphasize collective values, including community, cooperation, and collaboration. Raising a family is more about shared responsibilities, compassion, and positive guidance. The parents’ role is to nurture the child. Thus, the state, or government, should be a means to ensure that all citizens can meet their basic human needs.

Whether or not one subscribes to Lakoff’s taxonomy, we appreciate that political discourse in America (and surely elsewhere) is sharply divided, with both sides (in the U.S. we’re largely confined to two) apparently inhabiting not just a different worldview but a different planet altogether. The differences play out most profoundly in our nation’s capital. The Republicans treat Obama’s proposals with disdain and derision; Obama sticks to his guns—so far.

I certainly share Lakoff’s befuddlement. I have struggled to understand why so many people who are decidedly removed from the One-percent nevertheless vociferously defend privilege and plutocracy. (I’ve written about my struggles in previous posts, of which this is just one instance.) But if Lakoff is right about the importance of metaphors in how we see the world and react to it, then I’m not at all optimistic that the two sides will ever “just get along.”

For example, a letter to the local newspaper this morning called into sharp relief the fundamental divisions in our society. The writer has clearly picked up on Mitt Romney’s theme about nearly half of all Americans being “hooked on entitlements.” Consider the final paragraph:

Many Americans don’t aspire to be rich, but sadly many of these same Americans don’t want anyone to be wealthy either. The “pursuit of happiness” is no longer a valid American dream. Everything is to be shared equally since we supposedly contribute equally. This insidious shift to the left in not ugly for political reasons; it is ugly because we no longer hold in high regard the hard work and personal risks individuals take to get ahead in this country.

Though the writer stops short of mentioning the name, he clearly has in mind Karl Marx, that avatar of all things evil and pernicious. It was Marx who preached “from each according to his ability and to each according to his need.” How might this be accomplished in a modern capitalist economy? Tax the rich and give the proceeds to the poor—that is, redistribute the wealth.

Ah, taxes. In her lengthy essay in the November 26, 2012, issue of The New Yorker professor Jill Lepore chronicles America’s history of and debate over the income tax. After the federal income tax was established in the early 20th century, conservatives launched repeated efforts to overturn the enabling legislation. Lepore writes:

The campaign for repeal was remade to fit the Cold War. In 1948…Bertrand Gearhart, a congressional Republican from California, said on the House floor that Karl Marx was “the real father, the first real proponent of the income tax as we now know it” and that the Sixteenth Amendment [establishing the income tax] was “un-American.

I have my own confession. I am not rich, nor do I aspire to be so. Yet, contrary to the letter writer, I neither belittle nor begrudge “the hard work and personal risks individuals take to get ahead in this country.” Where I differ is that such hard work and personal risk can only find effect under a state with a long history of viable governance. In other words, individual wealth cannot occur in a vacuum. It requires an effective legal system that ensures property rights. It requires public infrastructures, including transportation and telecommunications, to facilitate the flow of goods and services. It requires an education system to produce competent workers of all stripes. We could go on.

That state necessary to protect, if not create, wealth in turn requires adequate revenues. How can the state acquire them? Taxes.

Instead of viewing taxes as confiscatory, the reasonable rich appreciate that they are crucial to their wellbeing, and without which they could not prosper. I have in mind Warren Buffett or Bill Gates, Sr., who readily acknowledge the importance of a well-functioning state and are willing to pay for it.

But I doubt that the letter writer would be susceptible to “unlearning.” I suspect that he holds firmly to his metaphors and, therefore, his prejudices. He clings to his mistaken belief that increasing taxes on the wealthy, as Obama proposes to do, is tantamount to making everyone financially equal, regardless of one’s contribution.

Besides, the rich did quite well under a higher-tax regime. They even prospered when the marginal tax rate exceeded 90 percent. Again, reasonable wealth at least nods in the direction of former justice Oliver Wendell Holmes, who wrote: “Taxes are what we pay for civilized society, including the chance to insure.”

Bipartisan deficits and a therapeutic rant

December 4, 2012 by davald

Let’s face it, the federal deficit owes its existence to both political parties. We can see this vividly in the following chart, with the Clinton era serving as a clear anomaly:

For all the talk of conservative rectitude in financial matters, Republicans, beginning in earnest with Ronald Reagan, embraced policies that pushed revenues far below spending. The principal culprit, of course, is tax cuts, mostly for the wealthy.

There was a time in my life that the rich behaved themselves, keeping their money relatively inconspicuous. That began to change under Reagan, certainly, when the wealthy decided that they would strut their stuff and demand that the Rest of Us not only acknowledge their financial success but actually praise their very being. They were better than us, don’t you see? We were somehow expected to emulate, knowing full well that we’d never join them in the socioeconomic stratosphere. After all, they had rigged the game against us.

Within this perverse narrative taxes were viewed by the privileged as impugning their greatness, an attempt to sap them of their potency, the very stuff that allowed the Rest of Us to exist, and thank you very much, dear plutocrat. Of course, the narrative was deeply flawed, as even a cursory glance at the numbers would reveal. As they finagled Congress, extracting a litany of concessions, they did not, as it happened, improve matters for the Rest of Us.

They stopped employing us, and when they did in decreasing numbers, they squeezed our paychecks and upped our contributions to “benefits,” which diminished in quantity and quality. Good luck getting your retirement after dozens of years working for the good of GM or HP. And when we grumbled and dared to even mention union, they shuttered the doors, giving our jobs to the poor slobs living in right-to-work states. When those wages, however minuscule, adversely impacted the corporate bottom line, the jobs were sent oversees to some foreign land that many of us had difficulty locating on a map. For all this, they received Congress’ blessing. Not surprising—the rich bought and paid for its members.

But the bastards never sleep. Greed is good and shall not be inhibited. If a million dollars feels good, a billion more is just plain exquisite. And it’s always about more.

So, the Republicans push to further the plutocratic agenda. Increase taxes on the rich? How “la-la-land,” says their speaker. Cut spending, is the refrain. You’re just wasting all this money on moochers, enabling sloth and dependency.

The beatings will continue until the blood runs dry.

Politics and the poor

December 4, 2012 by davald

The Republicans’ counter to Obama’s fiscal proposal , while short on details, essentially sticks to a recurring theme: coddle the rich, screw the Rest of Us. Here’s the New York Times‘ editorial board:

It would cut $1.2 trillion in spending, half of which would come from Medicare, Medicaid, and other health programs, including an increase in the Medicare eligibility age to 67.

So far, Obama has resisted the familiar tendency to compromise, though that could change, which would not bode well for the poor and much of the steadily shrinking middle class. The economic divide, what I call ‘the Great Divergence,’ has gotten only wider over recent decades, much to the evident delight of conservatives. They would continue their own brand of terrorism, impoverishing more while further enhancing the fortunes of the rich.

The percentage of Americans who live below 125 percent of the poverty threshold remans stubbornly high. Were it not for social insurance spending (Social Security, Medicare, and Medicaid) the rate would be even higher.

We’ll note that spending has actually declined since 2007. Here’s a chart showing the decrease.

Yet, Republicans want to spend even less. Their rationale, as far as I can tell, is that they do not want to increase the budget deficit. So why not raise taxes to generate more revenue? Here is where the moral values argument enters the picture.

When Mitt Romney told a gathering of über wealthy that nearly half the population would vote for Obama, because they are dependent on government handouts, the message was obvious to the assembled few, who have taken to the view that the current administration scolds rather than praises them. Without nary a wink and a nod Romney told audiences during the campaign that he wanted more Americans to share in his prosperity; and they would have that opportunity if only Washington would get out of their way. Remove the safety net (a term used by Ronald Reagan) and millions of Americans would begin to ape the virtues and appetites that made Romney et al. rich.

After rigging the economy to their advantage, the wealthy blame the poor for their plight. Rich.

Grand extortion

December 1, 2012 by davald

Corporations like Boeing threaten state leaders that they will depart their environs lest they not receive subsidies and tax breaks. The companies’ workers, especially if they’re unionized, join the ultimatum-mongering. Politicians quiver, then succumb. But do the incentives (bribes) do any good? Do the jobs stay or materialize? Do local economies prosper?

The New York Times looked at the data then concluded that the benefits promised may remain elusive. Indeed, after taking the money, often in the millions of dollars, corporations feel free to shaft the states, the local governments, and the workers. They shutter doors then move elsewhere with nary a thank you. Or, companies may have had no intention of picking up stakes, but pocket the extorted money anyway.

The Times analyzed more than 150,000 awards and created a searchable database of incentive spending. The survey was supplemented by interviews with more than 100 officials in government and business organizations as well as corporate executives and consultants.

A portrait arises of mayors and governors who are desperate to create jobs, outmatched by multinational corporations and short on tools to fact-check what companies tell them. Many of the officials said they feared that companies would move jobs overseas if they did not get subsidies in the United States.

Over the years, corporations have increasingly exploited that fear, creating a high-stakes bazaar where they pit local officials against one another to get the most lucrative packages. States compete with other states, cities compete with surrounding suburbs, and even small towns have entered the race with the goal of defeating their neighbors.

While some jobs have certainly migrated overseas, many companies receiving incentives were not considering leaving the country, according to interviews and incentive data.

Meanwhile, states and local government are forced to cut public spending because they lack sufficient revenues. As a result, infrastructures collapse, schools close or shrink, and the Rest of Us suffer.

Corporate executives, however, enjoy record compensation. Hey, they’re just maximizing shareholder value, even as they shake down elected officials.
