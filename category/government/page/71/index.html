There will be profits!

April 23, 2012 by davald

Last month the British parliament passed the Health and Social Care Bill. Over objections of health care professionals and against previous promises, the government will begin the privatization of the Britain’s vaunted National Health Service, in operation since 1948. In that year parliament guaranteed that every man, woman, and child in the country would have access to essentially free medical services. Now there will be a two-tiered system, with minimum coverage for most citizens and a market-based tier for those willing and able to pay. Unfolding in accordance with supply and demand, the NHS would eventually ape the awful, fragmented, and horribly expensive medical care structures of the United States.

Media Lens reported thusly:

Few political acts have exposed the sham of British ‘democracy’ like the decision to dismantle the National Health Service. In essence, the issues are simple:

1. The longstanding obligation of the UK government to provide universal health care has now been ditched.

2. The NHS is being carved open for exploitation by private interests.

I find this turn of events extremely disquieting, because I viewed the NHS as a worthy aspirational goal for the U.S., in someone’s lifetime. I cannot believe the British public will allow their system to be dismantled in the name of “reform.” Media Lens suggests that they have been kept in the dark by the British press, which had largely ignored the bill as it sat in parliament for over a year.

The Brits, we know, express extreme hostility to the American way of health care delivery. What will the British people do once they finally realize that PM Cameron is about to wreck the NHS?

As you read the linked Media Lens article you will discover that politics in London is not that much different than in Washington, D.C. Rich people with vested interests in health-care privatization have ensured the end of universal medical coverage.

Well, I’ll be darn

April 19, 2012 by davald

I’ve used the expression ‘The Great Divergence’ in several posts. I thought the term aptly described what happened in the U.S. beginning just before Ronald Reagan assumed office. I’m talking about the sharp upward turn in income by the top income brackets, from the One-percenters to fractions thereof. To illustrate the growing divide between the very rich and the Rest of Us I’ve often included this chart, based on data provided by UC Berkeley professor Emmanuel Saez:

We can see that the top 0.01% enjoyed the largest expansion of income. And only those in the top one percent saw any significant increases since the 1970s.

Much to my surprise, though it shouldn’t have been, Tim Noah is coming out with a new book (available for pre-order here) with the title—are you ready?—The Great Divergence. While I got there first, as far as I know, the fact that someone has written an entire book on the subject suggests that the topic is obvious. Or should be.

Speaking of inequality, Nicholas Lemann, writing for the New Yorker, asks if there is a “politics of inequality.” Lemann:

The most striking change in American society in the past generation—roughly since Ronald Reagan was elected President—has been the increase in the inequality of income and wealth. Timothy Noah’s “The Great Divergence: America’s Growing Inequality Crisis and What We Can Do About It” (Bloomsbury), a good general guide to the subject, tells us that in 1979 members of the much discussed “one per cent” got nine per cent of all personal income. Now they get a quarter of it. The gains have increased the farther up you go. The top tenth of one per cent get about ten per cent of income, and the top hundredth of one per cent about five per cent. While the Great Recession was felt most severely by those at the bottom, the recovery has hardly benefitted them. In 2010, ninety-three per cent of the year’s gains went to the top one per cent.

What especially bewilders Lemann is that against this obvious metric of our society, Americans are giving it the big ho-hum.

Since rich people are poorer in votes than they are in dollars, you’d think that, in an election year, the ninety-nine per cent would look to politics to get back some of what they’ve lost, and that inequality would be a big issue. So far, it hasn’t been.

Lemann reviews several books for his piece, including the aforementioned Tim Noah’s. The opinions under review differ widely, with some, like Charles Murray of The Bell Curve notoriety, suggesting that rich people are smarter to begin with, then acquire more wealth over their lifetimes because they attend college, meet their also-wealthy spouses there, then breed rich, smart offspring. Meanwhile, the poor lack those behaviors that could make them better off, behaviors that are natural to the wealthy.

In the end, Lemann offers this:

But if we are to go further—and get the political system to try seriously to reverse the trends of the past thirty years—somebody will have to figure out how to stitch together a coalition of distinct, smaller interest groups that, in their different ways, care deeply about inequality, and, together, can pressure Washington in favor of specific policies. It’s an unlovely business, but if you believe that government is the best instrument with which to address the problem it’s also a morally urgent one.

Ah, government. We Americans have become so cynical of government that we’re not about to place our faith in it. The notion that in some philosophical sense we are the government probably strikes most of us as quaint, in the least, and completely false, in the main. Government appears to us as a corrupt institution distinct from the Rest of Us. It’s run by “them,” the people we elect to office in the first place then quickly view with a jaundiced eye.

I’m beginning to believe that America is doomed. Not because its citizens lack ideas and ideals. Rather, the very structure of our government, the one originally fashioned by the Founding Fathers, is ill-equipped to facilitate necessary changes. Add Citizens United to the mix, and we may appreciate that matters will only get worse.

The NY Times sounds like the Observer

April 16, 2012 by davald

In a piece that I could have written, if I haven’t already, the New York Times provides some refreshing perspective from two economists, who believe that the U.S. is “radical” in its income inequality. Forget the Buffet Rule, with its 30-percent tax on millionaires. Emmanuel Saez (Berkeley) and Thomas Piketty (Paris School of Economics) argue for pre-Reagan era tax rate—just like the Observer!

Fancy that.

“The United States is getting accustomed to a completely crazy level of inequality,” Mr. Piketty said, with a degree of wonder. “People say that reducing inequality is radical. I think that tolerating the level of inequality the United States tolerates is radical.”

Anybody out there who believes that Democrats are extreme socialists or even communists, as Allen West claims, hasn’t been paying attention. The Republicans are so far to the right that the Rest of Us can’t see them. The Democrats are also positioned to our right, but up close and personal. There really are no American counterparts to even European centrists. Forget about Labor or Social Democrats. Not here in this country. The good ‘ole US of A.

Battle lines?

April 16, 2012 by davald

So, the minority blocked the majority again in the Senate, as Republicans prevented a vote on the so-called Buffet Rule, which would have imposed a minimum 30-percent tax on millionaires. I would have been spared, in any event.

It’s a tad bit interesting to consider that the Republicans have a clear majority in the House, which has no filibuster, but only a minority in the Senate, which has. That antiquated rule effectively neuters the majority there. For all intents and purposes, then, the GOP rules Congress.

And the GOP has spoken. We will coddle the rich. Not because all of us Republicans are wealthy. We just don’t like the government stealing money, even from the obscenely rich who could certainly afford to help the Rest of Us.

Now, will this vote (or non-vote) become a battle line in the upcoming elections? Will Americans line up on one side or the other of this debate? Of course they will. Even dirt-poor Republican schmucks from the South worship rich people. How perverse.

Is stagnation the norm?

April 16, 2012 by davald

In a previous post on “enforced austerity,” I posed this question at the end:

Are we in a new “normal” phase, one that defines the U.S. economy for decades to come?

A number of economists, including Paul Krugman and Ben Bernanke, have suggested that the U.S. economy is aping Japan’s. And we know that Japan has been in the economic doldrums for several years now. (source: St. Louis Fed)

Beginning in the early 90s Japan’s economy started to slow. Like the rest of the world, it fell dramatically with the Great Recession. It bumped up slightly from 2009 to 2010, the last year for data. What about the U.S.?

While Japan entered a period of relative stagnation in the 90s, the U.S. economy kept humming along. But, again, with the Great Recession, GDP per capita fell markedly. We can see the changes here:

A rather bumpy ride, going from year to year. After reaching a post-war peak in 1984, when the economy jumped 6 percent, the trend is downward, although we note all positives during the Clinton years. The Bush-era tax cuts did nothing to stimulate the economy, by the way.

What can we take away from these charts? Japan’s economy is doing worse than the U.S. economy, especially if we consider annual changes in GDP/capita:

Although the downward trend was evident in the 60s, Japan’s growth rate exceeded ours during those years, hitting 11 percent in both 1968 and 1969, as well as in 1961.

Another inference from the data is that both economies have been less robust of late than they were prior to the mid-80s. The U.S. economy never exceeded a four-percent change year-to-year after the peak year of 1984. Japan’s economy has rarely exceed two-percent growth, while managing to fall as much as six percent from 2008 to 2009.

Why are these two economies performing poorly in the last few decades vis-à-vis their relative boom periods in the 60s and part of the 70s? And do the data suggest a new “normal,” one marked by modest growth?

The editors of Monthly Review magazine opine that it is the very nature of capitalism, in particular “monopoly capital,” to be in stagnation. From the April 2012 issue, they begin:

For decades we have been arguing in Monthly Review that stagnation is the normal state of the mature monopoly-capitalist economies. Today the reality of stagnation is increasingly gaining the attention of the corporate media itself. Thus the New York Times carried an article on February 9, 2012, entitled, “In Europe, Stagnation as a Way of Life,” describing European economic conditions as a “grinding reality” with seemingly no way out. Of course much the same could be said today of the U.S. and Japanese economies.

If the Monthly Review editors are correct, what follows? The editors opine:

Chronic stagnation under mature capitalism usually leads to the systematic redistribution of income and wealth toward the rich, engineered by the state. For the capitalist class, the amassing of private riches is always the over-riding imperative. If this cannot be accomplished by appropriating the growth in wealth in the society as a whole, it will be accomplished by redistribution within society. Hence, the costs of slow growth are paid for primarily by those at the bottom of society—a process that has been institutionalized today under the form of a permanently entrenched policy of neoliberalism and financial dominance.

It’s certainly the case that the very rich have siphoned off most of the income gains over the last few decades, as this graph illustrates:

And this siphoning has occurred during modest economic times—stagflation? We’ve also seen movement away from manufacturing and toward financialization. The former provides family-wage jobs to support a genuine middle class. The latter, as we’ve discovered to our horrors, is all about the filthy rich playing Wall Street, otherwise known as the Big Casino. Oh, and they had the federal government backing up their bets via the FDIC then ultimately TARP. It also helped to grow “too big to fail.”

The very structure of our modern economy, at least since the mid 1970s, bodes ill for the Rest of Us, who have become surplus to the needs of financial-based wealth. Those union jobs enjoyed by my father and his WWII generation have become rarer and less secure. Instead, the globalization of the economy, combined with increasing financialization, has converted the Rest of Us into frightened citizens competing against desperate workers across the planet. Survival depends on racing to the bottom of the wage scale.

All of this begs the question: What law determines that only capitalists have capital?

UPDATE: (April 16, 2012)

A reader suggested I take a look at Tyler Cowen’s “The Great Stagnation.” Having watched his TED Talk, I appreciate the suggestion.

Cowen, a Harvard-educated economist, tells us that we reached an innovation plateau somewhere in the late sixties and early seventies. He offers a metaphor of sorts in the form of a graph showing real median household income over time. After following a healthy upward trend for several decades, the vector turns south and even descends in the last ten years. Why? Cowen believes that we’re not nearly as innovative as we think we are—or need to be. His proffered solution is to re-emphasize science and restore respect for scientists and those who teach science.

I concur.

I was entering high school at the time of Sputnik. U.S. policy makers feared not only a missile gap but a scientific achievement gap. The Soviets had beat us into space, presumably because they had better scientists. This led to a focused effort to increase the rigor and comprehensiveness of both science and mathematics. One offspring was SMSG. Almost overnight we high school students were expected to master mathematics, up to and later including calculus, and several science subjects, especially physics.

There are more current efforts to emphasize math and science in the U.S. One is known by the acronym STEM, for science, technology, engineering, and mathematics. There is a local initiative underway with a new Washington State University campus located within or as an adjunct to Everett Community College. The PUD is c0-sponsoring this nascent program.

All good stuff.

Suppose he’s right

April 16, 2012 by davald

So it’s hard to avoid a sense of despair. Rather than admit that they’ve been wrong, European leaders seem determined to drive their economy — and their society — off a cliff. And the whole world will pay the price.

— Paul Krugman, New York Times

Yes, he’s been on a crusade, trying to get Very Serious People, including politicians, to acknowledge that we’re still in a troublesome economic situation that requires action, the more and sooner the better. Judging by the responses, however, Prof. Krugman might as well be charging hell with a mouthful of spit.

The problem, first and foremost, is massive unemployment. It has especially struck the peripheral European countries, notably Greece, Ireland, Italy, and now Spain. Of the last, that country’s unemployment rate is above 20 percent and among youth it’s over half. Imagine that for a moment. These numbers, as Mr. Krugman reminds us, have not been seen since the Great Depression.

What to do?

The professor advocates both monetary and fiscal expansion—big time. But the principal leaders of the Eurozone, Germany and to a lesser extent France, demand that the peripheral countries tighten their belts as a condition for further bailouts. But that will only exacerbate the problems in those struggling economies, creating a vicious downward spiral, which appears to be evident as I write.

Another option, not mutually exclusive with the other two, is for Spain et al. to pull out of the Euro, much like Europe and the U.S. abandoned the gold standard during the Great Depression. Countries that still retain their own currencies (e.g., the U.S. and Great Britain) have managed to avoid the worst. They can still borrow money at nearly zero interest rates to pay their bills while keeping inflation in check. Not so Spain, Italy, Greece, etc. The interest rates they must pay on their debt is high and rising. Nor can they inflate their way out of the mess, which would have the effect of lowering real wages, thus increasing their competitiveness vis-à-vis the rest of Europe. (Prof. Krugman would like to see the Fed openly advocate for a higher inflation rate here, as well.)

The powers that be in Europe and on this side of the pond continue to ignore Krugman’s prescriptions, though Bernanke has sharply expanded the U.S. monetary base while keeping a lid on interest rates. If Krugman is right, then our problems have only begun.

A very dismal science, indeed.

By all means, we must have more austerity

April 14, 2012 by davald

People are taking their own lives in Europe, mostly Ireland, Greece, and Italy, but surely this suicide contagion will spread, given the German-led call for greater austerity. After a few years of boom, citizens are confronting a major bust, which has taken its toll on individuals’ psyches.

Researchers say the trend has intensified this year as government austerity measures took hold and compounded the hardships for many. While suicides often have many complex causes, researchers have found that severe economic stress corresponds to higher suicide rates.

Are there any bright spots in Europe? Once again, we can look to the Nordic countries.

Social scientists say that some nations, like Sweden or Finland, avoided a rise in suicide rates in times of crisis because they invested in labor-market projects — initiatives to help get people back on their feet — instead of cash handouts.

Work provides dignity, a necessary emotion for well-being. Of course, it also provides sorely needed income.

The lesson for us here, still facing unemployment rates above eight percent, is to provide jobs. The private sector is busy hoarding money in this protracted period of uncertainty. It will have to be the federal government, since state and local governments are revenue-starved.

If that sounds Keynesian, so be it. Stop the suffering.

Enforced austerity

April 10, 2012 by davald

Washington state lawmakers continue their stalemate. Nearing the end of a special session, they’ve been unable to agree on a new budget. Three nominal Democrats joined the Senate’s Republican caucus to prevent a House-passed measure. Despite some relief on the revenue side (a bit higher than projected), cuts will have to be made. Washington is hardly alone in wrestling with fiscal constraints.

The decline in state and local investment should give us pause. The following chart based on data from the St. Louis Fed.

We’ll note that after a period of annual growth, from roughly 1995 to 2002, investment held steady, then fell sharply with the onset of the Great Recession, or what Paul Krugman now calls ‘the Lesser Depression.’ If governments aren’t investing, schools aren’t being built or maintained; the same with roads and other infrastructure. Also, at-risk populations suffer most, as governments cut back on social services.

Suppose the 1995-2002 trend had continued. What would be the cumulative difference between actual and potential?

The area between the red trend line and the dark gray actual line is $659.8 billion. That’s the difference over the last nine years, or about $73 billion a year.

Washington state’s population (as of July 2011) is 6.8 million. The total for the U.S. is 311.6 million. So, Washington represents about two percent of the total. Using that percentage, Washington’s share of the investment shortfall is $13.2 billion for the nine years, or about $1.5 billion per year.

Washington’s two-year budget is about $70 billion, or $35 billion a year. The annual reduction in investment, $1.5 billion, is about four percent of spending.

Education epitomizes human capital investment. Reacting to declining revenues, Olympia acted in part by reducing funding on higher education.

The state budgeted about three-quarters of a billion dollars less for the present biennium. Assuming the trend from 1999 to 2009 (about 12 percent per year) the gap is closer to $2 billion for the 2011-2013 period.

Such cuts in education and elsewhere do not bode well for a robust future. Indeed, one wonders whether or not we’ll return to pre-recession output levels, including investments in human capital.

Are we in a new “normal” phase, one that defines the U.S. economy for decades to come? I’ll likely comment on this question in a subsequent post.

Speaking of duopolies

April 10, 2012 by davald

Some time ago I vowed not to cast a vote for Obama’s re-election. I had joined the ranks of the seriously disgusted, folks who had the audacity of hope buried in the shoals of “bi-partisanship,” whatever that means in a country marked by “polarized domestic politics,” as one Chinese observer put it.

But, let’s face it. We live in a duopoly. We have a choice between bad and worse. In simple game theory, the only option is to block the latter, which means, by all means, keeping Republicans in the minority, although my preference is that they disappear altogether.

I can only speculate that the Founding Fathers would be appalled by what their system of checks and balances has wrought. They would surely be horrified by the financialization of factions, wherein we collectively spend hundreds of millions of dollars a year to persuade largely ignorant voters (I did not say stupid) to vote for or against a candidate or ballot issue.

And that’s what it’s all about. Up or down for this or that. There are no other options. We live in a politically binary world.

Obama, as it turns out, is hardly the socialist defined by the right. He is very much the “centrist,” willing to compromise at the drop of a hat to secure something rather than nothing. How frustrating that he never negotiates from perceived strength. After all, he is the leader of the free world and commander in chief. Rather, he’s quick to accede, and the feral Republicans know this like they know they’re for god, guns, and guts.

So, we don’t have what many of us wanted, if not expected. We’re still mired in a struggling economy that has brought misery to tens of millions, with scant prospects for full recovery. It’s going to happen, you say, but when, pray tell? There is no longer any mention of global warming and what to do about it. Obamacare is on the ropes in the Supreme Court. Front and center is cutting, where and by how much.

Then there’s our nation’s longest war. Do you see the light at the end of this extended tunnel? How many soldiers have to go apeshit and slaughter innocents before we bring the poor lads home?

Yet, when they do return we may rightly ask, To what? Join the queue outside the unemployment office? Should the veteran manifest symptoms of distress and, well, oddness, will the government come to his or her aid? Or will their disorders be wiped away by a general’s fiat?

However disappointed I may be, I shudder in horror at the prospect of any of the GOP candidates sitting in the Oval Office, especially if the Republicans reclaim majority status in the Senate to go with their control of the House. Don’t we ever learn? It wasn’t that long ago, people, that G.W. Bush ran the show—into the ground, I might add. Can you imagine a Romney and a Ryan and a McConnell and a Cantor and a Boehner joined by the Supremes systematically tearing down the federal government while further enriching the wealthy as they eliminate assistance for the Rest of Us?

Therefore, I rescind my previous vow. Not because I am enamored of Obama. I’m just scared to death of the alternative.

Politics as economics

April 10, 2012 by davald

Our political system is a duopoly; only two parties call the shots. It will likely stay that way, because vested interests so desire.

A principal interest is the “money-and-media election complex.” As media coverage of elections wanes, increasing revenues flow into the coffers of mostly television stations and networks.

One acquaintance we know who was running for governor of a Midwest state as a Democrat in a general election complained to a TV station manager that his campaign was not getting any news coverage. “You want to get on the air?” the broadcaster replied. “Buy an ad.” Whether intentional or not, commercial broadcasters have little incentive to give away for free what has become a major profit center for them.

That quote appears in a comprehensive look at the “complex” by Robert McChesney and John Nichols. Writing for Monthly Review magazine, the authors report that political advertising is a “bull market.”

The United States is in the midst of its quadrennial presidential election, a process that now extends so long as to be all but permanent. The campaign is also drenched in more money given by a small handful of billionaires than has been the case in the past. Since the 1970s the amount spent on political campaigns has increased dramatically in almost every election cycle. It has led to the formation of what we term the “money-and-media election complex,” which has a revenue base in the many billions of campaign dollars donated annually, and has effectively become the foundation of electoral politics in the United States. Moreover, the rateof increase in campaign spending from 2008 to 2010, and especially from 2008 to 2012, is now at an all-time high.

They tell us that Madison Avenue eventually took control of political campaigns, coinciding with Joe McGinniss’s The Selling of the President in 1969. Pitching a candidate was much like pitching toothpaste, and in advertising repetition works. Message. Repeat. Repeat. Repeat.

The book, which involved McGinniss spending time with Nixon’s television advertising advisors including Roger Ailes during the 1968 presidential campaign, seemed shocking and a sharp departure from the political-driven campaign narratives provided by the likes of White. McGinniss documented how Nixon came to rely upon TV political commercials, based on Madison Avenue marketing principles, as the foundation of his campaign. In the book Ailes presciently concludes immediately after the November victory, “This is the beginning of a whole new concept. This is it. This is the way they’ll be elected forevermore.” It is ironic that today, when one reads the book, it seems downright quaint, even homespun, in comparison to subsequent elections. The liberal McGinniss is able to wander through the corridors of power in Nixon’s campaign like a serendipitous hippie roaming around at Woodstock.

Nor does political propaganda, like commercial advertising, attempt to differentiate products in terms of facts and figures. Watch any television commercial to gain factual data and you will be disappointed. It’s all about attitude and emotion. How do you feel after watching a piece on Product X? Similarly, how do you feel about Candidate Y after watching his commercial?

McChesney and Nichols focus on negative advertising, the emerging staple of political propaganda. The objective is to incense the viewer, making him or her angry at the other guy. This strategy departs from the commercial realm. The reason becomes clear: negative advertising about a competitor’s pickup truck, for example, will engender cynicism about all pickup trucks, even yours. In politics, however, creating cynicism and, better yet, complete voter apathy is a good thing.

Negative advertising can be tremendously effective, a smashing over-the-top boffo success, even if it does not generate a single new voter for the candidate (or supportive independent group) placing the ad. If it simply takes voters leaning toward the opponent and makes them less likely to vote for the opponent, maybe not vote at all, that is a victory. After all the point is to get the most votes and if you lower the number for the opponent, that has the same effect as increasing your own total. Moreover negative advertising can have the delicious side effect of forcing an opponent to respond to charges, no matter how spurious. Negative advertising can amplify spectacularly the classic political move captured by the story of the politician who told his campaign manager to start a rumor that his opponent was a child molester. “But he isn’t a child molester, is he?” responded the aide. “Of course not,” said the candidate, “but I want to hear him deny it.”

With the Supreme Court’s disastrous ruling in Citizens United we ain’t seen nothing yet. The hundreds of millions of dollars accruing in Super PACs will give new meaning to negative advertising.

In the April 2012 issue of Harper’s Magazine editor Thomas Frank tells us that “It’s a Rich Man’s World: How billionaire backers pick America’s candidates.”

While visiting Kansas City last December, I read a local newspaper story lamenting the gradual transformation of Missouri into a reliably Republican citadel—a red state, as we like to say. In the past, I read, Missouri had been different from its more partisan neighbors. It had been a “bellwether” state that “reflected national trends,” rather than delivering votes for any particular party. But now all that was over, and I assumed the article would go on to mourn the death of judicious public reason—the tradition of giving rival arguments a hearing and testing them with that famous “Show Me” skepticism.

I was wrong. Forget the death of open-mindedness. What was actually being mourned that day in the Kansas City Star was a possible loss of advertising revenue by the state’s TV stations. If Missouri was no longer a battleground state, then the two parties and their various backers would no longer fight their expensive electronic war over the airwaves between St. Louie and St. Joe, and “spending on TV ads in the state [would] plummet.”

Franks provides us with a clear example of the “money-and-media election complex.” In tightly contested political races, even at the local level, campaign dollars flow directly to the TV stations. McChesney and Nichols:

Wall Street stock analysts can barely contain themselves as they envision the growing cash flow. “Voters are going to be inundated with more campaign advertising than ever,” one investor service wrote in 2011. “While this may fray the already frazzled nerves of the American people, it is great news for media companies.” “No one loves a good political brawl like a U.S. broadcast company. The fiercer the fight, the more money broadcasters can expect from campaign advertising—particularly in an era when political rhetoric grows more heated every day,” Moody’s Investors Service wrote in a special 2011 report touting media stocks as a good buy. “There are good political years, and then there are years like 2012, when speculative-grade, pure-play television broadcasters expect an unprecedented frenzy of political advertising amid an intense battle for control of both the White House and a closely divided Congress.” As Carl Salas of Moody’s put it, “Virtually all U.S. broadcasters will benefit from spending on political ads in 2012.”

One analyst likened political ads to Christmas shopping for retail outlets. The bulk of television profits are from campaign checkbooks, and it’s easy to see why. There are virtually no costs assumed by the stations; the political ads are already in the can ready for broadcast. And this year, a presidential one, will likely generate massive and record net revenues for TV stations across the country.

It wasn’t supposed to be this way, of course. After all, the airwaves are “owned” by the public. Television networks and local stations receive licenses from the government on the condition that they operate in the public’s interest. One public service envisaged by Congress and the FCC was political coverage. Television was expected to report on electoral issues. But major media have essentially blocked efforts to give effect to the original intent. As mentioned above, if a candidate wants coverage he can pay for it. There is almost zero incentive for television stations to cover campaigns.

McChesney and Nichols conclude thusly:

The U.S. electoral system is wallowing in a sea of money, idiocy, and corruption precisely at the moment the nation’s growing problems demand solutions that work to the benefit of the vast majority of Americans—the 99 percent—who have no role in the current regime except to be manipulated and exploited. This is going to be a defining political struggle going forward, until it is resolved.
