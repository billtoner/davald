Was Nixon even more diabolical than we thought?

September 2, 2011 by davald

During my long walk this morning, the visage of Richard Milhouse Nixon popped into my head. I don’t know how it happened; and I’ve done a pretty fair job of keeping him at bay for many years. But today he was there, causing a momentary shudder, which may have appeared spasmodic to bystanders.

At any rate, it occurred to me as I was putting one foot ahead of the other, then repeat, that Nixon ended the draft in 1973 for pernicious, rather than virtuous, reasons. I shuddered some more.

Now, I am not always the brightest bulb traipsing down the streets of Everett, so I concede that what I’m about to say may have been made obvious to normal, sentient beings a long time ago. But, hey, I’m not proud.

So, here’s the epiphany, for me at least. And I recommend shushing your children and weak of heart out of the room.

As a Berkeley undergrad in the Sixties, I witnessed my fair share of anti-war protests. Heck, I joined a few myself, albeit marginally and timidly. Things, as some of you recall, got ugly, extremely so—especially in the Bay Area and later at Columbia, Washington, D.C., and that place called Chicago. There was literally blood in the streets.

Well, all this unrest didn’t sit well with old Richard M. It got in the way of prosecuting his war.

I surmise that he realized then that the draft was having the unintended consequence of putting resentful men in the military, those who would otherwise be in college, creating the next great gadget, starting a family, or just having fun—it was the Sixties, after all. These guys really didn’t want to be fighting a war in the hot jungles of Southeast Asia. Nor did draft-eligible folks at home have any desire to join their ranks. They’d start a revolution, if it would keep them out of uniform.

Nixon loved war, as did his successors. But he had all these annoying protesters, both in and out of the military. What a drag on one’s ambitions.

So Nixon had an idea, in a Suessian sense. Why, I’ll abolish the draft, I will, to rid the armed forces of saboteurs. And so he did.

Henceforth, only those poor schmucks who volunteered to join the military would fight the presidents’ wars. Those who had better things to do with their lives would eventually lose interest in all that organizing shit. They could join Wall Street, instead, and make a gazillion dollars. No need to worry about dying on the battlefield. That’s for some other guy.

And look at what’s happened: war after war after war—and really long ones, at that. All with nary a whisper of protest. Genius.

I have no idea if my epiphany is what Nixon had in mind when he decided to terminate conscription. But it makes a helluva lot of sense to me.

Perhaps a different kind of revolution

September 2, 2011 by davald

I asked in a previous post if a revolution could happen here. I had in mind the kind of revolution popping up in the Middle East, the kind where the people rise up to overthrow a hated dictator, or at least that’s how it seems. (There is always the chance that the corporate state is working in the background. Do you think?)

But there could be a different kind of revolution in America, one that the world witnessed and suffered through in the 1930s. I’m talking about the rise of Nazism, of course.

In his new book, The Death of the Liberal Class, Chris Hedges quotes from an interview he had with Noam Chomsky. Here’s an excerpt, apropos of the above:

I am old enough to remember the 1930s. My whole family was unemployed. There were far more desperate conditions than today. But it was hopeful. People had hope. The CIO [Congress of Industrial Organizations] was organizing. No one wants to say it anymore, but the Communist Party was the spearhead for labor and civil-rights organizing. Even things like giving my unemployed seamstress aunt a week in the country. It was a life. There is nothing like that now. The mood of the country is frightening. The level of anger, frustration, and hatred of institutions is not organized in a constructive way. It is going off into self-destructive fantasies.*

___________

*  Hedges, Chris (2010). Death of the Liberal Class (p. 35). Nation Books. Kindle Edition.

Obama as corporate stooge

September 2, 2011 by davald

Another headline you may find troubling. This one from the Washington Post.

Obama pulls back proposed smog standards, in victory for business

Not sure that this demands comment. It is what it is. But I can’t resist.

As a nominal Democrat, Barack Obama is supposed to show at least a modicum of concern and support for the little guy vis-a-vis business. He’s also supposed to champion a cleaner environment, rather than facilitate its further destruction (think pipeline). He was supposed to be the anti-Bush.

But because he presides over a country that exists only in his mind, Obama sought to entice, cajole, or otherwise reason with his nasty, take-no-prisoners opposition. He did this over and over again, somehow expecting a different result. (Insane?)

Now he finds himself in a corner of his own making, inundated by a deluge of dismal economic news and pummeled by a feral GOP openly hostile to and contemptuous of Obama qua Obama. His only out, it seems to me, is that, as bad as the Republicans behave, Obama will appear to be just a skosh more “reasonable” to the American voter. He may be humiliated. He may be small. He may even be “a socialist.” But he’s not crazy like “them.”

Yet, I have my doubts that this will be a winning strategy. Have you read the letters to the editor lately? Have you heard of Fox News? We, the people, have collectively lost it. We may be on the verge of taking a communal leap off a tall cliff—with our eyes closed, of course.

Can it happen here? Na.

September 2, 2011 by davald

The freedom to read anything leads me to a vast trove of opinions, and facts, and whatnot. Among my occasional reads is the Socialist Worker.

First of all, I find the term quaint, as I wonder if there are any—that is, working socialists. I’m sure that America has its share of socialists, but few, as far as I know, are on the job.

As to the question appearing in the title to this entry, the editors at SocialistWorker.org say yes, kind of. They’re talking about a revolution.

And why not? Revolution is in the air, just not over the United States. Middle eastern dictators are falling left and right, and even the stiff-upper-lip Brits have taken to the streets. Alas, the only people protesting in America these days are the crazy Tea Partiers, who seem to be mad about everything, especially socialists who don’t work.

The SocialistWorker, however, draws hope from the minor skirmishes that dot our landscape, including the recent, albeit short-lived, strike by Verizon workers. Borrowing a bit from Amory Lovins, the editors would like us to believe that a lot of smalls make a big. Amory is talking about distributed energy resources and not so much political revolution. The editors:

What individuals do now to build up networks of activists and political organization–in our schools, workplaces and communities–couldn’t be more important. By waging the important smaller battles of today, they are laying the basis for the larger showdowns of tomorrow.

So why not here? Well, one could write a book on the subject, and many have. But since we are a nation bereft of readers, the books and the editorials rarely find eyes.

Yet, there are the usual suspects to explore. In no particular order, here’s a brief list:

  • Americans, as a rule, have drunk the Horatio Alger Kool-Aid; if at first you don’t succeed, it’s your own damn fault
  • the rich are to be worshiped as role models, not as sources of government revenue
  • while the media may televise the incipient revolution, they’ll get it all wrong; besides, too many folks are watching American Idol
  • there may have been a class war, but the corporations won it a long time ago; they’ve got just about everything locked up, including a rather large police state, should they need it
  • the Republican crazies co-opted Jesus, who was all about getting rich and slamming the poor—didn’t you know?
  • admit it, you’d rather go apeshit over the Seahawks than join the revolution
  • Abe Lincoln was spectacularly wrong: the U.S. government is of, for, and by the corporations, who are, after all, just like you and me—Mitt Romney and the Supreme Court said so

I invite you to add to the list. I’m sure that it’s endless.

Fundamental divide

September 1, 2011 by davald

Dean Baker points out the division on the Federal Reserve’s Open Market Committee. A majority of those serving the financial interests voted against committing the Fed to a near-zero interest rate over the next two years. The five governors, who are appointed by the presidents, voted unanimously in favor of the commitment.

So what’s the big deal? A low interest rate places upward pressure on inflation, which tends to benefit borrowers and wage earners. A higher interest rate works in reverse, forcing inflation down, thus preferred by lenders and holders of financial assets.

The Fed has a dual mandate: keep people employed while controlling inflation. Over the last few decades it has focused on the latter, which helps explain why so many people are out of work.

Also, core inflation is down, way down, with no indications that it will rise higher any time soon. The Fed, then, has room to maneuver.  If the Fed targeted a higher rate, say closer to three or four percent, and committed to that over an extended period, employment levels would likely rise. This is the prescription suggested by Ben Bernanke in his paper on the Japanese stagnation. He recommended that the Bank of Japan publicly commit to a zero interest rate and higher inflation over several years. It appears that Bernanke lacks the support on the OMC to follow his own advice with respect to the US economy.

I am troubled by the sharp disagreements on what should be considered foundational economics, the kind learned in Macro 101. The country is in a world of hurt, demanding, one would think, extraordinary measures.

Of course, a troubled economy hurts the White House incumbent, which, as Martin Wolf pointed out, is the GOP’s recipe for success. That’s why I don’t expect any Republican support for Obama’s “bipartisan” plan to create jobs.

Meanwhile, lots of people are suffering, with no end in sight.

Skin in the game

August 31, 2011 by davald

A very rich stockbroker, let’s say one who was bailed out by Washington, happens upon a homeless man begging for whatever. The rich man says, “You, too, should have some skin in the game. So give me a dollar that I can send to the IRS.”

Well, it’s almost that bad. The New York Times‘ editors weigh in. They write:

Representative Eric Cantor, the House majority leader, and several senators have made similar arguments, variations of the idea expressed earlier by Senator Dan Coats of Indiana that “everyone needs to have some skin in the game.”

This is factually wrong, economically wrong and morally wrong. First, the facts: a vast majority of Americans have skin in the tax game. Even if they earn too little to qualify for the income tax, they pay payroll taxes (which Republicans want to raise), gasoline excise taxes and state and local taxes. Only 14 percent of households pay neither income nor payroll taxes, according to the Tax Policy Center at the Brookings Institution. The poorest fifth paid an average of 16.3 percent of income in taxes in 2010.

Don’t you just love America?

As I was saying…

August 31, 2011 by davald

With each passing day I feel abused, but have only myself to blame. Like many of you, I had presumed that Barack Obama was a bit different from the usual politician. Sure, there is the obvious physical difference, but that’s not what I mean. No, Obama, the one who brought tears to Oprah’s face in Chicago following his 2008 presidential victory, promised much—enough to earn my vote and those of millions. As president, however, Obama is Mr. Slime and Mr. Slither. In other words, the same old stuff.

Mark Bittman, who blogs for the New York Times, posted this critique of Obama today. He’s rightly mad at Obama for decisions that place profits before the environment. He writes:

I wasn’t surprised when the administration of George W. Bush sacrificed the environment for corporate profits. But when the same thing happens under a Democratic administration, it’s depressing. With little or no public input, policies that benefit corporations regardless of the consequences continue to be enacted.

FDR famously dared to invite the hatred of corporations. Obama licks their boots. FDR delivered the New Deal. Obama takes another vacation.

Life is full of surprises, and perhaps Obama is one of them. But there was another this morning on the pages of the Everett Herald. John Burbank invoked Joe Hill’s ghost: don’t mourn, organize. Burbank writes:

Whether it is coddling Boeing while it breaks federal labor laws, or figuring out how to take away Medicare and Social Security benefits, or bailing out the big banks while leaving homeowners under water, our political leaders have failed us. They are presiding over the demise of unions and our middle class quality of life, and the enthronement of global corporations.

Who can turn this around? The answer is as American as apple pie. It is workers organized into unions, to give ordinary Americans a voice and a lever to make a new New Deal. It will be unemployed workers organizing, minimum wage workers organizing, baristas and tellers organizing, along with machinists, teachers, engineers and grocery store clerks. Only a new labor movement can enable Americans to make a new New Deal.

Wow! In the Herald? Now let’s hear something from the Oval Office. I’m listening, Mr. President. Do you have anything to say?
