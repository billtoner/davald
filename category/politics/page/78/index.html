The crazy House

August 24, 2012 by davald

A reading of Timothy Egan’s column both informs and entertains. Forget about comedy writers. We’ve got real, live Republicans who provide all the humor necessary to split your gut. I refer you to Egan’s entire essay, but a few excerpts will surely entice.

The tutorial in 8th grade biology that Republicans got after one of their members of Congress went public with something from the wackosphere was instructive, and not just because it offered female anatomy lessons to those who get their science from the Bible.

Take a look around key committees of the House and you’ll find a governing body stocked with crackpots whose views on major issues are as removed from reality as Missouri’s Representative Todd Akin’s take on the sperm-killing powers of a woman who’s been raped.

But Mr. Akin will not go quietly. He’s determined to stay in the race for his state’s U.S. Senate seat. His fellow travelers would prefer that he withdraw for the good of the party, even though his fatuous opinions inform the GOP platform unveiled in Tampa.

On global warming:

We’re currently experiencing the worst drought in 60 years, a siege of wildfires, and the hottest temperatures since records were kept.  But to Republicans in Congress, it’s all a big hoax. The chairman of a subcommittee that oversees issues related to climate change, Representative John Shimkus of Illinois is —  you guessed it  — a climate-change denier.

At a 2009 hearing, Shimkus said not to worry about a fatally dyspeptic planet: the biblical signs have yet to properly align. “The earth will end only when God declares it to be over,” he said, and then he went on to quote Genesis at some length.  It’s worth repeating: This guy is the chairman.

And speaking of Genesis, Egan cites a recent Gallup poll that shows 58 percent of Republicans “believe God created humans in the present form just within the last 10,000 years —  a wealth of anthropological evidence to the contrary.” How could I even have a conversation with these people?

It seems that some members of the GOP don’t appreciate what’s become of their party. Egan brings in Joe Scarborough:

And in an on-air plea for sanity, Joe Scarborough, the former G.O.P. congressman and MSNBC host, said, “I’m just tired of the Republican Party being the stupid party.”  I feel for him.  But don’t expect the reality chorus to grow. For if intelligence were contagious, his party would be giving out vaccines for it.

I regret the refrain, so necessary, however: Only in America.

Don’t enable “the moochers”

August 24, 2012 by davald

In his column this morning Paul Krugman continues with his criticisms of Paul Ryan, who, despite all evidence to the contrary, is being put forth as an intellectual of sorts, one whose views should be taken seriously, even if you don’t happen to agree with all of them. How he rose to this vaulted position within the GOP is the subject of Ryan Lizza’s piece in The New Yorker, which I heartily commend to my dear readers. Here’s Krugman:

But he is deadly serious about cutting taxes on the rich and slashing aid to the poor, very much in line with [Ayn] Rand’s worship of the successful and contempt for “moochers.”

This last point is important. In pushing for draconian cuts in Medicaid, food stamps and other programs that aid the needy, Mr. Ryan isn’t just looking for ways to save money. He’s also, quite explicitly, trying to make life harder for the poor — for their own good. In March, explaining his cuts in aid for the unfortunate, he declared, “We don’t want to turn the safety net into a hammock that lulls able-bodied people into lives of dependency and complacency, that drains them of their will and their incentive to make the most of their lives.”

So, all those previously able-bodied adults who lost their jobs because of Wall Street shenanigans and the housing bubble collapse suddenly became dependent and complacent, preferring to while away their hours atop a hammock than combing the streets in proper desperation looking for the economy’s crumbs, sprinkled down from above by our nation’s “job creators”? Sure, you’ll buy that, won’t you?

Of course, this Randian notion of self-reliance tinged with abject fear of destitution requires an important ingredient: that the wonderful entrepreneurial geniuses so admired by Ryan et al. have already created more than enough jobs to go around. Work is simply an application away. Why, then, are you sitting on your butt?

 

See that sharp drop in the ratio beginning in 2008-2009? It depicts a booming market for hammocks—oh, and a lot more laggards.

 

Once again, the skyrocketing climb in the average duration of unemployment has nothing to do with lack of jobs. It’s all about too many people abruptly losing their desire for work as they enjoy the luxury of living on the dole. In Ryan’s world, these benighted fools could be busy transforming themselves into so many John Galts or Mitt Romneys. All they need are some tough love, a strong distaste for failure, and adoption of all those qualities that make men (forget about women) great.

You see, unemployment has everything to do with inappropriate incentives and a complete lack of values possessed by successful white males. Denying would-be workers assistance is the first step in opening the gates to unfounded riches.

Only in America.

If Socrates could speak

August 23, 2012 by davald

Gary Gutting, professor of philosophy at Notre Dame, imagines an ongoing conversation with Socrates, the ancient Athenian condemned to death for corrupting the morals of Greek youth. In this instance, Gutting asks the old man about America’s upcoming presidential election.

S[ocrates]: I think letting the American people decide is no different from leaving it to chance. The vast majority of you don’t know enough about the issues or the candidates to make anything like a reliable decision.

I recall a conversation on NPR several years ago between a host and an English-speaking German. The German was commenting on the profound and pervasive ignorance of Americans regarding basic facts about our country. He argued that he deserved to vote for the U.S. president for two reasons: first, he was more knowledgable about U.S. history than most Americans; and, second, a president’s policies and actions affect nearly every citizen of the planet, including Germans.

I find elections most depressing. They are pure theater, horribly produced. Politicians appeal to our baser instincts, which exhibit no signs of ever having been influenced by a brain. I cannot possibly watch what are called “debates.”

On this note, I caught the latest episode of HBO’s The Newsroom. The main character, news anchor Will McAvoy, desperately wants his cable news channel to produce the Republican presidential debate. However, his station’s ratings plummeted because McAvoy et al. had passed over the Caylee Anthony murder. (I confess that I didn’t know much about it.) So, to boost their chances of securing the debates, they finally climbed on board, ignoring, among other things, the impending debt ceiling fiasco. Meanwhile, forever optimistic, McAvoy and his team assembled a mock debate, with various staff members investigating Bachman, Paul, and so on then assuming their personae for a demonstration before two RNC representatives who would make the decision. McAvoy, as moderator, pummeled the fake candidates with very pointed but obvious questions. The effect was to reveal the complete inanity of the Republican field, whose statements and opinions exposed their unfathomable idiocy. Unsurprisingly, McAvoy’s new format was soundly rejected.

A bit later in the episode we see a video of the actual debate. The real-life moderator asked Michelle Bachman if she preferred Elvis to the Beatles(?).

One thing is for certain. Our leaders are just as dumb as we are. We get the democracy we deserve.

The big punch bowl

August 23, 2012 by davald

GOP presidential candidate, Mitt Romney, unveiled his “energy independence” plan, which, as one might guess, is simply “drill, baby, drill.” He would essentially streamline permitting processes for new oil exploration and broadly expand available drilling sites, including off-shore. Despite Obama’s enabling a rapid increase in domestic oil exploration and production, Romney said today, as quoted by the New York Times:

“The net net of all this, as you can see [from a chart that no one could see], is by 2020, we’re able to produce somewhere between 28 million barrels per day of oil and we won’t need to buy any oil from the Middle East or Venezuela or anywhere else where we don’t want to,” Mr. Romney said. [By the way, the New York Times reports that the U.S. produces 15 billion barrels of oil a day. The CIA estimates the total to be just under 10 million barrels a day.)

The petroleum market is truly global. I liken it to a giant punch bowl, with many spigots pouring oil in and many more straws drawing it out. The price of each output unit is determined the old-fashioned way: by supply and demand.

So, what would really happen if the U.S. increased domestic supply?

  • It would have negligible impacts on global aggregate supply and thus scant effect on market prices.
  • Oil is traded on a global scale. Exxon, for example, seeks the best deal on wholesale quantities from wherever then sells at retail based on a highest price the market will bear. If Exxon finds a better wholesale price elsewhere, it will likely keep domestic supplies in reserve until it makes economic sense to use them.*
  • If market prices rise, so do the revenues of the oil companies. Since revenue equals price times quantity, the greater the supply of oil the greater the revenues—at any given price.
  • Further environmental despoliation.
  • Increased greenhouse gases.
  • Undermining of conservation acquisition and renewable resource development.

Let’s look at a few charts. First, we’ll place U.S. production within the global context.

 

 

In percentage terms, here’s what it looks like:

 

 

What about consumption? The U.S. consumes 21 percent of the world total. (Note: Our population represents only 4.4 percent of the planet’s human inhabitants.)

 

Now let’s compare production with consumption.

Based on 2010 data the U.S. imports nearly 50 percent of the oil it consumes. From whom do we get our oil? (By the way, the data used in this post via the CIA World Factbook.)

In addition to illustrating the sources of our oil, the above chart reminds us that there is a vast global petroleum market. Since we must import so much of the oil we use, we spend an inordinate amount of our tax dollars on defending shipping lanes and both acquiring and protecting foreign supplies of petroleum (e.g., Iraq, Kuwait).

On a sour note, I conclude that neither Romney’s nor Obama’s energy strategies will make us energy independent or prevent a global warming catastrophe. Such is the state of politics in America.

Why do this? Chevron was sitting out the embargo until market prices were re-introduced. (Source: Google maps)

What you won’t see in Finland

August 23, 2012 by davald

Here is the cover of the Summer 2012 issue of NEA Today, a publication of the National Education Association:

For those who just stepped off the bus after a generations-long journey, teachers and their unions are under constant attack, which is nothing new, actually. Today, however, the barrage is truly bipartisan, with both Obama and Republicans preaching radical reform and especially teacher-accountability.

It is simply assumed that the present public education system sucks. Too many students flunk standardized tests designed to either leave no child left behind or fuel a race to the top. Whom shall we blame? The teachers, of course.

Now everyone who can read this went to school. No doubt the reader spent many years in classrooms ostensibly learning to read, write, and gain some proficiency in math and science. You may have also ingested a thing or two about history or how to incorporate perspective in drawing. All that seat time confers on the reader—and nearly every politician—the expertise to rant and rail about what goes on in our schools. In particular, everyone thinks they know not just what to teach but how.

It’s no wonder that teachers, like Rodney Dangerfield, get no respect. Even the apparently best and brightest educators go about their careers as if their principals “couldn’t care less,” in the words of one disaffected teacher. Parents are even worse.

As the title of this post suggests, the NEA headline wouldn’t appear in Finland, if Finnish Lessons is credible. Finland’s teachers need not be concerned with protection, since they are not under attack. Indeed, as Diane Ravitch has observed, those who teach in Finland enjoy as much prestige and respect, if not more, than that country’s doctors, lawyers, and other professionals. I should reprise her remarks:

First, Finland has one of the highest-performing school systems in the world, as measured by the Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA), which assesses reading, mathematical literacy, and scientific literacy of fifteen-year-old students in all thirty-four nations of the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), including the United States. Unlike our domestic tests, there are no consequences attached to the tests administered by the PISA. No individual or school learns its score. No one is rewarded or punished because of these tests. No one can prepare for them, nor is there any incentive to cheat.

Second, from an American perspective, Finland is an alternative universe. It rejects all of the “reforms” currently popular in the United States, such as testing, charter schools, vouchers, merit pay, competition, and evaluating teachers in relation to the test scores of their students.

Third, among the OECD nations, Finnish schools have the least variation in quality, meaning that they come closest to achieving equality of educational opportunity—an American ideal.

Fourth, Finland borrowed many of its most valued ideas from the United States, such as equality of educational opportunity, individualized instruction, portfolio assessment, and cooperative learning. Most of its borrowing derives from the work of the philosopher John Dewey.

The logic driving repetitive commercials is that sooner or later the message will register with a few viewers, enough, I guess, to compel them to buy a vehicle or, better yet, a particular one. Here, I preach the Finnish Way.

Repeat.

Another tool of suppression

August 23, 2012 by davald

A few posts back I developed an alarming hypothesis: The GOP, cognizant of America’s changing demographics, concocted a long-term strategy that would lead to de jure apartheid, cementing existing de facto segregation. Fearing a minority majority, if you will, white conservatives desire to dominate electoral politics despite being outnumbered by the year 2050. My hypothesis relies on connecting a few dots and positing an end game based on self-interest. (Source for chart: U.S. Census)

The de jure part, I suggested, is being accomplished on two fronts: voter ID laws and a racially motivated criminal justice system, one that Khalil Gibran Mohammed believes is used to subjugate Blacks and other minorities. Mohammed:

It [post-bellum Black Codes in the South] tells you something about the invention of the criminal justice system as a repressive tool to keep Black people in their place, from the very moment where 95 percent of the Black population became free. And it’s still with us. It’s still with us, because ultimately, as a social problem, crime has become like it was in the Jim Crow South, a mechanism to control black people’s movement in cities.

But there is a third leg to the strategy: immigration laws. Blacks are already here in abundance, thus the emphasis on crime and punishment and voter suppression. But what about Hispanic and Latino populations that arrive in America, often illegally?

In addition to voter-ID laws, the emphasis will be on keeping out brown-skinned people. Should they get past the border fences and patrols, conservatives would subject their children to all sorts of harassment. Above all, they would prevent them from ever becoming citizens.

So, what are Republicans doing about this in their platform? Julia Preston, writing for the New York Times:

In their debates this week in Tampa, Fla., over the party platform, Republican delegates hammered out an immigration plank calling for tough border enforcement and opposing “any forms of amnesty” for illegal immigrants, instead endorsing “humane procedures to encourage illegal aliens to return home voluntarily,” a policy of self-deportation.

You see, Republicans would like the Hispanic vote, but they don’t like Hispanics. Rather than forcibly evicting those who came here illegally, even though their ancestors once ruled huge swaths of America, the GOP elects to “encourage” self-deportation. What might that look like? It’s not clear. Preston:

A number of immigration amendments were offered by Kris Kobach, a conservative who is secretary of state of Kansas and was an author of laws in Arizona and several other states to crack down on illegal immigration.

Mr. Kobach proposed the plank calling for mandatory use by employers of a federal electronic system known as E-Verify to confirm the legal immigration status of new hires. “If you really want to create a job tomorrow, you can remove an illegal alien today,” Mr. Kobach said.

At his urging, the delegates also adopted a call to complete a double-layer border fence and to end federal lawsuits challenging state enforcement laws like Arizona’s.

I can’t imagine Hispanics rallying behind this platform.

Revisiting some numbers

August 20, 2012 by davald

Fiscal conservatives, whether or not they practice what they preach, bemoan the public debt. To be sure, it’s getting larger, as this chart shows (source: White House historical tables).

The y-axis is in millions of dollars. In 2010, the debt reached $2.7 trillion, the largest in history. Conservatives insist that the annual deficits and cumulative debt result from excessive government spending—alone. They completely ignore federal receipts, which are mostly in the form of taxes.

Here we see that in Reagan’s first term receipts began trailing spending, which did not subside, despite Reagan’s railing against big government; it only got bigger under his reign. We’ll notice a slight deviation from the trend. That would be the latter few years of Clinton’s second term; receipts actually exceeded spending. Imagine that.

Then under Bush II the parity started to unravel. Spending continued apace, but receipts really lagged behind. There are a few reasons for the latter: the Bush tax cuts, two wars, and a torpid economy. The Great Recession obviously exacerbated the trend.

When Obama assumed office, the economy was clearly in the toilet, and millions of people had lost their jobs. The federal stimulus package, aid to states, and increased spending on unemployment benefits and Medicaid for the rising number of poor forced expenditures up.

Conservatives argue several points about the federal government. They say that they believe in limiting it. But whenever they’re in power, they expand it. They say that the government should provide for defense, and not much more. But they have never met a subsidy they don’t like, and while bashing earmarks, embrace them (see Paul Ryan).

However, their most fervent opinions focus on taxation. Their own self-interest demands that they push for ever lower taxes. Moreover, they argue, lower taxes spurs economic growth. Well, history proves them wrong.

 

As Congress lowered the marginal tax rate the economy (in terms of real GDP) did not respond. The trend is downward. It’s easier to see in the following chart:

 

Better still, let’s glance at what happened to the economy under G.W. Bush, when tax rates were cut sharply.

 

The marginal tax rate in the last two years of Bush I was 31 percent. Clinton and Congress increased it to 40 percent. Let’s see the effects.

 

So, despite higher tax rates the economy did better not worse. The correlation was even stronger just after the war.

 

During this period the marginal tax rate remained above 90 percent. Real GDP grew at an average annual rate of 4.1 percent.

Last year the economy grew only 1.3 percent. It is not expected to do much better for several more years. The Republican push to lower taxes even further will do only one thing: exacerbate the deficit and debt.

Perhaps it’s the nature of the Congressional beast. But no one actually reduces spending, notwithstanding rhetoric to the contrary. Worse, if the government were to cut spending in this depressed economy, say hello to even more unemployment and lower growth rates.

The Rest of Us have a problem this November. Neither presidential candidate plans to do anything that will actually put people back to work and boost the economy.

The mother of all moral hazards

August 20, 2012 by davald

While I suspect that the following chart means something, I can only speculate for the moment. Here goes:

If we add up the total value of all U.S. residential and commercial property we get $4.3 trillion dollars, as of the first quarter of this year (Federal Reserve data). Last year the value of all U.S. based financial derivatives was $244 trillion, or about 57 times more than buildings and land (source—pdf).

Now it’s true that the value of anything is what people are willing to pay for it. For years we collectively bid up the price of housing, so that by 2007 home values were way off the charts. The bubble popped, with ruinous consequences.

But what about financial instruments, particularly derivatives, which, by definition, are based on some underlying asset, though it’s difficult, if not impossible, to discover just what that might be? We know that as the housing bubble expanded, so did the value of such derivatives, many based on the assumed value of real estate. When the bubble collapsed, so did the value of mortgage-backed securities, including associated derivatives.

The cynic in me suggests that there’s something not quite right about the increasing financialization of the economy. In a very literal sense, it’s not real, since, again, values are so intangible. Moreover, the value of the derivatives greatly exceeds the aggregate GDP of all the world’s economies (source). How can that be?

The derivative market strikes me as one giant house of cards that could very easily crumble, perhaps with only the slightest of what economists call “shocks.” What sustains it against so many potential disruptions, not the least of which is the loss of confidence in any number of institutions, precipitated by who knows what?

Now we enter the realm of too big to fail. However dodgy this derivatives market, we must acknowledge that it’s huge and represents a sizable chunk of investments by you, me, and thousands of pension funds, both public and private. With so much at stake, there is a strong incentive to keep the financial cards upright. And this is where governments come in.

When Lehman Brothers was on the brink of bankruptcy, America’s top financial advisors, including Ben Bernanke and Henry Paulson, first tried to pass it off. They firmly believed that markets self-correct without government intervention, and that the loss of Lehman posed no significant threat to the overall economy. Over several intense days, however, they eventually realized that the house of cards was ready to collapse along with the housing bubble. What would be the consequences? In divining the answer they grew horrified. Nothing short of a massive infusion of capital in troubled banks would prevent a global financial meltdown—thus TARP.

But this is perverse. Aside from enabling the mother of all moral hazards, the Rest of Us are serving as the insurers of a giant Ponzi scheme. And we know that such schemes, like garbage incinerators, demand ever-larger inputs. It’s as if we’ve signed a Faustian bargain but somebody forged our signatures, and if we don’t pay up when things go south, instant catastrophe.

However pernicious and absurd, the devils in this scheme, otherwise known as the oligarchs, have purchased the best Congress money can buy. This arrangement all but guarantees that forever forward, it’s heads they win and tails we lose.

Worse for the Rest of Us, both political parties are completely enthrall to Wall Street, for a very simple reason: that’s where the money is. If you want to get elected or stay in office, you learn quickly not to ruffle the oligarchs’ feathers. Should you even whisper that something’s askew with the financial sector and its control of the economy, you’re committing political suicide.

Sorry, ladies and gentlemen. I don’t have a quick solution to this mess, which emerged over time, beginning with the Great Divergence under Reagan. Actually, I’m rather pessimistic, since the incentives to prop up the financial cards greatly outweigh political opposition, which is paltry at best.

Just hold on as best you can.

Educational reform

August 19, 2012 by davald

The topic will not go away, nor should it. Educational reform, that is.

We value the education of children, whether for their own sake or in service to a greater good, which is often translated as “to compete in the global economy.” But whatever the reason, we would prefer all offspring to be “above average,” in a Lake Wobegon sense.

Today the New York Times‘ Frank Bruni places a soon-to-be-released movie in the broader context of what’s wrong with our schools and what can and should be done to improve them. The movie, which stars Maggie Gyllenhaal, pits an anxiously concerned mother against the tyrannical forces of entrenched mediocrity, aided and abetted by the recalcitrant teachers union. Don’t Back Down will surely contribute to the ever-widening chasm between what is and what needs to be, no doubt inflaming the passionate advocates for anything-but-public in education and elsewhere. Private charter schools. Yeah, that’s the ticket, despite no evidence that such institutions do any better than their public counterparts; they do worse, more often than not. But both political parties have thrown down the gauntlet: reform or else. And that bipartisan pressure undermines, if not altogether prevents, a reasonable conversation about the problems and their solution. Unions, I dare say, must be part of that conversation, should they escape persistent vilification.

Despite unswerving support from the NEA and the AFT, Barack Obama is no friend to either. Bruni:

Perhaps most striking are the rifts that have opened between teachers’ unions and Democrats, who had long been their allies. President Obama’s appointment of Arne Duncan as education secretary and the administration’s subsequent Race to the Top initiative weren’t exactly music to the unions’ ears.

Lost in the heated rhetorical skirmishes is the glaring fact that teachers unions in other countries have played key roles in reforming entire countries’ educational reform. As readers of this blog know, Finland is surely the most sterling example, almost all of whose teachers are represented by labor organizations.

But American unions have been forced into defensive positions, trying desperately to preserve teachers’ jobs and benefits against the constant, increasingly hysterical attacks from both Republicans and Democrats. Bruni:

“When did Norma Rae get to be the bad guy?” asks a union leader (Holly Hunter) in the movie. I don’t know, but that’s indeed the state of play when it comes to teachers’ unions, and it’s a dangerous one.

Finland, as I’ve noted previously, began that important conversation several decades ago. Its country’s leaders—drawn from the public, private, and political sectors—quickly agreed on a fundamental and decidedly obvious point: education is paramount, just as Washington state’s constitution declares. Okay, now what?

Here is where Finland took a road less taken, one that hardly exists in America. The primary focus of educational reform will be on teachers, ensuring that the country’s most gifted academic citizens enter the classroom fully prepared by the nation’s principal universities. Finnish leaders did not set about bashing educators or condemning unions or introducing choice and competition into the public school system. Instead, Finland selected the most talented, paid fully for their academic training, then, and only then, deployed them in the schools, where they were entrusted to autonomously develop curriculum, pedagogy, and evaluation methods.

What we have to understand from the Finnish experience, other than its wholesale departure from American attitudes and dispositions, is that it worked. I should also add that the Finns are a patient lot.

The reforms did not produce success overnight. They have been the product of stable and persistent strategies all based on a fundamental concept: education is paramount, and teachers will be the vehicle for achievement. Each decade of such constancy of purpose yielded better results, so that Finland now boasts the best education system in the world. And it’s all tuition-free.

If I were in charge of the NEA or the AFT I would turn the nation’s eyes across the pond and just over Sweden’s boarder. I would ask what passes for American leadership to suspend jingoism for a moment and open up to the possibility that another country may have the answer to what vexes us. Once I’d gotten leaders’ attention, I would ask my constituency, politely at first but forcefully and dogmatically if necessary, to embrace the notion that teachers must be the foundation for any successful reform initiative.

This is when things could get dicey. The uncomfortable truth about America’s teachers is that they are not drawn from our best-and-brightest pool. Yes, there are certainly exceptions, my wife for one. But for those with academic prowess, pursuing other professions becomes a no-brainer. Why spend four or more years in school, racking up huge financial debt, to become a poorly paid educator, and a reviled one at that? Far better for me to earn a medical or law degree, then recoup that educational investment and much more.

So, how would we go about enticing the intellectually gifted to embark on an educator’s career? Let me list a few possibilities:

  • states would fully fund the education of those preparing to be teachers, including graduate school
  • the selection process would be stiff, at least as stiff as for would-be doctors, lawyers, and engineers
  • teacher salaries would be higher than they are now and rise with longevity
  • once hired by schools, teachers would be fully entrusted with the education of children
  • there will be no high-stakes tests, save, perhaps, for one determining whether students advance to a college path or pursue an apprenticeship-cum-skills-based training
  • in the words of Parsi Sahlberg, the author of Finnish Lessons, the purpose of education, utterly foreign to American leaders, will be to: “Create a community of learners that provides the conditions that allow all young people to discover their talent.”
  • governments, fully committed to that purpose, would direct funds to facilitate its achievement; that means, among other things: ensuring that children receive basic support in terms of health and well-being; providing sufficient resources to help students struggling academically and socially; and staying the hell out of schools
  • abandon the entire teacher-evaluation edifice; after all, if the requisite strategies are in place to guarantee the highest caliber of teachers in the first place, trust them to do their jobs

Now, lest you think that education costs would mushroom under this scheme, consider this: Finland spends far less per pupil than does the U.S.

 

Finland has lessons to teach us. Don’t you think?
