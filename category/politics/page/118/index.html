Dreaming vs. doing

November 8, 2011 by davald

You may share my despair in our country’s inability to go beyond dreams. We have evidently lost the capacity to actually do. I’m not talking small, necessarily, but grand and, forgive me, wonderful.

I am of two minds on small vs. big, however. It may have to do with process. For example, Amory Lovins talks about a lot of smalls making a big. I get that in terms of energy development and consumption. I’m also open to the idea that a relatively small germ of an idea (say a modest dream) can serve as the genesis for something much bigger and, perhaps, better.

On the other hand, there was a time in this country when we could and did do big—from start to finish, from conception to execution. I think of the New Deal projects, for example, of which the Grand Coulee dam was just one. It certainly wouldn’t be built today. Yet generations of regional citizens and businesses continue to reap its benefits. I was alive to witness the great federal highway construction, although I judge it to have done more harm than good. Oh, we’ve always excelled at war making, the bigger and more expensive the conflict the better.

But bear with me a moment to consider an alternative idea on how our everyday lives could be different, with the complete understanding that none of it can be realized in our current fiscal and political climate—if ever. After all, we’re not French. Besides, we’ve grown bitterly cynical.

I bring France into this, prompted by an essay appearing in the Atlantic and featured on Crosscut. It was written by Seattle attorney Charles Wolfe. I was immediately struck by this image:

It’s a photo of a tram running through the southern French city of Nice. The cars themselves are sleek, pure, and functional, with lots of windows. Note, too, the grass strips between the rails. Nice touch, pun intended. Reasonable minds may disagree, but I find this image pleasing and attractive, enough to make me want to live nearby, say in one of the adjacent apartment buildings. I want to be closer, not farther away.

The tram is one part of a very extensive transit plan. A Google translation tells me that the embedded video was shown to Nice’s Community Council, which approved its portion of the plan in 2009. As you’ll note, a full buildout of the transportation system will connect several French cities along the Mediterranean and inland, including Alsace-Lorraine. (I gather from the palm trees that these places have temperate climates.)

The video includes futuristic images of multiple subways and overhead trams. All is neat and tidy, as are the human figures. The goal is to have everything in place by the year 2030. Good luck with that.

Such projects are difficult to accomplish, even in France. As I said above, they’re probably impossible here in the US.

The principal proffered reason would surely be economic. We simply can’t afford it, more so now than in better times. Yet, I have my doubts that we’ll ever see major public works projects in my lifetime if not also my children’s, even if the economy recovers.

I would suggest that the real reason that we can no longer do is that we’ve lost faith in government and in our ability to imagine together. It’s all about the individual rather than the collective we. Voters deny government sufficient revenues to properly fund the public sector, including schools and transportation. The only governmental activity we support is war, of which we can never get enough.

We’ve become mean, brutish, and narrow-minded. No permission is granted to dream to envision to wonder, from our schools to the councils of government. Work for “the man” or don’t work at all.

The rich have siphoned off all the money, and they don’t need trams or busses or public schools. And we said nothing.

Tragic.

Speaking of nominal Democrats…

November 7, 2011 by davald

Washington’s outgoing governor, Christine Gregoire, faced with more dismal revenue numbers, is determined to cut even more from public education budgets. The Herald article this morning focused on the proposal to terminate the levy equalization program, wherein those districts lacking significant commercial and industrial operations—and therefore property-tax revenues—receive some compensation from the state. (Mercer Island is different from all of us in nearly every respect. Its citizens have always supported public education. They can afford to do so.)

Taking the biggest hit in the county would be Marysville, where my wife has taught for 25 years. If the equalization program does indeed end, the state would cut the school district’s subsidy by over $4 million. Its total budget is $110 million.

This year, after the legislature cut funding for teacher salaries by around two percent, the Marysville school board asked teachers and support personnel to take seven half-days off—without pay. The union accepted, perhaps with few options available.

This is madness. For all the ranting about the importance of education and the perennial “crisis” in the classroom, slashing school budgets is hardly the solution. It is counter-productive. Big time.

Yes, I understand that the state’s revenue forecast has been lowered again and that the governor must submit a balanced budget, but Olympia betrays the constitutional mandate to prioritize public education above all else. Shrinking school budgets is also a recipe for long-term disaster.

While I’m at it, we should understand that the Great Recession, occasioned by wacky Wall Streeters, has disproportionately impacted public services in general and the at-risk in particular. Meanwhile, CEO pay has resumed its meteoric rise. As I write the majority of the unemployed have exhausted their benefits. They’re getting nada. Zilch.

We really should be mad as hell. Don’t you think?

By the way, most of us clearly weren’t thinking when we had the opportunity to impose a very modest tax on wealthy citizens’ incomes. Stupid, we are.

How I voted

November 7, 2011 by davald

Not that it really matters, but here is how I marked my ballot this year. I may even provide some rationale for the choices, however quirky.

On the initiatives, I should say first off that any measure run by Tim Eyman earns an automatic nay in our household. In the original Greek sense of the term, he is an idiot, who nevertheless delights being in the spotlight. Such unchecked ego, save for us who defeat his initiatives. But wait. He just comes back for more. So, no on 1125.

The liquor initiative, 1183, garnered two yes votes at the table during yesterday’s voting. I grew up in California, which has few restrictions on the sale of alcohol. If a minimart purveys small bags of potato chips and lottery tickets it probably sells Jim Beam, too. And there is no giant tax, although California could surely use the money. In an opinion this morning the Seattle Times editors cite Attorney General Rob McKenna’s change of heart, after he’d done some research. McKenna voted against the two previous ballot items on liquor sales. Now he supports 1183, discovering that California’s DUI rate is no higher than ours, with our current state-run stores.

I must add that when I moved up here from the Golden State I was taken aback by the backwardness of Washington in some respects, the sale of alcohol being one of them. (Another was the lack of paved sidewalks in new housing developments.) I simply don’t understand the Puritan hold on vice, which is making its way into the ads of initiative opponents. The anti-campaign is a whole lot of bullshit, in my humble judgment. Moreover, the state has no business selling booze or, for that matter, running a gambling operation—ostensibly to fund education. How’s that working out?

Initiative 1163, pushed by advocates of more training of long-term health care workers, received few, if any, newspaper endorsements. It scored two yes votes here.

The other initiatives struck me as innocuous house-keeping measures. I voted for both.

As for the local races, I kept my record intact of never voting for an official Republican. So I pinched my nose and marked my ballot for the nominal Democrat, Aaron Reardon. (Those who read my Herald “Viewpoints” piece know that I have no affection for our system of county government, which is myriad, unwieldy, induces acrimony, and offers almost zero accountability. I would prefer a council that selects the various county officers, including the executive.)

Though I’ve lived in Everett for a half-dozen or so years, I’m less familiar with its city council than I was of Marysville’s when I lived in that city. I’ve met Drew Nielsen and know Jackie Minchew. So I voted for them both.

Now for the embarrassing part. Regarding the other candidates, even after reading the voters pamphlet, I found little to distinguish one from the other. Some races were left unmarked on my ballot.

In my own case, I’m sure that many thousands of people either ignored the PUD race or voted for my opponent—based on the flimsiest of reasoning. However, I choose to believe that those who voted in my favor in 2002 and then again in 2008 did so because they liked what I stood for. Let me harbor illusions, please.

Still looking for a job? Good luck with that.

November 4, 2011 by davald

Whoopee. The economy added 80,000 jobs, nudging the unemployment rate down a tick. But don’t get your hopes up.

It appears that Marx was right about a few things, including this point:

The greater the social wealth, the functioning capital, the extent and energy of growth, and therefore also the greater absolute mass of the proletariat and the productivity of its labour, the greater is the industrial reserve army… [Capital, Vol. 1, 798; my italics].

Consider this sobering comment, too:

“We are very gradually moving to a labor-less society,” said Bernard Baumohl, chief global economist at The Economic Outlook Group. Firms, he said, “are making sure that the number of workers they have now are able to produce more if necessary. The problem is that there are going to be a lot of Americans who are going to remain unemployed for a very long time.”

Those lucky enough to be working are producing more with less.

About those one percent, they’re doing quite well, thank you very much. While the bottom 99 percent have seen their incomes stagnant or falling, the very rich are gobbling up more and more of the nation’s total wealth.

Notice that incomes were rising steadily, albeit modestly, following the end of WWII—until the mid-70s. (Chart based on data from Emmanuel Saez, UC Berkeley) The Congressional Budget Office has data to 2007. It’s most recent report includes this graph:

 

By the way, to be eligible for the top one percent you’ll need an minimum income of about $350,000. The first quintile’s income starts at zero dollars and ends at around $20,000. Entrance into the top 10 percent requires an income of almost $75,000. So, 90 percent of Americans make less than $75,000. The average income of those in the top .01 percent is above $4 million.

Yes, the rich are different, extremely so.

Bill’s coming back

November 3, 2011 by davald

Moyers, that is. Yep. He’s returning to PBS this January, having received a generous grant from the Carnegie Corporation. Oh, how I’ve missed him.

Here’s one reason why. Read this talk he gave in honor of Ralph Nader’s group Public Citizen. Some excerpts:

We hold elections, knowing they are unlikely to produce the policies favored by the majority of Americans.  We speak, we write, we advocate – and those in power turn deaf ears and blind eyes to our deepest aspirations.  We petition, plead, and even pray – yet the earth that is our commons, which should be passed on in good condition to coming generations, continues to be despoiled.  We invoke the strain in our national DNA that attests to “life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness” as the produce of political equality – yet private wealth multiplies as public goods are beggared.  And the property qualifications for federal office that the framers of the Constitution expressly feared as an unseemly “veneration for wealth” are now openly in force; the common denominator of public office, even for our judges, is a common deference to cash.

In true Moyers fashion, he gives us the sobering, daunting facts of life, of how the rich have planned their own plutocracy at our expense. But then he calls us to action, rhetorically genuflecting before the occupiers of Wall Street. Nothing achieved by the many has come without struggle.

But if the generations before us had given up, slaves would still be waiting on these tables, on Election Day women would still be turned away from the voting booths, and workers would still be committing a crime if they organized.

It’s time we wiped away the scales from our eyes that cloud the truth. This land, while it may be yours, has been purchased by the very rich, whom far too many of us choose to coddle. Face it. They don’t give a rat’s rectum about your life. Heck, they’d like all of us to live out the lyrics to Dylan’s Tombstone Blues:

Mama’s in the factory, she ain’t got no shoes.

Daddy’s in the alley, he’s looking’ for food.

Yet, Moyers tells us:

Who, in these cynical times, when democracy is on the ropes and the blows of great wealth pound and pound and pound again against America’s body politic – who would dream  such a radical thing?

Look around.

Learning more brings further confusion

November 2, 2011 by davald

Economics has rightly been called ‘the dismal science,’ both for its subject matter and methodologies. As for the latter, economists, at least those in academia, believe that they are being scientific when they collect data, develop models, and conjure theories. “We’re just like the physicists,” they might claim. Well, no, they’re not.

One reason for the difference is that it’s nearly impossible to conduct controlled experiments, isolating confounding variables and whatnot, as physicists, chemists, or even biologists do every day. After all, the economy is both huge—now global—and dynamic; it doesn’t sit still long enough to study cause and effect.

However, economists can certainly look backwards to seek understanding. The past is not quite as elusive as the present, not to mention the future.

Yet, even here, economists bicker over what caused the Great Depression that was 80 years ago. Worse, they can’t agree on what events or policies ended it.

Many fix the divide in economic thinking between “salt water” and “fresh water” economists. The latter dominated by the University of Chicago, especially under Robert Lucas, a Nobel winner. The former, as one would imagine, occupy faculty positions on the two coasts, but mostly Princeton and Harvard along the Atlantic and Berkeley bordering the Pacific.

Historically, the divergence in thought began during the 70s, when the economy was mired in both high unemployment and high inflation. This conjunction was judged unlikely by those schooled in the Keynesian tradition. Observation of historical data had led to wide acceptance of the Phillips curve. Given this anomaly, the freshwater economists declared Keynesian economics dead.

I have the luxury of engaging a professional economist at least once a week. He holds a PhD from the University of Washington. Let’s call him ‘Hume.’ I delight in our frequent discussions, picking the doctor’s brain while he’s forced to endure my babbling on about Paul Krugman or Brad DeLong or Christine Romer—all saltwater economists.

“Hume” rejects monetary expansion. Increases in money supply, he believes, ineluctably yield higher inflation. But when I counter that the money supply has tripled over the last couple of years without any inflationary effects, he says, “Just wait.” For how long?

Suppose, I ask, that the Federal Reserve commits to increasing the supply of money by the inflation plus two percent (a figure “Hume” often uses to describe his preferred amount). Suppose further that Congress refuses to enact fiscal stimulus. Would the economy right itself? “Hume” says yes.

But would it not be possible, as Keynes himself argued, for the economy to reach an equilibrium (a favorite term of economists) with unemployment sticking at around ten percent? “Hume” again says yes.

What, then, would a recovered economy look like with so many people permanently out of work? Since the end of WWII the average annual rate of unemployment is 5.8 percent. Could nine or ten percent become the new “normal”?

Professor Krugman, Dean Baker, DeLong, and others have been persistently and now almost angrily calling for massive fiscal stimulus to put people to work. Clearly low taxes aren’t doing the trick. Nor are businesses complaining all that much about government regulations. Rather, the complaint is about people not buying enough stuff—that is, insufficient aggregate demand.

“Hume” replies that the problem with fiscal stimulus is that it’s only temporary. Its effects, however salutary, wear off sooner or later to return the economy back to where it began. Well, I wonder aloud, why not a permanent stimulus?

Certainly there are enough public-sector projects to keep millions of people employed for many years, if not forever. We’ve got an aging, crumbling transportation infrastructure demanding urgent attention. Our public school buildings need repair and replacement.

But states are heading in the wrong direction, forced to dramatically cut budgets and, therefore, personnel. The effects are being felt in the schools, I can attest. There’s so much pressure to do more with a lot less. “Hume” knows this too, because his wife, like my wife, is a public school teacher.

I might dismiss my confusion as a trivial gap in understanding, similar to never having learned French. But given our current mess the study of economics seems so very important these days. What is to be done? And will a righteously correct one-armed economist come to the rescue?

Wall Street won’t help the Rest of Us. As Keynes averred, only the federal government can provide the proper analgesic during recessionary periods.

Physicists know how long it takes for a lead ball to hit the ground from ten feet high. (Gosh, I do, too.) Yet economists can’t reach consensus on prescriptions to cure our economic diseases. Dismal, maybe. Scientific? Hardly.

Skipping civilization

October 31, 2011 by davald

For most of my adult life I have tended to socialize with like-minded people. Not surprising, since that socializing was the byproduct of working on peace and justice issues, which attract few, if any, conservatives.

More recently, however, I have been in the company of individuals espousing viewpoints that I simply don’t share. Among these are support for capital punishment, which I strenuously oppose, and lower taxes on the wealthy. Yes, you read that correctly.

On the latter point the argument I heard can be summed up neatly: taxes should be imposed on the basis of need. Since the rich, almost by definition, need fewer public services, they should be taxed at a lower rate than the poor, who rely more heavily on government programs.

Neat, but nevertheless bizarre.

I admit to being taken aback by such thinking, probably because I’m just not used to it. How should the argument be countered? Wherein lie the defects?

Having spent a bit of time on this, I’ve come up with an analogy, influenced by Prof. Krugman: the U.S. government is one large insurance company. Each of us in this arrangement, whether rich or poor, is subject to risks outside our control. To hedge against individual catastrophe, we pool our risks. We do so by paying taxes, with the implicit understanding that the revenues gained will be used to mitigate the suffering incurred by others.

The arrangement presupposes commonality. At the simplest level, we are of one species, homo sapiens, who inhabit a particular chunk of real estate. Yet there is something more, I suspect: we are in a fundamental sense co-equals.

The equality has little to do with outcomes, which are extreme in the U.S. Rather, we share the myth of equal opportunity. With a bit of luck and a whole lot of pluck we can become the next Bill Gates or Warren Buffet.

In reading the Walter Isaacson biography I’m struck by how many fortuitous events conspired to yield the person of Steve Jobs: his being adopted into a home in which the father manifested an unusual concern for detail and aesthetics in his work; trekking up to Reed College, only to drop out so that he could audit classes he liked, one of which was on calligraphy and type; his accidental pairing with Steve Wozniak, whose talents complemented Jobs’s; his ouster from Apple, the company he established with Woz, which led to his developing the NeXT computer operating system that later served as the kernel for Mac OS X and buying Pixar from George Lucas; that the Apple board would agree to purchase NeXT and bring Jobs back to Apple; and so on.

As it happened, the innate character of Jobs enabled him to exploit these serendipitous events for personal and corporate gain. He died a very wealthy man, though not the richest in the cemetery. But we also know, as Steve Jobs knew, that things could have turned out very differently for him, including his becoming a starving artist. There might not have been an iPod, an iPhone, iTunes, the Mac, or the iPad.

All of us have read accounts of individuals whose lives did not turn out well at all. The Great Recession has produced millions of sad stories.

A few weeks ago I happened to overhear a middle-aged man in the grocery store tell a young clerk that he had lost his job, his pension, his wife, and his house. He said that he had a good, steady position that paid well. When the economy tanked, so did his life.

The man was now at the mercy of government programs, including Social Security and perhaps Welfare. The funding of the programs he desperately needed comes from all of us via taxes.

We cannot legislate sympathy. But upon some moment of reflection we should arrive at an appreciation for those whose fortunes have soured. It could happen to any of us.

My contrary friends presume that the incomes of the rich are justly deserved. Thus, taxation amounts to theft. The government has no legitimate claim to the money earned by its citizens. By extension, the poor have no claims against the rich.

In a recent essay Gar Alperovitz writes:

A half-century ago, in 1957, economist Robert Solow showed that nearly 90 percent of productivity growth in the first half of the 20th century alone, from 1909 to 1949, could only be attributed to technical change in the broadest sense. The supply of labor and capital – what workers and employers contribute – appeared almost incidental to this massive technological “residual.” (Solow received the Nobel Prize for this and related work in 1987.) Another leading economist, William Baumol, calculated that “nearly 90 percent … of current GDP [gross domestic product] was contributed by innovation carried out since 1870.”

The truly central and demanding question is obviously this: If most of what we have today is attributable to knowledge advances that we all inherit in common, why, specifically, should this gift of our collective history not more generously benefit all members of society? The top 1 percent of US households now receives far more income than the bottom 150 million Americans combined. The richest 1 percent of households owns nearly half of all investment assets (stocks and mutual funds, financial securities, business equity, trusts, nonhome real estate). A mere 400 individuals at the top have a combined net worth greater than the bottom 60 percent of the nation taken together. If America’s vast wealth is mainly a gift of our common past, how, specifically, can such disparities be justified?

All of us, not just Steve Jobs, are products of an endless string of fortuitous events beyond our control. Some will wind up doing well; others not so much. In no case do individuals deserve their fates.

A civilized society would have the winners compensating the losers via tax-funded government programs. Perhaps the problem here is that we’re not all that civilized. Oscar Wilde reckoned so:

America is the only country that went from barbarism to decadence without civilization in between.

Saving capitalism from the capitalists

October 27, 2011 by davald

John Maynard Keynes thought that it a worthy objective to save capitalism from itself. Unchecked by regulation “animal spirits” would produce excesses, both up and down.

The Times‘s Nicholas Kristof writes in his column this morning:

Capitalism is so successful an economic system partly because of an internal discipline that allows for loss and even bankruptcy. It’s the possibility of failure that creates the opportunity for triumph. Yet many of America’s major banks are too big to fail, so they can privatize profits while socializing risk.

The upshot is that financial institutions boost leverage in search of supersize profits and bonuses. Banks pretend that risk is eliminated because it’s securitized. Rating agencies accept money to issue an imprimatur that turns out to be meaningless. The system teeters, and then the taxpayer rushes in to bail bankers out. Where’s the accountability?

I submit that the absence of accountability has everything to do with a corrupt political system that allows for, if not also encourages, massive financial contributions to those ostensibly tasked with the responsibility to restrain the excesses—that is, legislators. Complicit in the corruption, of course, are the ideological conservatives dominating the present Supreme Court, which has issued a string of rulings that virtually ensures plutocracy. The Rest of Us have little skin in the game because we lack bribing ability comparable to the One Percent’s.

Alas, I see nothing on the horizon to correct the gross imbalance, save for one small possibility: that both sides of the political aisle concert to gradually eliminate subsidies and financial guarantees. Yes, we all have our favorite subsidy. But if the opportunity for quid pro quo ceases, then the plutocrats may find it worthwhile to invest their dollars outside the halls of Congress.

Meanwhile, the “animal spirits,” though impossible to eliminate since it’s inbred, can be contained by those who choose to play the casinos, Keynes’s metaphor for Wall Street. A simple, nominal tax on financial transactions could—and, I would argue, should—be established, the revenues from which to be deposited in a private insurance fund. Those who play would pay in the event matters sour.

Just stand and say nothing

October 26, 2011 by davald

Against the current crop of GOP candidates the Democratic contender need only to stand erect while keeping his mouth resolutely shut. He would be rightly deemed sufficiently serious to continue in office. Whom am I kidding?

Why is America such a stage for political idiocy? Television’s reality shows prove no match for the clown-filled circus starring the Republican presidential wannabes.

It’s not as if nothing’s wrong in this country, for god’s sake. Real people are suffering, and they’ve been at it for several years if not decades now. But suffering people are not the proper constituency of the GOP. Hell, if you’re poor, it’s your own damn fault. Praise be to Wall Street.

I admit to not watching a single second of the Republican debates. I read the fallout, which is filled with endless strings of quoted inanities.

One would like to think that we deserve better. Then again, perhaps not. These idiots may be precisely what we deserve.
