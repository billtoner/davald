Money and guns

February 21, 2013 by davald

We’ve learned that the National Rifle Association serves as the chief lobbying arm for U.S. gun manufacturers, many of whose executives sit on the NRA board. Suppose that we let money have its way through a kind of back door to limit gun-related deaths?

The idea, being proposed by several political leaders, is to mandate insurance for gun owners much as motorists are required to buy auto policies as a condition for driving their cars. The New York Times:

Lawmakers in at least half a dozen states, including California, Connecticut, Maryland, Massachusetts, New York and Pennsylvania, have proposed legislation this year that would require gun owners to buy liability insurance — much as car owners are required to buy auto insurance. Doing so would give a financial incentive for safe behavior, they hope, as people with less dangerous weapons or safety locks could qualify for lower rates.

Insurance companies have a lot of money and they continue to pile up the dollars. Assuming their appetite for profits is infinite, I should imagine that they’d eagerly welcome the opportunity to insure the Second Amendment crowd.

Worth a try. Appealing to greed is the American way, right?

Are we there yet?

February 20, 2013 by davald

A recurring theme of these posts is our sputtering economy. It just ain’t performing like it used to. Let’s look at a chart.

The red area represents the difference between actual GDP and what would have been the value had the economy kept pace with the 2000-to-2008 pace. In total, the gap amounts to almost $19 trillion. That’s a big number.

The Federal Reserve, in the absence of congressional action, has engaged in a series of “quantitative easings,” or monetary “accommodations.” These actions have kept the cost of money near zero. Ben Bernanke pledged to keep doing so until the unemployment rate falls below 6.5 percent.

But he has his detractors, even within the Federal Reserve. Today’s New York Times reports on the dissension.

An increasingly vocal minority of Fed officials are concerned that buying about $85 billion of Treasury securities and mortgage-backed securities each month is doing more harm than good. They argue the purchases may need to end even before unemployment drops, because the Fed’s efforts are encouraging excessive risk-taking and may be difficult to reverse.

Golly, who could be taking risks now? Not me. How about you? Would you believe that it’s the same folks who got us into this mess?

Some Fed officials have expressed growing unease that, even if inflation remains under control, asset purchases may disrupt financial markets. One concern is that low interest rates will encourage excessive risk-taking, inflating new asset bubbles that will inevitably pop. The Fed’s purchases also may disrupt the normal operations of financial markets by constraining the supply of safe assets.

Jeremy Stein, a Fed governor, said this month that he saw “a fairly significant pattern of reaching-for-yield behavior emerging in corporate credit,” referring to a rise in the sale of new junk bonds, or high-risk corporate debt.

I suppose that it must be tempting to borrow money for nothing then spend it on whatever or, more likely, just park it somewhere to earn a modest return. Volume counts. If you happen to be “too big to fail,” then you know, you just know, that you can gamble all you want, and the wonderful American people will cover your losses should you guess badly.

Oh, and have you noticed that the firms sitting on record quantities of money appear to be in no hurry to invest it in the things the Rest of Us need?

The multiplier

February 20, 2013 by davald

In a recent column for the New York Times David Brooks questioned the effect of data on changing people’s minds. He writes:

But let’s say you are trying to stimulate an economy in a recession. You don’t have an alternate society to use as a control group. For example, we’ve had huge debates over the best economic stimulus, with mountains of data, and as far as I know not a single major player in this debate has been persuaded by data to switch sides.

Au contraire, retorts Paul Krugman:

That said, if you look at players in the macro debate who would not face huge personal and/or political penalties for admitting that they were wrong, you actually do see data having a considerable impact. Most notably, the IMF has responded to the actual experience of austerity by conceding that it was probably underestimating fiscal multipliers by a factor of about 3.

So yes, it has been disappointing to see so many people sticking to their positions on fiscal policy despite overwhelming evidence that those positions are wrong. But the fault lies not in our data, but in ourselves.

It would seem that whether or not fiscal policy can work in a depressed economy depends heavily on the value of the multiplier. Unfortunately, opinions vary.

Let’s suppose that the federal government spends a dollar, and for our purposes here it really doesn’t matter on what. Will the economy realize a dollar’s worth of output? Will it be more or less? The answer, we can appreciate, is important. For if the multiplier is less than one, then efforts to fiscally stimulate the economy are doomed to fail.  Should the multiplier exceed unity, the economy would benefit from a fiscal jolt.

In a soon-to-be-published working paper (pdf) Valerie Ramey, joined by two colleagues, argues that historical data suggest that the multiplier is less than one. Their research concludes:

In contrast to some of the previous findings, we do not observe higher multipliers during times of slack in the U.S.

Unfortunately for most of us, including yours truly, the paper includes inscrutable text and formulas. For example:

Got that? Neither did I.*

Ramey et al. also include a table of “estimated multipliers”:

Ramey teaches economics at the University of California at San Diego. A few hundred miles up the interstate we find Brad DeLong. He teaches economics at my alma mater, the University of California. His specialty is macro, and he, too, has written a paper (pdf), this one co-authored by Larry Summers. In a chapter on the value of the multiplier they differ from Ramey et al.

For these reasons it is difficult, for us at least, to consider the empirical evidence on multipliers without reaching the conclusion that the base-case multiplier of 1.0 of section I is likely to be an underestimate, and perhaps a substantial underestimate, of the policy-relevant multiplier in excess- capacity economies at the zero bound like the United States today.**

Krugman cited the International Monetary Fund, or, more precisely, a group within the organization that did an about-face on austerity policies. The group also wrote a paper, wherein the authors address the policy of “expansionary austerity.” In brief, the previous estimates of “fiscal consolidation” proved mistaken.

This paper suggests that the standard method used to identify fiscal consolidation in the literature may bias the analysis toward finding support for the expansionary austerity hypothesis.

In so many words, when the government reduces spending the economy contracts. This implies that the multiplier may be greater than one, albeit we’re talking about reductions in federal spending rather than increases.

Previously on this blog I’ve cited the experience of Great Britain, whose prime minister, David Cameron, preached fiscal austerity, arguing that reducing budget deficits would restore “confidence” among investors. He did not get the memo from his deceased countryman, J.M. Keynes, that fiscal consolidation contracts depressed economies, not the other way around. In that prior post I quoted John Lancaster, writing for the London Review of Books:

Saying ‘I told you so’ is supposed to be near unbeatable fun, so it’s disappointing to report that, in the case of the government’s handling of the British economy, speaking for myself, no fun is being had. As George Osborne’s autumn statement made clear, the scale and speed and completeness with which things are going wrong are numbing. The Tories went into the 2010 election with a manifesto commitment to reduce the structural deficit – the amount by which the government’s spending in any given year exceeds its income, excluding temporary effects from the downturn. The first point in their economic policy read as follows: ‘We will safeguard Britain’s credit rating with a credible plan to eliminate a large part of the structural deficit over a Parliament.’ How? It’s on the next page: ‘We will cut government spending to bring the deficit down and restore stability.’

David Brooks argues that economists lack an “alternate society”—or we could call it “a natural experiment”—to test their hypotheses. But the UK would seem to provide one. Also, there’s no question that the U.S. emerged from the Great Depression during and after World War II, which involved massive government spending, though I appreciate that correlation does not necessarily entail causation.

(I should note that DeLong’s Berkeley colleague Christine Romer believes that the increase in GDP from the early 1930s to the start of WWII can be explained almost exclusively by monetary expansion. She provides her analysis here.)

So, where are we? If we are to believe Ramey et al., then fiscal expansion actually contracts the economy, since their multiplier is less than one. If we subscribe to the views of Krugman, DeLong, Summers, and a few IMF economists, then fiscal consolidation impedes economic output; better that the government spend more money. But DeLong and Summers offer caveats. The government should spend extra dollars only under limited conditions, when interest rates (e.g., Federal funds rate) are at or near zero. Those conditions obtain now.

That said, there would seem to be no way in hell for Congress to approve fiscal expansion. Indeed, the debate in D.C. is over how much to cut federal spending and how soon. The more interesting debate, the one that occurs within the hallowed halls of academia, appears to have no influence on policy makers. Rigid conservative orthodoxy holds sway, preventing even a cursory discussion of fiscal multipliers.

We could be stuck with a sputtering economy for a very long time.

Does the government owe me a job?

February 15, 2013 by davald

The short answer is no. If a professional athlete sustains a debilitating injury, few of us would argue that it is the government’s responsibility to find the man a new job, though he would likely receive some disability or unemployment benefits that may or not be permanent, but in any case far less than his previous salary.

So, if we might agree that employment for one is not government’s obligation, then it would seem to follow that the government has no role in facilitating work for the vast army of the unemployed, currently at around eight percent of the workforce.

But perhaps we’re looking at this the wrong way. We might, for example, “abstract up,” to view the economy as a whole, rather than as the sum of its constituent elements. Reprising a theme from a recent post, we could invoke the words of the Constitution’s Preamble, wherein the Founders thought that government should be about establishing justice, ensuring domestic tranquility, and promoting the general welfare. In so doing, it’s as if we’re turning the composition fallacy on its head: the government, in the view of Madison et al., has a duty to the whole and, by derivation, to the one.

Republicans and many Democrats insist that the private sector must provide the solutions. Some would go further to suggest that the government frustrates firms’ ability to hire and sustain workers. Among the obstacles cited are: too-high tax rates, cumbersome regulations, a rising federal debt. But our taxes are generally lower than those of other western industrial democracies. Regulations, as we witnessed during the housing bubble’s rise and collapse, are lax and few. However, the debt is rising.

Which brings us to the debate raging in Washington and on op-ed pages. Should the government incur even higher deficits to stimulate the economy?

Professors Krugman, DeLong, Bernanke, to name a few, hold that the lingering high unemployment rate bodes ill for economic growth both now and in the future. They often reference J.M. Keynes in describing current conditions as unique. Here’s Krugman, for instance, quoting Keynes on his description of the 1930s:

“a chronic condition of subnormal activity for a considerable period without any marked tendency either towards recovery or towards complete collapse.”

In other words, the economy is sputtering, operating far below its potential. Since the market is unwilling or unable to boost economic growth, Keynes—and his followers—believed that government is the only available entity that has the means to do so.

Monetary policy (printing money, if you will) doesn’t work too well against a zero lower bound in which interest rates are already as low as they can go (in real terms, they are negative).

That leaves fiscal policy. Can and should the government spend money that it does not have in order to employ more people and, in turn, increase demand for goods and services?

Those making the case would have to demonstrate, it seems to me, that the return on the federal investment exceeds either the costs of doing so or the opportunity cost of doing nothing. This would imply that there is a positive multiplier. That is, for every dollar the government spends, the economy realizes more than a dollar in value. Suppose further that increased economic activity increases federal receipts that keep pace with incremental spending, thereby preventing larger deficits, or, even better, revenues exceed spending, thus reducing the debt.

The government tracks both gross domestic product (GDP) and potential GDP. Here’s a chart showing the difference between potential and actual real GDP:

The curve since the Great Recession should give us pause. The economy is clearly underperforming. If Keynes’s description of the 1930s applies to our current situation, then it’s possible for the economy to continue indefinitely with higher-than-average unemployment and even rising debt.

Doing nothing or, worse, contracting government spending, would either prolong the sputtering or exacerbate “sub-normal activity.” Yet, that is the strategy championed by those Krugman calls “Very Serious People.” It rests on a true fallacy of composition: while it may be salutary for an individual to spend within current means, it may be disastrous for everyone, including the government on our behalf, to do the same.

Unfortunately, from the perspective of Keynesians and the unemployed, government spending has fallen recently, as the following chart shows.

And how has the economy done of late?

 

Pursuing fiscal constraint, as has been the norm in the UK, contracts, rather than expands, the economy.

The private sector, as I said, has not stimulated economic growth, despite low tax rates and minimal regulations. Firms need people to buy their goods and products (aggregate demand). But people need wages to make that happen. And wages require jobs.

It’s up to you, Washington, to get us out of this mess. Otherwise, we’re stuck.

The libertarian said to the …

February 14, 2013 by davald

Each Thursday I have lunch with two self-described libertarians. As it happens, and in the interest of felicitous digestion, we don’t typically engage in politics. When we do, however, the exchanges can get a bit, shall I say, testy. Today was one of those occasions, though I was not the instigator. By the way, my stomach has been better.

The charge was levied, and I paraphrase: All Paul Krugman advocates is more government spending, though the assertion included the adjective “mad,” as in “mad government spending.” As I prodded my fellow interlocutor I evinced the following, which I think is at least roughly accurate.

Government is hopelessly corrupt, so it should be denied more money. That money, it was suggested, is taken from the successful then redistributed to those who aren’t. Otherwise, it is wasted.

This blanket accusation is all too familiar, echoing the more egregious desires of one Grover Norquist, who famously threatened to shrink government to the point that it could be flushed down the bathtub drain. Never mind that Norquist and others of his ilk have no compunction about siphoning off federal dollars for themselves and their benefactors.

So, I wish to begin my response with some prefatory remarks.

Governments are human institutions. Though obvious, the implication is equally so: humans make bad and good decisions. But we tend to focus on the former.

The media are rife with reports of ill-spent government largesse, legal bribery (purchasing the best Congress money can buy), grandiose Wall Street shenanigans, and lots and lots of fraud.

Does it follow from the negative reports that government is inherently or necessarily evil? I would answer a decided no.

First off, government is necessary, pace my libertarian friends. After all, we live in a representative democracy, and the question always before us is How should we govern ourselves?

At the risk of invoking celestial angels singing patriotic songs, our Founding Fathers expressed their fundamental belief in government’s legitimacy in the Preamble to the Constitution, which opens with the words “We the people.” They continued with their manifest statement of purpose: “in order to form a more perfect union, establish justice, insure domestic tranquility, provide for the common defense, promote the general welfare…[my italics, of course’.”

Implicit in this statement is the notion that the new-found government would be a work in progress. They realized that the Constitution was a beginning, subject to revision over time and in response to changing circumstances. (That it has become “dead,” in the words of Justice Scalia, reflects a wanton disregard for the Founders’ original intent.)

Moreover the Founders were acutely cognizant of humans’ moral defects. They had something against a monarch, as I recall, whom they believed to be a tyrant, and they wanted no part of him, even if the Canadians proved more forgiving. They understood the tendency of people to behave badly, including the incitement of rebellions, which led to the Second Amendment’s oft-ignored clause: “A well regulated Militia, being necessary to the security of a free State…” (They loathed standing armies; militias would have to suffice.) Indeed, the Founders sought to curb injurious appetites by establishing equal and counterbalancing branches of government. A nice thought, even if it hasn’t quite worked out as they had imagined.

Now, how should we give effect to the Preamble’s declaration? I submit that what the government should be about is captured in those few sentences. Whether or not it succeeds has nothing to do with the fact of government and everything to do with who is running it, namely the people we elect to represent us.*

We Americans tend to think that there’s no other world out there. Thus, if our government behaves badly, then government itself must be the problem.

To this attitude, I suggest looking across the pond—to the Nordic countries, to be exact. A recent issue of The Economist magazine devoted its cover to four countries: Norway, Sweden, Finland, and Denmark.

The Nordic countries pride themselves on the honesty and transparency of their governments. Nordic governments are subject to rigorous scrutiny: for example, in Sweden everyone has access to all official records. Politicians are vilified if they get off their bicycles and into official limousines.

All of these countries, according to The Economist, have figured out a better way to govern themselves and to build “a more perfect union.”

The Nordic countries have not only largely escaped the economic problems that are convulsing the Mediterranean world; they have also largely escaped the social ills that plague America. On any measure of the health of a society—from economic indicators like productivity and innovation to social ones like inequality and crime—the Nordic countries are gathered near the top.

Let’s look at some comparative data, first at government social spending (data from OECD).

Despite being the US being the stingiest, our total non-defense government spending as a percentage of GDP has actually fallen from the initial post-War levels.

Now let’s look at government debt.

What about revenues? Are they sufficient to recover expenses?

The other Nordic countries run either small deficits or nominal surpluses, though oil-rich Norway is an exception to the latter. The US, again, stands out. We’re clearly not collecting enough money to pay our bills.

Close behind the overarching issue of governance is the matter of how to spend the money. Krugman does not advocate a Robin Hood approach, as my libertarian friend asserted. Rather, he would engage Keynes in support of a new New Deal—since the economy is mired in a liquidity trap in which the propensity to save sharply exceeds the propensity to invest, the federal government is the only entity able to stimulate growth. It should do so by building and repairing public infrastructures, just as FDR did in the 30s. Here’s Krugman from his recent book End This Depression Now!:

For we are still very much living in the shadow of the economic catastrophe that struck both Europe and the United States four years ago. Gross domestic product, which normally grows a couple of percent a year, is barely above its pre-crisis peak even in countries that have seen a relatively strong recovery, and it is down by double digits in several European nations. Meanwhile, unemployment on both sides of the Atlantic remains at levels that would have seemed inconceivable before the crisis. The best way to think about this continued slump, I’d argue, is to accept the fact that we’re in a depression. No, it’s not the Great Depression, at least not for most of us (but talk to the Greeks, the Irish, or even the Spaniards, who have 23 percent unemployment—and almost 50 percent unemployment among the young). But it’s nonetheless essentially the same kind of situation that John Maynard Keynes described in the 1930s: “a chronic condition of subnormal activity for a considerable period without any marked tendency either towards recovery or towards complete collapse.” [Krugman, Paul (2012-04-30). End This Depression Now! (p. 2). Norton. Kindle Edition.]

The Nordic countries are doing a better job of implementing our Founding Fathers’ wishes. Even the stodgy Economist discovered as much. And I don’t believe that libertarianism holds the key.

The robots did it

February 11, 2013 by davald

Why is American society so unequal? Some blame technology: robots and other electronic devices have reduced the need for human labor, which means an expanding surplus of people with nothing to do.

In a recent op-ed for the New York Times Harvard economist Greg Mankiw writes:

Over the last several decades, for the most part, the wages of workers without any higher education have stagnated, while the wages of those with advanced degrees have risen. The main forces driving these trends are technological change, which tends to increase the demand for skilled workers relative to unskilled workers, and, to a lesser extent, international trade.

But economist Dean Baker begs to differ:

Mankiw asserts as a fact that technology is responsible for the upward redistribution of income over the last three decades, but it is not clear that the evidence supports his story. After all technology had a much larger impact in increasing productivity in the decades from 1947 to 1973 yet workers shared in these gains more or less equally.

A chart may suffice.

What about productivity?

Hmmm. When worker productivity was at its highest, in the post-War decades, workers took a larger share of income.

As I recall, there was a lot of new technology in those days, especially at the consumer level, from wide-scale adoption of the telephone and television to cars and freeways and airplanes. (As a three-month-old infant I accompanied my mother on a very expensive voyage from New York to San Francisco. I’m told that the plane was a propeller-driven DC 9. That would have been in 1947. Fast forward to 1969, the year I graduated from Berkeley. I traveled by jet, I tell you, from San Francisco to Philadelphia. In that period our family switched from a rotary telephone on a party line to a touch-tone, single-line device. We purchased a color television, and transistor radios became ubiquitous. My first official job was working with business-class mainframe computers in New Jersey; there were none until the 60s. I could go on. Oh, and they put a man on the moon.)

Dean Baker offers some alternative explanations for rising inequality:

The villains that some of us would point to are anti-union measures by government and businesses that have weakened workers’ bargaining power, trade policy that was designed to put less-educated workers in competition with people in the developing world while largely protecting the most highly educated workers, patent and copyright policy that increased the rents pulled out of the economy for these monopolies, and macroeconomic policy that has led to more unemployment in the last three decades than in the early post-war period. High unemployment tends to disproportionately hit less educated workers, both by having more impact on their probability of being unemployed and reducing their wages.

Wrong medicine

February 8, 2013 by davald

Republican political doctors prescribe austerity—always. The government, we are to believe, spends too much money, which adds to the fiscal debt. So, the remedy is to curb spending now and forever.

Paul Krugman rebukes John Boehner in his column this morning. Boehner said that Congress had to “deal with its spending problem,” and that he’s tired of kicking the can down the road. Krugman argues that given current recessionary conditions, marked by high unemployment and sputtering GDP, we should continue kicking the can down the road. Now is no time to tighten our fiscal belts. Besides, where was Boehner when his man in the White House, G.W.Bush, squandered the Clinton surplus by cutting taxes and launching two expensive wars?

In the above chart we’ll notice that federal spending as a percentage of GDP remains fairly flat since 1981, between roughly 20 and 25 percent of GDP. The ratio fell under Clinton, then began to rise sharply under Bush II. We’ll also note that GDP (black line) dropped at the end of 2007, the beginning of the Great Recession. That is, the denominator fell, so that even if spending levels stayed the same, the ratio would increase.

Since 2010, however, federal spending has begun to fall as a percentage of GDP. In part that’s because GDP has started inching upwards. But spending itself has also stabilized.

Would Boehner want the line to fall? Has that ever happened?

 

We can see that only twice since 1947 has federal spending dropped against the previous year. On both occasions, 1950 and 1954, the country was in a mild recession. Also, military spending fell in the latter year with the end of the Korean War.

 

Krugman:

Start with a basic point: Slashing government spending destroys jobs and causes the economy to shrink.

He cites the experiences of Ireland and the U.K., whose governments both cut federal spending only to see their countries experience dramatic contractions in GDP. Since Washington has also cut spending, with defense down by 22 percent in the last fiscal quarter, our economy continues to sputter.

Yet, despite the problems associated with austerity, if we are to believe Krugman, the European Union leaders just capitulated to demands of U.K. prime minister David Cameron; the EU nations will cut spending. Why would the EU take advice from Cameron, whose economy is doing much worse than ours?

It’s this kind of major disconnect between policy makers and at least some economists that I find disturbing. Shouldn’t there be some sort of consensus on the prescriptions? At what point do Cameron, Boehner, et al. reconsider their positions? Do they know something that Krugman, Stiglitz, DeLong, and Bernanke don’t know? What could it be?

Carbon tax, continued

February 7, 2013 by davald

Should we tax the things we don’t want or desire less of? The economist Arthur Pigou believed that market transactions often create negative externalities. A good example would be our buying then consuming gasoline. Burning gas yields carbon dioxide, which in sufficient amounts will surely doom the planet. This negative externality is not (yet) incorporated into the price of the commodity; Pigou suggested a tax to rectify the “market failure.” A carbon tax is therefore referred to as a Pigovian Tax.

In a recent post I linked to a Monthly Review cover article on James Hansen’s proposed “fee-and-dividend” approach, a concept introduced by Maria Cantwell and others in the U.S. Senate a few years ago. It went nowhere.

One or more groups in Washington state are pushing a ballot initiative (pdf) that has several of the ingredients of Hansen’s proposal. It begins:

The intent of this initiative measure is to promote sustainable economic development in Washington state by funding transportation investments and tax reductions and by imposing a carbon tax. This measure proposes that fifty percent of the revenue from the carbon tax must be devoted to tax rebates and reductions, including the working families tax exemption, business and occupation tax relief for manufacturers, the high tech research and development tax credit , and property tax relief; and that the remaining fifty percent of the revenue must be devoted to public investments in transit, road maintenance, and freight mobility.

If enacted by the voters, the initiative would impose a tax of $30 per metric ton (2,200 pounds) on all fossil fuels, including coal, gasoline, natural gas, and diesel (carbon dioxide equivalent). The amount of the tax would rise with inflation.

Let’s look at the fuels. Here’s a chart showing carbon dioxide emissions in pounds per gallon burned.

If my arithmetic is correct, a $30/tonne tax on gasoline would add about 27 cents to a gallon (19.6 ÷ 2,200 = 0.009; $30 x 0.009 = $0.267). Assuming a $3.50/gallon price at the pump, the carbon tax would raise the amount to $3.767, a 7.6% bump.

We can imagine the horrified reaction to such an increase. Yet, it wasn’t that long ago that we were all paying over $4/gal. for gasoline. Using Wikipedia data, here is a comparison of retail gasoline prices of different countries.

Of course, the comparison won’t stop the outcry. I guess it would have something to do with our sovereign right to cheap gas, or whatever. But that low price we pay (in the Puget Sound area it’s down to below $3.40/gal. as I write) does not include the negative externalities. There are several: military spending on securing U.S. access to foreign oil; the pernicious effects of sprawl; toxic chemicals; carbon dioxide.

If we paid, say, $8/gallon of gasoline, that would be equivalent to a carbon tax of $500/tonne (again, if my math is correct). The tax would be $4.50/gal., which alone is more than the current U.S. average retail price of about $3.50/gal.

Yet, what the ballot measure proposes is to offset some existing taxes and fund several initiatives, including renewable research and development, manufacturing, and public transportation. Instead of cheap gas, we would get “relief” from property taxes, for example, better roads, and perhaps trains to replace private cars. Also, and most important, is that the Pigovian tax would reduce the consumption of carbons, an altogether good thing.

However, even the modest rate of the initiative may scare too many voters, in which case further actions to reduce carbons may be jeopardized. The very small optimist part of me believes that a comprehensive educational campaign that highlights the benefits of such a tax could enhance citizen support. After all, California now has a cap-and-trade scheme in place. Boulder, Colorado, has a municipal carbon tax as does Montgomery County, Maryland. Why not Washington?
