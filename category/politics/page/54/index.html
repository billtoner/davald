Bastard Baucus strikes again

April 18, 2013 by davald

If we’ve learned anything about our elected representatives it’s that they have neither courage nor conviction. They are pusillanimous poseurs, pretending to act nobly as they pocket dirty money from lobbyists and corporations.

Yesterday the U.S. Senate behaved “shamefully,” allowing a minority of its members to frustrate the will of the people to establish very modest restrictions on the sale of guns that kill people. Lots of them.

Among the minority was Bastard Baucus. Though a nominal Democrat, the man from Montana acts like a very good fascist beholden to the monied interests, which include the National Rifle Association, a group dedicated to the proposition that the answer to every problem is simply more guns, and the more lethal the better.

The Senate, by the way, is itself a tragedy, usurping democracy at every turn. Throw in the gerrymandered House and we’ve got the best Congress money can buy, but nothing close to the people’s body.

Rewarding wrong

April 15, 2013 by davald

One might think by now that those who have championed a particular set of policy initiatives, which we can place under the rubric of conservatism for now, and who have nevertheless been proven wrong in nearly every respect would find it difficult to either stay in office or get themselves elected. But one would be mistaken. The power of nonsense retains its ability to inflict increasing damage on the Rest of Us while enjoying our electoral support. Color us schizophrenic.

Among those policy initiatives are “austerity measures.” Governments must restrain spending at all costs, because spending is the problem. (Oh, and high taxes.) Europe’s political leaders can’t seem to get enough austerity, despite overwhelming evidence that tightening fiscal balance sheets exacerbates both debts and unemployment.

Here in the U.S. an entire political party preaches fiscal restraint in religious overtones for no good reason. Our unemployment rate, though not as high as most of Europe’s, remains stubbornly high, with millions of Americans unable to find jobs after months and even years of searching. Many have simply given up trying.

I confess to lots of head scratching over this rewarding-the-wrong phenomenon. I just don’t understand the rationales of those who vote against their economic interests, as in What’s the Matter With Kansas? Why are so many of us evidently comfortable with high unemployment and associated lost lives?

Working poor

April 12, 2013 by davald

The Bureau of Labor Statistics has a new report (pdf) on America’s working poor. To be quick about it, there are lots of them. Here’s a chart on “primary families,” which are defined as families with at least one person working at least half a year.

What the graph shows is the percentage of primary families who are poor despite a member’s working. So, in 2011 8.3 percent of those families live in poverty, up from 6.4 in 2007, just before the Great Recession. Among all persons the percentage of working poor in 2011 was seven, down slightly from  2010.

What’s going on? BLS takes a stab and comes up with a partial list. Among the culprits are the proliferation of part-time jobs and, more important, low wages. A good proxy for both would be so-called burger-flippers. Not only do they work less than full time, they don’t make much money, the minimum wage or perhaps a bit more.

It is a sad testament to our political culture that the needs of the poor go unmentioned let alone mentioned. Certainly the Republicans aren’t about to address the problem. They consistently resist raising the minimum wage and always champion the desires of employers over their past, present, and future employees. So it’s quite easy to jettison workers and deny them both benefits and job security.

While most modern nations have a labor party, America doesn’t. We have the Democrats, who seem just as eager to screw unions as their conservative counterparts, and it’s unions that brought us the eight-hour day, the end of child labor, the minimum wage, and a host of other benefits that are as niggardly as they are taken for granted.

Infuriating Obama

April 11, 2013 by davald

The G.O.P. would prefer that the poor and infirm get lost, as in out of sight out of mind. Their existence serves as a constant reminder that the great American experiment fails many, if not most, of the country’s inhabitants. So we are never surprised by the Republicans’ gleeful efforts to screw them even more.

What surprises, unfortunately, is Barack Obama’s willingness to play Clinton. As the slippery one from Arkansas did with African Americans and welfare recipients Obama now does to the elderly, most of whom rely on Social Security benefits to get by in their retirement years. Shameful and disgusting, I say.

The New York Times editors weigh in on Obama’s proposed budget. They write:

President Obama knew full well that many Democrats and liberals would be sharply critical of his decision to propose reducing the Social Security cost-of-living adjustment, one of the centerpieces of his 2014 budget, which was released on Wednesday. In fact, he was counting on it. He wanted to show that he was willing to antagonize his supporters to get a budget compromise, putting Republicans on the spot to do the same.

That sounds an awful like Slick Willy. We may have thought we were voting for someone with higher moral standards. We were wrong. The editors:

The Social Security proposal remains a bad idea, and, as this page has explained, it could hurt vulnerable retirees and stymie better ideas to improve the system, like raising the wage cap subject to the payroll tax. But it seems unlikely to happen if Mr. Obama holds to his demand for more revenues in exchange, given the Republican intransigence. For now, it has served its purpose — no one will be able to accuse Mr. Obama of refusing to touch entitlements, and no one can credit Republicans for being at all serious about a deficit-reduction compromise.

Playing politics on the backs of old people. I’m personally offended, since I’m officially old now.

Yet, I’ve long given up hoping that Obama will present different stripes. FDR’s would do, and it was Obama who encouraged us to think that he would be invoking the New Deal president in his approach to the economy, nearly destroyed under Bush II. Alas, he’s chosen a different predecessor, the one who “did not have sexual relations with that woman”—though he most certainly did.

Obama prefers the dissembler, the deceitful one over the president who dared welcome the wrath of Wall Street. Our first African American president welcomes their money as he screws the Rest of Us. All too familiar, I say.

What do the rich want?

April 10, 2013 by davald

We all know that the rich are different, right? First of all, they have more money than the Rest of Us. We also suspect, if not also know, that they are much more involved politically than we are. They do so through contributions, of course, but also personal contact with elected officials. And why not? They stand a better chance of having their preferences realized through the efforts of elected officials. All this sounds plausible, we think.

Via ginandtacos I am linked to a study done on the wealthy’s attitudes toward issues we likely deem important. It would seem that if we possess the best Congress money can buy, then the goals and objectives of the rich would hold sway in legislatures at all levels. Is that what we see?

Let’s look at preferences, specifically this table from the study:

 

The item at the top, investment in infrastructure, enjoys support from half of the wealthy surveyed. Yet we clearly are not seeing such investment. More interesting, perhaps, is the disconnect between the Rest of Us and the very rich on such items as farm subsidies, Social Security (huge disparity of 79 percentage points), and health care (63 percentage points). Also, note that the very rich are more likely to cut defense spending and environmental protection than the Rest of Us.

However, the study quickly disabuses us, judging from the following excerpt from the abstract:

We find that they [the very, very rich] are extremely active politically and that they are much more conservative than the American public as a whole with respect to important policies concerning taxation, economic regulation, and especially social welfare programs. Variation within this wealthy group suggests that the top one-tenth of 1 percent of wealth-holders (people with $40 million or more in net worth) may tend to hold still more conservative views that are even more distinct from those of the general public. We suggest that these distinctive policy preferences may help account for why certain public policies in the United States appear to deviate from what the majority of US citizens wants the government to do. If this is so, it raises serious issues for democratic theory.

That much I recognize.

Still not working

April 10, 2013 by davald

We’re told that the economy is recovering, albeit slowly. New jobs are being added, but overall unemployment remains high. Europe is worse, suggesting a widespread dysfunction. Let’s look at job openings.

If one looked at the curve starting at the end of 2009, one might think that things were improving. But despite the upward slope, we’ve still got a long way to go before we reach post-recession levels and further still to reach the Clinton numbers.

Now let’s look at the unemployed.

Again, the trend is improving, but we’re not close to where we were. If we divide the figures in the first graph by those in the second, we get this:

When Clinton left office (and I’m not necessarily praising anything he did here) there was nearly a one-to-one ratio between those out of work but looking and job openings. At the peak of the recession, the ratio had soared to nearly seven. It’s now down to around three. That is, there are three people out of work for every open job. Yet, again, we’ve still got a long way to go before we approach levels seen before G.W. entered the White House.

There’s wide disagreement about what, if anything, should or could be done to get people back to work. Some would argue that there is no role for government; the economy will right itself if left to its own devices. Others, including Paul Krugman and Brad DeLong, advocate for fiscal stimulus, even if it (necessarily) means increasing federal deficits.

One thing seems certain. The guy in the White House is the master of distraction. He exhibits no concern for the jobless and is forever willing to sacrifice the most vulnerable on the altar of “compromise,” which, for Obama, is a goal in itself. So, the president is all about attracting the Very Serious (in Krugman’s argot) as he sets out to screw the Rest of Us.

What a crock, this man.

Conviction

April 9, 2013 by davald

One trait evidently admired in Margaret Thatcher is conviction. Or you could say stubbornness, bullheadedness, and so on. She would not turn, the lady said. I think principles are nice, and I wish I had more of them, or at least the ones I admire. Hypocrisy invites justifiable scorn.

So, on these pages I made a big deal out of my canceling a subscription with the Seattle Times over their dreadful editors. I typically disagree with their opinions. I loathed the publishers’ blatant politicizing in the last elections.

I rue the day that the Seattle Post-Intelligencer shut down its print publication, as much because I preferred the paper’s editorial stance to its cross-town rival’s as because I think two newspapers in the same region are better than one. Indeed, the more the merrier.

But the economics of media these days ain’t what it used to be. I don’t know if people read less or, more likely, they get their news fixes online. At any rate, it’s becoming increasingly difficult to operate just one newspaper let alone two or more.

A couple of posts ago I waxed nostalgic about journalism, fearing that its practitioners are becoming an endangered species. I began with a quote from Thomas Jefferson that we’ve all seen before; he would choose  newspapers without government over a government without newspapers. In his day, the plural form obtained, as one quickly discerns from reading Tocqueville. I concluded that one should support their local paper.

Okay. Now I’m conflicted. The Times is the only game in town. It still pays reporters who get out and about covering events and issues. Do I hold fast to my previous conviction? Or do I practice what I just preached to readers?

This morning I decided to hold my nose and re-enter a subscription to the Seattle Times. I’ll consume the news and likely ignore the editorials, unless I’ve feeling a bit masochistic.

Signed,

The hypocrite.

Journalism as partial disinfectant

April 8, 2013 by davald

The basis of our governments being the opinion of the people, the very first object should be to keep that right; and were it left to me to decide whether we should have a government without newspapers or newspapers without a government, I should not hesitate a moment to prefer the latter.

— Thomas Jefferson

I’m in the middle of watching Netflix’s House of Cards, starring Kevin Spacey. I prefer the original British version, whose main character exhibited a splendid mischievous quality with a grin and a nod. Spacey is more severe, but intriguing nonetheless. Of peculiar interest in the U.S. series is lawmaking. We can appreciate the comparison to sausage. Tawdry business, to be sure, and loyalties are never rewarded.

I have no idea if the portrayal is accurate. I suspect that it comes close to showing us how bills become law, and I would have no part of it. Indeed, though I’ve had multiple opportunities to visit The Hill in my capacity as a local elected official, I’d sooner pine for a trip to the dentist or a sausage maker.

Many of us, I dare say, may concede a certain fascination with politics. For the most part, however, we loathe it, and for very good reasons. No matter what surveys say about our preferences, Congress does its best to frustrate the will of the people in its ongoing dedication to the proposition that the rich must be served while the Rest of Us are screwed. As disgusting as the process is, however, we should probably pay attention to who’s doing the screwing and how.

In an ideal world, islands would be made of candy, to borrow from the AT&T commercial. Also, laws would benefit the Rest of Us. That the world is hardly ideal means that bad people will do bad things, and Congress is the living, awful proof.

Recently I posted on Max Baucus, citing an article in the New York Times that chronicled his self-aggrandizement as a senator from Montana, of all places. Seniority put him in control of the Finance Committee through which major pieces of legislation must pass. Time and again he’s demonstrated his bias in favor of fat cats at the expense of the Rest of Us. But surely he’s not alone. Congress is a big cesspool, after all.

Growing up in the 50s and 60s I drank the civic Kool-Aid. Responsible members of a democratic society not only had a duty to “become involved,” they were expected to change things for the better. How? That part was not so clear. Fellow generational travelers took to the streets to protest racial discrimination then the Vietnam War then environmental assaults. Young people born after Reagan never lived through the “revolutions.” Heck, they probably didn’t read about them in their textbooks.

But I’m getting older, without the advancing wisdom, regrettably. I have little energy to protest, though there is much to oppose. Frankly, I doubt seriously if demonstrations work, if they ever did.

Yet, as much as I detest politics, I welcome and appreciate those who get close enough to expose the stink. No matter how bad the legislative outcomes are now, imagine how much worse they’d be without journalists at work.

Woe unto us should they become endangered species. Support your local newspaper.
