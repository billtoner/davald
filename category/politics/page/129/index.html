Conrad Wold expresses my sentiments of Obama in his Herald op-ed this morning. He writes:

I and others were entranced by everything about Obama — his age, his race, his oratory, his cool, his pretty wife, his jump shot, his un-George Bushness — but as it turns out, it was all superficial. Is debt ceiling a big deal?

July 15, 2011 by davald

I found this article most helpful in answering the above question. Here’s my takeaway.

The federal government, as I’m sure you’re aware, spends more than it takes in. This is a habit, evidently, and only few periods in our history are marked with surpluses.

Currently, the US Treasury’s revenues total $172.4 billion for the month of August. Spending obligations total $306.7 billion. To bridge the difference, the government borrows money. But if Congress fails to increase the debt ceiling, the government can’t borrow. It would have to make cuts equal to the difference between obligations and revenues, or $134.3 billion. Where to begin?

Andrew Leonard lays it out:

First on the list: $29 billion in interest payments on Treasury securities. Even most Republicans believe it would be a horrible idea to default on government debt. It’s the kind of thing that would almost assuredly destabilize global financial markets — perhaps as badly as the credit crunch problems of 2008, or maybe even worse. U.S. Treasury securities are considered the world’s safest haven for parking cash in uncertain times. If the world’s biggest economy can no longer stand behind its debt obligations, we could witness a financial panic that dwarfs the disruptions caused by the bankruptcy of Lehman Brothers.

OK, let’s not do that. Next up, let’s decide that we absolutely, positively must pay for Social Security benefits ($49.2 billion), Medicare ($28.6 billion), Medicaid ($21.4 billion). To do otherwise would invite immediate political annihilation. Republicans area already freaking out at hearing Obama say he couldn’t guarantee Social Security checks would go out on time. What if the GOP was blamed for that?!

Oh, and then there are the troops! Can’t mess with the national defense! Let’s add defense vendor payments ($31. 7 billion), military active duty pay ($2.9 billion) and Veteran’s Affairs ($2.9 billion).

Hmm, what else. Oh, how about unemployment benefits ($12.8 billion)?

Whoops! We just hit $178 billion — we’re $4 billion over the limit. Let’s see, who can we stiff? The unemployed? Defense contractors? The elderly poor? Chinese bondholders?

And what have we left out? How about all federal salaries, food stamps, the Justice Department, Homeland Security, air traffic control, the Centers for Disease Control, NASA, the EPA, and highway funding, for starters. And there’s much, much more.

We should now appreciate the enormity of the debt-ceiling fandango. There is just no way that even crazy Grover Norquist can make the required cuts without jeopardizing his own existence.

We’d like everything for free, thank you.

July 13, 2011 by davald

The NY Times‘ David Leonhardt pens a nice column revealing Americans’ insatiable desire for free lunches. We like all the good stuff, and will resist efforts to diminish them, but we’re sure as heck not going to pay for them. He writes:

Unfortunately, this nostalgic view depends on a misunderstanding of the budget. It imagines a budget in which the United States indefinitely has the world’s highest medical costs, its largest military, an aging population and, nonetheless, taxes that are among the world’s lowest. Economists have a name for that combination: a free lunch.

Members of Congress behave irresponsibly, reflecting their constituencies. They evidently believe that they can get away with such gimmicks as pledging not to raise taxes or even allow shameful subsidies to elapse. Why? Besides strict adherence to a loopy ideology I think the principal reason is that each of them are nearly anonymous to the whole. That is, being one of 435, as in the House, or even one of 100, as in the Senate, provides cover for the ridiculous. No single member, after all, is responsible to the whole.

But simple logic tells us that the aggregation of individual irresponsibility yields collective irresponsibility. I suggest that the only individual in D.C. who may genuinely feel some responsibility to the whole—as in country—is the president. Yet he is clearly hamstrung by the collective irresponsibility of the other branches. He can’t accomplish much of anything without the backing of Congress; and this Congress has no intention of acceding to presidential victories.

An obvious example of the irresponsibility is screaming bloody murder about the horrific federal debt (not really) while simultaneously refusing to raise taxes that would eliminate the debt. (Recall that the country was on a path to retire its debt, had the Clinton-era tax rates continued.)

I started to…then along came Paul.

July 12, 2011 by davald

As I’ve mentioned previously, I give David Brooks’s columns wide berth. I don’t, as a rule, enjoy homiletics. But I chanced on his column this morning, wherein he denigrated those who offer a seemingly simple solution to the nation’s economic ills as engaging in magic, as if we could pull “a magic lever” to make the problems go away. I was all set to critique Brooks until I read Paul Krugman’s blog. Krugman:

Stroking your chin and saying, well, I don’t believe in magical solutions because experience shows that raising growth is hard sounds serious, but it’s actually silly. It’s like saying that it’s really hard to extend the human lifespan, so it’s foolish to believe that an infection can be quickly cured with a dose of antibiotics.

Of course, Paul Krugman is an unabashed Keynesian and believes, with much evidence, that we’re living in a Keynesian moment. The usual strategies to adjust the economy up or down don’t work. Even monetary policy, which lies within Ben Bernanke’s purview, have little effect, given a zero-interest Fed rate.

That said, Brooks will remain forever undeterred. It’s as if he listens only to his own voice, and loves what he hears.

Focusing on jobs

July 11, 2011 by davald

There’s a reason to focus on jobs now rather than, say, budget deficits. There are simply too many of us out of work, and these are people who really and desperately seek employment. The loss of a job is certainly bad for the economy writ large; but surely imposes pain and long-term suffering for those who lack a source of income.

So if persistently high unemployment is a bad thing, what can and should be done about it?

First, acknowledge the problem. This would seem to be obvious, but it’s difficult to discern much anxiety or even sympathy for the long-term unemployed. The focus has been elsewhere.

Second, step back and consider whether or not private economic actors (i.e., employers and investors) can and will come to the rescue. In a normal business cycle, recoveries don’t take long. This one is different. Indeed, it has most of the characteristics of the Great Depression, including a “liquidity trap,” pervasive uncertainty, and, unfortunately, a divided, highly partisan Congress. Last week we learned that the private sector added only 18,000 new jobs in the month of June, while the public sector continued to shed workers. John Maynard Keynes argued that during protracted recessions aggregate demand declines—in a cause-effect-cause relationship. Simply put, people lack the money to buy things. Well, if there are no customers for your wares, why would companies step up production? They wouldn’t, of course, which explains the high unemployment rate.

Third, if the private sector is unwilling or unable to stimulate the economy, who’s left? Keynes concluded that only the federal government had the means and the ability to make a difference. But because we’re in a liquidity trap, monetary strategies like quantitative easing cease to be effective. Interest rates can’t fall below zero, where they are now. Thus, for Keynes, fiscal policy is the only thing left. The government has to spend money, money it lacks. Ideally, that money would be invested in public works: rebuilding and adding to existing infrastructure, public schools, conservation projects, etc. Of course, by spending money it currently lacks, the federal government would increase its debt. But Keynes surmised that once the economy recovered, interest rates would rise, tax revenues would increase, and the government could begin shaving its budget deficits. For Keynes the government should be frugal during good times and spendthrift during the bad.

Alas, the subject of federal spending invokes a Congressional impasse. On one side are the ideologues, blind to economic realities, and woefully unsympathetic to the plight of the unemployed. They oppose federal spending because they oppose “the government.” On the other are the timid and confused. They may accept Keynes’s prescriptions, but are easily overmatched by the feral opposition. Now that the ideologues control the House, they can effect much damage and destroy a presidency as they hold the economy hostage to purely political considerations.

The victims of economic depression have no constituency in Congress. Nor are any NGOs organizing the unemployed as they did during the Great Depression. They are without voice, and there are no sympathetic ears should they dare speak.

A country decidedly inured to the deprivations of over a tenth of its citizens can hardly be called—in the words of John Kenneth Galbraith—a “good society.” The American is truly ugly.

UPDATE (July 11, 2011):

Here’s some much-needed perspective on the unemployment situation. First, words from a victim:

“I have an associate’s degree, and when I lost my union grocery job last summer, it was four months before I found anything. And that anything was Wal-Mart. I lost my health insurance, I had health issues that began to spiral out of control, and I faced an imminent eviction. Now I can only work 25 hours a week or I lose my Medicaid, without which I can’t work or live.”

Now, in the midst of the worst recession since the Great Depression we learn that the rich are doing extraordinarily well, thank you, and they have an entire political party that listens intently to their demands, protecting them from the evil taxers.

OF COURSE, the misery isn’t being shared by America’s ruling class. In fact, Corporate America is profiting as a result. U.S. corporations raked in record profits of $1.68 trillion in the last quarter of 2010, even more than the boom years before the Great Recession, helped in no small part by the jobs crisis.

These profits were achieved in large part because companies were able to return production to pre-crisis levels, or close to it, while employing millions fewer workers–in other words, making their employees work harder to produce more for the same or less pay and benefits.

Ugly.

Who sees the desperate?

July 9, 2011 by davald

The reports, all of them, portend gloom and doom, from the employment data to states’ slashing budgets to an ineffectual Congress. The pain, misery, and frustration of millions of Americans fails to impress policymakers, who are much too busy with other matters.

I am not very hopeful about anything good happening over the next few years. The economy needs to add about 300,000 new jobs a month over an extended period to return to pre-recession levels. Yet, the important people don’t seem to care.

According to this graphic, to add 11 million jobs would require a net of 300,000 a month for almost 37 months. But the recent depressing news indicates that the private sector added a mere 18,000 jobs in June.

The chatter out of Washington is all about deficits and cuts. Even the Great Capitulator has drunk the Kool-Aid. And given the start of the presidential election cycle, there is virtually no possibility that solutions are imminent. Indeed, the Republicans are bent on blaming the bad economic news on Obama, and, I dare say, would sooner throw everyone under a bus for political gain than resolve the crisis.

I said “crisis,” but it hardly seems the case for those in office or on Wall Street. Corporate profits are soaring. Labor productivity continues to rise. What’s the problem?

I’m old enough to remember Robert F. Kennedy paying a visit to Appalachian slums. His encounters with the desperately poor were recorded by television crews. The resultant images startled. How could there by this kind of poverty in America?—many wondered.

I suspect that we’re in a similar situation today, with millions of desperate people out of sight and therefore out of mind. The major media could help inform and advise. But they’re obsessed with short-term ratings. So they focus on child-killers, the next idol, and who will survive the faux island competition.

Only in America.

How far will Obama go?

July 7, 2011 by davald

The news disconcerts. Obama, upon meeting with key leaders in both houses and a private tête-à-tête with Speaker Boehner, offers to modify Medicare and Social Security, although details are sketchy. The Times reports:

The president’s renewed efforts follow what knowledgeable officials said was an overture from Mr. Boehner, who met secretly with Mr. Obama last weekend, to consider as much as $1 trillion in unspecified new revenues as part of an overhaul of tax laws in exchange for an agreement that made substantial spending cuts, including in such social programs as Medicare and Medicaid and Social Security — programs that had been off the table.

Recently, Mr. Boehner proclaimed that tax cuts are “off the table.” He appears to be more stubborn than the president in adhering to his principles, such as they are.

As for increasing revenues, it seems that Republicans are open to eliminating the egregious subsidies to oil and gas companies and even tax breaks for “corporate jets.” But there have been no reports of agreements to let the Bush-era tax cuts expire without renewal.

A few Democrats grumble on the expectation that they may be asked to simply accept the negotiated deal emerging from the White House. The Times:

House Democrats have their own fears about the negotiations, which they expressed in an hourlong meeting Wednesday night with Treasury Secretary Timothy F. Geithner.

“Depending on what they decide to recommend, they may not have Democrats,” Senator Sheldon Whitehouse, a Rhode Island Democrat, said in an interview. “I think it is a risky thing for the White House to basically take the bet that we can be presented with something at the last minute and we will go for it.”

We’ve got to hand it to the Republicans. Despite being in the minority for the first two years of Obama’s presidency, they managed to thwart the more ambitious Democratic proposals and sharply modify others, including the stimulus package, comprised mostly of GOP-cherished tax cuts. Now that they control the House, their harebrained ideas ascend to prominence; they now set the agenda, because of the Great Capitulator.

How about this? Bernie Sanders for president. Pass it on.
