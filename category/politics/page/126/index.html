A simple remedy

August 19, 2011 by davald

For many of us, the problems we currently face are easily remedied. What’s in the way is not a lack of proper ideas but political will. Indeed, we have at least one party intent on throwing the entire economy under the bus rather than see another Obama term.

Writing for the London Review of Books professor Corey Robin offers a serious yet familiar solution to what ails us. He writes:

Had politics not intruded and had Obama heeded the advice of centre-left economists [among them, his own advisors and Prof. Krugman], these are the steps he might have taken. First, do nothing about the debt, at least not now. The debt can be significantly reduced only if the economy improves. The best way to encourage that is for the government to spend, which will add to the debt in the short term but reduce it in the long term through revenues generated by growth. Second, once the economy is healthy, increase taxes, particularly on the wealthy. As a share of GDP, revenues are at their lowest levels since 1950. With the exception of a brief interregnum in the late 1980s and early 1990s, top marginal rates are the lowest they’ve been since 1931. Corporate taxes in the US are the lowest of any OECD country. The notion that taxes shouldn’t be raised, not only to fund necessary and desirable expenditure but also to cut the debt, runs counter to common sense. Last, cut military spending. As the economic journalist Doug Henwood has observed, if the US simply returned to the spending levels of 2000 – when ‘the Pentagon didn’t have to hold bake sales’ and spending was 3.7 per cent of GDP as opposed to the current 5.4 per cent – it could save $3.6 trillion in the next decade, 72 per cent more than the debt-ceiling deal negotiated by Obama and Congress will save.

But, as he condemns the measures taken by Bush II, Robin excoriates Obama, who, he believes, holds much responsibility for prolonging a stagnant economy. It was Bush, as Robin reminds us, who cut taxes and launched two wars on borrowed money. Instead of paying off the massive U.S. debt, as would have occurred by merely perpetuating the Clinton policies, Bush added more to it than any president in history. As for Obama’s failures, here’s Robin:

Instead, Obama and Congress took the opposite path, which was paved 40 years ago by the anti-tax philosophy of the American right. In February 2010, Obama convened a bipartisan commission to balance the budget by 2015, effectively making debt reduction a top priority. After the November midterm election, when the Republicans took back the House with the help of the Tea Party, Obama froze the pay of federal workers and endorsed a more aggressive austerity programme. Cuts were proposed and tax increases dismissed: not just once (with the extension of the Bush tax cuts in December) but twice (with the first phase of the debt deal, which eliminates $900 billion solely through spending cuts) and now potentially a third time (with the second phase of the deal, which eliminates an additional $1.2 trillion solely through spending cuts if a congressional committee can’t produce an alternative package of tax increases and spending cuts by November). While the deal does include defence cuts – though it’s unclear if these are cuts or simply slower rates of growth, and whether and how most of them will happen – Obama’s latest comments, and those of his defence secretary, suggest they could be traded for benefit cuts.

Robin considers the widely divergent interpretations of Obama’s character, giving partial credence to all, while ultimately dismissing them as almost beside the point. These, too, are familiar to readers: Obama’s “preternatural aversion to conflict”; the now-evident fundamental flaws in our federal political system; and that Obama is not the progressive that many had hoped for.

However, the current problems, Robin suggests, should be viewed within a wider, historical context. Populations facing similar situations—war and associated fiscal crises—”have catalysed politically progressive advances and even precipitated revolutions.” The focus has now shifted to the few genuine progressives among us who allowed the present tragedy to unfold over decades. Robin concludes:

And here Democrats like Obama and his defenders, who bemoan the stranglehold of the Tea Party on American politics, have only themselves to blame. For decades, Democrats have collaborated in stripping back the American state in the vain hope that the market would work its magic. For a time it did, though mostly through debt; workers could compensate for stagnating wages with easy credit and low-interest mortgages. Now the debt’s due to be repaid, and wages – if people are lucky enough to be working – aren’t enough to cover the bills. The only thing that’s left for them is cutting taxes. And the imperialism of the peasants.

That last phrase, by the way, comes straight from Karl Marx.

Who needs whom?

August 19, 2011 by davald

I was recently treated to an email exchange that seems to have ended in a vitriolic rant against “the system.” The rest of the posts echoed a similar theme: it really doesn’t matter whom one votes for, the result will be the same—bad for us and very good for the well-heeled. The solution, as far as I can divine from the exchange, is to forget about electoral politics and concentrate on building a better future with like-minded individuals voluntarily providing the necessities of life to the self-ostracized group. Or something like that.

One of the exchangers linked to a webpage. The author announced that “we don’t need them.” “Them” being the rich and powerful and all who serve their interests, including both political parties, the corporate media, and the millions of voters who enable their hold on the Rest of Us.

As I write this I’m in front of my computer display with NORAD-like processors, hard drives, and so on. I’m surrounded by the accoutrements of our modern, technological economy. In other words, I benefit directly from “the system,” as much as I, too, would like to start all over. So, it’s rather hypocritical of me to bash that which has satisfied my needs as well as several wants.

That said, I wholeheartedly concur with the assessment that it really doesn’t seem to matter who sits in the Oval Office and the seats of Congress. The Rest of Us get screwed in a very bipartisan fashion. Instead of apologizing for our elected candidates or, pray tell, seeking to improve the system from within, the apparently more rational approach would be to first declare your independence from “them”—after all, who needs them?—then work with fellow travelers to live autonomously, freed from the vast, powerful tentacles of the military-industrial-political complex.

What might this alternative future look like? One of the posters cites Noam Chomsky. Asked that very question, Prof. Chomsky reportedly responded: “I don’t know how it will look, but it will be different. It will be up to you and on a local level what meets your needs. It will look different all over, but it will be different from what it is now.”

I must now confess that I’m either too old, too tired, or both. I lack the will and the energy to create my own world apart from “the system”—even with a little help from my friends.

On the other hand, we might try to understand that the rich and the powerful need us, even should we decide that we no longer need them. We can imagine what would happen should a critical mass of the Rest of Us withhold our time, energy, and skills from “the system.”

Well, it’s actually happening right now. It’s called unemployment, and it’s hardly voluntary.

The global economy, as it turns out, needs us minions to buy its products and services. If we no longer can, because we lack adequately paying jobs, then the rich and the powerful worry. They lose confidence. They decline to invest. They accumulate cash, then sit on it.

It took the Great Depression to effectively rattle “the system,” resulting in the most expansive government in our nation’s history. The New Deal programs, had they progressed, just might have morphed into the social democratic societies enjoyed by our counterparts across the pond. But something got in the way. Something that took people’s minds off building a better society. Something that finally got us to work.

That something was, of course, World War II. And that was no accident.

Upon further analysis…

August 17, 2011 by davald

…the economic news is worse than previously reported. That’s one takeaway from this article in the New York Times.

The Bureau of Economic Analysis renders judgments about the state of the economy based on the “best available data.” Unfortunately, the data themselves are largely projections or estimates; and too many policymakers rely on the initial BEA reports. The Times:

When the government announced in April that the economy had grown at a moderate annual pace of 1.8 percent in the first quarter, politicians and investors saw evidence that the nation was continuing its recovery from the depths of the financial crisis. The White House called the news “encouraging” and the stock market extended its bull run.

When calculating economic growth, the government makes an educated guess about spending on construction and cellphone bills. An estimate of growth in the first quarter was changed after a revision of the value of vehicles at dealerships and spending on imported oil.

Three months later, the government announced a small change. The economy, it said, actually had expanded at a pace of only 0.4 percent in the first quarter.

In other words, the initial estimate was off by -78%.

It would behoove policymakers and politicians to generally ignore the early reports, since the underlying data, as it turns out, are ephemeral and wrong. Don’t get too excited, then, whether up or down.

On the other hand, overall the news is grim, which should lead to some attempts to correct. I say “should.” But I doubt seriously that we can expect any real solutions before, let’s say, November of next year.

And speaking of entitlements…

August 15, 2011 by davald

Ironies abound. Here in Washington state voters rejected a splendid opportunity to impose an income tax on only wealthy individuals, something Oregon recently did. I scratched my head in search of answers. Was it really a fairness issue, as Seattle Times‘ columnist Danny Westneat argued on the eve of the initiative vote? Please.

In today’s New York Times we’re treated to another irony: a very rich man wants to be taxed more heavily. The Rest of Us choose to coddle the rich while some of the rich say that’s unfair—to the Rest of Us. Warren Buffett writes:

These and other blessings are showered upon us by legislators in Washington who feel compelled to protect us, much as if we were spotted owls or some other endangered species. It’s nice to have friends in high places.

Last year my federal tax bill — the income tax I paid, as well as payroll taxes paid by me and on my behalf — was $6,938,744. That sounds like a lot of money. But what I paid was only 17.4 percent of my taxable income — and that’s actually a lower percentage than was paid by any of the other 20 people in our office. Their tax burdens ranged from 33 percent to 41 percent and averaged 36 percent.

If you make money with money, as some of my super-rich friends do, your percentage may be a bit lower than mine. But if you earn money from a job, your percentage will surely exceed mine — most likely by a lot.

Buffett wants to send a message to the new super committee of twelve, which is charged with the task of reducing the federal deficit. The Republicans on the committee will surely reject any proposal to increase tax revenues. But Buffett argues otherwise:

…I would leave rates for 99.7 percent of taxpayers unchanged and continue the current 2-percentage-point reduction in the employee contribution to the payroll tax. This cut helps the poor and the middle class, who need every break they can get.

But for those making more than $1 million — there were 236,883 such households in 2009 — I would raise rates immediately on taxable income in excess of $1 million, including, of course, dividends and capital gains. And for those who make $10 million or more — there were 8,274 in 2009 — I would suggest an additional increase in rate.

My friends and I have been coddled long enough by a billionaire-friendly Congress. It’s time for our government to get serious about shared sacrifice.

Amen, Brother Warren. By the way, his taxable income last year was about $40 million.

It’s about jobs, stupid

August 15, 2011 by davald

Will Republicans thwart every effort to create jobs? Evidently, yes, if they believe continued high unemployment rates can be blamed on Obama and the Democrats. To what end? To regain the White House and control of the Senate.

The bigger question: Will enough voters be suckered into this narrative?

Imagine Michele Bachman in the Oval Office, aided and abetted by a Republican Congress. Scary stuff.

The New York Times editorializes on jobs. The editors:

By a large margin, Americans have told pollsters that job creation is more important than budget cuts. Yet Republican leaders are wedded to austerity and appear to think that high unemployment will hurt President Obama politically more than it will hurt them, so they will likely resist efforts to create jobs, no matter how great the need.

Without more jobs, both the economy and the budget will deteriorate further. It is past time for Mr. Obama to send a jobs plan to Congress that has popular appeal, one that he can use to try to shame Republicans. He will need cooperation from the Senate, which should bring one jobs-related bill after another to the floor, forcing its members to approve jobs initiatives or go on the record to show that they just don’t care.

Those “liberals”

August 11, 2011 by davald

It’s hardly scientific, but I suspect that we can gain a sense of what types of people live in our community from the letters they write to the local newspaper. One such letter caught my attention while reading this morning’s Herald.

The writer, who lives in Arlington, was responding to a piece the newspaper ran on the proposed coal-shipping terminal near Bellingham. The idea is to transport Wyoming coal to China by rail, passing through cities like Marysville situated along the tracks. Opponents of this project, including Marysville’s mayor, object on a couple of grounds: (1) the additional train traffic; and (2) enabling the combustion of coal, the main source of greenhouse gases. (We should appreciate that it really doesn’t matter where the coal is burned; the gases and their effects become global problems.)

The writer:

With 14 million of our citizens unemployed, a stock market in steep decline, and an economy that makes a morgue look like it’s in the business of recovery, any thinker with good sense and an equal amount of goodwill should see that, far from a moral shame, coal coming from the west and through the Puget Sound is a benefit to our economy.

I noticed that not once did the writer mention global warming or greenhouse gases. Perhaps, like Senator Inhofe, he believes that it’s all a “hoax” perpetrated by the same “liberals” who oppose the coal transport. After all, “any thinker with good sense” should understand the economic benefits of mining, shipping, and burning coal. It’s all plusses with no minuses. I suppose that even the mayor of Marysville should suck it up for the economy. What’s a few extra trains, after all.

Politics, as usual

August 10, 2011 by davald

Robert Reich:

Which gets me to the President. Even though the President’s two former top economic advisors (Larry Summers and Christy Roemer) have called for a major fiscal boost to the economy, the President has remained mum. Why?

I’m told White House political operatives are against a bold jobs plan. They believe the only jobs plan that could get through Congress would be so watered down as to have almost no impact by Election Day. They also worry the public wouldn’t understand how more government spending in the near term can be consistent with long-term deficit reduction. And they fear Republicans would use any such initiative to further bash Obama as a big spender.

So rather than fight for a bold jobs plan, the White House has apparently decided it’s politically wiser to continue fighting about the deficit. The idea is to keep the public focused on the deficit drama – to convince them their current economic woes have something to do with it, decry Washington’s paralysis over fixing it, and then claim victory over whatever outcome emerges from the process recently negotiated to fix it. They hope all this will distract the public’s attention from the President’s failure to do anything about continuing high unemployment and economic anemia.

If the nominal leader of the Democratic Party says essentially the same things as the opposition, then the horrible mess we’re in will likely last a very long time. Think Great Depression and Japan.

A bad few days for the press—and the president, again

August 9, 2011 by davald

Both the NY Times and the Seattle Times published wrong headlines this morning. Both attributed yesterday’s 600+ point drop in the Dow to Friday’s S&P’s downgrade of U.S. credit. If investors were supposed to be spooked by the downgrade, then they would have either bought fewer Treasury bonds or bid up the yield (interest rate). But that didn’t happen. Both stock prices and interest rates fell.

The former has everything to do with the continuing uncertainty in the economy exacerbated by a dysfunctional government unwilling and evidently unable to provide a remedy. The latter, the decline in bond interest rates, completely repudiates the S&P downgrade. After all, why buy Treasury bonds if you doubt that you’ll be repaid?

Roger Myerson writes:

So it is very difficult to see investors’ behavior today as a reaction to fears about inflationary deficits or a default on US government debt.  If serious investors were actually worried about the real value of US government liabilities then they should tend to move out of bond markets and into real investments like the stock market, which should drive stock prices up.

Our feckless leader told the nation that S&P’s downgrade should bring even more urgency to reducing the budget deficit. He said:

Our challenge is the need to tackle our deficits over the long term.

Oh, and what does he intend to do to fix the problems?

 I intend to present my own recommendations over the coming weeks on how we should proceed.

“Over the coming weeks”?

The president also said:

 The fact is, we didn’t need a rating agency to tell us that we need a balanced, long-term approach to deficit reduction.  That was true last week.  That was true last year.  That was true the day I took office.

And you’ve been in office how many days? It’s over 900 and still counting.

UPDATE (Aug. 8, 2011):

As for Obama’s oratory…Andrew Malcolm writes:

Have you noticed how many remarks Obama has been making in recent weeks? Not a coincidence that they coincide with his lowest approval ratings ever. When in trouble, give a speech. Even if there’s no visible audience.

Calling on Congress to do this and that. Calling for more job creation. Calling for green cars and more windmills. Calling for a balanced approach from others when he had none of his own.

Claiming his positions were quite clear, when they weren’t. Calling for compromise between Republicans and Democrats, as if he was in the middle and not the nation’s top Democrat.

So, it was natural Monday when — after a weekend’s stewing, global markets reacted negatively to the U.S. downgrade — Obama’s instinct tempted him to make a public statement.

Breaking the arc of history

August 7, 2011 by davald

We are in the summer of our discontents. Those of us, that is, who are either intellectually struggling with the mess we’re in, how we got here, and, most important, how we might extricate ourselves—or are physically and emotionally struggling with how to put food on our table, a roof over our heads, and a job to give us income and dignity. If any time cried out for a strong, significant leader it is now, with a sputtering, jobless economy, housing foreclosures, declining real wages, two costly wars, and a revanchist, greedy Wall Street.

But we seem to have elected the wrong person to the White House. Instead of a Roosevelt we got a, well, Great Capitulator or, in Krugman’s words, “President Pushover.” Whereas Teddy Roosevelt took on the powerful trusts and his cousin FDR welcomed the concerted hatred of the monied interests, Barack Obama gleefully takes their cash while throwing the rest of us elegant rhetoric that we can neither eat nor spend.

Many of us have taken to wonder who is this guy Obama. Were we misled? Is he well-meaning but woefully inadequate to the task? Does he know how to do only one thing well—speak nice words? We’re told that he’s bright by those closer to him. But I’ve seen no signs of wisdom, no indications that he is in any sense walking among us or feeling our pain. For me he remains aloof, disconnected, bewildered, overmatched.

The more charitable among us cling to the belief that Obama always meant well and that his words were substantive. Yet in his brief reign he’s somehow lost his way.

One such person is Emory University professor Drew Westen. In several pages of the New York Times, Prof. Westen ponders what might have been.

He was there for the inauguration of Barack Obama—with his eight-year-old daughter. He says that even then, as he listened to Obama’s words, “he had a feeling of unease,” as if he were witnessing a harbinger of darker days to come. Westen would have preferred a different narrative on that august January. Something like this:

“I know you’re scared and angry. Many of you have lost your jobs, your homes, your hope. This was a disaster, but it was not a natural disaster. It was made by Wall Street gamblers who speculated with your lives and futures. It was made by conservative extremists who told us that if we just eliminated regulations and rewarded greed and recklessness, it would all work out. But it didn’t work out. And it didn’t work out 80 years ago, when the same people sold our grandparents the same bill of goods, with the same results. But we learned something from our grandparents about how to fix it, and we will draw on their wisdom. We will restore business confidence the old-fashioned way: by putting money back in the pockets of working Americans by putting them back to work, and by restoring integrity to our financial markets and demanding it of those who want to run them. I can’t promise that we won’t make mistakes along the way. But I can promise you that they will be honest mistakes, and that your government has your back again.”

Of course, that’s not what Westen or any of us heard. Yes, we got elegance, nearly a given when Obama speaks formally. But absolutely no leadership, no hint that this new president understood the challenges that lay ahead or what he could and should do to address the huge problems facing this nation.

Obama talked then and later before Congress of a three-point agenda: health care reform, the environment, and energy. He only talked, leaving the “details” to a legislature that would eventually be stymied by a recalcitrant brand of “young guns,” dedicated to ensuring that Obama served but one term and dismantling both government and the public sector. As a result of his evident hands-off approach, the crazies seized the opportunity to undermine his entire agenda, demonizing him in the process.

Westen reminds us of what FDR did:

In similar circumstances, Franklin D. Roosevelt offered Americans a promise to use the power of his office to make their lives better and to keep trying until he got it right. Beginning in his first inaugural address, and in the fireside chats that followed, he explained how the crash had happened, and he minced no words about those who had caused it. He promised to do something no president had done before: to use the resources of the United States to put Americans directly to work, building the infrastructure we still rely on today. He swore to keep the people who had caused the crisis out of the halls of power, and he made good on that promise. In a 1936 speech at Madison Square Garden, he thundered, “Never before in all our history have these forces been so united against one candidate as they stand today. They are unanimous in their hate for me — and I welcome their hatred.”

Obama doesn’t welcome. He cowers and capitulates—or so it seems.

Westen is as confused by Obama as the rest of us. He writes:

Like most Americans, at this point, I have no idea what Barack Obama — and by extension the party he leads — believes on virtually any issue. The president tells us he prefers a “balanced” approach to deficit reduction, one that weds “revenue enhancements” (a weak way of describing popular taxes on the rich and big corporations that are evading them) with “entitlement cuts” (an equally poor choice of words that implies that people who’ve worked their whole lives are looking for handouts). But the law he just signed includes only the cuts. This pattern of presenting inconsistent positions with no apparent recognition of their incoherence is another hallmark of this president’s storytelling. He announces in a speech on energy and climate change that we need to expand offshore oil drilling and coal production — two methods of obtaining fuels that contribute to the extreme weather Americans are now seeing. He supports a health care law that will use Medicaid to insure about 15 million more Americans and then endorses a budget plan that, through cuts to state budgets, will most likely decimate Medicaid and other essential programs for children, senior citizens and people who are vulnerable by virtue of disabilities or an economy that is getting weaker by the day. He gives a major speech on immigration reform after deporting a million immigrants in two years, breaking up families at a pace George W. Bush could never rival in all his years as president.

With respect to divining the real Obama, I find myself sympathetic to Westen’s somber speculation.

A somewhat less charitable explanation is that we are a nation that is being held hostage not just by an extremist Republican Party but also by a president who either does not know what he believes or is willing to take whatever position he thinks will lead to his re-election. Perhaps those of us who were so enthralled with the magnificent story he told in “Dreams From My Father” appended a chapter at the end that wasn’t there — the chapter in which he resolves his identity and comes to know who he is and what he believes in.

Or perhaps, like so many politicians who come to Washington, he has already been consciously or unconsciously corrupted by a system that tests the souls even of people of tremendous integrity, by forcing them to dial for dollars — in the case of the modern presidency, for hundreds of millions of dollars. When he wants to be, the president is a brilliant and moving speaker, but his stories virtually always lack one element: the villain who caused the problem, who is always left out, described in impersonal terms, or described in passive voice, as if the cause of others’ misery has no agency and hence no culpability. Whether that reflects his aversion to conflict, an aversion to conflict with potential campaign donors that today cripples both parties’ ability to govern and threatens our democracy, or both, is unclear.

Aside from the increasing chances of another recession, I fear this outcome in 2012, aided and abetted by an inept president unwilling to confront evil and rally the rest of us in the manner of a Roosevelt: a total “shellacking” by the feral Republicans who then assume control of everything. That will be hell on earth.

UPDATE (Aug. 7, 2011):

Maureen Down provides a simpler gloss:

Barack Obama blazed like Luke Skywalker in 2008, but he never learned to channel the Force. And now the Tea Party has run off with his light saber.

UPDATE (Aug. 7, 2011):

Ted Van Dyk, writing in Crosscut:

I had grown increasingly impatient with Obama’s halting and inconsistent leadership style but was a bit surprised to find that even hard-core Democratic partisans also had run out of patience with him.  I also was surprised to find the depth of irritation with Obama held by even the most senior Democratic Hill leaders.  Ranking House Democrat Nancy Pelosi, for example, said publicly until the last minute that she was undecided about supporting Obama’s debt-limit deal with Republicans; she finally relented and voted “yes.”

One senior Senate and one senior House Democrat each told me they found Obama to be aloof and outrightly unfriendly to them.    Several noted the historically high White House staff turnover and laid it to Obama’s similarly abrupt and frosty in-house style.  All of this became evident late in the debt-ceiling negotiations when House Speaker John Boehner walked out of bargaining with Obama and Senate Democrats, led by Sen. Harry Reid, entered into independent negotiations with Boehner, leaving Obama a spectator for several awkward days.  Another frequent complaint:  That the White House increasingly was dominated by short-term political considerations and that 2008 campaign manager David Plouffe had become the dominant player there.
