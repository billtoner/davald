Building for no one

January 21, 2014 by davald

Last year Washington state’s legislature completed its regular term, then was called into multiple supplemental sessions. One item left unfinished, though serving as a pretext for sticking around Olympia, was an omnibus transportation package. What will we do with all these new cars trying to get to and from?

But suppose that the push for more and wider roads is based on faulty assumptions. What if the forecasted traffic never materialized?

Well, that’s the point being made in Sightline by Clark Williams-Derry. Forecasters continued to projected more and more vehicle miles traveled every year. Now look at this chart:

 

As a nation we are driving less than we used to. There are no doubt many reasons for this, including the usual suspects: increased reliance on alternative transportation (e.g., walking, bicycling, busses); shorter distances between A and B; and higher gas prices depressing the number and length of trips. Also, a stagnant economy doesn’t help.

I throw out another possibility borne of personal experience: Traffic has gotten so bad as to reach a tipping point; it’s grossly inconvenient to use a car to get anywhere. We travel less and less because the freeways are incessantly clogged.

At any rate, Williams-Derry has a point:

It’d be humorous, if the fiscal consequences weren’t so dire. Washington’s transportation debate is a case in point: existing roads are in desperate need of maintenance and transit is hurting for money, but state legislators felt that they needed a transportation package full of highway megaprojects, financed through regressive taxes, to deal with all the “new” traffic we haven’t been getting for about a decade.

Gun culture

January 16, 2014 by davald

The New York Times reports on schools adopting a new culture in response to recent tragedies. There will be more.

For students across the country, lockdowns have become a fixture of the school day, the duck-and-cover drills for a generation growing up in the shadow of Columbine High School in Colorado and Sandy Hook Elementary School in Connecticut. Kindergartners learn to hide quietly behind bookshelves. Teachers warn high-school students that the glow of their cellphones could make them targets. And parents get regular text messages from school officials alerting them to lockdowns.

Since this is my blog, and blogs are all about opinions, I’ll offer mine. I detest guns, find them absolutely unnecessary for “the security of a free state,” and cannot begin to fathom their justification, especially after yet another massacre. Even the slaughter of dozens of young children fails to rein them in. Indeed, in a perverse way, each travesty triggers an increase in gun purchases on the pretext that the government will react to the bloodshed with controls, however minimal and ineffective.

Among the gun nuts is the counterargument that the Swiss have lots of guns, yet they don’t go around shooting one another. So keeping and bearing arms, per se, does not necessarily produce violence and mayhem.

I would suggest that we have an unwell society, unlike the Swiss. Certainly we tolerate pervasive poverty and inequality—absent among the Swiss, whose Gini index (level of income maldistribution) is much lower than ours, 29 to about 47, respectively.

Inequality is important, I believe, because it’s virtually impossible that extreme wealth can peaceably coexist with vast numbers of poor and near-poor. Moreover, the U.S. is a giant country compared to, say, Switzerland, and its huge expanse contributed to America’s well-deserved reputation as a “gunfighter nation.”

Within the U.S. both gun-murder rates and Gini coefficients vary, though not as much as between the U.S. as a whole and western European nations. Here’s a graph comparing the two variables among the states:

 

Inequality explains almost half the variance. We note that the District of Columbia exhibits the highest inequality and murder rate. It’s also under the jurisdiction of Congress, which has been loath to pass meaningful restrictions on guns.

That said, given the current complexion of the Supreme Court and the considerable influence of the gun lobby I do not see much hope of restrictive legislation in the near future. The violence will continue—along with inequality, about which Congress is also loath to address

The water divide

January 15, 2014 by davald

We hear much these days about the division of economists into “salt water” and “fresh water.” The former refers to those who inhabit coastal academias; they tend to be more liberal and embrace Keynesian policies. The latter mostly reside at the University of Chicago; they are prone to reject fiscal and monetary stimuli.

We can see the division play out in this open letter to congressional leaders. I did not see any economist affiliated with Chicago. There are about a half-dozen Nobel laureates among the signatories—none close to Lake Michigan.

The letter urges Congress to pass an increase in the federal minimum wage to $10.10 by 2016, phased in over three years from the current $7.25/hour. Those working full-time at that amount would earn a whopping $21,000 a year.

Republicans, as a rule, oppose raising the minimum wage, or any minimum at all, because they say they’re worried about jobs. If employers must pay more, they’ll either lay off workers or shutter their doors—and we know how much Republicans care about workers. The letter avers that a higher minimum would have little, if any, effect on employment levels. They write:

In recent years there have been important developments in the academic literature on the effect of increases in the minimum wage on employment, with the weight of evidence now showing that increases in the minimum wage have had little or no negative effect on the employment of minimum-wage workers, even during times of weakness in the labor market. Research suggests that a minimum-wage increase could have a small stimulative effect on the economy as low-wage workers spend their additional earnings, raising demand and job growth, and providing some help on the jobs front.

I suspect that the economists will be largely ignored.

Common carrier

January 15, 2014 by davald

Suppose you build an electric transmission line. Under federal law a third-party resource developer would have access to those lines to deliver his/her electricity to a retail customer connected to a distribution grid. The terms and conditions for such access would be the same is it would for the transmission provider, should it also be in the business of generating electricity. The provision is called ‘common carrier.’ In theory common carrier keeps prices down for the consumer, because it prohibits the provider from limiting supplier competition.

Likewise, governments build highways for motor vehicles. Any driver of any car or truck (with some restrictions) can enter the road and exit it. The government cannot limit access only to Chevrolets, for example.

Now we turn to the Internet, initially billed as “the information superhighway.” If it were analogous to a public road, then anyone could get on and off under the same terms and conditions as anyone else. The “pipes” (or series of tubes) would serve as “common carriers.”

In practice, common carrier works as it was intended. How do we know? Because countries like France made it so. Those who provide telecommunication services (whether phone, Internet, or TV) can upload their content to an information superhighway—regardless of who built, owns, or operates it—that will be delivered to a retail consumer, say a Parisian resident. As a result of this arrangement, the French pay a small fraction of the price that U.S. citizens pay for comparable service.

But something happened along the way of establishing rules for the Telecommunications Act (1996). The FCC blew it, failing to call Internet service providers ‘utilities,’ which would have subject them to common-carrier provisions.

For a rather angry reaction to the DC Circuit court’s decision, negating the FCC’s belated attempts to put the Internet Genie back in the bottle, read this piece in The Verge by Nilay Patel. He writes:

So, this is going to be chaos. All you’re going to hear from now on is that net neutrality proponents want to “regulate the internet,” a conflation so insidious it boggles the mind. Comcast and Time Warner Cable and Verizon are not the internet. We are the internet — the people. It is us who make things like Reddit and Facebook and Twitter vibrant communities of unfiltered conversation. It is us who wield the unaffected market power that picks Google over Bing and Amazon over everything. It’s us who turned Netflix from a DVD-by-mail company into a video giant that uses a third of the US internet’s bandwidth each night. And it is us who can quit stable but boring corporate jobs to start new businesses like The Verge and Vox Media without anyone’s permission.

Comcast and Verizon are just pipes. The dumber the better.

Dead on arrival

January 15, 2014 by davald

John Quiggin writes:

The fact that Obama is not a wild-eyed socialist, or even a traditional US liberal, but rather a moderate conservative may be a revelation in some Republican circles, but it is scarcely news to the rest of us.

Quiggin, in the same post, is taking on Ross Douthat, the New York Times‘ “official Republican columnist,” who has urged his party to offer programs that appeal to the working class. But as Quiggin notes:

It’s patently obvious, however, that there is zero support for this program in any of the leading factions of the Republican Party, either among the leadership or in the activist base. There isn’t a single program benefitting the working class, from Social Security to the Earned Income Tax Credit to unemployment benefits to food stamps that can command the support of more than a handful of Republicans in Congress, and those few are likely to be driven out before long.

The political asymmetry might trouble many. Feral conservatives have a home in the new Republican Party. Their less exuberant counterparts on the left have nothing, save for their ability to organize outside established institutions. As a result, earnest progressives often avoid elections, which helps, rather than impedes, the country’s rightward trajectory.

There was a time in America when the Democratic Party stood for the working class. Indeed, that was its base and major source of income. But with the steady decline in union participation rates, both power and financial contributions also fell, leaving the party to look elsewhere. It turned to Wall Street and corporate America, beginning with Clinton.

Today presidential elections cost more than a billion dollars. The Rest of Us don’t have any money, which leaves us without official representation nor any chance, really, of influencing legislation.

The hope, I suppose, is that there are enough people with a conscience serving in Congress and state legislatures who haven’t yet completely abandoned the shriveling working class. Still looking.

Or, maybe it’s possible to somehow align the interests of domestic capital with domestic labor. Can something be good for both Wall Street and the Rest of Us? Only under Clinton’s second term did the incomes of all quintiles rise. The economic pie grew along with the wages of the Rest of Us. The rich, as always, did even better (data from Saez).

Perhaps we can only look in the rearview mirror. Those relative halcyon days of the 50s and 60s may have been an anomaly in an otherwise persistent trend in divergence.

Downton Abbey syndrome

January 15, 2014 by davald

If there is one takeaway from the PBS series it’s the stark differences between nobility and “staff.” Lines must never be crossed, and formalities must always be practiced. Even as the British lords and ladies grudgingly accommodate changing times, appearances count no less.

In watching the episodes I, for one, gain a dramatic appreciation for what is meant by conservatism. Royalty staunchly defends the status quo against progressive forces. After all, the status quo is good for the wealthy and a severe impediment to everyone else.

In the Republican Party I witness that top-down legacy at work. It is alive and well, operating on multiple stages, tragically aided and abetted by those it systematically screws.

Yesterday, Washington state governor Jay Inslee stood before the legislature to challenge the current status quo. He professed  support for increased education funding, higher pay for teachers, and a raise in the state’s minimum wage. The reaction from the Downton Abbey contingent was as swift as it was predictable: dead on arrival.

President Obama triggers a similar response each time he speaks (but rarely acts) in favor of reducing the chasm between the rich and the Rest of Us. Republicans counter with ideology devoid of facts, understandable since we know the latter have a known liberal bias.

And so we sit, unable and unwilling to move toward an economy that treats citizens with dignity rather than as mere cogs in an impersonal machine. Our collective shame is that we enable the continuing debasement, failing to understand that modern-day lords and ladies have no intention of letting us walk up the stairs, save for tending to their every need and desire. How easily purchased, we are.

 

Berkeley economist Emmanuel Saez, upon whose data the above chart is based, writes:

…the Great Recession has only depressed top income shares temporarily and will not undo any of the dramatic increase in top income shares that has taken place since the 1970s. Indeed, the top decile income share in 2012 is equal to 50.4%, the highest ever since 1917 when the series start.

As the rich accumulate more and more wealth they wield more and more political power. That power is used, as always, to keep the Rest of Us in tow. Some of us are still needed for service, though at ever diminishing wages. Those deemed surplus have seen the floor beneath them gradually weaken. Food stamps. Unemployment insurance. Wages. Pensions. Public education. All in the crosshairs of the lords and ladies.

Stratified

January 14, 2014 by davald

Higher education attainment levels correlate with higher employment rates. So we can understand, if not quite condone, the push by politicians on both sides of the political aisle for everyone to get a college degree. Ignored are the mountains of student debt.

However, even those with college degrees are still not working. It’s worse for those lacking a baccalaureate. Worse, still, for high school dropouts (data from St. Louis FED).

There are far too many people waiting in the unemployment lines (virtual rather than real) for too few job openings. FRED tracks the number of people who want a job now but keep striking out.

Dismal stuff, to be sure. Are the bad people we elect doing anything about it? Well, actually, they’re determined to make matters even worse by cutting off assistance and refusing to raise the minimum wage; nor are they willing to invest in much-needed public infrastructure repairs and expansion. Their message is as clear and consistent as it is wrong, on both moral and economic grounds: it’s your own damn fault for—take your pick—being unemployed, poor, hungry, homeless. The list is endless.

Being apolitical

January 13, 2014 by davald

Bad men need nothing more to compass their ends, than that good men should look on and do nothing.

— John Stuart Mill

Is it in my genes? My grandfather was heavily involved in politics from the left, being a strong union man when that meant something. My father followed in his footsteps, belonging to the Oil, Chemical, and Atomic Workers union for most of his adult life. (I recall the dark days of the extended strike at the Richmond, Calif., refinery when belts were severely tightened and food judiciously distributed at the table.) We were, as a breed, Democrats to the bone. The only mention of Republicans was in cursed language. The favored adjectives were “damn” and “bastard.”

With such background I inherited a history of workingman politics, in which the rich were scorned, the poor pitied and assisted, and the Rest of Us struggled to make a better life. It helped that I was raised in California, then under the governorship of the first Brown, where the education system was the envy of the world and good-paying jobs plentiful. I matriculated at Berkeley—tuition free.

To be sure, I did not quickly take to the political life, largely skating through high school and even college, generally oblivious to events around me, save for the Kennedy assassination, which struck me deeply. I pursued baseball, eschewing protests of any kind, though there was surely much to oppose, from overt racial discrimination to the Vietnam War, which I managed to avoid by lottery luck.

I married early, while still a junior at Cal, became a father before I graduated, then set out to earn a living at whatever. I had no plans and nothing, really, to suggest a career, let alone a successful one. Unlike my father and his father before him, the jobs that I landed required a coat and tie rather than overalls and gloves. I did not sweat. Nor did I get dirty, save for whatever food and drink may have dribbled onto my clothing during lunchtime.

But I eventually became political. When? I would say that it happened the moment that I decided to form a union at Pemco Insurance.

That was a fool’s errand back in 1977-78. The CEO at the time, Stanley O. McNaughton, would often tell the employees that capitalism was the “greatest human invention.” He detested unions. But I naively soldiered on, gradually gaining reluctant adherents to “the cause.” In the end, after months of frustrating efforts to win the hearts and minds of a majority, we canceled the vote, realizing that we potential unionists would lose big time. Within the year I was gone. Yet now I was hooked. Damn.

The title of this post is “being apolitical.” Let’s say that I’m imagining what it might be like to fit the description.

Now I imagine that one can be apolitical (as in not paying all that much attention to current events and governments at any level) because one is otherwise engaged in something really interesting that consumes all one’s waking hours. Being a musical director of a major symphony orchestra comes to mind.

Then, I suppose, there are millions of us who just don’t give a darn about who wins elections or what the legislature or city council is up to. You may believe that you can go about your everyday activities seemingly untouched by political acts. Your pursuits may be trivial or profound leaving scarce minutes to do anything else, including casting your ballot.

Many of us are likely cynical of politics in general. This article in the New York Times triggered my post. It’s about buying elections. Shocked? Well, we shouldn’t be. Yet, the amount of money and the degree of sophistication involved in skirting campaign finance laws, however toothless, could make cynics of us all.

Exploiting a loophole in the state law and a network of political action committees in Alabama and Washington, Mr. [Mike] Hubbard [head of the Alabama Republican party] shuffled hundreds of thousands of out-of-state dollars into the Republican organization in Alabama, vastly outraising the state Democratic Party. On Election Day, Republicans won majorities in both the State Senate and House of Representatives for the first time since Reconstruction — and Alabama joined the rapidly growing fraternity of states where government is controlled by a single political party, now the largest it has been in more than half a century.

Alabama’s transformation was the product, in part, of a sophisticated political apparatus designed to channel political money from around the country into states where conditions were ripe for Republican takeover. In 2010, the effort achieved striking success, moving a dozen states to sole Republican control, including presidential swing states like Ohio and Pennsylvania.

Oh, and you contributed $25 to your favorite candidate expecting to make a difference? Good luck with that.

So, why not simply turn off the political faucet, the one that gushes out at me every day? I could play computer games or watch reality TV or read the classics, start a hobby—do anything but let the cesspool of politics disturb my life, liberty, and pursuit of happiness.

Well, there are a couple of nagging considerations that prevent me from doing so. First, I am, by definition, a politician. I hold elective office, and it’s a responsibility I take seriously. Besides, in my tenure I believe that I have made a positive difference for the community.

Second, politics is really inescapable. We may choose to ignore what government officials do, but their impacts can be huge, from how much we pay in taxes and to what ends, to whether or not we kill thousands of civilians in foreign lands, to the degree of suffering visited on citizens existing at the margins.

Politics, then, is ultimately a moral endeavor. Those we elect (with or without our vote) can do good or evil. That’s why it matters.

Smith writes about Washington

January 11, 2014 by davald

Writing for the Los Angeles Times Hedrick Smith puts the Boeing-vs.-labor situation in a broader context. Along the way he debunks the argument proffered by corporate executives that smashing wages and benefits are deemed necessary due to competitive pressures. As Smith writes:

Boeing said it had to cut costs to meet “aggressive international competition.” But Boeing’s sole major global competitor is Airbus, based in highly unionized Germany and France, and Airbus puts a premium on maintaining its high-quality workforce and treating its workers generously.

German companies over the last 25 years have raised the pay and benefits of middle-class workers five times faster than U.S. companies. And German corporations have kept high-tech jobs at home, so that today 21% of Germany’s workforce is in manufacturing versus 9% in the U.S. What’s more, the German strategy has rolled up $2 trillion in export surpluses while the U.S. has suffered $6 trillion in trade deficits.

Boeing knows that it can throw bullshit all over the place and our politicians (shame on Inslee) will eat it then reward the company with the biggest state subsidy in history. And it’s not that Boeing can’t afford to also treat its workers generously. Smith:

Over the last decade, Boeing rolled up more than $35 billion in profits and paid no federal corporate taxes. In fact, Boeing reaped about $2 billion in federal tax rebates from 2003 to 2012, as well as the most generous long-term state tax subsidy in U.S. history from Washington state.
