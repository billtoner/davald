WFT WSJ

April 8, 2013 by davald

Among things that I would never buy is a subscription to the Wall Street Journal. I’m even less inclined to do so now that Rupert Murdoch owns it. It used to be said, by even Noam Chomsky, that one could rely on its reporting, though one should toss the editorial pages into the trashcan.

Dean Baker gives us another example of why we shouldn’t pay any attention to the self-important publication. In a blog this morning, Baker tells us in derisive tones:

Folks who follow the economy might have thought that cutbacks in government spending, the continued weakness of construction, or the large trade deficits were the causes of the slow recovery, but the WSJ has the real scoop: it’s workers going on disability.

Baker goes on to explain how the Journal gets it wrong, spectacularly so. First off, there are far more people actively looking for work than their are job openings. If, as Baker suggests, the Journal assumed responsibility for those on Social Security disability rolls and tossed the offenders to the street, “How exactly does this create more jobs? Are the companies that were already seeing many applicants for each job openings going to start offering more jobs when this ratio increases further?”

How, indeed.

Margaret and Ronnie sitting in a tree

April 8, 2013 by davald

There has been much written about Margaret Thatcher upon her death. The New York Times‘ piece is nearly hagiographic. Digby gives a nod to Glenn Greenwald’s take; she was hardly a saint and played the villain genuinely well. I, for one, liked Matthew Yglesias’s lessons learned. He writes:

And yet while the policies of the Thatcher and Reagan revolutions have been largely successful, the political legacy seems to me to be quite mixed. Listening to contemporary conservatives, you often get the sense that they want to just rerun the policy agenda of the late 1970s and early 1980s. But today the leading global problem is climate change, not Communism. That’s not because Communism isn’t bad, it’s because the Cold War is over. Privatizing state-run industrial companies won’t jump-start growth. That’s not because state-ownership of industrial firms is a good idea, it’s because we don’t have any. Reducing the power of labor unions to curb cost-push inflation doesn’t fix anything because labor unions aren’t powerful anymore. When you triumph, you triumph. But history doesn’t end, new issues come to the front of the stack. We have a crisis of inadequate demand. We have the problem of providing health care to an aging population. Trying to apply the “lessons of Munich” to Vietnam was a disaster. Trying to apply the economic policy solutions of a generation ago to the problems of today is equally inappropriate.

The modern Republican Party invokes the legacies of Reagan and Thatcher but would find no room for either. Come to think of it, the present-day versions of both occupy comfortable positions in the Democratic Party. I have in mind the likes of Max Baucus in the Senate and members of Olympia’s Road Kill Caucus.

No other lawmaker on Capitol Hill has such a sizable constellation of former aides working as tax lobbyists, representing blue-chip clients that include telecommunications businesses, oil companies, retailers and financial firms, according to an analysis by LegiStorm, an online database that tracks Congressional staff members and lobbying. At least 28 aides who have worked for Mr. Baucus, Democrat of Montana, since he became the committee chairman in 2001 have lobbied on tax issues during the Obama administration — more than any other current member of Congress, according to the analysis of lobbying filings performed for The New York Times.

To Montana voters, please send this guy back to his ranch. He’s as good a reason as any for why we loathe Congress.

Obama miffs [update] [update 2]

April 5, 2013 by davald

How to screw your constituency while pretending to be its benefactor? Oh, that would be Barack Obama. He’s keen on throwing us old farts under the bus to curry favor with bastard Republicans.

That’s the latest report on the president’s new budget proposal. He will cut “entitlements,” mostly Medicare and Social Security, in hopes, I guess, that the GOP will approve tax hikes on the wealthy. More deja vu.

The linked New York Times piece suggests that Obama is merely playing a political card. He will offer elements of the Grand Bargain he had negotiated with John Boehner, before the Speaker beat a hasty retreat following an encounter with hostile party members—as in all of them.

Congressional Republicans have dug in against any new tax revenues after higher taxes for the affluent were approved at the start of the year. [A rare exception, to be sure.] The administration’s hope is to create cracks in Republicans’ antitax resistance, especially in the Senate, as constituents complain about the across-the-board cuts in military and domestic programs that took effect March 1.

Mr. Obama’s proposed deficit reduction would replace those cuts. And if Republicans continue to resist the president, the White House believes that most Americans will blame them for the fiscal paralysis.

Obama has tried this ploy before. We can be thankful for Republican intransigence that entitlements survived intact. And if the party stands for anything it’s opposition to taxes of any kind and in any form.

But suppose this time the GOP calls Obama’s bluff? Responding to public polling, which consistently shows that Americans support increased taxes on the wealthy, party leadership could slightly modify its position, accepting modest tax increases in exchange for further impoverishing  the elderly, most of whom still rely heavily on Social Security benefits to get by. Whom would the people blame? It’s Obama’s proposal, after all.

UPDATE (April 5, 2013):

Krugman weighs in here. He writes:

Sigh. So Obama is going with the “chained CPI” thing in his latest proposal — changing the price index used for Social Security cost adjustments. This is, purely and simply, a benefit cut.

Does it make sense in policy terms? No. First of all, there is no reason to believe that the chained index is a better measure of inflation facing seniorsthan the standard CPI. It’s true that the standard measure arguably understates inflation for the typical household — but seniors have a different consumption basket from the young, one that includes more medical expenses, and probably face true inflation that’s higher, not lower, than the official measure.

SECOND UPDATE (April 5, 2013):

Economist Dean Baker compares Obama’s proposed hits on seniors and the wealthy. It’s not even close.

Once poor, (almost) always poor

April 3, 2013 by davald

We don’t like to read that message. But the research is strongly suggesting that in America, especially, upward mobility is a fiction, now more than ever.

Yet we learn that it’s nearly impossible to break the trend by investing more in education, the way we do it in the U.S. Here, we put far more of our education dollars in college than at other levels. But if we do so with the expectation that more children will graduate with degrees then go on to earn decent livings, we would be mistaken. Indeed, we’re mostly wasting money.

These depressing thoughts come by way of an article in the New York Times. All this Race to the Top and No Child Left Behind hoopla, however well-intentioned, distracts us from focusing on the underlying problem: poor kids perform much worse in school than their richer peers, and the gap either stays constant or, more likely, grows over time.

So, where should we be spending our dollars? The research cited tells us to invest in early childhood programs. We’d get a far bigger bang for the buck.

American students from prosperous backgrounds scored on average 110 points higher on reading tests than disadvantaged students, about the same disparity that exists between the average scores in the United States and Tunisia. It is perhaps the main reason income inequality in the United States is passed down the generations at a much higher rate than in most advanced nations.

That’s a scandal, considering how much the government spends on education: about 5.5 percent of the nation’s economic output in total, from preschool through college.

And it suggests that the angry, worried debate over how to improve the nation’s mediocre education — pitting the teachers’ unions and the advocates of more money for public schools against the champions of school vouchers and standardized tests — is missing the most important part: infants and toddlers.

Research by Mr. [James] Heckman and others confirms that investment in the early education of disadvantaged children pays extremely high returns down the road. It improves not only their cognitive abilities but also crucial behavioral traits like sociability, motivation and self-esteem.

We can, and should, do better.

Disparate clout

April 3, 2013 by davald

A couple of items in this morning’s newspapers betray America’s misplaced priorities and, I would suggest, convey the increasingly unmistakable message that we’ve succumbed to the loonies. The first is the NRA’s latest whacky initiative, The National School Shield, wherein the gun lobby reveals its answer to proliferating gun violence: more guns, of course. The second is a local matter, with several teachers targeting the home of state senator Steve Hobbs, a nominal Democrat who seemingly relishes the prospect of pissing off educators.

In what kind of universe would a gun lobby wield far more political clout and come closer to realizing its political agenda than associations of teachers? Well, that would be the United States.

Despite the senseless slaughters committed by deranged individuals with legally acquired arsenals of deadly assault weapons, the chances of Congress passing any restrictions on guns diminishes with each NRA lobbying push. Forget about the dead children of Newtown. It’s all about the damn Second Amendment. Oh, and I should mention the money. The NRA long ago ceased being a club of hunters. It’s the frontman for the weapons manufacturers, whose profits soar after each rampaging episode.

So, the gun nuts will get their way, sure enough. What about education and teachers? As I said, this is America.

In America, the politicians believe that teachers are second-class citizens who consistently fail in the classroom. The solution is two-fold: raise the bar and hold educators “accountable.” The connecting glue is standardized testing. Subject school children to yearly performance measurements, then base teacher evaluations on the results.

One need not be a genius to appreciate the consequences of this strategy. Teachers and their principals may be pressured to cheat, and that’s what has happened in several school districts, the ones we know about, with Atlanta being the latest. (We may recall G.W. Bush’s education secretary, Rod Paige, who gained fame for the near-miraculous test-score improvements in Houston. Not.)

In the main, however, educators will focus on the test, jettisoning most of what makes teaching rewarding, from science and literature to arts and music and the interactions between students and teachers that make learning special. For children in Washington state, the Measurement of Student Progress, or MSP, tests for reading and math, mostly. In the weeks leading up to the rigorous exams, teachers try to squeeze as much detail into their students’ heads as possible, hoping, I guess, that some of it will stick by test time.

Imagine that you’re a fourth-grade teacher. Next year your evaluation and, presumably, your pay will be based in part on how well your kids did on the standardized test. The results, we’ll imagine further, aren’t pretty. Few passed. So you get a lousy report card. In your defense you say that you can’t rely on a single year’s worth of scores. Besides, your students’ gaps were too large. Shouldn’t the third and second and first grade teachers bear some of the blame?

Sorry. It doesn’t work that way. Whether or not you are judged mediocre of superb depends only on last year’s results. No excuses.

Teachers and principals know that this system sucks. Yet, they have no clout when it comes to benighted politicians, who always know better than educators, even though they wouldn’t survive ten minutes in a public school classroom. The legislatures continue to pile on the requirements while denying basic funding to the schools. The inevitable decline in public education falls exclusively on the educators; the elected officials, with fingers pointing, escape to do the bidding of the gun lobby and otherwise fail completely at their jobs.

Fossil fuel addiction

April 1, 2013 by davald

The U.S. imports more oil from Canada than from any other country. Increasingly, that oil is extracted from Alberta shale deposits, and the process is both energy intensive and environmentally destructive. But Canada is hooked on oil and wants to export as much as possible. Existing transnational pipelines are near capacity; thus, the new Keystone XL, which would transport the shale oil to southern U.S. ports. Whether or not the new pipeline is built confronts President Obama. As you can imagine, the politics are as thick as the petroleum goo.

Are most of our northern neighbors in love with their oil and the revenues they bring? Writing for the New York Times, Canadian professor Thomas Homer-Dixon cites a recent poll showing 42 percent of his fellow citizens opposed to the pipeline. However, conservatives rule in Canada, and conservatives not only love the petrodollars, they deny global warming.

The Conservatives have slashed financing for climate science, closed facilities that do research on climate change, told federal government climate scientists not to speak publicly about their work without approval and tried, unsuccessfully, to portray the tar sands industry as environmentally benign.

What will Obama do? My guess is that he will approve the pipeline, notwithstanding his rhetoric on climate change. Sound familiar? Homer-Dixon, however, says that nixing the project “would be a major step toward stopping large-scale environmental destruction, the distortion of Canada’s economy and the erosion of its democracy.”

Stay tuned.
