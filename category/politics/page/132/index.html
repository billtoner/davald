The whole planet is my backyard

June 13, 2011 by davald

When toxic chemicals—or too much of a good thing, like carbon dioxide—enter the atmosphere, geographical boundaries cease to have meaning. Thus, a coal plant in China has almost the same effect on us in Puget Sound as Centralia.

Ironically, while Olympia passes a bill to eventually close down our state’s sole coal plant for environmental reasons, Washington may soon become a transportation hub for shipments of Wyoming coal to China. We’re told that it’s all about jobs.

But would we consent to creating jobs for the purpose of burning babies? I think not. That’s going too far, we’d say. Yet, coal-burning emissions surely cause human deaths, although we’re unable to write the words on toe-tags, “Death by coal.”

At some point, if it’s not already too late, more of us will have to accede to the mantra: The whole planet is my backyard. For increasingly it makes little difference where the bad stuff happens; whatever is emitted has equal-opportunity consequences.

The Whatcom County Council will render its decision soon as to whether it should allow Bellingham to host a terminal for China-bound coal. That city’s mayor is against it. But the complexion of the council, a majority committed to jobs, bodes ill for the planet, in general, and the citizens of that fair city, in particular.

For a thoughtful and well-written viewpoint on the topic, I commend this piece appearing in the Seattle Times.

As proposed, the Burlington Northern Santa Fe railroad will be transporting 48 million metric tons of the black rock every year — all the way from Wyoming, through Seattle, Edmonds, Mukilteo, Everett, Marysville, Stanwood and Burlington, then straight through our city center to a harbor just north of here called Cherry Point. From there, it is bound for China.

If you lined up all those coal cars for a year from end to end they’d stretch from Seattle to Beijing!

Oh, and someone will surely die. Would you trade babies for jobs?

If they held a revolution, would anyone show up?

June 12, 2011 by davald

I enjoy reading the blog “Gin and Tacos,” although I do so only sporadically and haven’t yet delved into the author’s background. But he writes well and poignantly on a variety of topics, including baseball, of which I can’t get enough. Here’s one of his latest posts, entitled “Nope.”

He considers the question:  “What are the chances the U.S. economy could eventually trigger violence in our country?”

His conclusion:

1. Americans are too lazy, uninterested, disheartened, docile, or busy supplicating themselves before their economic betters to put together a half-decent protest rally.

1a. The closest we’ve come to a half-decent sized rally involved elderly, ignorant, porcine reactionaries waving around misspelled signs and screaming for more upper class tax cuts. I don’t think we can even rebel correctly.

2. There’s no common purpose. Sure, everyone’s angry. Maybe even violently angry. At what? Half of us are ready to start a class war and the other half are polishing up their guns to defend upper class tax cuts, shoot at Mexicans / Muslims / black people, and basically form a circular firing squad rather than focusing on the problem. We’d fight about what we are fighting about. I can’t think of anything more pointless.

3. As individuals get closer to their own breaking point, resorting to crime or lone acts of violence seems more probable than any kind of collective action.

Noam Chomsky has suggested that in a putative democracy such as ours, with its pretense to a people’s government but awash in politics of the worst kind, riddled with blatant, legal corruption, the state can actually keep the rabble in line more easily than under authoritarian regimes. In his books Necessary Illusions and Manufacturing Consent Chomsky lays out a propaganda model as a testable hypothesis. The model yields a couple of the explanations provided above as to why the people don’t engage in collective action for their own benefit, when we clearly outnumber the monied interests. In particular, the people are atomized, making it difficult to organize into collective dissent. Instead of active, well-informed citizens we have been “manufactured” into placid, ignorant consumers who spend much of our free time sitting before their individual televisions (guilty). 

Those few who read my words can attest to a certain level of cynicism and, alas, futility. While I can easily point out the hypocrisy and dysfunction of our current state of affairs, especially regarding politics and economics, and at times even dare to offer possible solutions, I realize that wholesale change requires concerted action by a significant number of us; but, like “gin and tacos,” I don’t see how this can happen here, for the reasons listed.

Despite the hagiography of the protesting colonists who actually succeeded in revolting against the then-dominant imperial power, Americans as a rule abhor Chomskyan dissent. What has become fashionable is railing against “big government,” “illegal aliens,” and “socialist Obama.” However, should you have the audacity to gainsay those who truly wield the power, fie on you. A true patriot, it would appear, is really nothing more than a white nativist who is scared shitless that “his country” is gradually succumbing to the colored hordes. Geesh, there’s even one of them in the Oval Office.

See how they run

June 11, 2011 by davald

The Herald gave us a list this morning of people who have filed for various elected positions throughout the county. Few seats are unchallenged; there are lots of folks who aspire to hold office, even one or two who have run and lost, again and again.

Why do people choose to launch campaigns, some more costly than others, to sit behind a dais and vote on x, y, and z? How will they make a difference and on whose behalf?

I have stood for political office three times: once as a failed candidate for the Marysville city council; the last two as PUD commissioner, both successful. In the Marysville race I tried to offer a distinct alternative to the incumbent, who was also a former county council member. I did not care for how city government, and its citizens, were being exploited for the benefit of the real estate industry, which has long had its way in that city.

As for the PUD, I said in 2002 that I was a “reluctant” candidate. I was drafted by a small group of people familiar with the utility and me. That first time there were five of us vying for one seat, including the incumbent, who failed to survive the primary. Things were bad at the utility following the West Coast energy debacle, engineered in large part by the spectacularly corrupt Enron. So, 2002 was an opportune occasion to run for the PUD board.

Last time, in 2008, the utility—I do not hesitate to proclaim—was in very good shape, under the able leadership of a new general manager. Yet that didn’t stop someone from filing against me, in the last hours of the final day. As it turned out, the challenger, when pressed as to why he was running, allowed that he didn’t want my name to appear alone on the ballot. Huh? He had no sense of urgency, and why should he? There was really nothing wrong with the PUD that warranted a major adjustment—as had been the case in 2002. During the campaign I mostly scratched my head, wondering why an individual, who had shown absolutely no interest in the utility before his filing, would put thousands of his own dollars into a feeble attempt to unseat yours truly. Of course, he had every right to do so; my position on the board was not inherited. But his challenge seemed to be a waste of time, money, and effort. I spent $175 and won re-election handily—on the strength of a revitalized organization.

You’ll notice that one of my colleagues on the PUD board has filed for county council. I have no idea why, but, again, she has every right to do so. Perhaps she wants to fry bigger fish. And, should she win, her wages from holding office will more than triple.

Jackie Minchew is running again for Everett city council. His heart is certainly in the right place, and we agree on many issues, especially with respect to the environment and energy policy. But Jackie risks becoming the Harold Stassen of local politics. Maybe the fourth time will be the charm, if I’ve counted correctly. As an Everett resident, I’ll surely vote for him—again.

Before I leave, a few words about the county executive race. I’m sure that Mike Hope, who is challenging incumbent Aaron Reardon, suggested to voters when he ran for his Olympia seat that he belonged in state government and would better serve the citizens of his district than the incumbent, Liz Loomis, whom he defeated in an extremely close race. Now it appears that he will make similar claims about the county, about how he belongs in the executive’s seat, and that the people from the Sound to the Cascades will benefit from his running the government. Don’t hold your breath. The acrimony has already started, judging by the accompanying Herald article:

“I think the executive’s race is going to be particularly interesting,” said Bill Phillips, chairman of the Snohomish County Democratic Party. “It’s going to be Mike Hope’s rhetoric versus Aaron Reardon’s record of accomplishment.”

His GOP counterpart couldn’t disagree more.

“There’s been more people than normal standing up here because, frankly, there’s a lot of people who are tired of where the country is at and want to step up and do something about it,” said Bill Cooper, the Republican Party’s Snohomish County chairman.

I have written elsewhere on what I perceive to be the county’s dysfunctional governance model. There are five independently elected council members and an independently elected executive. How is that working? Despite Democrats in the majority on the council and Reardon’s being a nominal Democrat, the council and the executive have publicly clashed on more than one occasion. Mike Hope is a Republican. Should the council remain Democratic, and Hope should prevail (what a campaign slogan, by the way), what can we expect to see beginning in January? Smooth or rough waters? I’m guessing the latter.

At any rate, we have dozens of people with aspirations, which is not a bad thing. I remain too cynical to expect the kind of results we deserve from our public offices, with one notable exception, of course.

Contrasting cycles

June 8, 2011 by davald

When it comes to combatting climate change, our political system is ill-equipped; it has much to do with time-cycles.

Yesterday the PUD board and staff were treated to a presentation by Mike Davis, who holds the mouthful-title of “Associate Laboratory Director of the Energy and Environment Directorate of Pacific Northwest National Laboratories.” (Since his slide presentation is now a public record, I invite you to take a look.) I want to focus on one of his slides, in particular.

Notice how the graphic is divided, with “special interests” on the left and “special outcomes” on the right. On the left we find “news media,” “earnings reports,” and “political elections.” Each of these has time-cycles, very short with respect to the news media, quarterly with respect to company balance sheets, and between two and six years with respect to elections.

On the right Davis places “science and technology,” “energy infrastructure,” and “climate.” To develop innovative devices and protocols can take up to ten years, or so. Establishing an energy infrastructure requires even more time. But the “climate” can best be appreciated in century-long time scales. We know, for example, that carbon dioxide molecules last at least a hundred in the atmosphere.

We can understand that the solutions (“special outcomes”) on the right demand sustainable and durable policies extending far beyond the timeframes of the news, earning reports, and elections. Yet, the rules and incentives that exist on the left side of the chart prevent those long range solutions.

Davis, who has spent much time in China, spoke of recent policy prescriptions coming out of Beijing. The government leaders, all technically sharp, Davis adds, produce five-year plans consisting of just a half-dozen pages. The desired outcomes and the persons accountable for achieving them are clearly defined. But the plans don’t spell out how the outcomes are to be achieved, with the exception of a few boundary conditions; that’s up to those responsible for solving the identified problems.

Davis contrasts this simple procedure with Congress’s. We have become inured to the documents (laws) running thousands of pages. Each stack of paper incorporates the desires of a myriad of special interests, who spend an inordinate sum of money lobbying and electing their favorite senators and representatives. But after a bill survives the news-cum-special interests-cum-election cycles and is eventually signed by the president, there is no pretext to durability. There is nothing to bind the policy over successive elections.

Yet that durability is precisely what’s required to meet the challenges of climate change. Ironically, the Chinese may be better suited to accomplishing the “special outcomes” than Americans.

What a tradeoff: democracy and more greenhouse gas emissions or hybrid socialism/capitalism and climate solutions.

That said, China has huge environmental challenges, including rising emissions, that may overwhelm even its streamlined governance model.

But given our contrasting cycles, I wouldn’t bet on the US getting serious about climate change any time soon. Sorry, children.

Cut taxes, cut spending. So, there, public.

June 6, 2011 by davald

Grover Norquist still wants a government small enough to drain from a bathtub. So he’s got a majority of congressional Republicans signed on to his no-taxes pledge. And for Norquist, even letting tax cuts or corporate subsidies expire is tantamount to raising taxes, which is verboten.

I’m always baffled by the focus on taxes, as if the people of this land were overly burdened. We’re not. Indeed, we have the lowest tax burden in the developed world. (Chart based on OECD data.)

Let there be no mistake, Norquist really, really wants to cut federal spending to the bone. Yet, we spend less than our European counterparts, for sure.

Norquist’s straitjacket has nothing to do with sound fiscal policy. It has everything to do with ideology, a dangerous one at that.

You see, Norquist and his band of merry GOP pranksters don’t give a rat’s rectum about what we call ‘the public.’ In their worldview, there is no place for public schools, or public transportation, or public television, or public health care. Any good or service of merit belongs in the private sector. You want to properly educate your child, send her to a private institution. You want to drive your car from home to work, pay a toll to the highway’s owner. You want to watch a decent program on the tube, endure the advertising or pay a premium. You want doctors and hospitals and pills, pay for them out of your pocket.

Norquist’s world would be nasty, brutish, and short for all but the extremely rich, the Haves and Havemores that formed G.W.’s base. I fear that “the public” may commit collective suicide next November by putting too many anti-public politicians in office, including the White House.

It’s not going away

June 2, 2011 by davald

…[T]he working man demands…the indispensable minimum without which human life cannot be lived at all. Enough to eat, freedom from the haunting terror of unemployment, the knowledge that your children will get a fair chance.

— George Orwell, “Looking Back on the Spanish War”

The New York Times tells us that no president since FDR, save Ronald Reagan, has been re-elected with an unemployment rate above 7.2 percent. Reagan won, apparently, because the unemployment rate was falling. Barack Obama, then, must defy the odds if he is to retain the Oval Office.

However, the economic news is grim and may likely get worse.

Roughly 9 percent of Americans who want to go to work cannot find an employer. Companies are firing fewer people, but hiring remains anemic. And the vast majority of economic forecasters, including the president’s own advisers, predict only modest progress by November 2012.

The Times article provides a nice overview of the policy differences between the two parties. The Republicans abhor government spending, so they will block any attempts at further stimulus. The Democrats, it seems, won’t even try.

Could the Federal Reserve do more?

The Federal Reserve, the nation’s central bank, has the means and the mandate to reduce unemployment by pumping money into the economy.

As financial markets nearly collapsed in 2008, the Fed unleashed a series of unprecedented programs, first to arrest the crisis and then to promote recovery, investing more than $2 trillion. The final installment, a $600 billion bond-buying program, ends in June.

Now, however, the leaders of the central bank say they are reluctant to do more. The Fed’s chairman, Ben S. Bernanke, said in April that more money might not increase growth, but there was a growing risk that it would accelerate inflation.

The rest of us sit, with far too much patience, in the Theater of the Absurd, watching the tomfoolery. Shouldn’t we be concerned about the “haunting terror of unemployment,” if not for ourselves than for our neighbors?

…Christina Romer, who headed the president’s Council of Economic Advisers until fall 2010, said in a recent speech at Washington University in St. Louis that no part of the government was addressing unemployment with sufficient urgency or hope.

“Urgency, because unemployment is a tragedy that should not be tolerated a minute longer,” she said. “And hope, because prudent and possible policies could make a crucial difference.”

Studies have shown that the longer we’re unemployed the more difficult it is to get re-hired. Our skill-sets may wane. Employers may understandably infer that if no one else wants this person than why should I offer him a job?

Meanwhile, as unemployment benefits dry up, what does the poor schmuck do? The public services designed to help those who can’t help themselves are being slashed at the state level.

Olympia cut funding for vocational training, which targets the unemployed, teaching them new skills that employers presumably desire.

“The employment needs are there; the jobs are going to be there,” said Steve Hanson, president of Renton Technical College. “That’s what’s so frustrating.”

So, what do you think will happen should the GOP retake the White House and control both houses of Congress? If we believe their rhetoric, they will:

  • cut taxes on the rich and corporations
  • install the Ryan Medicare plan
  • kill “Obamacare”
  • impose a debt ceiling
  • reduce all federal spending, save for the Pentagon

Will such a strategy put people back to work? Herbert Hoover believed as much.

The New York Times does Krugman

May 31, 2011 by davald

Apropos of yesterday’s post on unemployment, today the New York Times editors remind us that we’re still in economic doldrums. Worse, the data suggest more of the same for an indefinite duration.

A month ago, when an initial gauge of first-quarter economic growth came in surprisingly weak, many policy makers and economists expected the bad news to prove fleeting. But when revised data were released last week, the growth estimate remained stuck at an annual rate of 1.8 percent, compared with 3.1 percent at the end of last year.

More troubling in the latest figures, consumer spending — the largest component of the economy — was especially slow. Stagnant wages and higher prices for gas and food are squeezing family budgets, while falling home equity hurts consumer confidence. That suggests more bad news to come.

The editors, echoing Keynes and Krugman, tell us that the government must do more to stanch the hemorrhaging. For example, and as Krugman suggested, the government could do more on the housing front: “The administration could work to ease the rules for refinancing mortgages owned by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the government-run mortgage giants. Easier refinancings would lower monthly payments for potentially hundreds of thousands of borrowers in good standing, and in that way, free up spending money to boost the economy.”

But I fear that the problems are more systemic, having much to do with the growing gap between the privileged few and the rest of us. Labor, now judged a haplessly quaint term, has been losing substantial ground to Capital over the last few decades; and it was never in parity, to be sure.

The signs are all around us, and chronicled in these pages. Let me list a few:

  • reduced marginal tax rates
  • declining union participation rates
  • stagnating real wages
  • rising “normal” unemployment levels
  • increasing concentration of wealth in fewer hands
  • eroding safety nets (e.g., pension funds, niggardly unemployment benefits)
  • ineffectual financial regulations
  • continuing subsidies to big oil and big Pharma

Alas, it has also become increasingly difficult merely to talk about potential solutions, because the political arena is rife with ideological intransigence marinated in rhetorical excrement. That is, our political “leaders” are blathering nonsensical shit. As the Times put it:

Republican lawmakers have responded to renewed signs of weakness with a jobs plan that prescribes more of the same “fixes” that Republicans always recommend no matter the problem: mainly high-end tax cuts, deregulation, more domestic oil drilling and federal spending cuts.

Such irresponsibility in the face of dismal news reveals pathological adolescence. It’s long since time to have some adults take over. However, the current crop of GOP presidential hopefuls offers no relief from “more of the same.”

The reserve army

May 24, 2011 by davald

Against the austerity crowd, who continue to warn of inflation “just around the corner,” data disappoint. Inflation remains low, too low, in fact, for the likes of Federal Reserve Board chair Ben Bernanke. Investors keep buying treasury bonds, despite low yields. This is important, since rising inflation and bond yields are the oft-cited arguments for tightening the nation’s fiscal belt.

So why haven’t “soaring” deficits and “burdensome” debt led to inflation and a decline in investor confidence? I think we need look no further than the awful employment numbers.

Corporate America has what it’s always wanted: a reserve army of workers. That millions of people lack jobs keeps a lid on wages. Moreover, those who are working realize that, for the grace of God, they could be receiving pink slips tomorrow. So, they’re willing to work harder and put in more hours to avoid being laid off.

Labor productivity, as it happens, is steadily increasing. But instead of sharing the fruits of doing more with less, corporate heads are skimming off the higher profits for themselves, leaving their employees’ wages stagnant or diminished. They know that they can get away with this, refuse to share the wealth, because of rampant job insecurity in the labor force.

In turn, that insecurity results from extremely low and ever-declining union density rates. Consider this chart from OECD.

Look how far down the list America is, with only 11.9 percent of the workforce belonging to unions. See which countries are at the top.

Now we should look at comparative unemployment rates and, most especially, comparative inequality (the Gini coefficient).

We see that the US has the lowest union density rate, the highest unemployment rate, and the highest metric of inequality of these selected countries. I submit that there’s a causal relationship. The less political clout enjoyed by labor relative to capital’s, the greater the unemployment rate, the greater the inequality, and the more depressed workers’ wages. (Note: The per capita GDP of Americans is higher than most of the above countries’. However, our greater inequality means that income and wealth are more heavily concentrated in fewer hands. We know what happens to average income in a bar when Bill Gates enters.)

Privacy concerns: US government vs Apple

May 17, 2011 by davald

Former comedian and now senator Al Franken is taking Apple and other technology companies to task for tracking the locations of their customers. Apple explains that it doesn’t use the information; it’s merely complying with US law, which requires Apple et al. to record the GPS data for the police state.

Meanwhile, I have to wonder why Franken doesn’t yet seem to be dismayed by the US government’s domestic surveillance, a clear violation of federal statutes. Glenn Greenwald quotes from the New Yorker‘s Jane Mayer:

Jack Balkin, a liberal law professor at Yale, agrees that the increase in leak prosecutions is part of a larger transformation. “We are witnessing the bipartisan normalization and legitimization of a national-surveillance state,” he says. In his view, zealous leak prosecutions are consonant with other political shifts since 9/11: the emergence of a vast new security bureaucracy, in which at least two and a half million people hold confidential, secret, or top-secret clearances; huge expenditures on electronic monitoring, along with a reinterpretation of the law in order to sanction it; and corporate partnerships with the government that have transformed the counterterrorism industry into a powerful lobbying force. Obama, Balkin says, has “systematically adopted policies consistent with the second term of the Bush Administration.”

That New Yorker piece, by the way, is a must read. It certainly reveals Obama to be a major hypocrite. What he said before he became president and what he now sanctions from the Oval Office do not comport.

Global warming vs. climate change

May 11, 2011 by davald

I suppose that I should not be baffled by this: that a greater percentage of Republicans discount anthropogenic global warming than Democrats. Why should political ideology affect judgments on science? I leave that for the social scientists.

One aspect of the controversy, which most climate scientists believe shouldn’t exist, is the difference between the terms ‘global warming’ and ‘climate change.’ On occasion, some writers and speakers interchange, if not also conflate, the expressions, which, I think, confuses a key point. So I, the humble layperson, will try to correct the misunderstanding—in necessarily simple language.

Planet earth, as a whole, is warming. Why? Because of the accumulation of greenhouse gasses in the atmosphere. Without those gasses we’d have frozen to death a long time ago. With too many GHG, the planet warms. According to scientists, overall global temperatures will increase to a point by the end of this century as to make large areas, perhaps entire continents, uninhabitable.

Global warming changes climates; but not in a uniform manner. Those living in southwestern United States will experience prolonged drought conditions, as will Australia and much of Africa. The Pacific Northwest, on the other hand, will likely get wetter, with the Puget Sound region beginning to look like Monterey, Calif.—foggy and damp mornings and evenings.

The contrast between warming and cooling climates will yield volatile weather patterns: more and stronger hurricanes and tornadoes; flooding in some areas; extended dry periods adversely impacting agriculture.

It’s possible, then, if not highly likely, that the average temperature of the planet, as measured by thousands of devices distributed across the globe, will rise while particular areas cool. Thus, it would be wrong for those getting chillier to say that global warming is a hoax. At the same time, it’s possible that no single weather event can be irrefutably explained by global warming.

The problem for all of us, especially succeeding generations, is that while we metaphorically fiddle, the planet burns. We will continue to treat each weather catastrophe as a local phenomenon, unrelated to global warming, while far too many of us persist in denying the science altogether. Before a critical mass of appropriate people realize that earth is in deep do-do, it will be far too late to reverse the trend.

Such is our legacy: willful ignorance and wanton greed got in the way of doing the right thing. Sorry, children.
