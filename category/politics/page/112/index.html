Hitch arraigns

December 19, 2011 by davald

Christopher Hitchens, commissioned to cover our national party conventions for the London Review of Books, found much to be desired. He took little comfort in this, because he declared America his new home, officially so, and thought we deserved better. Here he uses his journalistic assignments to inveigh against our electoral system and its evident follies. There is no candidate left unscathed. As always, the writing is preciously provocative.

He regrets what has happened to presidential elections, particularly the respective parties’ anointment of their chosen ones long before the November balloting.

A Republican National Convention had been arranged, as a sort of sound-stage or a mixed-media event, entirely for the convenience of the press and TV. The delegates were mere extras on the set, the coronation of the two nominees was a sure thing, the feral extremists and fundamentalists had been tidied out of sight, the corporate-sponsorship logos were beautifully placed, the camera angles and background briefings were the chief preoccupation of the Party managers and – there was exactly nothing to cover, nothing to transmit, and nothing to write about.

While he would eventually champion the invasion of Iraq, he found nothing to like in George W. Bush, whom he excoriates for his gubernatorial  murders and the glee with which he approached this dreadful practice.

I say ‘twisted’ about the dislikably boyish Governor of Texas, because he has signed more than a hundred death warrants during his entirely undistinguished tenure, and because he was only criticised (and that faintly) for two of these capricious crowd-pleasing atrocities. In one case, a Canadian citizen was condemned to death without any notification to the Canadian Embassy, as is reciprocally mandated by both US and Canadian law. In another, a woman was executed despite good evidence that she had undergone a transforming redemption while on Death Row. Bush met the Canadian objections by saying that the man ought not to have committed a crime while in Texas (which would mean that as President he would not care about consular representation for Americans arraigned overseas). He countered the Christian conservative petition on behalf of Karla Faye Tucker by mimicking her last-minute entreaties (‘Oh, please don’t kill me’) to a reporter for Rupert Murdoch’s Weekly Standard. That does it for me, and I don’t even believe – as Bush is supposed to – in Biblical repentance.

It’s worth going through the list, providing Hitchens’s unique perspective.

John McCain – nobody’s idea of an intellectual but likes to talk dirty and got himself shot down while scattering high explosives over someone else’s country. (People keep describing him as a POW but I think that there must be some mistake – no war on Vietnam was ever declared.)

Donald Trump – a ludicrous figure, but at least he’s lived it up a bit in the real world and at least he’s worked out how to cover 90 per cent of his skull with 30 per cent of his hair.

Bill Bradley – boring and pompous and tenth-rate but used to play basketball and take showers with African Americans.

The weird one is [Al] Gore. When he isn’t robotically normal he is decidedly odd. Who would claim, as he did, to have been (with his wife) the model for Erich Segal’s treacly novel Love Story? Who would opt, as he did, for paid advice from a simpering Clintonoid feminist about how to become an ‘alpha male’? Who would make as little as he does of having had the ravaged and husky Tommy Lee Jones for a college room-mate? Who would get his wife to proffer an on-the-record interview, asserting that he was a secret Tamburlaine in the sack? He’s not a psychopath like his boss, and he inspires piercing pity for his writhing attempts to break free, but still.

Alas, we won’t see this kind of writing again.

Political incest

December 19, 2011 by davald

For Obama and Democratic leaders who are trying to set an election agenda focused on income inequality, wage stagnation, and downward mobility for the middle and lower class, the prominence of Democratic lobbyists has become problematic.

Writing for the New York Times, professor Thomas Edsall treats us to some more disturbing news. Former Democratic members of Congress, who may have championed the needs and aspirations of the Rest of Us, let greed do their walking—straight to K Street. They now peddle on behalf of corporate interests as they put the screws to the narrowing middle class.

First on the list of malevolent turnarounds is Dick Gephardt, who used to defend labor and unions. Edsall:

Upon leaving Congress in 2005, Gephardt joined the Washington offices of DLA Piper as senior counsel. Two years later, he founded his own lobbying firm, the Gephardt Government Affairs Group.

By 2010, annual firm billings had shot up from $625,000 in 2007 to $6.59 million. Gephardt’s client list was blue chip, Goldman Sachs (paid Gephardt $200,000 in 2010); Boeing Co. ($440,000); Visa Inc. ($200,000); Ameren Corp, the energy holding company ($200,000); and Waste Management Inc., the leading provider of trash and garbage removal ($320,000).

A normally voluble political strategist and committed liberal who has worked with Gephardt in past elections said about the former Congressman’s lobbying clientele, “I don’t want to talk on the record, it sucks. It’s a sad situation for us.”

Union leaders like Richard Trumka (president of A.F.L-C.I.O.) spoke to this issue in a recent speech, quoted in the article:

Our role is not to build the power of a political party or a candidate. It is to improve the lives of working families and strengthen our country. It doesn’t matter if candidates and parties are controlling the wrecking ball or simply standing aside — the outcome is the same either way.

Money corrupts, and never more so than in Washington, D.C. The Supreme Court, in a series of rulings, has catapulted the role of money into the stratosphere. Obama is expected to raise and spend a billion dollars in his campaign for reelection.

And speaking of campaigns, they are obviously far too long, which makes them even more expensive. Parliamentary democracies limit the blabbering season as they also limit campaign spending.

More insidious, it seems, is that the corrupting power of money has left the Rest of Us devoid of effective advocates. Why represent workers for a pittance when you can siphon off millions in defense of the strong and mighty?

I’ve never been to Washington, D.C., despite many opportunities to do so on the public’s dime. I suspect that were I to travel there, the stench would overwhelm from miles away, through the plane’s ventilation system.

The Brandeis ratio

December 19, 2011 by davald

Neither had I—heard of it. It’s featured in a New York Times op-ed this morning.

Quoting Supreme Court justice Louis Brandeis, the authors:

“We may have democracy, or we may have wealth concentrated in the hands of a few, but we cannot have both.”

The ratio, per the authors, is the average income of the One-percenters divided by the average middle-class income. Before the Great Divergence, roughly associated with Ronald Reagan’s terms, the ratio was 12.5. As of 2006, the last year of data, the ratio had skyrocketed to 36.

The authors, both university professors, Yale and Berkeley, propose a one-time law by Congress. (Already I’m squirming.) Each year the IRS would calculate the Brandeis ratio of the previous 12 months. If the number exceeded 36, the One-percenters would be charged a marginal tax on incomes in excess of the “poorest” in the fractile, that is, incomes above $330,000 a year.

There’s a Rawlsian* element in here, too. The law would allow for income increases of the middle class, and as long as the ratio remained at or below 36, the marginal tax would not kick in.

The authors:

Part of our goal is to change the way politicians speak about income equality. Framing the income of the wealthy in relation to the median income will help us all keep in mind the relative success of the middle class. Our grandparents would be shocked to learn that the average income of the 1-percent club has skyrocketed to more than 30 times the median income — just as we will be shocked if 20 years from now 1-percenters make 80 times the median, which is where we will be if inequality continues to grow at the current rate unabated.

 

As much as I might applaud this idea, I’m sure that you would agree with me that our current caustic politics precludes any meaningful considerations of our tax structure. The Republican-controlled House prefers that the Great Divergence become even greater. The GOP in both houses wants to lower taxes on the very wealthy, arguing—against reason and facts—that putting more money in the hands of the extremely privileged will create jobs.

In a previous post I drew attention to the conclusion of the International Monetary Fund, via New York Times columnist Charles Blow, that countries marked by significant income inequality were less able to grow their economies. If so, then further enriching the One-percenters will impede, not promote, economic growth.

* John Rawls, who wrote A Theory of Justice.

Brown wants green, and so should we all

December 18, 2011 by davald

California governor Jerry Brown blasted global warming deniers in a recent all-day conference in San Francisco, called “Extreme risks and California’s future.”

“We have an entire political party who has a doctrine of absolute denial,” said Brown, who was wearing a green tie. “All the denial in the world doesn’t change the facts that greenhouse gases are building up. The main thing we have to deal with in climate change is the skepticism, the denial and the cultlike behavior of the political lemmings that would take us over the cliff.”

Golden State has 840 miles of coastline. Increased warming of the planet will melt glaciers and expand ocean volumes, threatening shoreline developments.

Max Auffhammer, an environmental economist at UC Berkeley, said if there is a 4-foot rise in sea levels by the end of the century, and then a storm surge, both the San Francisco and Oakland airports would be under water. So would parts of Silicon Valley, he said.

Then-governor Arnold Schwarzenegger signed into law the nation’s most ambitious program to curb greenhouse gas emissions. Other states have adopted energy resource portfolio requirements, including Washington with Initiative 937, which governs electric utilities; however, it doesn’t touch transportation, Washington’s biggest source of carbon dioxide emissions.

Despite such efforts, last year global CO2 emissions jumped by the highest amount on record, after a decline in 2009 owing to the recession. Because modern economies run on fossil fuels, economic growth yields higher emissions.

The challenge, which receives scant attention from our political leaders, is to grow the economy without adding more carbon dioxide to the atmosphere. Since coal is the most carbon-intensive fuel, any serious strategy must effect the closure of coal-fired power plants.

Washington has but one coal plant, in Centralia. It will closed by 2025, as directed by Olympia. But many states have dozens of coal plants, which supply almost all the electricity consumed by their citizens. Nationwide, coal plants represent nearly half of all electricity generation.

Sourcewatch.org has an interactive map with an inventory of each state’s coal plants. I clicked on Ohio.

Unlike Washington, states like Ohio lack significant hydropower resources. If not coal, what?

Natural gas-fired plants emit about half the CO2 of coal plants. If every coal plant could be and were converted to natural gas, carbon dioxide emissions would fall significantly. The question then becomes natural gas supply, the extraction of which also emits greenhouse gases.

Here’s a table comparing CO2 emissions by type of generation (source).

The Snohomish County PUD, of which I’m a board member, derives almost all of its electricity from hydro facilities, in particular those along the Columbia and Snake rivers. We have no carbon in our supply portfolio, save for what we purchase from the market pool, which is a small fraction of the total.

But this has much to do with geography, aided and abetted by the massive New Deal projects like Grand Coulee dam. Washington’s utilities will only get greener over time, because of the initiative.

But that’s not the case in Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Kentucky, Alabama, Texas, and the list goes on. If their coal plants were shut down, economies would collapse and the people would wilt in the summers and freeze in the winters.

The problem with carbon dioxide is that it doesn’t matter whence it’s emitted. Ohio carbons mix with all of the other emitted carbons in the atmosphere where they remain for more than a hundred years. So, we can be green in Washington and feel all the better for it. But we’ll suffer the consequences of climate change if others can’t find a viable path to green. Yet, one has to start somewhere, and since our national governments can’t commit to meaningful solutions it may be up to individual regions, states, counties, and cities—hoping, I guess, that a sufficient number of entities adopt effective programs to actually reduce carbon emissions.

It certainly doesn’t help that the two most powerful Republicans in Congress come from states heavily dependent on coal. Even if they were convinced that climate change is serious and mostly human-caused, neither could advocate the closing of their dirty plants should they wish to remain in office.

As you can tell, I am downright pessimistic on all this. There’s no political will. Besides, it’s probably too late to stabilize, let alone reverse, global warming.

Our descendants will somehow have to adapt to much harsher weather and flooded coastlines. There will be prolonged droughts in already dry regions and likely more precipitation in wet ones.

I foresee the day when the entire Southwest begins a mass exodus, after the wells run dry. To where will they run? The Pacific Northwest, of course.

A wee bit of the Irish

December 16, 2011 by davald

Until the Great Recession, Ireland was an economic jewel, lovingly called “the Celtic Tiger.” From Wikipedia:

From 1995 to 2000 GDP rate growth ranged between 7.8 and 11.5%. The rate then slowed to between 4.4 to 6.5% from 2001 to 2007. During that period the Irish GDP rose dramatically to equal then eventually surpass that of all but one state in Western Europe.

The Irish are well educated and labor is “flexible,” a euphemism for low wages. Thus, many technology firms, including Apple, established facilities there. Construction boomed in the early aughts, real estate prices soared, rainbows ubiquitous.

In 2008 the economy tanked, as it did across the western industrialized world (with few exceptions). The Irish government responded with sharp budget cuts, unemployment rose, bankruptcies increased. Not a few observers claimed that Ireland’s responses warranted praise. Austerity was precisely the correct approach. Some went so far as to claim that such frugality would lead to economic growth and greater employment.

Via Paul Krugman this morning we are treated with a blog site called “The Irish Economy.” Contrary to the more optimistic judgments, it seems that Ireland is not doing all that well. From the blog:

I especially liked a commenter’s take:

I’m shocked. Contractionary economic policy is contractionary.

Any chance we can increase gov’t spending now and start hiring loads of people and building the infrastructure we need? Or must the beatings of the poor and middle class continue until they finally get pissed off enough to start lopping off heads?

Bring out the pitchforks, Ma.

Denial rises with greenhouse gases

December 16, 2011 by davald

There was a time in this country when the issue of global warming occupied center stage. We citizens vaguely understood the science of how carbon dioxide thickens the earth’s protective blanket thereby warming the planet. We wanted governments to do something about it. Some did. Conspicuously absent from the signature pages was the United States, the highest per-capita contributor of greenhouse gases. All told, there are more carbons in the atmosphere emitted by the U.S. than by any other nation. China is working hard to overtake us in that miserable regard.

But the modern industrial economy is literally fueled by oil, and a majority of America’s electricity is generated by combusting coal, with the highest carbon intensity. It should not surprise us that our vast military apparatus has much to do with preserving access to petroleum and protecting oil-shipping lanes. Without a reliable supply of oil, the modern economy collapses.

Extracting, refining, transporting, and purveying oil is big business. The companies involved in the petroleum industry would suffer mightily under an effective carbon-reduction scheme. So they’re working very hard and spending a lot of money to ensure that no such scheme materializes. And they’ve been amazingly successful at it.

We know that it’s fairly easy to influence public perception of world events and conditions. Want a war? Concoct an enemy. Want people to divert attention from climate change or question its validity? Fund think tanks, members of Congress, and media to create doubt about the science, minimize the risks of global warming, or simply to talk about something else.

Media Lens, a British meda watchdog, recently commented on the failure of the media to accurately report the scientific consensus on global warming, enlighten the public on the implications of climate change, and keep the issue where it belongs—front and center. Here’s Media Lens:

With the Kyoto Treaty due to expire in 2012, the so-called ‘international community’ has failed abysmally to fulfil its commitments to protect the planet. This should surprise no-one. As senior Nasa climate scientist James Hansen pointed out after the previous climate summit in Mexico in 2010, UN talks are ‘doomed to failure’ since they do not address the fundamental physical constraints of the Earth’s climate system and how to live within them.

Public concern about climate change continues to rise. According to the latest Eurobarometer opinion poll (October 2011), 68% of Europeans polled consider climate change a very serious problem (up from 64% in 2009). Altogether 89% see it as a serious problem (either ‘very serious’ or ‘fairly serious’). On a scale of 1 (least) to 10 (most), the seriousness of climate change is ranked at 7.4, against 7.1 in 2009.

Meanwhile, media interest in the subject has crashed. Dr. Robert J. Brulle of Drexel University describes a ‘collapse of any significant coverage of climate change in the [US] media. We know that 2010 was a record low year, and 2011 will probably look much the same. If the media doesn’t draw attention to the issue, public opinion will decline’.

Media Lens quotes a former journalist: ‘Capitalism is trampling on journalism.’ Of course, the trampling knows no bounds.

BBC produced a multi-part documentary called “Frozen Planet.” It’s narrated by David Attenborough. As the title suggests, it’s about the Arctic and Antarctic regions. In marketing the program for distribution BBC made the last segment optional. And you can guess what that dealt with: “the threat of climate change.” America’s public television network, PBS, declined to purchase the series’ final installment.

Media Lens interviewed James Hansen, the world’s leading authority on climate change. Here is an excerpt:

We then asked him to give a rough figure to indicate how far he felt governments had moved towards tackling climate change. Would he say that governments were 1%, 20%, 50%, 70%,… of the way there? We knew this was imprecise, but we wanted to get an idea of his gut feeling. He responded:

‘0%, because they are starting down a wrong track, requiring 1-2 decades to reset. “Goals” for emission reduction, cap-and-trade with offsets, while continuing to build more coal-fired power plants and developing unconventional fossil fuels is a disastrous path. It is meant to fool people, even themselves. A strategic approach would instead recognize the geophysical boundary conditions, specifically that coal emissions must be rapidly phased out.’

He added some disturbing analysis:

‘The fundamental economic requirement concerns the price of (cheap, subsidized) fossil fuels relative to alternatives (energy efficiency, renewables, and other carbon-free energies) — there must be a rising price on carbon emissions (a fee, at the coal/oil/gas source or port of entry). As that price rises and the competition ensues we would reach a point where alternatives suddenly take off and we move beyond the dirty fossil fuel era. The fear that this will in fact occur is what drives the fossil interests who have totally taken control of our governments’ actions.’

We’re doomed.

The Republicans are all about financial responsibility

December 15, 2011 by davald

No? Well, you’re right. They’re not. Certainly the record of Republican administrations belies the conservatives’ claim to fiscal austerity.

Writing for the Huffington Post Geoffrey Stone does us the favor of comparing the last several presidencies. The biggest budget buster of them all is the sainted Ronald Reagan, who swallowed the Laffer Curve, hook, line, and sinker. Reagan believed that sharply reducing taxes would spur economic activity thereby boosting tax revenues. It didn’t happen. Stone:

The tax reform plan that was designed to stimulate business and produce a substantial budget surplus instead drove the United States deeper into debt and triggered a recession. By mid-1982 unemployment stood at 9.7%. Businesses failed right and left, and Reagan’s approval rating plunged to 35%. Instead of reducing the federal deficit, Reagan managed to do the impossible: he tripled the cumulative national debt of the United States in only eight years. Reaganomics did not work.

The above graphic is included in Stone’s article. It plainly shows that both Carter and especially Clinton were exemplars of frugality compared with their GOP counterparts. If we were to count the post-administration effects of Bush II’s tax cuts, the last column would be much higher. We might recall that Bush II converted an inherited $7 trillion budget surplus into a $7 trillion dollar deficit, which greatly exacerbated our present economic misery.

Low-carbon fuel standards and tar sands

December 15, 2011 by davald

Several states have either adopted or have proposed a low-carbon fuel standard, Washington being among the latter. The most comprehensive is California’s, whose goal is to reduce upstream carbon emissions, all the way to the extraction process. Transportation is the second largest source of carbon emissions, behind electricity generation (coal and natural gas).

Our neighbors to the north discovered a huge volume of oil trapped in Alberta tar sands. The process to separate the oil from the sands is every bit as pernicious as the ultimate release of vehicle carbon emissions. Big Oil and the Canadian government want to build a pipeline to pump the separated oil to the lower United States, where it would be refined for vehicle use. For those of us concerned with climate change the pipeline poses a very serious threat. It would facilitate the combustion of more carbons, which will be trapped by the atmosphere and contribute to planetary warming.

The extraction, transportation, and refinement of the Alberta oil requires a large capital investment. But the returns are even greater. Thus, there are significant political forces arrayed to fight carbon-reduction laws. Indeed, should a sufficient number of states adopt low-carbon fuel standards, the market for Canadian oil would be sharply curtailed or even disappear.

Salon.com has the story on how the Canadian embassy and U.S. lobbyists conspired to thwart low-carbon standards. The online magazine obtained hundreds of pages worth of emails chronicling the joint efforts.

When President Barack Obama decided in early November to delay a decision on TransCanada’s Keystone XL pipeline until after the next election, America’s environmental movement celebrated one of its biggest victories in recent memory. And no doubt the news came as a blow to Alberta’s tar sands industry, and to Canada’s oft-stated dream of becoming the next global energy superpower.

But behind activists’ jubilation lurked a somber reality, an untold story with much wider implications. The broader fight to reform Alberta’s tar sands, the one which actually stood a chance of breaking America’s addiction to the continent’s most polluting road fuel, has been quietly abandoned over the past several years. For that we can thank the planet’s richest oil companies and their Canadian government allies, who’ve together waged a stealthy war against President Obama’s climate change ambitions.

Adding insult to injury Canada’s recently re-elected conservative government just announced its withdrawal from the Kyoto Accords. Of course, the U.S. refused to become a signatory in the first place.

None of this bodes well for the future.

Worried about Social Security?

December 15, 2011 by davald

Before my very mirror I have become an old fart. That means that within a few months I’m eligible for Medicare, which I won’t take just yet, since I’m covered at work. My wife and I expect to begin taking Social Security benefits the following year, when we both turn 66.

Since I was self-employed for over a decade, I’ve paid a bucketload of dollars to SS; I had to contribute as both “employer” and “employee.” So, I’m not too shy about taking this money. Nor is my wife, who worked her tail off for 25 years teaching Marysville children.

Yet, as we reach these “golden years,” we have become more sensitive to the chatter about the program’s viability. In particular, will there be any money in the federal till when we decide to take our share?

The answer, from an actuarial perspective, is a decided yes. Social Security is healthy, and even without a few minor tweaks, will not exhaust its fund for decades, by which time the Mr. and Mrs. will be occupying urns.

The New York Times has included a valuable chart with its article today on threats to SS. If you take a look you’ll note that revenues exceed expenditures, and they have done so since the mid-80s.

But, as always, Republicans have never cared for “entitlements,” even those paid for by workers. They’d love to let their benefactors siphon off public dollars for private gain. Why, they want to privatize Social Security, and we know how that’s working in health care, where we pay more and get less than our European counterparts.

As you know Obama would like to extend the payroll tax cut, which has added another $800 a year to my wallet. Those tax revenues go into the Social Security fund. The Republicans see this decision point as yet another opportunity for brinkmanship, to force concessions from Democrats on even unrelated matters. The Times:

Citing the Bush-era tax cuts that were supposed to end in 2010, they [a group of liberal senators and representatives] expressed fear that the payroll tax cut would become permanent as politicians shied from letting them lapse. Mindful of some Republicans’ goal of privatizing Social Security, the Democrats suggested that tinkering with the payroll tax “may be used as the first step in a larger battle to fundamentally dismantle Social Security.”

Robert Reischauer, is one of two public trustees for Social Security. He told the Times:

“The nightmare that I have is that when it comes time to raise the tax back up to 6.2 percent, conservatives are going to propose that these two percentage points of payroll tax be devoted to individual accounts.” Mr. Reischauer said. “That will precipitate a huge fight and could change Social Security in a fundamental way.”

The “individual accounts” is a feature of privatization. Should the Republicans succeed in their effort, we might as well say goodbye to the program and hello to more wealth extraction by the One-percenters.

Oh how I long for an Eisenhower Republican these days. The current GOP is a haven for loonies, who have managed to buffalo the voters and gain control of the levers of government. Historian Thomas Frank calls them “the wrecking crew.” Should they achieve a majority of both houses and the White House, very soon we won’t recognize this country. We’ll certainly prefer to be elsewhere.
