Could politics trump science?

December 7, 2011 by davald

Silly question, of course. Politics in America almost always trump science. Why just today we get another example via the New York Times.

Health and Human Services Secretary Kathleen Sebelius on Wednesday overruled the Food and Drug Administration’s decision that emergency contraceptives be sold freely over the counter, including to teenagers 16 years old and younger.

The article goes to some length to detail the FDA’s studies, analyses, and conclusion—that the so-called morning after pill is safe and effective. Other medical professionals have confirmed that conclusion, which the Times reports.

Sibelius issued a statement, an excerpt of which appears in the article:

After careful consideration of the F.D.A. summary review, I have concluded that the data submitted by Teva do not conclusively establish that Plan B One-Step should be made available over the counter for all girls of reproductive age.

Now you might be wondering about Ms. Sebelius’s qualifications as a medical or scientific expert. Briefly put, she has none. She’s a lawyer by profession and training. Oh, and she’s a Roman Catholic.

The Church along with the Right Wing believe that conception confers personhood. Since the Plan B pill works to either prevent conception or, worse in the minds of conservatives, hinder a fertilized egg’s attaching to the uterus, as in a normal pregnancy, the emergency contraceptive is anathema, a favorite expression of popes.

We can be certain that Sebelius, notwithstanding her religious views, had the blessing of Obama, her boss. The risk of alienating independent voters in Iowa or Kansas outweighs the risks of unwanted pregnancies.

The president has his priorities, and poor teenage girls are way, way down the list. Let’s see how NOW reacts to this.

Speaking of the environment…

December 1, 2011 by davald

As we busy ourselves saving Columbia River salmon, our neighbors to the north, specifically in the Athabasca oil sands of Alberta, are wreaking substantial environmental destruction on an almost unimaginable scale. And were it not for mounting protests, Pres. Obama would have approved the Keystone XL pipeline to deliver the Canadian oil to refineries and terminals in the U.S. He’s postponed the decision until after next year’s general elections.

You can locate the oil sands on Google Earth. You’ll find it in the upper east quadrant of Alberta. Here’s a shot from NASA via Wikipedia:

The area under destruction is much larger than this clip. But by all means, go drive your car.

I’ve just started reading John Bellamy Foster’s essay in the December 2011 issue of Monthly Review. (Disclosure: It’s Marxist.) He quotes historian William McNeill:

…catastrophe is the underside of the human condition—a price we pay for being able to alter natural balances and to transform the face of the earth through collective effort and the use of tools.

The value of the oil accumulated in the Alberta tar sands is estimated to be an astonishing $14 trillion. The Rest of Us and our descendants are not in line for any of that money. Rather, we’ll pay the price for its extraction, transportation, refinement, and consumption. With so much at stake, it will be extremely difficult to keep the stuff in the ground. The “market” and ordinary greed argue for pollution on an unprecedented scale.

An appropriate nod back to MI-5, the BBC television series I’m watching. In the last episode some members of an environmental group called, I think, Divine Nature, set out to hold London hostage to an impending flood, occasioned, the show says, by global warming. The Brits have built a sophisticated barrier in the Thames that will prevent the flooding, which is the target of the group.

The group is demanding the release of a top secret report called “Aftermath.” Circumstances have conspired to compel a member of the British government to join ranks with Divine Nature, details of which I’ll forego here. Over the course of the episode it’s revealed that “Aftermath” is a joint study by the UK and the US. The essential points are as follows:

  • global warming is real
  • it will cause the deaths of millions
  • there will be strife and violence on a planetary scale
  • the countries are resigned to this, since there is insufficient political will to curb our addiction to fossil fuels
  • therefore, steps must be taken to control the oil and coal for the survivors

Scary stuff, but it rings true. I can certainly imagine that the Pentagon, the CIA, MI-6, and a host of other government agencies on both sides of the Atlantic have developed and continue to develop worst case scenarios related to climate change and its impacts. It occurs to me that there is a “doomsday” syndrome afoot not unlike General Buck Turgidson’s monologue in Kubrick’s Dr. Strangelove. From Wikidpedia:

Turgidson [played by George C. Scott] tries to persuade [President] Muffley [Peter Sellars] to seize the moment and eliminate the Soviet Union by launching a full-scale attack on the Soviet Union. The General believes the United States is in a superior strategic position and a first strike would destroy the majority of the Soviets’ missiles before they could retaliate.

Then-Secretary of State Madeleine Albright told then-Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff Colin Powell: “What’s the point of you saving this superb military for, Colin, if we can’t use it?” Well, we’ve still got this superb military, which really has less to do with fighting wars than acquiring, preserving, and protecting the earth’s natural resources, oil being chief among them.

Perception primed

November 29, 2011 by davald

I’ve been making my way through Thinking, Fast and Slow, by Nobel prize winner Daniel Kahneman. (It was the prize for economics, oddly enough.) It’s an unsettling read, because one discovers that our mind/brain seems to be a very unreliable window onto the world. I am especially struck by what psychologists call ‘the priming effect.’ Simply put, what we think we see is easily influenced by some rather minor pre-cognition events.

For example, Kahneman writes about test subjects given a pencil. In one set of tests the subjects are asked to hold the pencil between their teeth horizontally. In another the same subjects are asked to purse their lips around the eraser end of the pencil. Then the subjects were shown cartoons from Gary Larson’s The Far Side. Those in the first group, with the horizontal pencil, were more likely to find the images funny than the second group, with their lips pursed around the pencil’s eraser end. What’s going on? The researchers concluded that clenching the pencil horizontally yielded a “smile,” with cheeks pinched back and the outer edges of the mouth turned upwards. Those holding the pencil’s eraser end in their mouths yielded a “frown.”

I’m sure that this experiment has many implications. I should imagine that if you walked into a room where everyone was smiling and perhaps laughing you’d feel more comfortable and perhaps also smile than if you entered a room filled with glum faces and no conversation.

Here’s another example from Kahneman’s book. Half of a group of college students were asked to arrange brief sentences including at least one of the words Florida, forgetful, bald, gray, or wrinkle. The other half were presented with none of these words. After completing their task, the students were told to walk down the corridor to another room. The experimenters, unbeknownst to the subjects, recorded the time the students took to walk that short distance. Are you ready? Those students in the first group walked more slowly than those in the second group. The experiment was dubbed ‘the Florida effect.’ The unconscious association of terms commonly associated with being old affected the students’ walking pace.

Today’s New York Times features an article on memory. It’s not what it’s cracked up to be—extremely unreliable. This is especially true with respect to witness testimony in trials. What witnesses say that they saw does not often correspond with reality, say as recorded on video cameras.

Many years ago I was in an audience of law enforcement personnel listening to and observing a presentation by Elizabeth Loftus, then a psychology professor at the University of Washington. She’s now at the University of California, Irvine. Her presentation then was about traffic accidents and witness statements. I don’t recall the particulars, but I was convinced, after one of her demonstrations, that we can be easily fooled or distracted. The Times:

In a 1974 study published in The Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior, she asked participants to view films of fender-benders in which no car windows or headlights were broken. Later, the subjects who were asked how fast the cars were going when they “smashed” into each other — as opposed to “hit” — were more likely to report speeding and describe shattered glass they never actually saw. [The link delivers you to a paper co-authored by Loftus.]

There is a famous experiment discussed in Kahneman’s book about people observing a film of a basketball game. The players were divided up so that one side wore black shirts and the other white. Subject viewers were asked to count the number of passes made by the team dressed in white, but to ignore the behaviors of the “black” team. I’ll quote from Kahneman:

Halfway through the video, a woman wearing a gorilla suit appears, crosses the court, thumps her chest, and moves on. The gorilla is in view for 9 seconds. Many thousands of people have seen the video, and about half of them do not notice anything unusual. It is the counting task—and especially the instruction to ignore one of the teams—that causes the blindness. No one who watches the video without that task would miss the gorilla.

Kahneman includes many examples of how easily our perceptions are influenced. Here’s another simple one. If you’re given the word EAT then asked to complete the word with these letters SO_P, you’ll insert an U for SOUP. If you’re presented with WASH, you’ll likely insert an A to produce SOAP.

Now on to money. This gets interesting. As Kahneman says, “Reminders of money produce some troubling effects.” Here’s Kahneman:

Money-primed people become more independent than they would be without the associative trigger [e.g., performing a task with a “money-related object in the background, such as a stack of Monopoly money on a table”]. They persevered almost twice as long in trying to solve a difficult problem before they asked the experimenter for help, a crisp demonstration of increased self-reliance. Money-primed people are also more selfish: they were much less willing to spend time helping another student who pretended to be confused about an experimental task…Money-primed undergraduates also showed a greater preference for being alone.

The general theme of these findings is that the idea of money primes individualism: a reluctance to be involved with others, to depend on others, or to accept demands from others.

Perhaps in no other society does money feature so prominently as in America. We are obsessed with money, certainly if we’re struggling just to survive, an increasing percentage of our population. But the obsession may be even more pronounced among the über rich, the fraction at the top with multi-million-dollar incomes.

A couple of days ago the Times ran a story on Ronald S. Lauder, heir to the Estée Lauder cosmetics fortune. He’s a billionaire three times over. Yet, he spends significant resources on avoiding taxes. The super wealthy have access to all sorts of sophisticated tax shelters and tax-avoidance schemes, and Mr. Lauder appears to take advantage of them all. Nevertheless, there are exceptions among this class. The Times:

A handful of billionaires like Warren E. Buffett and Bill Gates have joined Democrats in calling for an elimination of the breaks, saying that the current system adds to the budget deficit, contributes to the widening income gap between the richest and the rest of society, and shifts the tax burden onto small businesses and the middle class. Republicans have resisted, saying the tax increases on the wealthy would harm the economy and cost jobs.

Kahneman’s own research and that of others mentioned in his book suggests that a focus on money has a pernicious effect, including an unwillingness to help others. I would add that they also want to keep their distance from lesser mortals.

Yet, money and its associations are ubiquitous and, evidently, quite powerful influences on our behaviors. Perhaps we need to collectively change the subject. Love and Peace? Quick, grab a pencil and clench it between your teeth. Better yet, hand pencils out to bankers with instructions. The world will at least seem a bit more humorous.

What if we’re too dumb for democracy

November 28, 2011 by davald

Let’s call it ‘scientific intelligence.’ I don’t have in mind scientists, who are obviously smart and know a thing or two about their field of study. I’m thinking of the non-scientific cadre, which is most of us. How are we to appreciate some very complex topics so as to inform our policy judgments?

The most obvious topic here is climate change, or global warming. The overwhelming majority—close to unanimity—of climate scientists concur on several fundamental points:

  • atmospheric carbon dioxide warms the planet, serving as a “blanket,” to use Al Gore’s term
  • too much CO2 can render the planet too warm to support species as we know them; these first two are about physics
  • if the earth warms more than a couple of degrees centigrade, there will most certainly be extreme weather, prolonged droughts, habitat erosion, and the end of species

I’ll leave the facts there. Now what to do about it?

Yet we can’t go from fact to policy because far too many of us are ignorant of science in general and climate change in particular. Not only do we fail to comprehend, we Americans have a proclivity to call scientists liars and their “theories” fabrications. It’s as if we’re making this deduction: if we can’t or don’t understand science, then science is false. It evidently doesn’t occur to us to look in the mirror and wonder about the state of our grey matter.

Such things are the subject of a new book, Fool Me Twice: Fighting the Assault on Science in America, by Shawn Laurence Otto. I haven’t read the book, but the author has been interviewed by Rolling Stone magazine. Julian Brookes, writing for RS, begins:

“Whenever the people are well informed” an optimistic Thomas Jefferson wrote, “they can be trusted with their own government.” Sure – but what if the people have no clue? 

In response to one question Brookes replies:

Look at the 94 of 100 newly elected GOP members of Congress who have either said flat-out that they believe climate change is a vast hoax or that they have signed pledges to oppose any mitigation efforts. And this goes against all the evidence presented to every government around the world, including our own. This also extends to people like John Boehner, who has advocated in the past for teaching creationism in science classes, and who claims to believe that climate scientists are saying that carbon dioxide is a carcinogen.

My concern runs beyond the elected officials, who are entitled to their own opinions and, it seems, their own fact. They were elected, after all, which means that a majority of their constituents put them in office. The representatives and senators are scientifically illiterate because too many voters are even more so.

Although U.S. colleges and universities continue to churn out brilliant and innovative scientists, the bulk of American society stumbles on simple math and couldn’t tell the difference between Newton’s laws of motion and his fig-filled cookies. (I am kidding about the latter, of course.) If you ask the man on the street what happens if something goes up, he’d be unlikely to respond that “it must go down.” What’s gravity got to do with Sarah Palin or Rick Perry or just about any Republican, for that matter?

I have virtually no confidence in the American breed of homo sapiens to think its way out of climate change. Other species will either succumb or adapt to altered conditions occasioned by an ever-warming planet. Humans must rely on our brains, which, alas, appear to be in short supply.

Sorry about that, descendants.

So, now what?

November 18, 2011 by davald

Scientific pundits are agape. Could Einstein have been wrong?

In 1905, the miracle year if there ever was, Einstein developed his special theory of relativity. (He wrote four other papers that year, each of which could have earned a Nobel Prize; Einstein got one.) That theory surmised that nothing in the universe could travel faster than the speed of light.

Now comes a group of experimenters (they call themselves ‘Opera‘) who believe that neutrinos may break the sacred barrier. They’ve run at least two separate tests. The sub-atomic neutrinos win, by a few nanoseconds—beyond statistical significance.

Should other experiments replicate the results and consensus ensue that yes, indeed, there are particles that exceed the speed of light, what does that mean, other than disproving by a nano-eyelash that Herr Einstein’s theory is mistaken? Are there implications beyond simply showing up the wild-haired German?

If you think yours truly is going to answer that question, forget about it.

More wedges

October 19, 2011 by davald

In 2004 two scientists collaborated on a study of climate change. They came up with the idea of wedges, each equivalent to a gigaton of carbon saved in the last, the 50th, year. The scientists, Robert H. Socolow and Stephen W. Pacala, assumed 2006 as a starting point for each wedge. In all, they identified seven wedges.

The seven wedges would have totaled 175 billion tons of carbon not emitted into the atmosphere over 50 years. However, they argued that it was imperative to begin as soon as possible; delay would add more wedges.

 

Well, as it turns out the world did nothing to curb emissions. Indeed, emissions have increased since the authors’ initial report, which appeared in Science. They’ve revisited the issue, writing in the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists. The authors:

Today, nine wedges are required to fill the stabilization triangle, instead of seven. A two-segment global carbon-dioxide emissions trajectory that starts now instead of seven years ago — flat for 50 years, then falling nearly to zero over the following 50 years — adds another 50 parts per million to the equilibrium concentration. The delayed trajectory produces nearly half a degree Celsius (three-quarters of a degree Fahrenheit) of extra rise in the average surface temperature of the Earth. (Note that there is a three-year lag in the posting of authoritative global data. We used 2001 data in our 2004 paper, and 2008 data are available now. Thus, available data do not yet reflect the recession. Between 2001 and 2008, the emissions rate climbed by more than a quarter.)

Fine and dandy, humans. Although, some like it hot.

So, why exactly are we in Afghanistan?

October 4, 2011 by davald

Most, if not all, electronics, from iPhones to LED lightbulbs to X-ray machines use rare-earth elements. As the name suggests, the elements are not found in abundance. The US is 100% dependent on imported rare-earth elements for the manufacture of its devices. China happens to have 92% of the known world total. But guess where we’ve found a bunch more?

Yep. Afghanistan.

The October 2011 issue of Scientific American tells us:

Geologists say newfound deposits in the embattled country could fulfill the world’s desire for rare-earth and critical minerals and end opium’s local stranglehold in the process.

Gosh. So that’s why all the hardware and killing.

Oh, and those costs. Past $461 billion and counting.

The Great Divergence and the withering of empathy

September 13, 2011 by davald

We are, of course, entitled to our own opinions. Some might go further to argue that we’re equally entitled to our own facts, which belies ignorance of the the term ‘fact.’ However, it’s always been the case, and seemingly more pronounced in these times, that passions rule reason, as Hume suggested. In other words, what we may lack between our ears is more than offset by the quantity of our guts.

In response to my recent post on “So Why is America So Great?,” a reader offered these words:

Okay, best national defense in the history of earth.  That’s a plus, though it is a bloated bureaucracy.

This is obfuscation.  The issue is medical care, not health care.  There’s more MRI machines in Philadelphia than in all of Canada and England . . . combined.

The richest (owned houses, cars tv’s, cable, autos, electronics, health) and fattest “poor” in the history of humankind, and with a standard of living higher than the average (not “poor”) person in, say, Paris or Rome or Madrid.

The rich keep doing what made them rich and the poor (the few who remain such for as much as a decade) keep doing what made them poor.

My congratulations and admiration to the billionaires.  keep up the good work.

I really don’t know what to make of this. But it’s clear that the writer and I live on different moral planets. It also seems as if presidential candidate Ron Paul lives on the same planet as the commenter.

Last night emcee Wolf Blitzer asked the congressman, “Are you saying that society should just let him [a quite sick man near death who opted not to buy private health insurance] die?” Several members of the audience yelled out, “Yes!”

Despite having studied several years’ worth of philosophy, I would not expect to put forth an argument that would persuade Mr. Paul and the commenter to change their minds. Rather, I suspect that we either have empathy or we don’t. It’s not so much a matter of the gut as a function of two other factors: evolutionary biology and early childhood development.

I read the Wikipedia entry on empathic concern. Consider this excerpt from the article:

Human beings are strongly motivated to be connected to others.[4] In humans and higher mammals, an impulse to care for offspring is almost certainly genetically hard-wired, although modifiable by circumstance.

To be fair to Congressman Paul, in answering the question he recounted his professional medical practice and charitable works. He implied that he cares about others. But he also said something to the effect that freedom means assuming personal responsibility for your own welfare and not relying on “socialism.”

One common thread that runs through all of the Republican candidates for president is abhorrence for governmental programs, especially public social programs like Medicare, “Obamacare,” and even Social Security, although there seems to be some retrenchment from the latter after Texas governor Rick Perry called it a “lie” and “a Ponzi scheme.” And while Ron Paul is decidedly libertarian in his views, which places him a bit more out there than others in his cohort, he believes with his competitors that government should keep its hands off the private sector, which brings me back to my commenter and his praise of billionaires.

I happen to think that highly unequal societies—and the U.S. is at or near the top of this category—are unhealthy societies. There is considerable research to support this view. At the forefront of this research is Prof. Richard Wilkinson of the UK. With Kate Pickett, a professor of epidemiology, he has written his latest book, entitled The Spirit Level: Why Greater Equality Makes Societies Stronger. Here’s an excerpt from the dust jacket:

Further, more unequal societies are bad for everyone within them—the rich and middle class as well as the poor.

Interesting. As it happens, inequality is bad for your health, even Mr. Paul’s and Mr. Buffett’s.

Wilkinson and Pickett show that inequality tracks with the following syndromes:

  • obesity rates
  • morbidity rates
  • mortality rates
  • and a host of psychological diseases.

Consistent readers of my posts know that I focus on inequality. For example, I recently wrote about what I call the ‘Great Divergence.’ It began in the 7os and has continued unabated, enlarging the gap between the extremely rich and the Rest of Us. (Coincidentally, the Washington Post has begun a series on inequality, replete with charts and graphs.)

I am deeply disturbed by the brazen selfishness of the Tea Party, as evinced by members of the debate audience. Likewise, all of the Republican presidential hopefuls exhibit what I believe to be a perverse disregard for the wellbeing of others. We will recall that at the previous Republican debate some in the audience cheered governor Perry’s unseemly record of state executions.

Robert Reich, now teaching at my alma mater, talks about America being two societies. One of the very rich and the other populated by the Rest of Us. (He also wrote the foreword to the Wilkinson book.) The Great Divergence may have already reached a point of no return. The very rich and their odd supporters have become increasingly inured to the misfortune of others, no doubt for a very simple reason: they have no exposure to the Rest of Us. There is no overlapping of these disparate societies. Yes, we know of the rich. We see them on TV and in magazines. But they do not, perhaps cannot, know us.

And that’s a problem, because the rich have all the money, and money translates into political power, which is put to the further service of the rich, and so on. Yet, rather than inducing a revolution we get “admiration.”

I don’t get it.

That’s all, folks

June 22, 2011 by davald

The laws of physics may limit human intelligence. The brain is only so big with room for only so many neurons. However, as Scientific American reports, there are options:

The human mind, however, may have better ways of expanding without the need for further biological evolution. After all, honeybees and other social insects do it: acting in concert with their hive sisters, they form a collective entity that is smarter than the sum of its parts. Through social interaction we, too, have learned to pool our intelligence with others.

And then there is technology. For millennia written language has enabled us to store information outside our body, beyond the capacity of our brain to memorize. One could argue that the Internet is the ultimate consequence of this trend toward outward expansion of intelligence beyond our body. In a sense, it could be true, as some say, that the Internet makes you stupid: collective human intelligence—culture and computers—may have reduced the impetus for evolving greater individual smarts.

Gosh, how is this going to work in a faux libertarian society, one dominated by the cult of individualism and “rational choice” economic theory? Which other societies might adopt the social connections, in particular the need for cooperation, to advance humans’ collective intelligence?

Granted, we’ve been pretty good in the USA at developing technology, from the Internet to iPads. But we’re not all that good at social cohesion. Our society is sharply divided along several lines, from income to education to ethnicity, not to mention the stark contrasts between the two major political parties. Moreover, we’re possessed of sickly dysfunctional governance at all levels of government. We can’t seem to get things done.

Just off the top of my head, I’d offer China, which has a lean, mean governance structure, and perhaps the western European nations, which have much lower Gini indexes than the US and a higher level of social cohesion.

We’ll see.
