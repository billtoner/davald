A catastrophic note

August 23, 2012 by davald

Elizabeth Kohlbert, a staff writer for The New Yorker, penned Field Notes from a Catastrophe: Man, Nature, and Climate Change. She had interviewed dozens of climate scientists who all painted a pretty pessimistic picture: unless people significantly curbed their greenhouse gas emissions, the planet would suffer a catastrophe. One such scientist, David Rind talked to her about water. She wrote:

He found that as carbon dioxide levels rose, the world would begin to experience more and more serious water shortages, starting near the equator and then spreading toward the poles. When he applied the [Palmer Drought Severity] index to GISS’s model [Goddard Institute for Space Studies] for doubled CO2, it showed that most of the continental United States would be suffering under severe drought conditions. [pp. 107-109]

Well, it seems that we’re already on that path. The New York Times reports today that drought now affects over 63 percent of the United States. And with drought comes diminishing water supplies. The Times:

The plunge in the water supply is mostly the result of a simple geological process. Without rain, aquifers deep beneath the ground have not been getting the water that seeps into the earth to replenish their supplies. In one part of northwest Indiana, Mr. Basch said, the groundwater level had dropped as much as 40 feet, or about eight times more than it normally does.

Compounding the problem for homeowners relying on smaller aquifers is the fact that municipalities and farmers who consume water on a much larger scale have been draining the groundwater supply at a faster rate this summer because of the drought.

There has been “a drop in the water table all over the state of Missouri, not just in one specific area,” Mr. Flynn said. “I’ve never seen anything like this in the almost 30 years I’ve been running the company.”

While Obama hasn’t done a damn thing about global warming, the Republicans actually deny that there’s a problem. Something about a hoax. Ironic, in a tragic sort of way, that those states impacted most by prolonged drought will cast their votes for those least likely to address the underlying cause of their water shortages.

I don’t think ignorance brings bliss. It will surely deliver catastrophe.

Inequality’s correlations: murder

August 13, 2012 by davald

Americans love their guns, or at least an alien would draw that conclusion from a casual glance at television news. Perversely, the affection increases immediately following the latest slaughter by some arsenal-ladened psychopath, previously described as bright, quiet, but a bit of a loner. No one would have suspected, and all that rot. But it happened, nevertheless, after acquiring a small-army’s worth of weapons and ammunition. As has been said often enough, there is no such thing as bad publicity. So when the crazies massacre, the National Rifle Association can count on adding members. The more atrocious and horrifying the accounts, the better.

Louisiana, home to the French Quarter and crayfish, enjoys a peculiar status: people there shoot each other—all the time. Bang. You’re dead. Next. It’s also a state of relatively high income inequality, though not as high as New York’s.

There are a few interesting takeaways from the above chart. New York, for example, has the highest Gini index but a “modest” murder rate, somewhere in the middle of the pack.

The states with the lowest murder rates also have the lowest level of inequality. Fancy that. So what might explain the correlation?

In their book The Spirit Level researchers Richard Wilkinson and Kate Picket emphasize the correlation. As to possible causation they quote a few psychiatrists. One, James Gilligan of Harvard Medical School, suggests that violent acts “are attempts to ward off or eliminate the feeling of shame and humiliation—a feeling that is painful, and can even be intolerable and overwhelming—and replace it with its opposite, the feeling of pride.” He found after interviewing murderers that they acted out of “perceived threats…to pride.”

The researchers also cite the work of Margo Wilson and Martin Daly who wrote a book entitled Homicide. You may find the following a bit off-putting, but here goes, as quoted by Wilkinson and Picket:

…they use statistical, anthropological and historical data to show how young men have strong incentives to achieve and maintain as high a social status as the can—because their success in sexual competition depends on status.

We should not be surprised that more unequal societies, or states, increase “the stakes in the competition for status.” The more pronounced the income differentials the more susceptible young men are to “perceived threats” to their image.

Whether or not you subscribe to such hypotheses there is no question that the correlation between inequality and violence is quite strong. Here’s a chart from The Spirit Level on international comparisons:

Gosh, look at the U.S. We are to the other countries as Louisiana is to the other 49 states, though even more so. Look at the lower left of the chart. Note the Nordic countries and Japan, all having much more equal societies than the U.S. and also much lower homicide rates.

Ah, but it’s nice to be number one, isn’t it?

Keep on walking, nevertheless

August 2, 2012 by davald

I may have mentioned that I had lost about 70 pounds over a year and a half, following a diagnosis of Type II diabetes. My regimen consisted of diet austerity and long walks—about four to five miles a day. But, heck, I’ve got the wrong genes, as family photographs attest. Despite continued walking, a few of those lost pounds found their way back onto my frame. So, I’m a bit pudgier than during my brief lean months.

For those readers who have struggled with weight, trying this or that diet to little or no permanent avail, you can empathize. It’s hard and frustrating to lose then gain then repeat.

Many Americans, otherwise chained to desk or couch, believe that a thinner body is just a matter of burning off calories in daily exercise. So, we enroll in a gym. Or we purchase a stationary bike. Or we strap on a pair of running shoes then shuffle out the door and, typically, waddle up the street.

Darn, it just doesn’t work that way. Relying on exercise to shed pounds doesn’t make us leaner. Indeed, we can wind up actually gaining weight. So says Gretchen Reynolds writing for the New York Times. Citing studies, Reynolds reports:

The problem for those of us hoping to use exercise to slough off fat is that most current calculations about exercise and weight loss assume that metabolism remains unchanged or is revved by exercise.

The damn body loves you fat.

Nevertheless, despite adding back some weight, I will pursue my daily walk. I feel a bit better and my vitals are very much improved. Oh, and pass the ice cream.

Mitt’s misses, continued

August 2, 2012 by davald

So Mitt Romney praises Jared Diamond’s book Guns, Germs and Steel as the basis for his political beliefs. But he got the book wrong, in my judgment. I read it when it first came out after attending a lecture by Diamond himself at the University of Washington. Now Prof. Diamond also wants to set the record straight, and I’m sure that he was appalled to discover that Mr. Romney found support for his ideology in the professor’s words. Writing for the New York Times Diamond admonishes the Republican presidential candidate:

It is not true that my book “Guns, Germs and Steel,” as Mr. Romney described it in a speech in Jerusalem, “basically says the physical characteristics of the land account for the differences in the success of the people that live there. There is iron ore on the land and so forth.”

That is so different from what my book actually says that I have to doubt whether Mr. Romney read it. My focus was mostly on biological features, like plant and animal species, and among physical characteristics, the ones I mentioned were continents’ sizes and shapes and relative isolation. I said nothing about iron ore, which is so widespread that its distribution has had little effect on the different successes of different peoples. (As I learned this week, Mr. Romney also mischaracterized my book in his memoir, “No Apology: Believe in America.”)

And this election is considered close.

Only in America.

Stinking tomatoes

June 30, 2012 by davald

The last tasty tomato I consumed was from a former colleague’s garden. And that was over 20 years ago. When I survey the produce at grocery stores I go for the reddest, believing that they must be better. I’ve been fooled.

The New York Times reports:

The unexpected culprit is a gene mutation that occurred by chance and that was discovered by tomato breeders. It was deliberately bred into almost all tomatoes because it conferred an advantage: It made them a uniform luscious scarlet when ripe.

Now, in a paper published in the journal Science, researchers report that the very gene that was inactivated by that mutation plays an important role in producing the sugar and aromas that are the essence of a fragrant, flavorful tomato. And these findings provide a road map for plant breeders to make better-tasting, evenly red tomatoes.

These researchers have been able to turn the gene back on while also keeping the “red” gene active. The result, reports the Times:

To test their discovery, the researchers used genetic engineering to turn on the disabled genes while leaving the uniform ripening trait alone. The fruit was evenly dark green and then red and had 20 percent more sugar and 20 to 30 percent more carotenoids when ripe.

Now when will we get a chance to buy and eat the new and improved tomato? It could take a while.

…do not look for those genetically engineered tomatoes at the grocery store. Producers would not dare to make such a tomato for fear that consumers would reject it.

Oh, well. Pass the sucking red tomato.

Meritocracy and government

June 7, 2012 by davald

Michels’s grim conclusion was that it was impossible for any party, no matter its belief system, to bring about democracy in practice. Oligarchy was inevitable. For any kind of institution with a democratic base to consolidate the legitimacy it needs to exist, it must have an organization that delegates tasks. The rank and file will not have the time, energy, wherewithal or inclination to participate in the many, often minute decisions necessary to keep the institution functioning. In fact, effectiveness, Michels argues convincingly, requires that these tasks be delegated to a small group of people with enough power to make decisions of consequence for the entire membership. Over time, this bureaucracy becomes a kind of permanent, full-time cadre of leadership. “Without wishing it,” Michels says, there grows up a great “gulf which divides the leaders from the masses.” The leaders now control the tools with which to manipulate the opinion of the masses and subvert the organization’s democratic process. “Thus the leaders, who were at first no more than the executive organs of the collective, will soon emancipate themselves from the mass and become independent of its control.”

— excerpted in The Nation

Hayes is talking about Robert Michels, a German social theorist, who, born of wealth, nevertheless embarked on a political journey, moving further and further left. In each case he found that organization leads ineluctably to oligarchy, rule by a few.

In our own political systems, more properly described as representative government, we ostensibly select those we prefer to govern us. Hayes goes on to say about our current Congress, especially, that we are increasingly represented by elites beholden to monied interests, from which many, if not most, of them spring.

Hayes begins this lengthy excerpt chronicling his own experience as a young boy scoring well enough on a three-hour exam to gain entrance to Manhattan’s most elite educational institution, Hunter College High School, free to those few who attend. The only criterion for admittance is the score on a once-administered three-hour exam. As pure a meritocracy as we have in America. Or, at least it was. Over time, Hayes reports, New York City’s wealthy denizens invested in private tutors and test-taking classes for their young children. And the preparation and investments worked, so that the demographics of the school changed over time, becoming whiter and richer.

Meritocracy, Hayes suggests from his reading of Michels, requires adherence to two principles: difference and mobility. The first assumes that we have vastly different capabilities, which should be not only acknowledged but embraced. The second principle, mobility, assumes that, though we are different, we should all have equal opportunity to compete for the more attractive positions.

I’ll step in here with some comments. The above sounds an awful like conventional conservative thinking: the role of government is to ensure equal opportunity, but also to recognize that the better should prosper and the worse should suffer. After all, that’s what markets are all about. Scarce goods are distributed on the basis of merit rather than, as liberals would argue, to ensure fairness. Hayes has his own take:

But this ideal, appealing as it may be, runs up against the reality of what I’ll call the Iron Law of Meritocracy. The Iron Law of Meritocracy states that eventually the inequality produced by a meritocratic system will grow large enough to subvert the mechanisms of mobility. Unequal outcomes make equal opportunity impossible. The Principle of Difference will come to overwhelm the Principle of Mobility. Those who are able to climb up the ladder will find ways to pull it up after them, or to selectively lower it down to allow their friends, allies and kin to scramble up. In other words: “Who says meritocracy says oligarchy.”

America, for better or worse, and I believe strongly that it’s the latter, is enthrall to “The Iron Law of Meritocracy.” Born rich, you’re likely to stay that way. On the other end of the scale, you should have picked more successful parents.

In such a pseudo-merit system, those who are first when the metaphorical race begins are far more likely to finish first. They will have taken every advantage to ensure that once rich they will leave the world rich. Hayes:

But the Iron Law of Meritocracy makes a different prediction: that societies ordered around the meritocratic ideal will produce inequality without the attendant mobility. Indeed, over time, a society will become more unequal and less mobile as those who ascend its heights create means of preserving and defending their privilege and find ways to pass it on across generations. And this, as it turns out, is a pretty spot-on description of the trajectory of the American economy since the mid-1970s.

Notice the dates. They are too familiar to readers of this blog. They signal the start of the Great Divergence, a term that is both apt and increasingly popular. It coincides most sharply with Ronald Reagan’s occupation of the Oval Office.

While I doubt that he was the architect of the Great Divergence he certainly allowed it to happen, giving it a big nudge, certainly, with the wholesale firing of the air-traffic controllers. He also spoke the language of “meritocracy,” singing the praises of the private sector and its inbred social Darwinism while defaming government as “the problem” rather than the solution.

It has become all too fashionable to denigrate government as did Reagan. Conservatives would go further than crusade against it; they spend their waking hours conjuring up new ways to give effect to their attitude. Government, they say, must be put on a diet. It must be denied revenues, because its expenditures frustrate the “Iron Law of Meritocracy,” nurturing the undeserved at the expense of the privileged.

I hope that you see what’s at stake here, especially in light to what’s been happening in conservative-controlled states like Wisconsin and Ohio, where public employees are under siege. Government, for all its ills, is about the only mechanism available to rein in the extremes, rendering both the rich and poor less so.

Okay, this sounds like socialism! Put down your swords for a moment, wipe off the spittle, and allow yourself to be mentally transported to northern Europe. If you don’t like government doing things on behalf of the Rest of Us, you’ll hate Finland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, et al. Their governments are huge in comparison to the U.S.’s And what do their citizens get in return? Free education. Free health care. Scant poverty. Far less inequality. Higher living standards.

Well, yes, education and health care are not exactly “free.” The citizens of these lands pay higher taxes for these benefits, in most cases willingly if not enthusiastically.

But the Nordic people largely embrace the exchange: higher taxes and larger government for those things we Americans increasingly expect the private sector to provide, in accordance with the “Iron Law of Meritocracy.”

Mark my words. The more we support, either by action or inaction, the shrinking of government, the more the Rest of Us will suffer and the more wealth the already wealthy will siphon off from a protracted, sputtering economy.

Having issued this caveat, I quickly offer another. There are many obvious reasons why we should distrust our government, though not necessarily the concept of government. Congress is the home of “institutional corruption.” And as Hayes points out, regulatory capture is rife in D.C., with federal agencies serving as the source of future employees for the firms they are supposed to regulate.

I’ve got to believe that the Founding Fathers, Abe Lincoln, and certainly Teddy Roosevelt would be aghast at what has happened to the federal government, especially the deluge of billionaire dollars ensuring that the Rest of Us get screwed as the “malefactors of great wealth” extract greater shares of GDP. They would be appalled by Citizens United, which brought unbridled bribery into the open and onto the pages of the U.S. Code.

But in particular for those who cobbled together the Constitution, they would be horrified by how the very concept of democracy as they conceived it has been greatly sullied beyond their recognition. Of course, they wanted no part of monarchy, and none of the associated pomp and circumstance. And they also preferred a limited, less intrusive government than the one they had endured under King George. And they also believed in the entrepreneurial spirit, about fashioning a respectable life through dint of labor and discipline.

However, the virtues of self-reliance and self-actualization cannot be practiced today by the Rest of Us. Those attitudes are reserved for the extremely privileged, for the most part, and unavailable to the masses. We’ve also lost our sense of innovation and imagination, if we take economist Tyler Cowen’s word. With the Great Divergence we may have entered a protracted state of stagnation.

You see, handing everything over to the plutocracy has been bad for business. The plutocrats know no limits. If left to their own appetites and devices, they will cause the whole damn thing to come crashing down on the Rest of Us. After all, they came awfully close to doing that just a few short years ago. They must be constrained for their—and our—own good.

Who regulates, or at least is supposed to? Government, preferably one whose agents are themselves virtuous.

Who can lift the poor? Government.

Who can restrain the malefactors? Government.

Who can provide education, health care, and guaranteed retirement income? Government.

Who can enable scientific research? Government.

But that kind of government, now just a mental figment, can be realized only by a citizenry committed to those achievements. Yet, it’s proved to be far too easy to manufacture the consent of the governed. Actually, we’re pretty damn cheap.

Why do we collectively put up with this? Is the challenge too large? Have we already lost? Did we ever struggle in service to the Rest of Us?

I guess that’s why some people feel the need to occupy something. Doing something—anything—is better than nothing. Lean out your window and scream that you’ve had enough.

We’ve recently witnessed real revolutions. After suffering a long train of autocratic abuse, citizens of the Middle East erupted, literally taking to the streets then occupying them. Something or perhaps several things sparked the collective outrage.

What might be the spark that sets off the Rest of Us? How low do we have to go before we scream “enough”?

Or, horror of horror, perhaps most Americans are quite comfortable with the growing inequality, the decline in public education, the erosion of our public infrastructure. Perhaps they have other things to do than to arrest these developments. Maybe, just maybe, they like not only oligarchy but the plutocrats who run it.

Spark. Where are you?

Send in the clouds

May 3, 2012 by davald

Climate change skeptics proffer counter-theories they believe debunk the grand theory of global warming. Yet each attempt is ultimately foiled, usually upon revelation that mistakes were made. But the last bastion of counter-argument focuses on clouds, with at least one scientific heavyweight averring that they will save the day and the planet.

The New York Times presents us with a comprehensive look at the players and the issues in this final frontier. The Times:

[The skeptics’] theory exploits the greatest remaining mystery in climate science, the difficulty that researchers have had in predicting how clouds will change. The scientific majority believes that clouds will most likely have a neutral effect or will even amplify the warming, perhaps strongly, but the lack of unambiguous proof has left room for dissent.

Chief among the dissenters from the majority opinion that climate change is real and mostly human-caused is MIT’s Richard Lindzen. Because of his credentials, he’s become the champion of the skeptics, including members of Congress and the British Parliament. The Times:

At gatherings of climate change skeptics on both sides of the Atlantic, Dr. Lindzen has been treated as a star. During a debate in Australia over carbon taxes, his work was cited repeatedly. When he appears at conferences of the Heartland Institute, the primary American organization pushing climate change skepticism, he is greeted by thunderous applause.

Under investigation are two major types of cloud formations: those that allow for excess heat to escape (lower altitude) and those that prevent heat escaping (higher altitude). The Times:

On balance, in today’s climate, clouds cool the earth. Dense, low-lying clouds are responsible for most of that effect, because they reflect considerable sunlight back to space. Many high, thin clouds have the opposite influence, allowing incoming sunshine to pass through but effectively trapping heat that is trying to escape.

Lindzen’s views have been widely criticized, especially among the vast majority of scientists who hold that the climate is warming because of continuing greenhouse gas emissions. Many scientists issue alarming predictions, advocating for immediate action. James Hansen, the godfather of climate change theory, calls for the shutting down of coal plants, the largest source of carbon dioxide emissions. He and others believe that Congress and international bodies should impose a carbon tax, preferably, or cap-and-trade, if that’s the only viable political option. Doing nothing in the face of business as usual guarantees rising sea levels, widespread drought, species destruction, food scarcity, and wild, robust weather events causing billions of dollars of damage and deaths to thousands. Lindzen gives aid and comfort to the do-nothings, further imperiling the planet. The Times:

Ultimately, as the climate continues warming and more data accumulate, it will become obvious how clouds are reacting. But that could take decades, scientists say, and if the answer turns out to be that catastrophe looms, it would most likely be too late. By then, they say, the atmosphere would contain so much carbon dioxide as to make a substantial warming inevitable, and the gas would not return to a normal level for thousands of years.

Yesterday was Arbor Day. This op-ed in today’s New York Times tells us “Why Trees Matter.” They’re kind of miraculous, when you consider their benefits.

This is what I see out my window as I write.

There are lots of trees, although the photo is a bit dark. It gets cloudy up here in the Pacific Northwest. Despite living in “downtown,” I’m surrounded by trees.

The op-ed writer suggests that we should be concerned about the dramatic loss of trees across the planet.

We have underestimated the importance of trees. They are not merely pleasant sources of shade but a potentially major answer to some of our most pressing environmental problems. We take them for granted, but they are a near miracle. In a bit of natural alchemy called photosynthesis, for example, trees turn one of the seemingly most insubstantial things of all — sunlight — into food for insects, wildlife and people, and use it to create shade, beauty and wood for fuel, furniture and homes.

The inhabitants of Easter Island cut down all their trees, which were crucial to human survival. The people died. Imagine being the last person cutting down the last tree on the island. Hey. You needed it for firewood.

Global warming skeptics

April 11, 2012 by davald

William Nordhaus, a Yale economics professor, wrote a piece for the New York Review of Books wherein he pronounced global warming skeptics “wrong.” What prompted Nordhaus was an op-ed that appeared in the Wall Street Journal, which gainsaid the scientific consensus. I offered my two cents here.

The skeptics have focused on the apparent absence of warming over the last several years. Nordhaus includes this chart in his initial essay.

We’ll note that since roughly 1920 temperatures have risen, modulating during WWII, then resuming a steady climb to the beginning of the last decade, when temperatures appeared stable. Nordhaus, correctly I believe, judges a decade’s worth of data misleading. After all, one could take any ten-year span along the above curve and find that temperatures have stabilized or even fallen. What’s important is the long-term trend.

I’ve constructed a chart based on data obtained here.

Again, we could take any 10-year chunk along the curve to “show” that temperatures had cooled. What counts is the trend, which I’ve depicted as a dashed red line.

Consider this chart, which shows temperature anomalies from 1750 to about 2005. The base years are 1951-1980. A temperature change outside the base would be an anomaly. (source)

What strikes me about this chart is the absence of negative anomalies since roughly 1980. From that year forward all anomalies have been positive.

The climate scientists (their day jobs) at realclimate.org recently looked at a 1981 paper by James Hansen et al., published in Science, one of the top two peer-reviewed scientific journals. The scientists draw attention to the prediction of increased warming, despite the fact that temperatures had declined during the period of their research. (At the time, at least one weekly magazine had a cover article on the coming ice age.)

As it happens, actual temperatures were 30 percent higher than Hansen et al. had predicted. Nevertheless, the authors were confident that they had the science correct, which allowed them to make their prediction.

I especially like this concluding paragraph from realclimate:

To conclude, a projection from 1981 for rising temperatures in a major science journal, at a time that the temperature rise was not yet obvious in the observations, has been found to agree well with the observations since then, underestimating the observed trend by about 30%, and easily beating naive predictions of no-change or a linear continuation of trends. It is also a nice example of a statement based on theory that could be falsified and up to now has withstood the test. The “global warming hypothesis” has been developed according to the principles of sound science.

Back to the skeptics. In their reply to Nordhaus’s original essay, they write:

Professor Nordhaus presents two graphs from the IPCC 2007 report that purport to show that without anthropogenic emissions, models successfully simulate the global mean temperature until about 1970 but cannot do so thereafter. This is the basis for the IPCC’s claim that it is likely that most of the warming over the past fifty years is due to man’s emissions. Such a procedure absolutely requires that the model include correctly all other sources of variability. However, the failure of the models to predict the hiatus in warming over the past fifteen years is acknowledged to indicate that this condition has not been met. Furthermore there is the embarrassing fact that the models do not reproduce the 1910–1940 warming, which is nearly identical to the 1970–2000 warming but occurred before man’s emissions became large enough to be considered important.

I would reply by asking the skeptics to explain global warming over the long term. Why have temperatures risen? Natural cycles? Shifting of the earth’s axis? Sun spots? The real climate scientists have factored in a host of such input variables, none of which explain the warming.

It really is physics. Carbon dioxide provides a “blanket” of warmth, without which earth would be a ball of ice. But what happens if we keep adding more blankets? We would expect further warming.

The scientists of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change—some 1,000 or so—acknowledge that the climate is complicated. So much so that their initial reports were heavily qualified. Over time, however, as more data have been accumulated and models further refined, the scientists have become bolder in their predictions. Their latest summary (pdf) said that global warming was “unequivocal.”

Realclimate.org says this about using arbitrary time slices to prove or disprove global warming:

Short term (15 years or less) trends in global temperature are not usefully predictable as a function of current forcings. This means you can’t use such short periods to ‘prove’ that global warming has or hasn’t stopped, or that we are really cooling despite this being the warmest decade in centuries.

So there.
