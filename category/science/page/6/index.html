Carbon tax, continued

February 7, 2013 by davald

Should we tax the things we don’t want or desire less of? The economist Arthur Pigou believed that market transactions often create negative externalities. A good example would be our buying then consuming gasoline. Burning gas yields carbon dioxide, which in sufficient amounts will surely doom the planet. This negative externality is not (yet) incorporated into the price of the commodity; Pigou suggested a tax to rectify the “market failure.” A carbon tax is therefore referred to as a Pigovian Tax.

In a recent post I linked to a Monthly Review cover article on James Hansen’s proposed “fee-and-dividend” approach, a concept introduced by Maria Cantwell and others in the U.S. Senate a few years ago. It went nowhere.

One or more groups in Washington state are pushing a ballot initiative (pdf) that has several of the ingredients of Hansen’s proposal. It begins:

The intent of this initiative measure is to promote sustainable economic development in Washington state by funding transportation investments and tax reductions and by imposing a carbon tax. This measure proposes that fifty percent of the revenue from the carbon tax must be devoted to tax rebates and reductions, including the working families tax exemption, business and occupation tax relief for manufacturers, the high tech research and development tax credit , and property tax relief; and that the remaining fifty percent of the revenue must be devoted to public investments in transit, road maintenance, and freight mobility.

If enacted by the voters, the initiative would impose a tax of $30 per metric ton (2,200 pounds) on all fossil fuels, including coal, gasoline, natural gas, and diesel (carbon dioxide equivalent). The amount of the tax would rise with inflation.

Let’s look at the fuels. Here’s a chart showing carbon dioxide emissions in pounds per gallon burned.

If my arithmetic is correct, a $30/tonne tax on gasoline would add about 27 cents to a gallon (19.6 ÷ 2,200 = 0.009; $30 x 0.009 = $0.267). Assuming a $3.50/gallon price at the pump, the carbon tax would raise the amount to $3.767, a 7.6% bump.

We can imagine the horrified reaction to such an increase. Yet, it wasn’t that long ago that we were all paying over $4/gal. for gasoline. Using Wikipedia data, here is a comparison of retail gasoline prices of different countries.

Of course, the comparison won’t stop the outcry. I guess it would have something to do with our sovereign right to cheap gas, or whatever. But that low price we pay (in the Puget Sound area it’s down to below $3.40/gal. as I write) does not include the negative externalities. There are several: military spending on securing U.S. access to foreign oil; the pernicious effects of sprawl; toxic chemicals; carbon dioxide.

If we paid, say, $8/gallon of gasoline, that would be equivalent to a carbon tax of $500/tonne (again, if my math is correct). The tax would be $4.50/gal., which alone is more than the current U.S. average retail price of about $3.50/gal.

Yet, what the ballot measure proposes is to offset some existing taxes and fund several initiatives, including renewable research and development, manufacturing, and public transportation. Instead of cheap gas, we would get “relief” from property taxes, for example, better roads, and perhaps trains to replace private cars. Also, and most important, is that the Pigovian tax would reduce the consumption of carbons, an altogether good thing.

However, even the modest rate of the initiative may scare too many voters, in which case further actions to reduce carbons may be jeopardized. The very small optimist part of me believes that a comprehensive educational campaign that highlights the benefits of such a tax could enhance citizen support. After all, California now has a cap-and-trade scheme in place. Boulder, Colorado, has a municipal carbon tax as does Montgomery County, Maryland. Why not Washington?

A provocative piece

February 6, 2013 by davald

Apropos of a recent post yesterday’s mailbox contained the latest issue of Monthly Review. The cover article is entitled James Hansen and the Climate-Change Exit Strategy. The author, John Bellamy Foster, is both the editor of the publication and a University of Oregon professor. He takes Hansen’s analysis and comments seriously, arguing that we face an ecological disaster beyond our imagination.

But Foster believes that Hansen’s proffered “fee-and-dividend” proposal to curb carbon consumption will be undermined by corporate profit-takers, led by the fossil-fuel industry. Foster asserts that the underlying cause of climate change, in particular, and pervasive human misery, in general, is capitalism. That’s a very big target, and good luck with that.

At any rate, I encourage you to read the article, then offer your comments here—or anywhere else, for that matter. And one more thing…

It occurs to me that if carbon were to become more expensive, fossil fuels would be less desirous, obviating the presumed need for America’s global military reach. Just a thought.

Modeling falsity

November 29, 2012 by davald

A friend provided me with a copy of a study done of a German prisoner-of-war camp during WWII. For those of you who have seen the movie The Great Escape you have an idea of the bartering that takes place between prisoners and, less frequently, between prisoners and their captors. The author concluded that the introduction of a new commodity in relatively large quantities increased the “price” of the prisoners’ other objects. In other words, the prison experienced inflation.

Most economists hold that increasing the money supply eventually drives up the price of goods and services, including wages. When the Federal Reserve began doing just that following the Great Recession, predictions came fast and furious: we would be soon suffering from hyperinflation. “Zimbabwe” was the example of choice, although Weimar Germany received much attention, too.

Well, inflation did not rise, despite a dramatic explosion of the monetary base. Why not?

Paul Krugman argues that a Keynesian model, one that assumes a constraint in aggregate demand, explains low inflation during “quantitative easing.” Keynes himself upset conventional wisdom when he suggested that people’s unwillingness or, more likely, their inability to buy things would prolong a depression. He created controversy when he said that only a federal government could stimulate the economy during severe recessions. Moreover, under depressed conditions expanding the monetary base would not trigger inflation. John Hicks et al. subsequently encapsulated Keynes’s thinking in a macroeconomIic model called “IS/LM.”

Krugman has not shied away from skewering those who first predicted inflation following the Fed’s actions then held fast to their beliefs and models. On his blog this morning he offers some advice to mistaken colleagues:

So here’s what should have happened: economists propounding these other approaches should have said, “Gosh, I seem to have been wrong. I need to rethink my approach.”

Many, if not most, individuals are loath to abandon hypotheses and models. In the case of macroeconomists who predicted inflation, a common rationale is that “it will happen,” while stopping far short of telling us when. Krugman includes a link to television interview with Peter Schiff, who is challenged on his hyperinflation prediction, going back to 2008. Rather than admitting that he was wrong or that whatever model he may have been relying on was incorrect, he merely replies that if we continue with business as usual (“printing money” and expanding government) then we will see high inflation, upwards of 30 percent or more. And the Russians were always coming.

Thomas Kuhn, in his famous book The Structure of Scientific Revolutions, argued that scientists get comfortable with a “paradigm,” which seems to work—that is, it explains observations. Until, of course, new phenomena case to fit. Rather than jettison their models, however, scientists question the data or the methodology or even the motives of researchers. Kuhn suggested that old models die hard, and that it can take years before a new paradigm finally emerges.

Krugman cites Cullen Roche, who writes the blog Pragmatic Capitalism. I find his approach healthy. In discussing how Shiff and others got it wrong, Roche says:

Anyhow, there’s a real power in being wrong. The quote “there are no mistakes, only lessons” is completely true if you actually live your life that way. I like to say “it’s in being wrong that we learn to be right”. So when I see people being called out for their mistakes (in a respectable and mature way) I think that should generally be applauded because it provides the platform for learning and improvement.

Now there’s an interesting motto: Dare to be wrong! You might actually learn something.

Rising seas and hurricanes

November 1, 2012 by davald

Can we blame Sandy on global warming? Climate scientists hedge their bets. Their answers range from possible to likely, but with a huge degree of uncertainty.

That said, ocean surface temperatures have increased about one degree Fahrenheit over the last 40 years. Sea levels have risen about eight inches since the beginning of the last century. These two statistics, reported in this morning’s New York Times, give some credence to the climate-change argument, though few scientists are willing to finger global warming as Sandy’s cause.

Nevertheless, this excerpt from the Times article should give us pause:

Scientists say most of the rise is a direct consequence of human-induced climate change. Ocean water expands when it warms, accounting for some of the rise, and land ice is melting worldwide, dumping extra water into the ocean. Scientists say they believe the rate will accelerate further, so that the total increase by the end of this century could exceed three feet.

Future Republicans, it seems, will be perfectly happy to go glub, glub under water after they give one more shout out to lower taxes.

The nature of scientific understanding

September 11, 2012 by davald

Okay, not an original title, but given the present state of willful ignorance about all things scientific, some discussion of knowledge, what it is and how we come by it may be in order. The following was prompted by a comment to my recent post on climate change, which featured a link to the National Academy of Sciences and its brief documentary on the causes and effects of global warming.

Wow, a propaganda video, the NAS produced a propaganda video. Thanks for this, its an eye opener.

The comment, in my judgment, reveals volumes about the writer, none of it flattering. The individual, I should imagine, not only rejects the proposition that the planet is warming and that most of the warming is the result of combusting carbon but also believes that science itself is an untrustworthy discipline, populated by self-aggrandizing charlatans bent on securing research dollars in exchange for promulgating known falsehoods. Besides, most scientists are liberals. Of course, I may be wrong about the writer, and that his comment was facetious, though I doubt it.

Unlike most areas of science, global warming and, we could add, evolutionary theory, give rise to controversy. The latter, because life can be explained without god, though natural selection does not, in itself, refute a deity. Fundamentalist Christians, however, would prefer to adhere to a literal interpretation of the Bible, going so far as to believe that the earth is only several thousand years old and that humans were created by a divine being—just as we are today.

Nevertheless, one does not come easily to an understanding of evolutionary theory, which involves a paradigmatic shift in how life moved from the simple to the complex. In particular, we are to abandon teleology—that, for example, a giraffe has a long neck in order to better reach tall-hanging fruit—in favor of random molecular mutations that render an organism more or less likely to survive under a set of conditions. Since Darwin’s concepts may seem counter-intuitive, we may feel compelled to not only reject evolutionary theory but to question science itself.

Global warming presents similar muddles. First of all, it’s very, very complicated. One must be facile in a number of disciplines to understand the science, which involves chemistry, physics, paleontology, geology, and so on. Lacking the necessary expertise, how should we defer to scientists, even as we have no problem deferring to a cardiologist when it comes to matters of the heart? We may explicitly trust the doctor but suspect the scientist, even though the chemist or physicist has greater formal training and experience.

Moreover, the fundamental cause of global warming—humans burning fossil fuels—strikes many as, well, “political.” Some on the religious right, for example, cling to the belief that god will determine the earth’s future, and that man has nothing to do with it. Others, mostly conservative, reject potential remedies for climate change because the strategies almost necessarily require government actions, from setting a carbon tax to removing fossil-fuel subsidies to favoring renewable energy over traditional coal and petroleum and gas.

Ironically, perhaps, science as a discipline is conservative, though its practitioners may be otherwise. In general, we observe the world around us, form hypotheses about how things work, test them against further observations, then ultimately form theories. The progression from hypothesis to theory or, in rare occasions, “law,” takes time, argument, and repeated confirming evidence. Yet, both the hypotheses (models) and theories are tentative and always subject to revision, if not wholesale replacement.

The ancient Greeks believed that the earth was the center of the universe and that the stars and planets revolved around it in perfect circles. When later observations called into question this explanatory scheme of a geocentric universe, attempts were made to produce ever more complicated mathematics to accommodate the perceived planetary perturbations. Eventually, the model was scrapped in favor of a solar system in which earth was just one of several planets orbiting the sun in ellipses, not circles. Likewise, Newton’s notions of gravity and his three laws of motion, which continue to work for the most part, were superseded by Einstein’s theories of relativity and quantum mechanics.

The philosopher David Hume dove into the process of knowing. He produced a troublesome conclusion, what has come to be known as ‘the problem of induction.’ We can approach this through a standard syllogism:

  • If p, then q.
  • p,
  • Therefore q.

Logicians from Aristotle’s time call this form of argument modus ponens: affirming the antecedent (p) permits inference of the consequent (q). For example, if Socrates is a man then he is mortal. Socrates is a man, therefore he is mortal.

But for those looking for causes of events, problems arise, in particular the one of induction. Hume reasoned that if B follows A we cannot conclude that A necessarily causes B. We can only infer a correlation between A and B, and that correlation may obtain in countless observations. This constant correlation permits only a probabilistic inference: B followed A before, so it’s more or less likely to follow in the future.

Bertrand Russell devoted a chapter of his short book The Problems of Philosophy to induction. Russell, ever the wit, wrote:

Domestic animals expect food when they see the person who usually feeds them. We know that all these crude expectations of uniformity are liable to be misleading. The man who has fed the chicken every day throughout its life at last wrings its neck instead, showing that the more refined view as to the uniformity of nature would have been useful to the chicken.

Science, or its study in various guises, is really about induction. In legal terms, we might say “based on the preponderance of evidence, x is true.” The more confirming observations we gather that support the correlation between the consequent and the antecedent the more likely the correlation is true.

However, it is fallacious to infer p from q, but quite easy, in a logical sense, to negate p under the argument form modus tollens:

  • If p, then q.
  • Not-q,
  • Therefore not-p.

Modus tollens is certain. Negate the consequent and the antecedent is also negated, thus destroying the correlation as fact.

Scientific modeling is an attempt to formalize the correlation between p and q. With respect to climate change, a simple model might assume p as ‘an increase in atmospheric carbon dioxide’ and q as ‘an increase in planetary warming.’

We know that the correlation itself was established a century or more ago. Indeed, the Swedish chemist Svante Arrhenius postulated in 1889 that an increase in carbon dioxide emissions would warm the planet. He even calculated the relationship, and that calculation nearly 125 years on still holds—with remarkable precision.

But, if we observed that the planet was not warming, despite the increase in carbon dioxide, then the simple model above would not obtain. We would have encountered modus tollens.

If one were serious about discrediting the science and apparent fact of global warming, then one could set out to discover instances of cooling temperatures. And that’s exactly what some have been doing on several fronts, though unsuccessfully.

We should appreciate that there are huge quantities of temperature data published in the public arena; they are available to one and all. None of the data sets, whether gleaned from satellites or ocean- and land-based thermometers, has so far negated the antecedent. The crude correlation posited above survives an attack on the temperature record. The chart below is based on NASA data. It reflects anomalies in temperature, using the 1951-1980 period as the base.

Well, So what? the skeptic says. I’ll grant you the rising temperatures, but the cause or causes may have nothing to do with increasing carbon dioxide emissions, notwithstanding the established chemistry. The favored alternative seems to be “natural variability.” Since the sun is the source of earth’s external energy inputs, we would expect there to be a correlation between the sun’s output and our planet’s temperatures. But take a look (source):

The geological record (e.g., ice cores) shows that the planet’s temperature has risen and fallen over millions of years. Here is a chart showing variations over the last 400,000 years.

The curve at the top represents CO2 concentrations; the one below, temperature. There would seem to be a correlation, at least visually. But there could be a confounding variable, say changes in the earth’s rotational axis, that could account for variations in both carbon dioxide and temperature.

How might a scientist go about addressing the presence of such a confounding variable? One strategy is to assume no post-1880 carbon emissions. Another is to determine how well current climate models predict the past.

We should understand straightaway that without any atmospheric carbon dioxide the planet would likely be a frozen ball of ice. Carbon dioxide provides a greenhouse effect, a blanket, if you will, that keeps temperatures stable and habitable for planetary species, including us. Here’s an excerpt from NOAA:

Without a natural greenhouse effect, the temperature of the Earth would be about zero degrees F (-18°C) instead of its present 57°F (14°C).

We can intuit that if atmospheric carbon dioxide keeps the planet from freezing, an increase in carbon dioxide would raise temperatures, ceteris paribus.

As for predicting the past, consider this excerpt from skepticalscience.com:

So all models are first tested in a process called Hindcasting. The models used to predict future global warming can accurately map past climate changes. If they get the past right, there is no reason to think their predictions would be wrong. Testing models against the existing instrumental record suggested CO2 must cause global warming, because the models could not simulate what had already happened unless the extra CO2 was added to the model. All other known forcings are adequate in explaining temperature variations prior to the rise in temperature over the last thirty years, while none of them are capable of explaining the rise in the past thirty years. CO2 does explain that rise, and explains it completely without any need for additional, as yet unknown forcings.

Well, not quite, as the previous discussion about induction warns. Repeated observations of B following A does not prove that B will always follow A. The chicken may have reason to believe that he’s going to be fed the same as every other day, until he loses his head one morning.

On the other hand, the virtue of “hind-casting” is that models are tested against already observed data. If a model could not account for, or explain, the known past, then one would have a very good reason to question its reliability in accurately predicting as-yet-unknown events.

Models happen to be crucial in understanding climate—past, present, and future. So, much of the skepticism of global warming science involves modeling, with even noted physicist Freeman Dyson questioning their validity.

To be sure, building models is a complicated, tortuous process, with lots of trial and error, fine tuning, and the like. The challenge is to develop models that “fit” the evidence over time, taking into account a long array of actual and potential factors, including the role of clouds, water vapor, solar cycles, etc. Basically, climate models attempt to answer the question What if? In the present case, what if carbon dioxide emissions increase?

Now all of this I readily confess to being far over my head. I am not a scientist, though I have considerable experience working with fundamental, Newtonian physics and associated mathematics. (I used to be a forensic consultant, among other occupations.) I do have an appreciation, then, for the application of formulas to collected data. I also have profound respect, if not admiration, for scientists of all stripes, including those like Einstein who just sit and think without spending a minute in the lab.

Science, says my dictionary, is

the intellectual and practical activity encompassing the systematic study of the structure and behavior of the physical and natural world through observation and experiment.

The study of climate, in particular the effects of increasing greenhouse gas emissions, was probably at one time on par with studying species of flora and fauna. Interesting to researchers, but ho-hum to the ordinary layperson.

Then came warming. Thermometers around the globe were recording rising temperatures, at first ever so slight. Were the observed increases “normal” and therefore explainable by conventional theories, including axis tilts and solar output variability? The scientists involved stepped carefully, initially concluding that the temperature variations were within expected bounds.

But the planet kept warming. Now the scientists began speaking and writing about “anomalies,” which appeared to be increasing in frequency and magnitude over time.

In the 1980s NASA’s James Hansen addressed Congress. Having done his doctoral paper on the Venusian climate, which is all about the greenhouse effect, Hansen began to focus on earth’s climate, seeing some modest parallels. If greenhouse gases increased, so did temperatures. This happened on Venus, of course. Now it seemed to be happening on our own planet. Hansen issued a warming, which placed him far out on a political, if not also intellectual, limb: if we humans keep burning fossil fuels, the earth’s climate may become inhabitable for many of its species, including homo sapiens.

Meh, came the reply. Yet, temperatures continued to increase, just as Hansen and others before him had predicted.

Still, the scientists hedged. They began tentatively, talking about modest likelihoods. As research continued and models were refined, the likelihoods became bolder. We can see this progression in the reports of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, or IPCC. Today the world’s climate scientists are repeatedly using the term “very likely” to describe future events, including rising temperatures.

In my discussion above, I mentioned probabilities within the context of induction. Here is the IPCC:

Again, the hedging and “uncertainties”—scientists adamantly avoid making statements of the form: X is true, without a doubt. The history of science, replete with even wholesale revisions in understanding, engenders modesty and formal tentativeness. Over the first four IPCC reports, a period of 17 years, the scientists have escalated from “more likely than not” to “very likely.” The trend suggests that the next report will begin to move to the next level or levels of likelihood.

The challenge to those who would question the scientists and their reports looms ever larger. That’s because the evidence accumulates in favor of the proposition that increasing greenhouse gases raises global temperatures. It is becoming more difficult to find the countervailing fact in a modus tollens argument. Nor do alternative explanations for planetary warming pass muster.

To everyone’s benefit, however, the constant, even hysterical, critiques serve a purpose. The have forced the scientists to double-down on their research and model-building. As a result, their products are even more trustworthy, since they are increasingly rigorous in connecting the proliferating dots in anticipation of further assaults.

Yet, I have no doubt that the skeptics and deniers will neither retreat nor wave the white flag. But their criticisms have less and less to do with the science, which is only getting stronger, and more and more to do with politics and religion.

Unfortunately for the fate of the planet, they blather and purvey on fertile ground.

Converging on teachers

September 5, 2012 by davald

Having spent some time trying to teach adolescents I can attest to the challenges confronting those who would presume to educate our nation’s youth. Each classroom presents its own unique problems, among them the broad disparity in skills, curiosity, and self-discipline. Nevertheless, teaching has become a thankless endeavor, as reformers—always the reformers—seek to blame teachers for poor student performance and everything else.

Writing for Salon.org, Paul Tough argues that such focus is not only wrong but counterproductive, given persistently high poverty in America. He offers a dubious syllogism to explain the reformers’ logic:

First, scores on achievement tests in school correlate strongly with life outcomes, no matter what a student’s background. Second, children in low-income homes did much worse on achievement tests than children in middle-income and high-income homes. And third, certain schools, using a very different model than traditional public schools, were able to substantially raise the achievement-test scores of low-income children. The conclusion: if we could replicate on a big, national scale the accomplishments of those schools, we could make a huge dent in poverty’s impact on children’s success.

Against this argument Tough cites research linking poverty with arrested intellectual and, I would add, emotional development. Simply put, poor kids struggle with learning to a much higher degree than privileged pupils. Thus, placing the onus on teachers for reducing or eliminating poverty is doomed to failure.

Tough asks why no one talks about poverty anymore. It used to be front and center in the national dialogue, especially during the Kennedy and Johnson administrations. The Republicans mention the poor only to blame them for their own misfortunes. Tough:

Where the typical conservative argument on poverty falls short is that it often stops right there: Character matters … and that’s it. There’s not much society can do until poor people shape up and somehow develop better character. In the meantime, the rest of us are off the hook. We can lecture poor people, and we can punish them if they don’t behave the way we tell them to, but that’s where our responsibility ends.

Democrats have joined their conservative counterparts in pushing reform by accountability, embracing “value-added” performance criteria, as if developing better teachers were only a matter of data. Collect it. Analyze it. Act on it. Today it’s “race to the top,” which exacerbates the flawed approaches in No Child Left Behind.

But, again, both political parties and a host of educational theorists and pundits completely ignore the elephant in the room: poverty. The percentage of Americans who are poor reached 15.1 in 2010 and is expected to be almost 16 percent this year. These are the highest levels since the early 60s.

 

Several years ago I painstakingly combed through data from the Washington State Office of the Superintendent of Public Instruction, or OSPI for short. I correlated the results of standardized test scores (it was called the WASL back then) with the percentage of students receiving free or reduced lunch, a proxy for poverty. The conclusion was obvious: the higher the poverty rate the lower the test scores.

I compared two schools: Shoultes Elementary in Marysville, Wash., and Mercer Island Elementary. Here is a chart showing fourth grade math scores on the WASL:

 

Now let’s look at poverty rates, again using the free-or-reduced lunch as proxy:

 

What a glaring contrast. Mercer Island’s poverty rate is so low as to barely register on the graph, whereas the Marysville school’s is above fifty percent as of 2010.

It would seem that the answer to improving academic performance is to make kids wealthier. But reformers mistakenly put it backwards: wealth follows higher test scores. Author Tough:

So when we’re looking for root causes of poverty-related underachievement, why do we tend to focus on the wrong culprits and ignore the ones that science tells us do the most damage?

Tough suggests that the science is difficult to understand and not widely publicized. He also believes that we’re uncomfortable talking about parenting, and that good parenting skills tend to be more prevalent in wealthier households and neighborhoods. Finally, Tough agrees that character matters. However, he adds, those traits that lend themselves to higher academic performance (e.g., perseverance and optimism) are teachable themselves and not the product of genetic code.

As I’ve written about Finland’s education system, Finnish students with special needs—and nearly all students have them—receive special attention, including free meals, free health care, and targeted intervention. It helps, of course, that the Finns are dedicated to social justice and generous welfare for all. Less than four percent of the population lives in poverty, although, again, being poor does not deny Finns access to health care or a quality education.

Diane Ravitch, who was a big proponent of the NCLB law, did an about-face. She now condemns it and Obama’s Race to the Top. She writes:

Here’s an interesting contrast: We claim to be preparing students for global competitiveness, and we reward mastery of basic skills. Our guiding principles: Competition, accountability, and choice. Finland has this singular goal: to develop the humanity of each child. Isn’t that a shocking goal? Their guiding principles: equity, creativity, and prosperity.

Finland rightly deserves attention today as a nation that treats its children as a precious resource and that honors the adults who make education their passion and their career. Someday, I hope, we will recognize the failure of the behaviorist approach now in vogue; someday we will see that our current “reforms” are appropriate for the industrial era of the early 20th century, not for the needs of the 21st century. When that day arrives, we will understand the deep wisdom of Finland, with its love for children and its respect for educators, and we will be grateful that there is a successful alternative to our own failed model.

Hmm. What a novel idea: “develop the humanity of each child.” Education as if children mattered.

The crazy House

August 24, 2012 by davald

A reading of Timothy Egan’s column both informs and entertains. Forget about comedy writers. We’ve got real, live Republicans who provide all the humor necessary to split your gut. I refer you to Egan’s entire essay, but a few excerpts will surely entice.

The tutorial in 8th grade biology that Republicans got after one of their members of Congress went public with something from the wackosphere was instructive, and not just because it offered female anatomy lessons to those who get their science from the Bible.

Take a look around key committees of the House and you’ll find a governing body stocked with crackpots whose views on major issues are as removed from reality as Missouri’s Representative Todd Akin’s take on the sperm-killing powers of a woman who’s been raped.

But Mr. Akin will not go quietly. He’s determined to stay in the race for his state’s U.S. Senate seat. His fellow travelers would prefer that he withdraw for the good of the party, even though his fatuous opinions inform the GOP platform unveiled in Tampa.

On global warming:

We’re currently experiencing the worst drought in 60 years, a siege of wildfires, and the hottest temperatures since records were kept.  But to Republicans in Congress, it’s all a big hoax. The chairman of a subcommittee that oversees issues related to climate change, Representative John Shimkus of Illinois is —  you guessed it  — a climate-change denier.

At a 2009 hearing, Shimkus said not to worry about a fatally dyspeptic planet: the biblical signs have yet to properly align. “The earth will end only when God declares it to be over,” he said, and then he went on to quote Genesis at some length.  It’s worth repeating: This guy is the chairman.

And speaking of Genesis, Egan cites a recent Gallup poll that shows 58 percent of Republicans “believe God created humans in the present form just within the last 10,000 years —  a wealth of anthropological evidence to the contrary.” How could I even have a conversation with these people?

It seems that some members of the GOP don’t appreciate what’s become of their party. Egan brings in Joe Scarborough:

And in an on-air plea for sanity, Joe Scarborough, the former G.O.P. congressman and MSNBC host, said, “I’m just tired of the Republican Party being the stupid party.”  I feel for him.  But don’t expect the reality chorus to grow. For if intelligence were contagious, his party would be giving out vaccines for it.

I regret the refrain, so necessary, however: Only in America.
