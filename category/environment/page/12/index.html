The downside of growing up

February 7, 2012 by davald

Recessions spur our appreciation for economic growth. We don’t have any right now, or at least not very much. To boost employment, the economy must grow. In our case, and in most industrial nations, growth depends heavily on people buying things. But making and consuming and eventually throwing away stuff, however necessary for our current well-being, jeopardizes the long-time health and happiness of the planet and its inhabitants.

We’ve seen these charts before, depicting the earth’s carrying capacity, or biocapacity, along with ecological footprint, typically defined as the total of: energy production and consumption, which includes resource depletion; and waste disposal, including plain old garbage and harmful emissions.

As it turns out, and this has been going on since the mid-70s, we’ve become too big for our planetary britches. About 40 years ago the total of human activity exceeded the planet’s ability to sustain us and other species. Something has to give. (data source)

Millions of the world’s inhabitants are paying the negative externalities of energy-intensive economic growth. The payments are in the form of disease, malnutrition, dehydration, and death—in addition to a downright shitty existence. Because the gap between footprint and capacity is widening, we have guaranteed that future generations of species, including our own, will continue to suffer, perhaps at greater and greater rates.

Each day we unconsciously make a Hobson’s choice between growth and sustainability. And we almost always choose the former, perhaps because we have to in the short term. Nor can the countercultural choice of the individual for the latter overcome the aggregate choice for the former. It’s not easy being totally, completely, and globally green. Indeed, we’ve so far found it impossible.

Such a legacy.

Dancing with the cars and other matter

February 2, 2012 by davald

Each day I pound the pavements of downtown Everett, the cigarette and dog shit capital of the Northwest. And each day I have to contend with jerks behind wheels.

While most motorists are gracious and accommodating, too many would prefer that I become instant road kill. It’s as if they’re saying to me, “Get the f**k out of my way, you stupid pedestrian!” Why bipedal should be considered so escapes me. But who am I to challenge two tons of rolling steel?

In between the invariable encounters with prickly drivers I breathe smoke, cigarette smoke. It seems that everyone who works downtown is addicted to cancer sticks. This is one downside to the law that prohibits smoking inside buildings. The nicotiners nearly explode out of their offices at break times to light one up; and since they have to get the whole thing down to the butt in a handful of minutes, they suck on these things as if gasping for the last molecule of air. Also, they tend to congregate, thereby polluting a good chunk of real estate.

As for the other salient descriptor, there is no walk free of negotiating turds. These canine piles and droplets force my eyes forever downward, since there’s nothing worse than removing my shoes and having to smell shit that I have inadvertently collected on the soles.

My guess is that the hound owners who allow their pets to poop on the public rights of way are also the motorists who mentally curse me every morning.

There. I feel better now.

Federal subsidies

February 1, 2012 by davald

The almost certain demise of electric vehicle manufacturer Think made the pages of both the Everett Herald and Seattle Times this morning. The article tells us that the government’s pushing this or that technology is no guarantee of success, as we discovered with Solyndra.

For politicians betting on electric vehicles to drive job growth, the view from inside Think City’s plant here is their worst nightmare: 100 unfinished vehicles lined up with no word whether they will be completed.

The vehicles, two of which could fit into a parking space, were to cost $40,000. That’s a tough sell in any economy, but especially so under current conditions.

Conservatives generally loathe subsidies for at least three reasons:

  1. they distort the market;
  2. governments can make bad bets; and
  3. the government shouldn’t be in the business of picking winners and losers, which is a variation on the first.

For politicians, however, bringing home the bacon is the staple of electability. And it’s a gift that keeps on giving. The senator or congressperson gets elected in the first place. He or she somehow manages to secure federal dollars for this or that project. The folks back home love bacon, so they re-elect the politician—and so on. One can appreciate that such a cycle improves constituency fortunes if the politician remains in office; the longer the better.

We can liken the subsidy process to a giant punch bowl filled with a favorite liquid. All of us contribute to the supply. But there are only so many straws, and some straws have larger diameters than others. The trick for politicians is to grab the bigger straw to siphon off more of the liquid—that is, subsidies.

I happen to be in the energy business, as an elected commissioner for the Snohomish County PUD. Our board adopted a rather simple and straightforward policy on energy resources: Acquire as much conservation as financially and technically feasible, then make up the difference with renewable resources, preferably developed and operated by the utility in our own backyard. Thus, we’re exploring both tidal and geothermal energy. We’ve purchased an extensive portfolio of wind power, and we’ve been pushing solar energy as well.

Regardless of how one feels about the broader issue of government subsidies, as long as they are in play, the PUD would be derelict in its duty to customers if it refused them. So the utility has applied for and received millions of dollars from the federal government for out smart grid initiative and our tidal and geothermal explorations.

But as a private citizen, I’m not so sure about subsidies, for the reasons mentioned above. I’ll discuss my views within the context of energy policy, since that’s in my bailiwick.

The Energy Information Agency publishes data (pdf) on energy subsidies. In 2010, the Department of Energy doled out $37.2 billion to the energy sector; and, yes, the PUD is among them. The End-Use category is mostly for low-income citizens through LIHEAP.

Each of these expenditures were no doubt the product of commercial interests and bringing home the bacon. Of the $37.2 billion total, $14.8 billion came by way of ARRA, the stimulus package—conservation and renewables (mostly wind) comprising 84% of that. With the expiration of ARRA, it should follow that the subsidy total will decline. Indeed, in fiscal year 2007 the total amount was $17.9 billion.

We can appreciate that the environmental community supports renewable and conservation resources, but objects to subsidies for fossil fuels and likely nuclear, though for different reasons. The politicians representing coal states prefer that subsidy continue; likewise with nuclear and natural gas.

What would the energy sector look like without the subsidies? I should think that coal and other fossil fuels would hold their own; renewables not so much. Coal is cheap and abundant. Solar and wind, while abundant, are more expensive to harness and transmit. Without subsidies and resource portfolio standards renewable resources cannot compete with fossil fuels on cost alone.

As someone concerned about climate change, I support the development of renewable resources and energy efficiency. Those who care not a whit about global warming may prefer that renewable subsidies go away.

However, there is another factor that distorts markets. The total social cost of resources is not reflected in their retail price. Fossil fuels, in addition to their greenhouse gas emissions, cause all sorts of health problems because of their particulates and toxic chemicals. Long-established hydropower projects adversely impact fish and wildlife, though I should add that BPA customers pay handsomely for salmon mitigation measures.

Without wading into the political challenges, suppose we could determine the externality costs of different resources. Then those costs are embedded in the price utilities and consumers pay. Without subsidies across the board, what resources would be preferred in a competitive market?

Certainly fossil-fuel resources would become more expensive. How much more? Various studies have estimated coal’s externality costs, and the estimates range from $10 an emitted ton of CO2 to as much as $100 or more. Ignoring greenhouse gases for a moment, coal-burning damages the environment and animal species, including humans.

According to the National Research Council report, in 2005 the total annual external damages from sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, and particulate matter created by burning coal at 406 coal-fired power plants (which produce 95 percent of the nation’s coal-generated electricity), were about $62 billion.

A Harvard study found those costs to be much higher. The study found that at the low end the costs were $345 billion; at the high end $523 billion.

“Our comprehensive review finds that the best estimate for the total economically quantifiable costs, based on a conservative weighting of many of the study findings, amount to some $345.3 billion, adding close to 17.8¢/kWh of electricity generated from coal. The low estimate is $175 billion, or over 9¢/kWh, while the true monetizable costs could be as much as the upper bounds of $523.3 billion, adding close to 26.89¢/kWh. These and the more difficult to quantify externalities are borne by the general public.” The average residential price of electricity at the time of the report is 12¢/kWh.”

Yet the study authors suggest that even these costs do not represent the total social costs of coal.

“Still these figures do not represent the full societal and environmental burden of coal. In quantifying the damages, we have omitted the impacts of toxic chemicals and heavy metals on ecological systems and diverse plants and animals; some ill-health endpoints (morbidity) aside from mortality related to air pollutants released through coal combustion that are still not captured; the direct risks and hazards posed by coal sludge, coal slurry, and coal waste impoundments; the full contributions of nitrogen deposition to eutrophication of fresh and coastal sea water; the prolonged impacts of acid rain and acid mine drainage; many of the long-term impacts on the physical and mental health of those living in coal-field regions and nearby MTR sites; some of the health impacts and climate forcing due to increased tropospheric ozone formation; and the full assessment of impacts due to an increasingly unstable climate.”

Coal-burning plants generate about half of the nation’s electricity. Should the price of coal be raised to reflect the externalities, millions of people would experience much higher electricity bills—at least in the short run.

Over time, the market would adjust to the price differences to deliver cheaper alternatives, which would be renewable resources and conservation.* Their externality costs would be at or near zero.

Nevertheless, both conservatives and liberals should be willing to kill subsidies and devise a suitable mechanism to embed total social costs in the price of all resources, preferably by incremental steps over time. Then let the market do its thing.

How conservative of me.

___________

*  Actually, conservation is already and has long been the cheapest available resource. The reason utilities, especially investor-owned, stop far short of maximizing their conservation potential is that, by definition, conservation measures reduce consumption. Lower consumption, all other things being equal, reduces revenues. Since IOUs are in the business of making profits (unlike consumer-owned utilities like the PUD), shareholders balk at doing more conservation.

You want models?

January 13, 2012 by davald

Whether or not our political leaders take global warming seriously (and they’re not), global warming continues, mostly as a result of combusting coal. But there’s another specter threatening under seas and tundra: methane “burps.”

The climate researchers tending to the blog site Real Climate have given us an interactive model. We get to put in some numbers, push the calculate button, and, voilà, instant graphs.

CH4, methane, is an especially pernicious greenhouse gas, over 20 times more potent than CO2. Huge pockets of the gas are trapped under frozen tundra and cold seabeds. But as the climate warms, the tundra thaws—same with the oceans. The resulting burps promise further catastrophe.

Have a nice day.

I feel a little bit better

January 13, 2012 by davald

Several weeks ago I started writing a post that was pure speculation, and it was about Apple, the company. It had occurred to me, without much evidence at all, that Tim Cook, who replaced Steve Jobs, would move differently on three fronts: employee relations, supplier labor, and the environment.

Having attended Michael Daisey’s one-man show, The Agony and Ecstasy of Steve Jobs, I was appalled by the working conditions at Foxconn, Apple’s principal supplier of iPhones and iPads. Nor was Apple faring well within the environmental community. Greenpeace, in particular, gave the company black marks for its production processes.  I believed then that Jobs could easily afford a kinder, gentler Apple, one that respected both labor and the environment.

Well, my speculation about Tim Cook may have been correct. He just sent an email to employees announcing significant changes. Among the highlights:

  • the release of a supplier responsibility report, which provided details on over 200 audits of supplier processes and policies, in particular the use of child labor (Daisey’s revealed that young teenagers were employed by Foxconn to assemble by hand hundreds of iPhones per day; after they developed disabling injuries from repetitive tasks, they were discarded then replaced with another batch.);
  • Apple has just joined the Fair Labor Association, the first technology company to do so;
  • Tim Cook is pushing for a “greener” Apple.

However, Tim Cook is no Steve Jobs, and here I mean that as a compliment—with respect to how he relates to employees. He comes across as modest, unassuming, and gracious; traits absent in Jobs. Almost immediately after assuming the reins, Cook told Apple employees that the company would match their charitable giving. From Walter Isaacson’s biography, we learned that Jobs was prickly at best on the topic of philanthropy, shunning invitations by Bill Gates and Warren Buffet to join their endeavors.

It’s about time, Apple. Now keep it up.

In the West, most people live in sprawling urban centers not far from the public-land playgrounds that shape so many lives on the sunset side of the United States. When the worst of our world spills over into that other one, it is people like Anderson who hold the line.

We are (probably) not alone

January 11, 2012 by davald

Assume that the standard cosmological model is correct, that everything there is began as a singular point just before the Big Bang. It would seem to follow that matter and physical processes are homogeneous throughout the universe. That is not to say that everything looks, feels, or behaves the same. Obviously, one of the defining characteristics of this great expanse is diversity. From essential building blocks emerge incredible variation.

We inhabit a small speck of a planet in the Milky Way galaxy. Astronomers estimate that it is home to over 100 billion stars, including our sun, around which several planets revolve. Again, assuming homogeneity, we would expect that other stars are surrounded by planets.

It should not surprise us, then, that there are likely more planets than there are stars. The New York Times:

The more astronomers look for other worlds, the more they find that it’s a crowded and crazy cosmos. They think planets easily outnumber stars in our galaxy and they’re even finding them in the strangest of places.

A rather whimsical essay appeared as the Everett Herald‘s editorial this morning, “Why ET isn’t phoning us.” The writer, I presume Bob Bolerjack, the editorial page editor, reacts to a couple of observations proffered by columnist Charles Krauthammer and physicist Stephen Hawking, who just turned 70.

Krauthammer wonders why we haven’t heard from extraterrestrial counterparts.

As we inevitably find more and more exo-planets where intelligent life can exist, why have we found no evidence — no signals, no radio waves — that intelligent life does exist?

“So why the silence?” Krauthammer asks. “Carl Sagan (among others) thought that the answer is to be found, tragically, in the final variable: the high probability that advanced civilizations destroy themselves.”

Hawking, too, fears that homo sapiens are making a muck of their planet. Time to colonize other terrestrial bodies, he advises.

Which reminds me of an answer given by a parish priest, who would marry my wife and me. Back then (I don’t know how things go now) couples needed to confer with a priest prior to their wedding, evidently to ensure that we were worthy Catholics. At any rate, the subject of birth control inevitably surfaced. The Church, of course, forbids all forms of contraception, save for coitus interruptus. I posed the question: Well, what about over-population? Without hesitating the priest replied, “We’ll colonize the moon.” He preceded Hawking by almost 50 years.

The Herald editorial concludes:

Perhaps it will occur to Krauthammer and Hawking that there is, in fact, intelligent life out there and they have determined the smart thing to do is to stay the heck away from humans and the earth they are apparently destroying, and will one day seek to escape. With eyes on colonizing someone else’s earth-like planet.

Who exactly would want to befriend us?

Indeed.

Good luck with this, Bay Area

January 3, 2012 by davald

I grew up in the Bay Area, specifically Concord, Calif. (Mention of this typically invokes puzzlement. But when I say that my hometown neighbors Walnut Creek the mental lights return.) The eastern part of this region is quintessential sprawl. Over the last 60 years massive housing tracts have gobbled up agricultural land. They are interconnected by thousands of miles of asphalt, enabling monumental traffic jams each weekday morning and evening as mindless workers take the journey from their garages to San Francisco offices or blue-collar venues along the waterfronts, then return at the end of the day. And the days are long, with commute times easily exceeding one hour each direction.

You can appreciate that this haphazard arrangement between suburban sleep and metropolitan employment wastes time, money, energy, and the environment. There’s got to be a better way.

To the rescue, however too little or too late, is a new initiative called Bay Area One. As this article suggests the challenge is “daunting.” That ostensible challenge is to reduce transportation-related carbon emissions. The trick is to build up rather than out, curbing reliance on private automobiles, a remarkably inefficient means to get from A to B.

Another 2 million residents are expected to live in the Bay Area by 2035, with most of them getting around by automobile, the source of an estimated 41 percent of greenhouse gas emissions in the region.

Planners say they want to steer new development toward public transit corridors and stations and ease reliance on the auto.

Bay Area One is a joint effort of the Metropolitan Transportation Commission and the Association of Bay Area Governments. Neither possesses the authority to direct new patterns of urban development. But they do hold the purse strings. Judicious distribution of transportation dollars, it is hoped, will lead to increased population density situated near and around public transit hubs.

Not everyone is on board. Opposition comes from—who else?—real estate and home-building entities.

“They base their utopian model on high-density housing with shops underneath, no parking, but a lot of cycling and walking,” said Heather Gass, an Alamo real estate saleswoman, in a blog post critical of the growth plan. “What these people don’t seem to understand is that people move to the suburbs to get away from this type of urban lifestyle.”

Invoking the Big Brother specter, Gass adds: “At least the Soviet Union’s big plans were for five years. This plan will transform our area for 25 years.”

These are the same folks who fought Washington state’s Growth Management Act, which attempts to confine population growth to urban centers. If you find yourself on I-5 trying to make your way to Seattle you can reach your own conclusion as to the Act’s success.

We still have suburban tracts, but they tend to be smaller than before. The housing industry has managed to create greater density, but not in the cities so much as in the suburbs—bigger houses on tinier lots. We still lack adequate public transportation; so there is no viable alternative to the automobile—thus, more cars and traffic congestion. Nice try, Olympia.

The Bay Area has B.A.R.T., a rapid transit system that alleviates some highway congestion. But it’s hardly adequate, as the following anecdote underscores.

My brother in law was a San Francisco cop who lived in Antioch, about 50 miles from Baghdad by the Bay. As a police officer he was able to ride B.A.R.T. for free. However, to get a seat he drove his car into Concord, where he caught an outbound train. When it arrived in Antioch, the terminus, he simply remained for the trip into the city.

There is always a chicken-egg syndrome in new urbanism. Attracting existing or would-be suburbanites into downtowns—like Everett, where I currently reside—requires amenities (e.g., entertainment, restaurants, public safety). But the amenities can only be built and sustained with more city dwellers. Who goes first?

Neither cities nor suburbs were deliberately planned; they evolved. Seattle, for example, began as a shipping port, since it was on the water and proximate to raw materials—lumber, for the most part (“skid row“). The growth of suburbia coincided with the Eisenhower-era federal highway act and its thousands of miles of cement corridors. As it became more expensive and probably dirtier and scarier to live in cities, young couples sought a cheaper and cleaner alternative to raise families. Since land cost less as one got farther from the city, suburbia sprouted on dirt once farmed. (At the same time agriculture became more efficient requiring less acreage, which freed up large, easily convertible areas for houses and roads.)

Now many of us recognize that suburbia is unsustainable—too many carbons. And those carbons are becoming ever more costly at the pump and to the environment.

But old habits die hard. Moreover, there are substantial financial interests wedded to the status quo (e.g., oil and car companies) along with new generations pursuing their “American dream.”

My guess, hardly sanguine, is that even if government efforts to change our inherited arrangement succeed they will be too little and much too late. Our whole economy runs on oil, and oil is our doom.

Another punished good deed

December 30, 2011 by davald

Those of us not living under a rock these days can attest to the utter failure of Congress to act responsibly on climate change. Hell, an entire party rejects the science and many in their rank call global warming a “hoax,” perpetrated to attract government subsidies. Shame on Jim Hansen.

So, it is left to the states to tackle the problems. And that’s precisely what California is trying to do, boasting the nation’s best conservation programs and now trying to reduce the carbon content of transportation fuels. But a judge won’t let them. The San Francisco Chronicle:

A federal judge on Thursday blocked California from enforcing its first-in-the-nation mandate for cleaner, low-carbon fuels, saying the rules favor biofuels produced in the state.

Out-of-state entities, including biofuel producers, sued California charging that the law violates the Interstate Commerce clause by giving preference to in-state refiners.

California’s rules for fuels that comply with the law assign a 10 percent advantage to ethanol produced in the state. State officials said the preference was justified because the transportation of ethanol from other states produces climate-changing greenhouse gases, and because California producers have more access than those in the Midwest to low-emission hydroelectric and nuclear power.

Should this ruling stand (California may appeal to the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals) it may have repercussions in Washington state. In 2005 Olympia passed SB 5111, which provides a tax credit for solar equipment manufactured in the state.

One company making such equipment is Outback Power, which is located near the Arlington airport. Itek Energy is another.

I am unaware of challenges to the Washington law from, say, out-of-state manufacturers of solar equipment. The California judge’s ruling may spark one.

Stay tuned.

We’ll save lives, but will it stand?

December 22, 2011 by davald

E.P.A. head Lisa Jackson announced yesterday that electric-generating plants will have to substantially reduce mercury emissions and other toxins. The Times:

The new rule, unless blocked by Congress or the courts, will be the first time the federal government has enforced limits on mercury, arsenic, acid gases and other poisonous and carcinogenic chemicals emitted by the burning of fossil fuels.

Mercury is bad stuff. It kills thousands of people a year, although you won’t find ‘mercury’ on the toe tag. That it took 20 years to finally get the regulation in place attests to the strength of financial interests vis-a-vis clear scientific evidence that mercury is not good for your health.

The biggest source of mercury, however, is coal combustion. About half of the electricity produced in the U.S. comes from coal-burning plants. Although the new regulation will not force closure of the plants it will require utilities and independent power producers to install scrubbers and other devices to prevent the mercury from entering the atmosphere. Here’s Wikipedia:

Mercury is a highly toxic element commonly found in coal and municipal waste. Wet scrubbers are only effective for removal of soluble mercury species, such as oxidized mercury, Hg2+. Mercury vapor in its elemental form, Hg0, is insoluble in the scrubber slurry and not removed. Therefore, additional process of Hg0conversion is required to complete mercury capture. Usually addition of the halogens to the flue gas are used for this purpose. The type of coal burned as well as the presence of a selective catalytic reduction unit both affect the ratio of elemental to oxidized mercury in the flue gas and thus the degree to which the mercury is removed.

Burning coal, a relatively cheap way to generate electricity, will now get more expensive, though not so much as to render the plants economically inviable. Nevertheless, there are more than 600 coal plants in the United States and both their owners and endusers wield bipartisan political influence. So, it remains to be seen whether or not E.P.A.’s new regulation will survive.

Of course, for the good of the planet and its numerous species all coal plants should be terminated. It’s going to get too damn hot if they’re not.
