Low-carbon fuel standards and tar sands

December 15, 2011 by davald

Several states have either adopted or have proposed a low-carbon fuel standard, Washington being among the latter. The most comprehensive is California’s, whose goal is to reduce upstream carbon emissions, all the way to the extraction process. Transportation is the second largest source of carbon emissions, behind electricity generation (coal and natural gas).

Our neighbors to the north discovered a huge volume of oil trapped in Alberta tar sands. The process to separate the oil from the sands is every bit as pernicious as the ultimate release of vehicle carbon emissions. Big Oil and the Canadian government want to build a pipeline to pump the separated oil to the lower United States, where it would be refined for vehicle use. For those of us concerned with climate change the pipeline poses a very serious threat. It would facilitate the combustion of more carbons, which will be trapped by the atmosphere and contribute to planetary warming.

The extraction, transportation, and refinement of the Alberta oil requires a large capital investment. But the returns are even greater. Thus, there are significant political forces arrayed to fight carbon-reduction laws. Indeed, should a sufficient number of states adopt low-carbon fuel standards, the market for Canadian oil would be sharply curtailed or even disappear.

Salon.com has the story on how the Canadian embassy and U.S. lobbyists conspired to thwart low-carbon standards. The online magazine obtained hundreds of pages worth of emails chronicling the joint efforts.

When President Barack Obama decided in early November to delay a decision on TransCanada’s Keystone XL pipeline until after the next election, America’s environmental movement celebrated one of its biggest victories in recent memory. And no doubt the news came as a blow to Alberta’s tar sands industry, and to Canada’s oft-stated dream of becoming the next global energy superpower.

But behind activists’ jubilation lurked a somber reality, an untold story with much wider implications. The broader fight to reform Alberta’s tar sands, the one which actually stood a chance of breaking America’s addiction to the continent’s most polluting road fuel, has been quietly abandoned over the past several years. For that we can thank the planet’s richest oil companies and their Canadian government allies, who’ve together waged a stealthy war against President Obama’s climate change ambitions.

Adding insult to injury Canada’s recently re-elected conservative government just announced its withdrawal from the Kyoto Accords. Of course, the U.S. refused to become a signatory in the first place.

None of this bodes well for the future.

This isn’t good

December 8, 2011 by davald

You know all those batteries we use in all our gadgets, the ones we’re not supposed to throw in the trash for fear of wrecking the environment and the animals who inhabit it? Well, if you’re one of the conscientious citizens, dutifully discarding your used AAs and AAAs, you’re not going to like this bit of news.

From the New York Times:

 The spent batteries Americans turn in for recycling are increasingly being sent to Mexico, where their lead is often extracted by crude methods that are illegal in the United States, exposing plant workers and local residents to dangerous levels of a toxic metal.

The rising flow of batteries is a result of strict new Environmental Protection Agency standards on lead pollution, which make domestic recycling more difficult and expensive, but do not prohibit companies from exporting the work and the danger to countries where standards are low and enforcement is lax.

It’s like a game of whack-a-mole. We think we’re addressing a problem here, only to find that it pops up over there. The difference in this case: we Americans aren’t directly harmed if our old batteries wind up in Mexico; but the Mexicans are. For some numbers, the Times reports:

An analysis of trade statistics by The New York Times shows that nearly 20 percent of spent American vehicle and industrial batteries are now exported to Mexico, up from 6 percent in 2007. About 20 million such batteries will cross the border this year, according to United States trade statistics, and that does not take into account batteries smuggled in as mislabeled metal scrap or second-hand goods. In September, more than 60 18-wheelers full of old batteries crossed the border each day, American trade records show.

Spent batteries house up to 40 pounds of lead, a highly toxic metal that exposure can cause high blood pressure, kidney damage and abdominal pain in adults, and serious developmental delays and behavioral problems in young children because it interferes with neurologic development. When batteries are broken for recycling, the lead is released as dust and, during melting, as lead-laced emissions.

No good deed goes unpunished. Oh, and here’s another interesting tidbit from the article. The lead recovered by Mexican workers from the recycled batteries is sent to China, which uses it in the manufacture of products that we buy, no doubt requiring more batteries. A very vicious circle, it seems.

Too many stilettos

December 6, 2011 by davald

I’ll hazard an easy guess. Most people concerned about the environment and the planet’s species accept as a given that increasing carbon dioxide emissions will cause the earth to warm. Moreover, they acknowledge that the bulk of those emissions are the result of human activity.

Pretty safe conjecture, right?

Moreover, these same individuals support in a broad sense efforts to curtail greenhouse gases. They advocate energy efficiency; renewable, rather than fossil-fuel, resources; and strategies to curb tailpipe emissions.

Now comes the more complicated part. Many of these same people realize their environmental sentiments via specific, narrow agendas.

For example, the Snohomish County PUD recently received a long-term renewal of its license from the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission to operate the Jackson hydroelectric project. That process lasted a full decade. Yep. Ten years. The utility spent millions of dollars to study, study, and study some more. The data collection resulted in thousands of pages of documents. Yesterday the Board of Commissioners recognized the internal PUD team that worked, worked, and worked some more to achieve a successful conclusion.

In this lengthy process PUD staff met with, listened to, and incorporated the views and opinions of 28 stakeholders, including the Tribes. The utility reached settlements with all of them in furtherance of the re-licensing process.

Among the stakeholders were individuals and groups concerned about: hydro projects in general; whitewater rafting and canoeing; fish and wildlife preservation; and outdoor recreation. I have not exhausted the list.

Again, I think it safe to assume that all of the stakeholders appreciate the science and implications of global warming. So does the PUD board. Which is one reason that we’re busy developing renewable resources in our own service territory, from tidal power and geothermal to small hydro and solar. Conceptually, the stakeholders and most of the utility’s customers support the PUD’s resource strategies.

Yet, with respect to individual projects, the organizational stilettos come a-poking. In a variety of ways, the PUD is told that it shouldn’t build the low-impact hydro here, or it shouldn’t disturb potential nesting habitat here, or it shouldn’t locate a tidal-power turbine there.

I hasten to add that I do not denigrate the concerns, which if not scientifically grounded are nevertheless sincere. The PUD is busy constraining its ecological footprint as it conserves electricity and develops or buys the output from non-carbon generating facilities. We get it. I, for one, would hope that the stilettos would step back from their myriad micro-causes to gain a broader understanding how the utility should behave in the face of climate change.

Here’s a particular example, and it involves the PUD’s efforts to explore and perhaps develop tidal power in Admiralty Inlet. The utility has invested a lot of ratepayer dollars and received significant grants from the federal government. The Department of Energy strongly supports the tidal project; the PUD is at the forefront of this pioneering technology, which holds substantial promise to meet growing electricity demand. After all, there’s lots of water along the west side of the utility’s service territory, which includes Camano Island. And most of the planet is covered with water that rises and falls in predictable patterns.

The PUD is working closely with the University of Washington to study the project site, paying close attention to the tides, of course, but also marine animals and their movements. We are in the design phases of an initial pilot project. Provided we receive a preliminary license, we’ll place two specially designed turbines on the floor of the inlet. Each will be equipped with sophisticated monitoring devices, including stereo cameras. The PUD, UW, and the various governmental agencies want to find out if tidal power is feasible with respect to costs and the environment.

I emphasize that we’re presently preparing for a small-scale pilot project. Even if all goes well, the utility wouldn’t begin commercial operation for several years. That would entail several more turbines on the bed of Puget Sound.

However, the utility finds itself stymied by a staff member of the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, specifically its National Marine Fisheries Service. Keep in mind that the overarching purpose of the pilot project is to collect data. But here’s the problem: the staffer wants the utility to immediately shut down the two turbines should an orca whale appear in the vicinity. To the PUD and its several project partners, this is unacceptable. Besides, it’s counterproductive.

How will marine species interact with the turbines? That’s one thing the PUD wants to find out. While we seriously doubt that the turbines pose any risk to orcas and other marine animals, we really won’t know for sure until we conduct our studies. But we can’t conduct our studies if we have to abruptly stop the turbines in the presence of orcas or other endangered species. Besides, stopping and starting the turbines would probably damage them.

I will assume that the NMFS employee genuinely cares about orca survival. I will also presume that the staffer understands and appreciates that there are many actual environmental assaults that are already adversely impacting the survivability of this and other marine species.

As it happens, there is an organization dedicated to preserving the Puget Sound habitat. It’s called the Puget Sound Partnership. Here’s a quote from its website:

Gerry O’Keefe, Executive Director of the Puget Sound Partnership said, “Ecology’s latest assessment highlights that if we want to protect vital elements of Puget Sound such as our orcas and salmon, we must prevent and remove toxic pollution…”

The most recent evaluation of Puget Sound, which you can download here, addresses orca populations and their threats.

These animals continue to face threats to their health from a number of stresses including PBTs and other contaminants and declines in prey. The whales are also at risk from major oil spills and from increased noise from whale-watching boats and other vessels.

As of 2009 Puget Sound was home to 85 orca whales, up a bit from the 1976 low of 71, but down from its highest level of 98 in 1988. The report tells us that before the “pre-European settlement” there were between 150 and 250 whales.

If were to array all of the environmental assaults on orca populations by most harmful to near-zero, my guess is that chemical pollutants, ocean acidification, and declining food supplies would be at or near the top. At the very bottom would be “engagement with turbines.”

The NMFS staffer may imagine giant Cuisinarts slicing and dicing orca whales. So what would the actual turbine look like? (from Open Hydro’s website)

 

Open Hydro, an Irish company with several installations under its belt, is designing the two pilot-project turbines. Each will have a diameter of six meters (19.7 feet). On its website the company asserts that this design will allow marine animals to either pass through the hole in the middle or otherwise avoid injurious contact. The slatted rotor is the only moving part, and it rotates very slowly—less than 12 revolutions per minute, or one revolution every five seconds. Contrast this to a long-play phonograph record at 33 1/3 RPM, or a ship’s propeller at between 70 and 120 RPM, or a food processor at 16,000 RPM. Besides there are no exposed blades on the Open Hydro turbine as there are on propellers.

I’ll conclude this post by suggesting an alternative approach to environmental considerations. I’ll do so in a chronological list comprising an overall process.

  1. Identify the problem (e.g., dwindling orca whale populations)
  2. Locate that problem amongst others according to relative significance (e.g., an impending bubonic plague vs. a typical flu season)
  3. Investigate the probable causes (e.g., water pollution, acidification)
  4. Prioritize the causes from most to least harmful
  5. Evaluate the costs and benefits of eliminating or mitigating the causes
  6. Focus resources (including stiletto intentions) on achieving the biggest bang for the buck

We should understand that some problems pose such significant risks that the costs to remove them, however high, must be borne to ensure species survival. Since humans are both writing and reading this post, I’ll put homo sapiens at the top. (Selfish, perhaps. Though I have no problem with orcas blogging about their own interests.)

I am astonished, then, that our species will spend an inordinate amount of money to preserve NW salmon populations (billions of dollars over the past several years) but little, if any, to reduce greenhouse gas emissions that truly threaten the planet’s biosphere and the human species that inhabit it. Would the NOAA staffer prefer a coal plant over underwater turbines? Would you?

Speaking of the environment…

December 1, 2011 by davald

As we busy ourselves saving Columbia River salmon, our neighbors to the north, specifically in the Athabasca oil sands of Alberta, are wreaking substantial environmental destruction on an almost unimaginable scale. And were it not for mounting protests, Pres. Obama would have approved the Keystone XL pipeline to deliver the Canadian oil to refineries and terminals in the U.S. He’s postponed the decision until after next year’s general elections.

You can locate the oil sands on Google Earth. You’ll find it in the upper east quadrant of Alberta. Here’s a shot from NASA via Wikipedia:

The area under destruction is much larger than this clip. But by all means, go drive your car.

I’ve just started reading John Bellamy Foster’s essay in the December 2011 issue of Monthly Review. (Disclosure: It’s Marxist.) He quotes historian William McNeill:

…catastrophe is the underside of the human condition—a price we pay for being able to alter natural balances and to transform the face of the earth through collective effort and the use of tools.

The value of the oil accumulated in the Alberta tar sands is estimated to be an astonishing $14 trillion. The Rest of Us and our descendants are not in line for any of that money. Rather, we’ll pay the price for its extraction, transportation, refinement, and consumption. With so much at stake, it will be extremely difficult to keep the stuff in the ground. The “market” and ordinary greed argue for pollution on an unprecedented scale.

An appropriate nod back to MI-5, the BBC television series I’m watching. In the last episode some members of an environmental group called, I think, Divine Nature, set out to hold London hostage to an impending flood, occasioned, the show says, by global warming. The Brits have built a sophisticated barrier in the Thames that will prevent the flooding, which is the target of the group.

The group is demanding the release of a top secret report called “Aftermath.” Circumstances have conspired to compel a member of the British government to join ranks with Divine Nature, details of which I’ll forego here. Over the course of the episode it’s revealed that “Aftermath” is a joint study by the UK and the US. The essential points are as follows:

  • global warming is real
  • it will cause the deaths of millions
  • there will be strife and violence on a planetary scale
  • the countries are resigned to this, since there is insufficient political will to curb our addiction to fossil fuels
  • therefore, steps must be taken to control the oil and coal for the survivors

Scary stuff, but it rings true. I can certainly imagine that the Pentagon, the CIA, MI-6, and a host of other government agencies on both sides of the Atlantic have developed and continue to develop worst case scenarios related to climate change and its impacts. It occurs to me that there is a “doomsday” syndrome afoot not unlike General Buck Turgidson’s monologue in Kubrick’s Dr. Strangelove. From Wikidpedia:

Turgidson [played by George C. Scott] tries to persuade [President] Muffley [Peter Sellars] to seize the moment and eliminate the Soviet Union by launching a full-scale attack on the Soviet Union. The General believes the United States is in a superior strategic position and a first strike would destroy the majority of the Soviets’ missiles before they could retaliate.

Then-Secretary of State Madeleine Albright told then-Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff Colin Powell: “What’s the point of you saving this superb military for, Colin, if we can’t use it?” Well, we’ve still got this superb military, which really has less to do with fighting wars than acquiring, preserving, and protecting the earth’s natural resources, oil being chief among them.

Judge Redden

November 30, 2011 by davald

The Seattle Times‘ editors showered praise on retiring federal judge James Redden. Among the accolades:

U.S. District Judge James Redden has been a good and demanding steward of the Endangered Species Act and the protection of Columbia River wild salmon.

It depends.

To be sure, he rejected several biological opinions he thought flawed, mostly for not doing enough to preserve salmon, a Northwest icon and food.

Here’s the strange part. The salmon were listed as “endangered,” so urgent, critical steps were made necessary to make them less so. To what end? Well, to be eaten.

Remember, save for habitués of Tennessee hollers, we don’t serve Spotted Owl for Thanksgiving dinner. It, too, is an endangered species, and a whole lot of lumber firms and their workers were mad as hell when the courts declared huge swaths of forest off limits to logging.

Efforts to save salmon qua food have not been cheap. For Bonneville Power customers the costs are in the billions of dollars. If one were to factor those costs into the price of fish, salmon would also be off limits for the Rest of Us—we simply couldn’t afford them. But those costs are borne by electricity consumers in the form of higher rates.

I’m not here to argue the merits of saving fish for our diets. I only wish to point out that preservation hasn’t been free.

Salmon for dinner? Enjoy.

The death of suburbia

November 26, 2011 by davald

I grew up in the suburbs, specifically in Concord, Calif., about 35 miles east of San Francisco, via the Bay Bridge and Caldecott Tunnel. It would not surprise me to learn that our housing tract was one of the first in the region, since the suburbs really didn’t take off until after WWII and the start of the boomer generation. I was five years old at the time of the move from a North Richmond apartment project. It was not until a few years ago that I departed suburbia for the higher-density of downtown Everett, Wash., which is in the throes of spasmodic transformation. It’s not quite a revitalization.

Over time I came to detest suburbia, with its endless sprawl, induced enervation, and necessary reliance on the automobile. Those few found walking along the sidewalks there were judged, in Jim Kunstler’s parlance, “short of their meds.” Unless they were teenagers, who were forced to walk long stretches to school or the local strip mall, since they had no cars of their own but the insatiable desire for social interaction.

And speaking of the automobile, in my last post I discussed briefly the notions of ecological footprint and carrying capacity. When the aggregate of the first metric exceeds the rather fixed volume of the latter, shit happens in the form of pollution, climate change, obesity, and, of course, wars. Planet Earth is a serious zero-sum game.

There is a strong correlation inverse between population density and gasoline consumption. How could it be otherwise? Consider this graph posted on Wikipedia:

Houston sits atop this chart, a big Texas city whose political leaders jettisoned the terms ‘zoning’ and ‘planning’ from their vocabularies. Along with Phoenix, it is the essence of auto-dependent sprawl.

In the lower left corner we find European cities, marked by high population density and robust public transport. To be fair, Europe’s land-use patterns reflect historical politics and economics developed long before the advent of the car—indeed, during the age of kings and queens and fuedalism. In America the car dictated land use.

Now that the housing bubble has collapsed and municipal governments struggle to pay bills, what’s to happen with suburbs? Christopher Leinberger, professor of urban and regional planning at the University of Michigan, believes that suburbia is dying, if not already dead. Writing for the New York Times, Leinberger tells us:

DRIVE through any number of outer-ring suburbs in America, and you’ll see boarded-up and vacant strip malls, surrounded by vast seas of empty parking spaces. These forlorn monuments to the real estate crash are not going to come back to life, even when the economy recovers. And that’s because the demand for the housing that once supported commercial activity in many exurbs isn’t coming back, either.

The truth of the last sentence must still be proved. Americans have been in thrall to their Dream since the end of WWII. It remains to be seen if the housing bubble collapse permanently tossed the Dream into history’s dustbin.

Leinberger avers:

The shift is durable and lasting because of a major demographic event: the convergence of the two largest generations in American history, the baby boomers (born between 1946 and 1964) and the millennials (born between 1979 and 1996), which today represent half of the total population.

Many boomers are now empty nesters and approaching retirement. Generally this means that they will downsize their housing in the near future. Boomers want to live in a walkable urban downtown, a suburban town center or a small town, according to a recent survey by the National Association of Realtors. [My wife and I fit this profile.]

The millennials are just now beginning to emerge from the nest — at least those who can afford to live on their own. This coming-of-age cohort also favors urban downtowns and suburban town centers — for lifestyle reasons and the convenience of not having to own cars.

He reports that only 12 percent of future homebuyers favor the conventional suburbs; most prefer higher-density urban centers. Perhaps the trend is toward a Europeanization of America: moving closer together, aided and abetted by attractive amenities and public transportation, including fixed rail. Leinberger cites Portland, Seattle, and several other cities that are in the midst of such transformation.

Yet, we can appreciate that old ways die hard. Suburbia attracts because it is relatively inexpensive, at least initially. The dirt under the houses is typically converted farmland, requiring little preparation for foundations, lumber, sidewalks, and streets. The houses themselves, as Leinberger affirms, are poorly constructed (as in fast as possible) out of cheap materials. Heretofore low gasoline prices, ready credit, and government-funded roadways have also conspired to make the suburban house affordable.

The embedded costs, we should understand, have been artificially constrained by governments at all levels. The price we pay at the pump does not include the cost of war or damage to the environment. The roads upon which we drive have long been subsidized. Mortgage interest deductions, uncommon around the globe, encourage home ownership, as do low-credit thresholds and government-backed loans. These homeowner enticements may not survive the current economic doldrums.

If we agreed that the suburbs have met a deserving death, what might we do to facilitate, if not accelerate, the transition from suburbia to urbanity? Another professor, Louise A. Mozingo, suggests we focus on offices. Also appearing in the same issue of the Times, Mozino writes:

Rethinking sprawl might begin much more effectively with these business enclaves [i.e., suburban offices, mostly of the expansive campus variety]. They cover vast areas and are occupied by a few powerful entities, corporations, which at some point will begin spending their ample reserves to upgrade, expand or replace their facilities.

Here, too, we find massive subsidization. Local governments, anxious to attract new business, routinely grant tax exemptions, modify zoning, and build connecting roads. Employees of these suburban offices usually pay nothing for parking, which further encourages the use of automobiles and consumption of gasoline—contributing to greenhouse gas emissions. But subsidies, however noble in intent, impose costs on others, as I’ve discussed above.

Mozingo concludes:

All three steps — a halt to agricultural land conversion, connecting dispersed employment centers with alternative transit, and encouraging downtown development — are needed to create renewed, civic-minded corporate workplaces and, in the process, move toward sustainable cities. Even leaving aside climate change, very soon the price of energy will make the dispersed, unconnected, low-density city-building pattern impossibly costly. Those jurisdictions and businesses that first create livable, workable, post-peak-oil metropolitan regions are the ones that will win the future.

We’ll see.

About that spending

November 25, 2011 by davald

That our economy requires people to spend lots of money is both a truism and ultimately self-defeating. Spending is part of an overall process the planet cannot sustain.

The problem relates to the amount and extraction of raw materials, pollution-causing manufacturing, and ultimately disposal of non-biogradeable stuff—garbage that sticks around for hundreds if not thousands of years. The more things produced, the greater the waste.

In between, of course, there is all that energy needed at almost every stage of the process. And insofar as we continue to buy and use motor vehicles, we’ll continue to burn billions of tons of carbon. Even our industrial agricultural practices rely heavily on fossil fuels.

While the short-term solution to economic recovery demands that we somehow increase our spending, the long-term viability of earth and its inhabitants necessitates the reverse, much less spending and perhaps more saving. Otherwise we’ll destroy the only planet we have.

What might such an economy look like, one that relied far less on consumption and the rest of the aforementioned process? Would it necessarily entail many steps backward, if you will, with people ultimately shunning cars, electronics, and modern appliances? Would we have to return to the Stone Age? Your guess, by the way, is as good as mine, probably much better.

You’re probably familiar with the concepts of ecological footprint and carrying capacity. The World Wildlife Fund‘s 2010 report includes this graphic.

As you’ll note we humans exceeded the earth’s carrying capacity way back in the mid-70s. The increase in the total ecological footprint since translates into unaccommodated waste, habitat destruction, species elimination, heavier pollution and ultimately a huge environmental debt to successive generations of people, plants, and animals.

Not to be too depressing, but we’ve headed in the wrong direction fast with scant hope of reversing this process. Even if a critical mass of us finally awoke to the disturbing trend and called for concerted action to arrest the human onslaught, our modern, energy-intensive, consumer-dependent capitalist system precludes responsible collective behavior. We are all creatures of the inner dynamics of a global economy that pushes and pulls us along in the same earth-destroying direction.

We’re up to seven billion now, we humans, and while the rate of population growth has declined, we’re still on track to add another two billion or so by the middle of this century. If everyone of us today lived in the manner of North Americans, say, the human species would need another three or four planets. But as I said above, we have only the one.

There is a significant matter of fairness, then. Even our relative poor have much more than most of the rest of the world. The large majority of the planet’s human population want and deserve a higher standard of living. But clearly this is a zero-sum game, though a very serious one. If the poor of the earth are to have more, the rich must have less.

Yet we know that the opposite is happening. The world’s wealthiest are getting even richer, which means that everyone else must struggle with less.

Is it any wonder there is so much political strife? The egregious inequity between the global Haves and Havenots cannot be sustained as the planet itself risks destruction by its own inhabitants.

However, despite the gloom and doom I’d like to end on a more hopeful note. First, consider this graph, also from the WWF report:

Look at the gray area, which represents carbon emissions. The graph suggests that if we could curb those emissions, we could fall back within the earth’s carrying capacity. Indeed, all of the other measures’ contribution to the expanding ecological footprint has been rarely stable over time. It’s those damn carbon molecules.

As always, it’s a matter of political will. And you know how that’s going.

Okay, not all that hopeful.

Dreaming vs. doing

November 8, 2011 by davald

You may share my despair in our country’s inability to go beyond dreams. We have evidently lost the capacity to actually do. I’m not talking small, necessarily, but grand and, forgive me, wonderful.

I am of two minds on small vs. big, however. It may have to do with process. For example, Amory Lovins talks about a lot of smalls making a big. I get that in terms of energy development and consumption. I’m also open to the idea that a relatively small germ of an idea (say a modest dream) can serve as the genesis for something much bigger and, perhaps, better.

On the other hand, there was a time in this country when we could and did do big—from start to finish, from conception to execution. I think of the New Deal projects, for example, of which the Grand Coulee dam was just one. It certainly wouldn’t be built today. Yet generations of regional citizens and businesses continue to reap its benefits. I was alive to witness the great federal highway construction, although I judge it to have done more harm than good. Oh, we’ve always excelled at war making, the bigger and more expensive the conflict the better.

But bear with me a moment to consider an alternative idea on how our everyday lives could be different, with the complete understanding that none of it can be realized in our current fiscal and political climate—if ever. After all, we’re not French. Besides, we’ve grown bitterly cynical.

I bring France into this, prompted by an essay appearing in the Atlantic and featured on Crosscut. It was written by Seattle attorney Charles Wolfe. I was immediately struck by this image:

It’s a photo of a tram running through the southern French city of Nice. The cars themselves are sleek, pure, and functional, with lots of windows. Note, too, the grass strips between the rails. Nice touch, pun intended. Reasonable minds may disagree, but I find this image pleasing and attractive, enough to make me want to live nearby, say in one of the adjacent apartment buildings. I want to be closer, not farther away.

The tram is one part of a very extensive transit plan. A Google translation tells me that the embedded video was shown to Nice’s Community Council, which approved its portion of the plan in 2009. As you’ll note, a full buildout of the transportation system will connect several French cities along the Mediterranean and inland, including Alsace-Lorraine. (I gather from the palm trees that these places have temperate climates.)

The video includes futuristic images of multiple subways and overhead trams. All is neat and tidy, as are the human figures. The goal is to have everything in place by the year 2030. Good luck with that.

Such projects are difficult to accomplish, even in France. As I said above, they’re probably impossible here in the US.

The principal proffered reason would surely be economic. We simply can’t afford it, more so now than in better times. Yet, I have my doubts that we’ll ever see major public works projects in my lifetime if not also my children’s, even if the economy recovers.

I would suggest that the real reason that we can no longer do is that we’ve lost faith in government and in our ability to imagine together. It’s all about the individual rather than the collective we. Voters deny government sufficient revenues to properly fund the public sector, including schools and transportation. The only governmental activity we support is war, of which we can never get enough.

We’ve become mean, brutish, and narrow-minded. No permission is granted to dream to envision to wonder, from our schools to the councils of government. Work for “the man” or don’t work at all.

The rich have siphoned off all the money, and they don’t need trams or busses or public schools. And we said nothing.

Tragic.

Fossil-fuel subsidies

October 5, 2011 by davald

All told, economic research seems to suggest that massive fossil-fuel subsidies actuallyhinder economic growth in the long run. The Global Subsidies Initiative conducted a review of six studies and found that “fossil-fuel subsidy reform would result in aggregate increases in gross domestic product (GDP) in both OECD and non-OECD countries. The expected increases among the studies ranged from 0.1 per cent in total by 2010 to 0.7 per cent per year to 2050.” Not too shabby.

Brad Plumer, writing for a Washington Post blog (Ezra Klein’s), argues that government subsidies to offset rising fuel costs are not only bad for the environment (remember global warming?) but also bad for the economy. Subsidies actually retard economic growth.

The linked study also found that “reforming” subsidies would cut greenhouse gas emissions by six percent. Not too shabby.

But here’s the kicker. Removing those subsidies during recessionary periods (we’re in one of those now) could exacerbate the aggregate demand problem. That’s because, in part, so much of our economy and so much of our transportation, both private and public, rely on fossil fuels. Moreover, coal-fired generation still accounts for over half of the nation’s electricity output.

Can you imagine a politician getting elected or staying in office championing higher fuel prices? It would clearly be the right thing to do, certainly for the environment but now, it seems, also for the long-run economy. However, electoral politics is all about the here and now.

As a people we’re quite content to kick all our problems down the road. Yes, we should protect the environment. But not now. Yes, we want long-term, sustainable economic growth. But not now. Yes, we should pour more money into our schools. But not now. Name your urgent concern. But not now.

I feel a sense of dread for my children and, should they arrive, grandchildren. They will be buried under so many cans.
