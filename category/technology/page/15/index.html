A most confounding essay

July 25, 2011 by davald

The Seattle Times‘ Brier Dudley enters some sort of Twilight Zone in his column this morning. He writes:

Don’t be surprised if you walk into the corner minimart one day soon and find that the corn dogs cost $1.50, instead of 99 cents.

This will have nothing to do with spiraling health-care costs, fuel prices or the federal debt.

Blame Steve Jobs instead.

Huh?

The comments have been fast and furious, one reader asking how the Times‘ editor could have missed this “piece of crap.” Jon Gruber, of Daring Fireball, responds thusly:

Microsoft-centric writers are losing their minds.

I found Dudley’s rant as clueless as it was misguided. How, pray tell, does a purported professional newspaper permit a columnist to engage is one giant non sequitur? I should think that there are dozens of local tech writers capable of penning worthwhile and informative posts.

Dudley writes for Microsoft’s hometown paper, so we should expect some anti-Apple bias, I suppose. But what about a little fairness along with reason?

Dudley throws down many words to arrive at his apparent assertion: business people’s appetite for the newest and best Apple devices, in particular the enormously successful iPad, will ultimately drive up the price of products, including corn dogs.

It seems to me that before Jobs’s return to Apple, when Microsoft ruled the computer world, companies large and small spent a substantial fortune on computers and Redmond software. They still are, judging by Microsoft’s recent financial results. Despite a pathological lack of innovation, Microsoft continues to rake in billions of dollars a quarter from Windows and Office.

Since most corporate IT departments have yet to discover the wonders of Apple as they remain fixated on all things Microsoft, Dudley should have pointed his finger toward Redmond rather than Cupertino—assuming, of course, that there was any logic to his assertion. There happens to be none, however.

The price Company A charges for its Product X has little to do with its costs. Unless, of course, Company A were a monopoly.

To illustrate, consider a major US airline. If its fuel costs rise, as they have recently, it cannot simply pass through those costs to the consumer, especially if one of its competitors has figured out a way to absorb some or all of the fuel-cost increases. If Company B, say, is more efficient in other areas than Company B and is therefore able to keep ticket prices stable or otherwise lower than Company A’s, then, all other things being equal for the consumer, Company B sells more seats and makes more money than Company A.

Here’s Dudley again, concluding his column:

When the owner of the minimart gets her new PC, she’ll discover that the manufacturer of her printer declined to produce a Windows 7 driver for that model, forcing her to buy a new printer.

The minimart will end up paying $2,000 to Apple, $900 a year to AT&T, $500 to Dell, $100 to Microsoft and $100 to the printer company.

With higher prices of corn, pork and cooking oil, the owner’s profit on a corn dog is down to about 35 cents. So the store will have to sell an extra 10,285 corn dogs to cover her new gadgets over the next year, or 28 more every day.

Meanwhile. the increased production of printers will enable the manager of an electronics factory in China to buy an iPad. Then his sales manager will ask for one to increase productivity and social engagement.

The factory’s finance and operations managers will be envious and worry they look out of touch if they don’t bring iPads to company meetings, so more will be ordered from Apple.

Last quarter, Apple sales in China grew sixfold and international sales accounted for 62 percent of its overall sales.

Fortunately, the new gadgets really will increase productivity and social engagement.

Instead of going down to the corner store, these people will have lunch at their desks and use their iPads to send tweets griping about the ridiculous price of corn dogs lately.

Did you follow that? If so, maybe you, too, are losing your mind.

Technology musings

July 15, 2011 by davald

I’ve come across a few articles of late proclaiming the rise of mobile computing in the enterprise. One pundit suggested that whoever rules mobile computing will eventually rule IT departments, since mobile computing is expected to overtake the desktop in the foreseeable future.

IT departments have been predictably averse to innovation, preferring the tried and true for a couple of reasons: (1) purported security concerns; and (2) entrenched platform bias. The first is legitimate. The second wrong-headed.

For any of you who have spent some time working in a medium- to large-size business you’re no doubt familiar with the conventional computer structure. You, as a client, have a cubicle, most likely, furnished with desks, drawers, shelves, and the ubiquitous tower computer-cum-display-cum keyboard. In turn, your central processing unit (CPU) is connected to a server infrastructure via Ethernet cables. The software applications, overwhelmingly Microsoft, reside on your CPU’s hard drive. There you may also store files or keep them on any number of servers, usually with peculiar names. You’ll have access to the Internet and email.

Increasingly, more and more of your time is spent in front of the computer display, hands fixed above or resting upon the keyboard, eyes glued to the screen. More often than not you’re engaged in email exchanges with other employees or individuals and groups external to the organization. You have occasion to write and read many emails. Some have attachments, either produced in Microsoft’s office suite programs (e.g., Word, Excel) or transformed into portable document format (pdf). You may, from time to time, access corporate data, which you then manipulate (not necessarily a pejorative term) for, say, report purposes.

It’s not unusual to walk through the labyrinth of cubicles to see dozens of ensconced employees doing pretty much the same thing: typing or reading at their desks. Even employees in adjacent cubicles will use email to communicate with each other rather than chat face to face.

There are meetings, of course. A small group of employees seated around one or more tables with an overhead screen at one end of the room and a projector at the other. Someone in the group has a laptop or a desktop system, physically tethered to the projector. That person will run the slide show, the lingua franca of all modern organizations. The group members will gaze at the screen while the presenter literally reads from the slides as they appear in order onto the screen. It’s not unusual to be provided with a printed copy of the presentation. Sounds like fun? You know the answer.

Let’s try to envisage a new paradigm of corporate communications.

Every employee will have a portable computer that fits into a pocket or in the palm of a hand—think smartphone and tablet. Gigabytes of data in various forms are instantly available on these seemingly miniature devices. That information is stored in “a cloud” and everything on the smart devices is automatically synced with the cloud. Make a change on your tablet and it’s automatically available on all your portable devices. Want to show other employees what’s on your tablet, say, then simply display the information on one of many LCDs scattered around the workspaces. No need for wires; the information is sent wirelessly.

Much work done in organizations is project-based. That means teams of employees, probably from different departments representing different organizational functions. Collaboration is critical, necessitating increased contact, either physical or virtual. Each team member, under the new paradigm, will have instant access to the same information via the cloud-cum-smart devices. Instead of employees passively gazing at a PowerPoint presentation, each will interact with the other co-creating the project-based material and outcomes.

It’s easy to imagine that the traditional desktop computer infrastructure and those thousands of wires will go the way of the dinosaur. Instead all will be wireless and data will be stored in the cloud rather than reside on cubicle hard drives or even laptops. That information will be available 24/7 to any employee with the requisite credentials. Communication will be seamless between internal and external customers, using web-based apps, virtual face-to-face exchanges (e.g., Apple’s Facetime) via smart devices, and virtual conferencing involving multiple parties.

I’ve had this hunch for some time. Microsoft, which has coasted on its Office and Windows application licenses, raking in billions of dollars a quarter, will either recognize the shifting paradigm or not. Its track record on innovation is hopeless, so even if Mr. Balmer finally gets it, it will be too late. The new technology giants are Google and Apple, with the former aping many of the strategies of Redmond including market fragmentation (running software on multiple, disparate devices made by a multitude of different companies). Apple, as always, works to integrate the software with the hardware, a model scoffed at by both Bill Gates and Steve Balmer.

But consider this piece.

Apple currently dominates the tablet market, having sold 25 million iPads to date and possibly more than 14 million more iPads in the third quarter. But one analyst believes Apple will sell a billion of them.

OK, OK. It’s clear that Apple’s iOS is beyond successful, and threatens to dominate global mobile sales in the foreseeable future.

The reason this is significant is that as Apple comes to dominate mobile computing, mobile computing is increasingly dominating computing in general.

Over the next five years, we’ll see more people using phones and tablets as their main computing devices. And I think we can also expect to see current desktop platforms like Mac OS X, Windows and Linux replaced by touch-friendly interfaces like iOS, Android, Metro and so on.

In other words, Apple’s iOS is poised to take over mainstream computing.

If Apple should prevail, we may finally be able to say goodbye to paper and those insufferable PowerPoint presentations. Kicking and screaming to the new paradigm will be the IT staff, all too comfortable with their legacy Windows’ systems.

We’ll see.

Mortal Kombat as free speech

June 27, 2011 by davald

I have mixed feelings about the Supreme Court decision to overturn California’s ban on selling violent video games to children. Here’s the New York Times:

Justice Antonin Scalia., writing for five justices in the majority in the video games decision, Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Association, No. 08-1448, said video games were protected by the First Amendment.

“Like the protected books, plays and movies that preceded them, video games communicate ideas — and even social messages — through many familiar literary devices (such as characters, dialogue, plot and music) and through features distinctive to the medium (such as the player’s interaction with the virtual world),” Justice Scalia wrote. “That suffices to confer First Amendment protection.”

Let’s suppose that viewing violent video games is harmful to children, or adults, for that matter. The state, nevertheless, cannot bar their sales, because images of mayhem and murder have the same constitutional protections as a corporation giving a million dollars to fund a campaign hit piece.

The California law would have imposed $1,000 fines on stores that sold violent video games to people under 18. It defined violent games as those “in which the range of options available to a player includes killing, maiming, dismembering or sexually assaulting an image of a human being” in a way that was “patently offensive,” appeals to minors’ “deviant or morbid interests” and lacked “serious literary, artistic, political or scientific value.”

It’s ironic that the California law incorporates language from prior Supreme Court cases banning sales of pornography to children. Killing, maiming, and dismembering a female image is okay, but not having virtual, explicit sex with the same image.

What might have been the decision had a group of enterprising programmers devised a video game that featured the killing, maiming, and dismembering of current Supreme Court members? I can imagine a psychopathic teenager living in D.C. attempt to transform the virtual into reality. I can also imagine one or more psychiatrists connecting the dots, concluding that but for the deranged adolescent incessantly playing the game he wouldn’t have tried to act on his learned impulses.

Across the pond several European Union states ban the sale of violent vide games—because they are considered harmful to minors. But those nations don’t go to war like the US, so they don’t need to raise young adults to become crazed butchers.

The trojan horse

June 25, 2011 by davald

Let’s imagine a company that makes really cool devices, which are not only aesthetically incomparable, they “just work”—amazingly so. Suppose further that so many people start using these devices for tasks that they had never thought about doing on gadgets, that they wind up mothballing their now-antiquated and, really, useless gizmos. Now let’s take this one step further. These millions of people, who are now enmeshed in a new technological culture, bring their devices to work. Gradually, a critical mass develops within the company that the heads of IT eventually relent, implementing protocols to accommodate the magical devices. If the CEO gets hooked, then a wholesale shift ensues—overnight.

Of course, I have in mind Apple and its unique combination of operating systems and devices. The software and hardware fit together seamlessly, yielding a superior mobile-sans-desktop experience. Apple has, whether or not intentionally, created a technological trojan horse: first the consumer, then the world of work.

Well, this scenario is playing out before our eyes, and some technology pundits are beginning to notice. Consider Mike Elgan’s recent post in Computerworld. He writes:

OK, OK. It’s clear that Apple’s iOS is beyond successful, and threatens to dominate global mobile sales in the foreseeable future.

The reason this is significant is that as Apple comes to dominate mobile computing, mobile computing increasingly dominates computing in general.

Over the next five years, we’ll see more people using phones and tablets as their main computing device. And I think we can also expect to see current desktop platforms like Mac OS X, Windows and Linux replaced by touch-friendly interfaces like iOS, Android, Metro and so on.

In other words, Apple’s iOS is poised to take over mainstream computing.

I’ll go out on a limb. Those IT departments who insist on clinging to the old Wintel culture will condemn their respective companies to the dungheap of history. Only those firms that energetically embrace Apple’s iOS will be competitive.

One more thing.

Government agencies may find it difficult to join the revolution. Why? Because of outmoded bidding requirements, along with unworkable governance models. Typically, an agency is required to award contracts to “the lowest qualified bidder.” But what if the only really qualified bidder is Apple and its various distribution channels? I fear that public institutions will be forced to settle for inferior technology on the pretext of “open competition” and the need to avoid “sole source” awards. Thus, cities, counties, states, school districts, and public utilities will purchase a hodgepodge of hardware and software products that will be job security for IT departments but holy hell for employees, who will have to endure routine glitches, incompatibilities, and grand dysfunction.

By the way, there is a huge, growing economy that is set to adopt iOS devices big time. That economy belongs to China. Within a decade, if not before, China is expected to surpass the US in GDP. It will do so because it’s peculiar hybrid of top-down governance and an increasingly robust infrastructure gets things done, from building vast high-speed rail networks to adopting the newest technologies on a humungous scale (e.g., renewable energy, smart grid, mobile computing).

Meanwhile, the United States is stuck in a political quagmire with no easy path to action. Oh, and if you think that the private sector will build high-speed rail or fix the country’s aging, crumbling infrastructure, then you just haven’t been paying attention.

That’s all, folks

June 22, 2011 by davald

The laws of physics may limit human intelligence. The brain is only so big with room for only so many neurons. However, as Scientific American reports, there are options:

The human mind, however, may have better ways of expanding without the need for further biological evolution. After all, honeybees and other social insects do it: acting in concert with their hive sisters, they form a collective entity that is smarter than the sum of its parts. Through social interaction we, too, have learned to pool our intelligence with others.

And then there is technology. For millennia written language has enabled us to store information outside our body, beyond the capacity of our brain to memorize. One could argue that the Internet is the ultimate consequence of this trend toward outward expansion of intelligence beyond our body. In a sense, it could be true, as some say, that the Internet makes you stupid: collective human intelligence—culture and computers—may have reduced the impetus for evolving greater individual smarts.

Gosh, how is this going to work in a faux libertarian society, one dominated by the cult of individualism and “rational choice” economic theory? Which other societies might adopt the social connections, in particular the need for cooperation, to advance humans’ collective intelligence?

Granted, we’ve been pretty good in the USA at developing technology, from the Internet to iPads. But we’re not all that good at social cohesion. Our society is sharply divided along several lines, from income to education to ethnicity, not to mention the stark contrasts between the two major political parties. Moreover, we’re possessed of sickly dysfunctional governance at all levels of government. We can’t seem to get things done.

Just off the top of my head, I’d offer China, which has a lean, mean governance structure, and perhaps the western European nations, which have much lower Gini indexes than the US and a higher level of social cohesion.

We’ll see.

If only you’d invested in Apple…

June 16, 2011 by davald

As a self-proclaimed, unabashed Apple aficionado, I often hear these fateful words from my darling wife: “If only we’d invested in Apple stock…” The sentence is never completed, but it would be safe to fill in phrases like “we’d be rich by now.”

Unfortunately, she’s right. Had we bought at $12 a share way back when, just at the time before Steve Jobs returned to Cupertino from Pixar, our net worth would be higher than it is. Of course, the magnitude of our gains depended on how many shares I had purchased, and therein lay the problem: I was decidedly cash poor when Apple was struggling for its very survival.

To make matters worse, as if to throw salt on my wound, one enterprising fellow has dared to pose the question: Suppose I had bought Apple stock rather than its products? I read this little story and I mildly weep.

Let’s say that I had $5,700 to buy Apple’s best laptop just before Christmas, 1997. That would have been the Apple PowerBook G3 250 (Original/Kanga/3500). Assuming I held on to the stock, by April of 2010 that $5,700 would have grown to, gulp, $330,563.

Financially speaking, then, I would have been far better off investing in Apple stock than buying the product—unless it’s now museum-worthy and someone with lots of play money might bid a million bucks for it. No matter. I bought neither the product nor the stock.

However, I have bought a lot of other Apple products, from which I’ve derived considerable use and pleasure. From a purely economic viewpoint, though, bad investments, to be sure.

There’s blood on my iPad

May 23, 2011 by davald

I have been a devoted Apple consumer since the original Macintosh, with credit card receipts to prove it. Yes, I momentarily strayed to the dark side during Cupertino’s wilderness years, when Steve Jobs was in exile, but quickly returned after immediately experiencing the dreaded blue screen. I have not been tempted away since.

We Apple aficionados have gladly, obsessively paid premiums for our products, believing, with much evidence, that the Macintosh (just “Mac” today), iPod, iPhone, and now iPad are superior to their dark-side counterparts in every respect. And did I mention that we like things to work, with simple, efficient elegance?

I’ve known for some time that those magical products originate in mainland China, having tracked multiple FedEx shipments—from Taiwan, to Anchorage, to Seattle, to Bothell, then “out for delivery.” But I have been unwilling to encounter the ugly underbelly of Apple’s production system.

On the recommendation that I would laugh my head off, I accepted an invitation to see Michael Daisey’s one-man performance of “The Agony and Ecstasy of Steve Jobs.” Yes, I laughed and chuckled and chortled, appreciating the familiar vocabulary of “geekiness” and all things Cupertino. I even learned a few nuggets of Apple lore. But then I started to squirm, as did fellow audience members.

Daisey had contrived to enter the fortress of Foxconn in Shenzhen, China. What he witnessed and the facts he poured out last Saturday evening at the Seattle Rep scrubbed away the carefully burnished Apple sheen. Did you know?

  • The labor costs of an iPhone are 80 cents.
  • Foxconn manufactures or assembles 52 percent of all of the electronics in the world.
  • Foxconn’s Shenzhen plant employs over 400,000 workers.
  • The plant has 23 cafeterias, each built to hold 10,000 workers at a time.
  • The onsite dormitories’ rooms are about 10 by 12 and contain as many as 15 beds stacked vertically.
  • Many of the workers are in their early teens.
  • All of Apple’s products are assembled by hand; there are no robots.
  • Workers put in as many as 18 hours a day, often six or seven days a week.
  • Most are not paid overtime.
  • The Chinese government bans labor unions. Anyone suspected of belonging to one may spend the rest of his or her life in prison. (Karl Marx, where are you?)

For workers, having spent several years on an assembly line doing the same micro task over and over and over again, repetitive stress syndrome becomes the norm. Hands and joints grow gnarled and disfigured. When a worker can no longer perform the menial task, he or she is discarded, like a broken iPod. Ironically, in a terribly tragic way, workers assembling Apple’s products cannot afford to actually buy one, so they’ve never used an iPad, despite touching millions of them.

The Foxconn plant that recently exploded, killing several workers, was erected in 70 days to meet the burgeoning demand for the iPad2. Following a spate of suicides at their giant high-rise facilities, Foxconn installed nets around the top of the roofs. Daisey mockingly calls this “corporate responsibility.”

We Mac fans tend to be more liberal than PC devotees. I, for one, having gladly paid a premium for Jobs’s magical devices, wouldn’t hesitate to pay more to improve the working conditions of Foxconn. Or, perhaps Jobs would be content with a little less profit.

Consider that the iPhone claims just five percent of the mobile telephone market, but 20 percent of the revenue, and a whopping 55 percent of the profits. That’s obscene. The iPad has about three fourths of the tablet market and, no doubt, a huge lion’s share of the net revenues. Apple dominates the music scene, being the largest seller, even greater than Walmart.

Jobs sits on over $60 billion in cash, which has investors wondering what, if anything, he plans to do with all that money. Why not spend a little bit of that wad on the welfare of Chinese workers?

Apple could insist that Foxconn establish an 8-hour-day norm, abolish child labor, provide health care and social security, and a pay scale that would allow workers to make a decent living. The Chinese government won’t lift a finger.

Beijing conceived of a Special Economic Zone for Shenzhen, once a fishing village of 700 and now the third most populous city in the world. If you have electronics in your house, regardless of brand, there’s a good chance they were made in Shenzhen by nearly-slave labor, and very young, at that. No doubt profits are being siphoned off by the enriched party leaders in Beijing, as well as by Foxconn executives and Cupertino, of course.

Michael Daisey told his audience that we Americans made Shenzhen, insofar as we have demanded relatively cheap gadgets. Upon leaving the theater we were handed a piece of paper entitled “What happens next?” Daisey wrote, “That lies in your hands.” He asks at the bottom that we “tell others.”

This is just a monologue [he wrote]—a single voice telling a story of a single experience. But if I have opened the door for you, consider opening a door for others. We do not like to think about China and its implications in our country, but that silence can only exist if we are complicit with it. Talking about it, thinking about it when making purchasing decisions, and understanding it is not just symbolic. They are the first seeds of actual change. Do not be afraid to plant them.

Pass the shovel.

$8.5 billion for a verb

May 11, 2011 by davald

The Seattle Times puts it thusly:

In a giant deal to buy Internet phone company Skype, Microsoft finally got what it has long sought: a consumer brand so powerful that it’s a verb.

According to some reports, Microsoft’s Steve Balmer was tricked into believing there were other serious bidders, including Google. So he overpaid by a factor of two.

By the way, Skype lost $376 million over the last two years. Its 2010 revenues were $860 million.

Even Times resident Microsoft propagandist, Brier Dudley, was circumspect:

But if you can ignore the insane amount of money being thrown around…, it makes some sense.

True to form, Microsoft can’t seem to innovate its way toward being competitive with Apple and Google. So it throws a trainload of cash at a money-losing venture in hopes of integrating the new acquisition’s services into—what? We’ll see.

Nice, but now what?

May 5, 2011 by davald

This morning’s P-I features a new “concept” store for Microsoft, replete with images of what might be. My question: What, pray tell, would you buy once inside?

After all, Microsoft doesn’t really sell any hardware, save for the XBox 360. Its business is developing and selling ponderous, expensive software, mostly the Windows OS and Office Suite. So, what gadgets would be available to wow the customers, as is the case in any Apple store?

Microsoft has always been late to the party. But its sheer size has allowed it to bully or purloin, depending on circumstances, so that it eventually achieves parity with competitors, if it doesn’t succeed in vanquishing them. Until now.

Notice that I’ve said nothing amount ingenuity or creative genius. Those traits are in scant supply in Redmond. And therein lies the biggest challenge to Balmer et al.

Microsoft is no Apple and Steve Balmer is no Steve Jobs. Balmer, after first mocking Apple’s new products, ultimately winds up in a big sweat, trying frantically to play catch up. He does this, not by bringing something different to the market, but by merely copying Apple, and doing a terribly lousy job at that.
