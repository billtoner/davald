Maps

September 23, 2012 by davald

I’ve got an “old” iPhone 4. So many of the new and reportedly neat features of iOS 6 don’t work, in addition to Siri, which never did on this phone. I do have the Apple maps program, however. And judging by the comments, and a tiny bit of use on my part, the app sucks.

Joe Nocera believes that this less-than-sterling roll out by Apple signifies a return to Cupertino’s dark ages, when Steve Jobs spent a dozen years away from the company he co-founded.

…Jobs isn’t there anymore. It is rare that a company is so completely an extension of one man’s brain as Apple was an extension of Jobs. While he was alive, that was a strength; now it’s a weakness. Apple’s current executive team is no doubt trying to maintain the same demanding, innovative culture, but it’s just not the same without the man himself looking over everybody’s shoulder. If the map glitch tells us anything, it is that.

I think Nocera is premature, at best. As John Gruber is wont to do, Nocera’s piece will be filed under “claim chowder” for future reference.

Yet, the maps app has been roundly and, it seems, deservedly bashed and mocked—in the manner of the Spanish octogenerian’s “restoration” of “Ecce Homo.” Evidently one can see bridges, including Tacoma Narrows, “melting.” A British park with the name “airfield” has been designated a landing strip, prompting a warning from UK aviation authorities not to use it as such. Madison Square Garden is shown as a park. And so on. Many can’t find obvious locations. I, for example, couldn’t find my daughter’s house, even though her address is in my Contacts app. (I’ve re-entered the address, and now the app properly locates it.)

Nocera’s point, and one might agree, is that Steve Jobs never would have permitted a rollout of such a mediocre product. Yet, Jobs evidently approved Siri, which is still a work in progress. He repeatedly confessed that the Apple TV was just a “hobby,” not quite ready for prime time; he was right. Despite his being at the helm, poring over the slightest details, he allowed for the launch of MobileMe, though after its release to a disapproving public he summarily dismissed the project head. For all his genius, Jobs was far from perfect when it came to unveiling products and services.

So, why the fuss about Tim Cook and the end of Apple? One simple, but less-than-edifying explanation, is that Apple can’t stay atop the heap forever; like all other companies, its reign will cease. Thus any sign of impending demise looms large in the punditocracy, and I’m sure that there is a competition, of sorts, to be first with the revelation.

Moreover, success invites wannabes. If Apple can make so much damn money with its gadgets, there must be opportunity to capture pieces of the pie. So now we have Samsung, Amazon, Google, Microsoft, Nokia, and a host of other companies vying to topple Apple, by any means necessary. Should Apple slip, others will be quick to pounce.

I appreciate that it can’t be easy to replicate Google’s map application, and it shows with Apple’s initial foray. Although it was originally dubbed “amazing” by those who had seen the program in its fledgling state, Apple’s version suffers in embarrassing ways. But if anyone can overcome the difficulties, it’s Apple. Besides, as those who know something about map applications, the more consumers use the product the better the data get over time. And a maps app is largely about data. Google, we may recall, didn’t release a top-of-the-pile app in the first iteration.

I’ll go out on a short limb to predict that Apple will produce that “amazing” maps app along with dozens of new “insanely great” products and services. In a matter of weeks or months we’ll look back at Nocera’s prognostication and have a nice chuckle.

Can I resist?

September 13, 2012 by davald

I return for a moment to truly important considerations, Apple’s new iPhone.

As yesterday’s event approached thoughts began filling my head of a most horrible nature. Maybe, just maybe, I really didn’t need to have the latest and greatest. Perhaps I can go on about my silly, petty little existence using my, gulp, iPhone 4. That’s right. I skipped the last iteration. Can you believe it? The 4, after all, handles all the tasks I need done, like answering the very few phone calls I get and checking the weather. Throw in a few games of solitaire and a news aggregator, and I’m good to go.

Then came the reveal (geeks like using verbs as nouns; I’m borrowing, of course). The new iPhone is bigger, yet lighter, much faster, yet thinner, sporting an even better camera, with panorama, no less. How did Apple squeeze so much into such a limited space? Engineering par excellence. Oh, and it will run on LTE networks, which are blazing fast. I know, because I access Verizon’s using my iPad (v. 3). There’s better voice quality, better Siri, and awesome 3D maps, with turn-by-turn navigation. And if I were a frequent flyer and lived amongst a plethora of fancy restaurants I could use Passbook.

Amazing stuff. Tim Cook said as much, often.

Crap.

Taxes and death spirals

September 12, 2012 by davald

Mayor Bloomberg says that Congress should get on with it and just pass the Simpson-Bowles recommendations, even though the committee’s report failed to gain sufficient support from its members. The mayor also believes that lower taxes help businesses.

Well, let’s see. I’ll try a little thought experiment.

Businessman A, who makes Widget X, believes that he could become more competitive if his company’s tax burden were less. To be sure, his costs would decline, all other things being equal, which would free dollars to, say, hire more workers to boost production, even though aggregate demand remains weak.

Businessman B also makes Widget X. If A gets a tax break, so will he. So, both A and B will have no comparative advantage against the other—again, all other things being equal.

Now we could introduce Businessman Chang. He’s Chinese, of course, and he also makes Widget X. Both A and B find it difficult to compete with Chang. So they push for lower taxes in hopes of taking some market share from Chang. A does not better B, nor B, A, but A and B, with lower taxes, better Chang—to limited extent and only temporarily.

Chan has a government that builds all the infrastructure he needs, from high-speed rail shipping, to modern factories, to adequate schools that train his workers. But A and B, because their taxes have been lowered—along with every other business person in America—have lousy infrastructures. The roads are congested or crumbling. Railways are clogged and unable to accommodate high-speed trains. Nor do the would-be workers have the requisite skills to improve production, because the schools are underfunded.

Both A and B press for even lower taxes to compensate for Chang’s advantages. All levels of government comply with their request. But the governments, in turn, have less and less tax revenues to provide those things that Chang takes for granted. Schools, roads, and other transportation options continue to fall apart. Nevertheless, A and B complain about the lousy infrastructure and the failing schools, as they keep lobbying their government representatives to do more with less and, of course, to further lower their taxes.

A and B have created their own death spiral. The more they clamor for and receive tax breaks, the more difficult it is for them to compete against Chang.

But A and B are idiots, of course. They never see beyond the tip of their noses. Indeed, they shout and scream that “they built it” and that they are “job creators” answerable to no one—if only government would reduce their tax burdens and stay out of their way.

For their parts, the different levels of government have no choice. The elected representatives depend on A and B for their campaign donations. Nor do they have any money to get in A and B’s way. They can’t pay for schools and roads and railways. And the governments can no longer invest in research to spur technological innovation.

Mr. Chang’s future looks bright. Not so much Mr. A and B’s.

The dark side

August 27, 2012 by davald

I use technology as a tool to accomplish things like writing this blog. I do a fair amount of spreadsheets and some graphics. Of course, I use the Internet constantly in search of information and opinion. Since I’m old enough to appreciate a slide rule and an Olivetti typewriter, the emergence of personal technology impresses the hell out of me. I have more computing power on my iPhone than the IBM 360 that I used back in the 60s.

As regular readers of this blog know, I’m also in league with Apple, Inc., having acquired my first Macintosh in the mid-80s. What struck me about that product and its successors were its ease of use, simplicity of design, and functionality—Cupertino’s products “just work.”

As readers also know, my wife teaches public school. Her particular district switched from Apple to Windows about 20 years ago. I predicted at the time that the school district would incur mounting support costs, integration nightmares, and frustrated users. I was right, of course. Cash-strapped administrators are enticed by the promise of cheap, then forever forced to suffer the consequences of their shortsightedness.

Meanwhile, Apple continues to grow, both technologically and financially. For some time now it has reigned as the world’s most valuable company. Its products sell like no others, reaping inestimable returns for the company and its millions of customers.

But for those school districts that switched to the dark side not only have they become blind to Apple’s innovation, they have acquired technological albatrosses that consume precious time and dollars best spent on education. All of this came into immediate relief on a recent visit to my wife’s school.

She had been told on several occasions that iPads would be incorporated into the classroom. As an avid iPad user, I can attest to the power and creative potential of the device. For teachers, an iPad linked to an Apple TV connected to a widescreen monitor spells presentation success. Everything appearing on the iPad can be simultaneously mirrored on the television. The teacher can be anywhere in the room casting images while free to assist her pupils individually.

During that visit I looked for iPads. Nada. What I discovered, to my chagrin, were dozens of Windows machines, tiny Thinkpads. Even to a casual observer these devices represent industrial design from hell. There are so many buttons and keys and ports that serve to overwhelm rather than invite. Then there is the red felt ball. Ghastly, I say. Kids are supposed to use these things?

When I returned home I looked up the Thinkpad. It’s made now by Lenova. It comes in a gazillion different varieties, recalling the tyranny of choice.

Both Steve Jobs and Tim Cook attribute much of Apple’s success to just saying no. There are so many brilliant ideas awaiting tangible expression. But Apple’s CEOs understand something their competitors completely fail to grasp: endless features and myriad design options introduce complexity that subtracts from those qualities ingrained in Apple’s products but so lacking elsewhere.

That said, I get geek. I appreciate the lure of granularity, of digging down into the infinite variables of the operating system and software applications. Why, you can do this and that and don’t forget about x, y, and z. Using a Wintel machine presents the user with endless customization.

However, not everyone is a geek. Indeed, most of us have no need whatsoever for variation. We prefer usability above all. And usability demands simplicity in those tasks we do all the time, every day.

Should you visit an Apple store, go to the section displaying iPads on short-legged tables. There you will find several small children poking and swiping different applications, producing oohs and aahs from kids and parents alike. Should they be fortunate enough to have one in their homes, children will graduate to ever more sophisticated programs and higher levels of learning. If their schools should have them in abundance, knowledge limits cease to exist. And if their teachers have them and employ them extensively in the manner suggested above, the education experience improves dramatically for all.

But that experience is being denied teachers and pupils at my wife’s school. They are stuck with complicated devices, too few in number, with an antiquated projector system attached to a dark-side machine. A pity.

Things I hate to see

August 13, 2012 by davald

The last few days I had occasion to visit the closest Apple Store, this one at Alderwood Mall, Wash. As always, the place was abuzz with customers and too many blue-shirted employees to count, all with iPads and iPhones and electronic devices seemingly implanted in their ears. Hectic. Noisy, though not in a bad way. Successful—for me.

After reading the New York Times expose on Apple employees I found myself staring at their faces looking for signs of disgruntlement or frustration or fatigue. Perhaps I’m getting too old to recognize such traits, but I found none of the above. All the workers appeared to be eager and enthusiastic, some a bit more than others. The Times article suggested that Apple employees were buckling under the pressure of always having to do more with little compensation.

So, today I read this:

Over the past couple of weeks, we’ve been receiving reports that Apple has been laying off a number of recently-hired staff members at its retail stores. Most of the reports have been coming from Apple retail employees in the United Kingdom, but several of these reports claim that similar actions are taking place at stores around the world. We’ve also been receiving reports of long-term cutbacks in hours for part-time staff in the United States and Canada.

You see, I don’t like this shit. From the richest company in the world I expect much, much better. Apple should pay all of its employees at least a family wage and full benefits. Also, there’s no need—I emphasize need—to subject workers to either too many or too few hours. Apple has so much money on hand that it could afford to make its workers not only proud but comfortable.

Just do it. Oh, that’s another company’s logo. Think different? Yeah, that’s the ticket.

Mountain Lion: initial, very brief impressions

July 28, 2012 by davald

One can readily find comprehensive reviews of Apple’s new OS elsewhere (e.g., here and here). As an extreme early adopter, I downloaded my copy of Mountain Lion at the break of dawn Wednesday then installed it on my Mac Pro (the oldest version compatible with the new operating system—whew).

Unlike Microsoft’s Windows 8 or Metro or whatever Redmond is calling its latest OS, Mountain Lion’s improvements are mostly under the hood. The interface looks very much like its predecessor, Lion. This is a good thing and allowed me to load the OS onto my wife’s MacBook Air—she is my technological antithesis. It also helps that one purchase of $20 is good for multiple devices.

What I noticed first off, other than the appearance, was speed. This puppy is much more responsive than Lion, and the updated Safari shows the speed boost most of all. Web pages flash onto the screen—almost instantaneously with a mouse click.

That’s it for now. Like I said, very brief.

Instant replay and baseball

July 11, 2012 by davald

Baseball resists calls for modernization. The purists proclaim the integrity and history of the game. Introducing technology to the sport will only degrade the experience. So, the MLB has only gradually acknowledged the utility of instant replay, allowing umpires beginning three years ago to review tapes to determine whether or not a ball left the park or hit the wall. Even that limited use of technology amounts to a painful interruption of play, since the squad of umpires repairs to some room under the stands to see what really happened.

I’ve been around baseball for a very long time, watching the few televised games in my youth, then playing at ascending levels through college. I, too, appreciate its rich history of talent, records, and statistics.

Yet, I feel the game is under threat by the league’s reticence to confirm or refute umpire calls, from balls and strikes to balks and steals. We television viewers know what really happens on the field, because we can see every pitch and every play, both live and by instant replay, including “slo-mo” video.

With that technology comes embarrassing exposure of human failings. Umpires blow calls, sometimes spectacularly so. And those calls can mean the difference between becoming world champions or the losing team.

The New York Times as weighed in a bit. Its article cites one of those blown calls in the 1985 World Series. The Kansas City Royals beat the St. Louis Cardinals in seven games, but only after an umpire got it wrong. In this case, the umpire was Don Denkinger.

Denkinger was the first-base umpire for Game 6 of the 1985 World Series between the Royals and the St. Louis Cardinals. He missed a crucial call at first base that sparked a ninth-inning comeback for the Royals, who won the championship the next night. It was probably the most significant missed call in baseball history.

Every person watching television that game knew that the call was blown. But not the umpires, who are required to make instant calls on split-second events. They are human. All too human.

So, I propose the following. It will surely rankle the traditionalists.

We’ll start with a spring-training game. There will be no umpires on the field. Instead, all actions on the field will be immediately captured by multiple television cameras. All balls and strikes will be “called” in accordance with pitch trackers, which are able to record speed, rotation, and, most important, location.

Every play on the diamond will be instantly “called” by televisions-cum-computers. I emphasize ‘instantly.’ There is no need for deliberation, second thoughts, video review. Just as we television viewers “know” what’s going on the moment it happens, and are granted several follow-up replays to make sure, the players and benches and fans in the seats will “know” as well.

The human element, which is merely a euphemism for getting things wrong from time to time, will be eliminated. All focus will be on the players and their competition.

Despite resistance to such a proposal, I believe it inevitable.

The Great Divergence: Are we surplus?

July 10, 2012 by davald

Comes now Thomas Edsall, writing for the New York Times, to address the headline: “The Hallowing Out.” The middle class is shrinking, while the poor and rich proliferate. Is this phenomenon a result of “natural” forces or the effect of deliberate policies, whether or not their outcomes were intentional? Edsall:

The issue of the disappearing middle is not new, but credible economists have added a more threatening twist to the argument: the possibility that a well-functioning, efficient modern market economy, driven by exponential growth in the rate of technological innovation, can simultaneously produce economic growth and eliminate millions of middle-class jobs.

While I question the premise of “efficient modern market,” since there’s little evidence that it exists in reality—more likely the figment of ideology—if “the rate of technological innovation” drives more of us into superfluity, we’ve got problems. Whatever arrangements obtain, it’s clear that a small slice of the population continues to do exceedingly well while the Rest of Us stagnate or fall further behind. Edsall includes this chart:

The faint, dotted line represents all after-tax profits. After plunging during the Great Recession, profits have risen even more sharply and attained a much higher level than at any time since January of 1995. GDP has risen only slightly, while the employment-to-population ratio has plummeted. In other words, the chart depicts the suggested narrative that this new economy, however efficient, doesn’t require as many workers.

Edsall quotes an MIT researcher, Andrew McAfee:

As computers race ahead, acquiring more and more skills in pattern matching, communication, perception, and so on, I expect that this decoupling will continue, and maybe even accelerate.

The decoupling is another word for labor disenfranchisement. If you can’t master technological skills or know how to marshall technology for productive outcomes, then this economy doesn’t need you.

Let’s step back a bit from this thread to ask ourselves some fundamental questions. I’ll begin with this: For whom or what is an economy for?

Some might think this an odd question, perhaps because many if not most of us assume that the economy just is. It has no moral purpose, no conscience, no sense of self. In the textbooks we might find something to the effect that ‘economy’ is descriptive: millions of people buying and selling goods and services for which millions of people are needed to produce. It is not, strictly speaking, for anyone or any thing.

If so, the questions of morality or human development must be raised and answered outside economic textbooks. This may be the proper stage for philosophers.

I’ll jump ahead in this inquiry by assuming that philosophers emerge from their deliberations with an answer, one that suggests that the economy’s outcomes are immoral in that, say, there’s too much money at the top and not enough at the bottom. Whatever their answer, I’m more interested at the  moment in another question: If the economy’s outcomes fail some morality test, what can and should be done to produce better results, perhaps along some Rawlsian distribution?

For those who believe that the economy is what it is and that it’s unfortunate, but inevitable, that the rich get richer and the poor more so, attempts to engineer the economy to produce different outcomes are deemed foolish. On the opposite side, those who reject such inevitability, the answers can be found in history.

One would be naive to suppose that the rich and powerful have simply stood on the sidelines over the last several decades while enjoying their inevitable desserts. Actually, they have been busy, as numerous accounts have chronicled (e.g., Stiglitz, Cassidy, Baker). They have been busy in Congress, persuading its members to cut the marginal tax rates, reduce financial regulation, and stifle labor.

In turn, these legislative actions have yielded a different economy than the one that existed during the relative halcyon days of the 50s and 60s. This different economy has disproportionately benefited the few at the expense of the Rest of Us. Wealth has become more concentrated, and with that concentration of wealth comes concentration of political power, which is used to increase concentrations—and so on. This new economy, dominated by the financial sector and, perhaps, technology, manages to produce record profits for some while rendering the Rest of Us surplus.

The evidence, then, indicates that the economy can be changed. The challenge for the Rest of Us is to somehow persuade Congress and state legislatures to modify the economy to produce outcomes that benefit the many at the expense of the few. Don’t worry. They can afford it.

Again, history provides guidance. During those relative halcyon days, a byproduct of the Great Compression during the late 30s to the end of WWII, marginal tax rates were higher as were union participation rates. Corporate taxes were also higher. The country experienced economic growth and low inflation. Moreover, and much to the point, America’s Gini index was much lower than it is today: the gap between the rich and poor was narrower.

To those who stress the inevitability of perverse outcomes I say “poppycock.” Things can be different. Things should be different. Will they be different? That’s the challenge for the Rest of Us.

Apple’s ugly

June 27, 2012 by davald

Apple, as we know, is the world’s richest company. Its market capitalization (stock price times outstanding shares) is greater than Amazon’s, Google’s, and Microsoft’s—combined. It dominates the emerging tablet market, sells a gazillion iPhones, and extracts more profits than any other technology company. Apple sits on over $110 billion in cash.

Yet, only a small percentage of Apple’s money reaches its retail employees’ wallets. These are the people, mostly young, who toil in Apple’s stores recording sales of “amazing” products. This huge imbalance is the subject of a four-month-long investigation by the New York Times. Just before the paper printed the results, Apple announced a twenty-five percent hike in pay.

Apple stores generate more dollars per square foot than any other retail outlet, and nearly twice as much as Tiffany’s, which ranks second in this category. If you divide Apple’s total sales by the number of retail employees, you get almost a half-million dollars. But the average store employee makes just $11.91 an hour, according to the Times.

Within this world, the Apple Store is the undisputed king, a retail phenomenon renowned for impeccable design, deft service and spectacular revenues. Last year, the company’s 327 global stores took in more money per square foot than any other United States retailer — wireless or otherwise — and almost double that of Tiffany, which was No. 2 on the list, according to the research firm RetailSails.

Worldwide, its stores sold $16 billion in merchandise.

But most of Apple’s employees enjoyed little of that wealth. While consumers tend to think of Apple’s headquarters in Cupertino, Calif., as the company’s heart and soul, a majority of its workers in the United States are not engineers or executives with hefty salaries and bonuses but rather hourly wage earners selling iPhones and MacBooks.

The Times article reports that, despite the paltry wages, the demand for the retail positions far outstrips supply. There are thousands of people, most with college degrees, who would love to work in Apple’s stores, and, indeed, most current employees remain stunningly enthusiastic about selling the company’s gadgets.

But Apple’s success, it turns out, rests on a set of intangibles; foremost among them is a built-in fan base that ensures a steady supply of eager applicants and an employee culture that tries to turn every job into an exalted mission.

This is why Apple can do something unique in the annals of retailing: pay a modest hourly wage, and no commission, to employees who typically have college degrees and who at the highest performing levels can move as much as $3 million in goods a year.

“When you’re working for Apple you feel like you’re working for this greater good,” says a former salesman who asked for anonymity because he didn’t want to draw attention to himself. “That’s why they don’t have a revolution on their hands.”

For its part, Apple, like any other company, is in business to make money. Its managers realize that they have great products that mostly sell themselves. (Apple’s retail employees are not called “sales people”; they are “specialists.”) Millions of people flock to Apple’s 327 retail stores each year. And they buy things, without assistance. They know the product before they enter the store. Their goal is to purchase the iPhone or the iPad or the iMac or any of the dozens of accessories lining the shelves. Since there are so many young, enthusiastic, Apple aficionados anxious to work in their stores, and the products sell themselves, company officials see no need to pay higher wages. Nor are commissions ever in the picture.

However, it’s not as if Apple couldn’t afford to pay its workers more, reduce their hours, and boost their benefits. There are roughly 30,000 “specialists” and “geniuses” working in Apple’s retail stores. If the company paid an average compensation of $100,000 a year (salary plus benefits), that would take a $3 billion chunk from profits. Apple made almost four times that amount of profits in its last quarter alone.

Steve Jobs worked for only a dollar a year in salary. He made his fortune by owning Apple stock, whose value went from the low single digits upon his return to Cupertino in 1997 to about $500 when he died in 2011, a period that included two 2:1 stock splits. His successor, Tim Cook, made more money last year than any other American CEO, reports the New York Times. While his salary is “only” $900,000, he took options on Apple stock now worth more than $600 million.

One might suggest that Cook should be the richest CEO, since the company he leads is also the richest. But Cook and other CEOs take home many multiples of the typical worker’s pay. The Times says that the average CEO compensation for last year was $14.4 million; the average salary of the Rest of Us was a bit over $45,000. That’s a ratio of 318:1.

Oh, and at $11.91 an hour, a full-time Apple “specialist” earns less than $24,000 a year, assuming he or she works a 40-hour week for 50 weeks in the year. Cook made over 25,000 times as much as the person who purveys his products. Even just taking his official salary into the equation, he makes 37.5 times the wages of the average “specialist.”

We exist in a “market economy,” where the intersection of supply and demand determines prices and wages. The “market” determines that Apple “specialists” are to receive just $11.91 an hour. The same “market” sets no limits, really, on CEO incomes, though no one should doubt that Tim Cook is more valuable to Apple than any one “specialist.”

In Marx’s lingo, Apple “specialists” provide surplus labor value to the company, which is then either saved or invested or used to pay Tim Cook’s salary. Is this fair? The “market” doesn’t care about such matters.

Those of us who prefer fairness will have to come up with a different economic-political system. Neither of our two major parties gives a damn about the Rest of Us, so don’t hold your breath that Congress will enable another “great compression” any time soon.

Praise be the market.

When you don’t have Siri

June 21, 2012 by davald

I’ve still got the iPhone 4—no S. A couple of weeks ago I was doing my usual walk, listening to music via my iPhone using Sennheiser headphones with buttons and a remote microphone. I occasionally take phone calls on my walks, and everything works splendidly. The phone rang (I’ve got a Glenn Gould ringtone), it was someone wanting to deliver some furniture to my son, who happens to live in the same building as I do. The store had my phone number rather than my son’s. So after I hung up, I pushed the pause button on my headset, holding it until I heard a beep or two. I then said, “Call [my son].” The voice answered, “Mobile or work?” I said, “Work,” and I was immediately connected.

So this morning I received a phone call. It was the PUD’s general manager reporting on an issue. After we’d finished, I said that I’d see him next Monday, as I recall my calendar having Monday, rather than the usual Tuesday, as the day of the next board meeting. I was mistaken.

After we’d completed the conversation, I held down the pause button again to get the beeps. I then said, “When is my next board meeting?” I thought that if it could place a phone call it might be able to answer the question. I was wrong.

The voice came back, “Playing Misuko Uchida.”

Oh, well. I’ll have to wait for the next iPhone this fall.
