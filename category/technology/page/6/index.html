The next big thing: a combination

May 19, 2013 by davald

Bloomberg News surveyed technology investors to discover that over 70 percent of them believe that Apple has lost its innovative edge, either permanently (28 percent) or temporarily (43 percent). It would seem that this perception correlates with the company’s stock decline.

So, this got me wondering. What would Apple have to produce to rekindle investor enthusiasm? What could be its next big thing?

Steve Jobs allowed that one reason for his success was a penchant for saying no. His goal, apparently, was to create a unique combination of hardware and software that “just worked,” and getting complicated things to work well together meant sticking to the principle that less is more.

The brief histories of the iPhone and iPad suggest an initial “revolution” followed by continuous improvement and refinement—as opposed to copy first then offer feature after feature with the hope that something sticks with consumers. Each iteration of Apple products is superior to its predecessor. Operations become faster and more elegant. Quality reigns supreme. And this strategy has been handsomely rewarded in the form of outrageous profits. People are almost fanatically anxious to pay Apple a sizable premium for the devices Jobs wrought.

There is much speculation, fueled to a large extent by Jobs himself and now by his successor, Tim Cook, that Apple has several major products in the pipeline. What could these be, and could they be as revolutionary as the iPhone and iPad?

I suspect that Apple’s “next big thing” will be a new combination of existing concepts and technologies, designed with exquisite attention to detail and ease of use. That’s the Apple way, one that investors have yet to figure out, it seems, but that millions of consumers recognize. Also, remember that many of these same investors went apeshit over mortgage-backed securities, sub-prime loans, and financial derivatives that no one could understand.

A media future

May 14, 2013 by davald

I’ll go out on a short limb here. My guess is that in a more perfect media world, all content, and I mean all, would be instantly available at any time on any device of our choosing. That world does not exist, but I think it inevitable.

What prevents the ideal from becoming reality sooner rather than later is money, of course. It’s always about money. Right now the major factions divide between content and providers. The former include creators and owners. Cable and satellite dish companies compose the latter. The networks occupy both, more or less. Against the tide of consumer demand, all are jockeying to either hold on to what they have or exploit the inevitable, albeit grudgingly.

As things now stand, I can watch some sporting events live on my iPad. I have a subscription with MLB.com that allows me to view major league baseball games, including my beloved Giants, on my iPhone, iPad, computer, and Apple TV-cum-television. The streaming quality is still inconsistent, but getting better. ESPN also streams live to my portable devices, except the Apple TV.

I pay an exorbitant amount of money to Comcast each month. Channels, in the hundreds, are “bundled” with each other and with a land line and Internet. I can also watch some programs on my Apple devices, with emphasis on some. There’s also “on demand.” But that’s a hit-and-miss proposition. Comcast doesn’t provide all episodes of all seasons of all series.

Frustration rules. Why can’t I just watch this or that when I want to?

First, baby steps. ABC recently said that it would begin streaming live content via cable providers to phones and tablets. Will that move spur others to follow? Probably.

There’s another problem with my ideal provisioning scenario: the lack of a central hub or portal through which content is acquired then streamed. I’m sure that Apple would like to play that role, and it’s already captured the lion’s share of digital music. Many of us buy television programs and movies from the iTunes store. But there’s nothing “live” in the online library.

Amazon, I suspect, stands the better chance of becoming that digital hub, since Jeff Bezos seems intent on dominating the retail world. He began with books several years ago. Now I can have just about anything delivered to my front door. As a “prime” member I can also stream media content for no extra charge. Still, nothing live.

The Nation magazine used to print a double-paged spread illustrating in graphic form the concentration of the media companies. I haven’t seen one for a while, but I recall there being only a handful of corporations pretty much owning everything. They have lots of money, which means that they can pay for bushels of lawyers and lobbyists, not to mention purchase their requisite lot of senators and representatives. They are unlikely to let the future catch them with their pants down. Rather, they will make sure that whichever way the consumer winds blow, they’ll steer things in their direction, with dollar bills attached.

But it will happen, ladies and gentlemen. We will have everything everywhere anytime. It just won’t be cheap.

Amazonian juggernaut [u]

April 25, 2013 by davald

Matthew Yglesias suggests the reason investors like Amazon, driving up its stock price, despite minuscule profits.

The company’s press release is headlined “Amazon.com Announced First Quarter Sales up 22% to $16.07 Billion” and the company’s clearly articulated strategy is to maximize sales by any means necessary. Those means include low and falling levels of profit. But that’s Jeff Bezos’ strategy, [he] persuaded his investors to back the strategy, and he is unquestionably executing the strategy. It’s a boon to consumers and a terror to the competition. How do you beat a company that’s not even trying to make money? I sure don’t know.

Yglesias calls Bezos “a remarkable figure.” Why? He set out to establish Amazon as the dominant force in multiple markets, beginning with books. Remember those early days? Now the company threatens the very existence of brick-and-mortar retailers.

Next stop: food. Yesterday, while my wife and I were strolling the streets of Capitol Hill with our daughter and new grandson, I saw an Amazon truck delivering groceries to two different residences within a span of a few minutes. Should Safeway and QFC be worried?

Amazon made $82 million in profit for last quarter. Meanwhile, Apple records net income of $104 million a day! Yet Cupertino’s stock price keeps falling and falling, dropping from over $700 last fall to around $400 recently. Amazon’s shares climbed 2.2 percent today.

Strange.

UPDATE (April 26, 2013):

Well, maybe investors are taking a second look at Amazon. As I write the company’s stock has fallen by almost eight percent. Meanwhile, tickets for Apple’s World Wide Developers Conference in June, priced at $1,600 a piece, sold out in 90 seconds. Last year, it took 12 hours to sell out. In 2010, all the tickets were bought by the eighth day.

Post-PC world or Redmond’s undoing?

April 11, 2013 by davald

The numbers disturbed. PC shipments fell dramatically last quarter. But why?

Some have speculated that it’s Microsoft’s fault in coming out with Windows 8 and “tiles,” which, according to some reports, have confused long-time Windows users. Where’s the “start” button? Others suggest that replacement cycles have lengthened, owing to the sluggish economy. (Businesses purchase the bulk of PCs.) Could it be a switch to tablets as substitutes for laptops and desktops? Have we entered the post-PC world, as Steve Jobs hinted?

Matthew Yglesias wonders:

To really know what’s up, we’re going to need to see how Mac sales compare to the industry as a whole.

Here are some numbers from Gartner via Electronista.

 

We know that Apple had supply-chain issues, likely the result of a brand new design for the iMac. Wait times were in months initially; they’re now down to days or one can pick up a new Mac at the nearest Apple store. Even so, the data in the table indicate that Apple shipped more devices from a year ago, while other vendors suffered a decline. So Apple has increased its share of the non-tablet universe, which may bolster claims that it’s all Steve Balmer’s doing.

Blogging for free

March 6, 2013 by davald

Matthew Yglesias tells us that if you have something to say, say it. But don’t expect to get paid for it.

He started out in college blogging for nothing, then eventually landed paying jobs, the latest of which is at Slate. He writes:

The fact remains that if you have things to say that you think are worthwhile and nobody is offering to pay you to say them, you ought to say them anyway for free. If enough people agree with you that those things are worthwhile, it just may lead to something.

That worked out fine for him. I presume he’s getting a comfortable paycheck now; enough to buy a new house in the D.C. area. I, however, am under no illusion that the New Yorker will be extending me an offer to write for its magazine. Ain’t gonna happen.

As you may have notice from reading this site, there are no ads. I actually have to pay WordPress for the privilege of being ad-free.

I detest ads, for one thing. So, I make no money researching issues, then expressing my viewpoints. Besides, I’d hate to become dependent on people’s visiting The Observer so that a few mills are sent my way upon each click of the mouse.

And speaking of ads, I often wonder, more so recently, why Google’s stock continues to climb while, for example, Apple’s falters. After all, Google gives away its Android operating system to the likes of Samsung and HTC and a myriad other cellphone manufacturers. So it doesn’t make any money off its OS. The lion’s share of Google’s revenues come from mobile ads, the kind that populate webpages—the good, the bad, and the ugly. It also charges retailers to appear in its search findings. I understand that Google’s price-to-earnings ratio is about three times that of Apple, and Apple makes more profit in a quarter than Google makes in a year.

This article in Bloomberg News offers an explanation for the divergence.

Investors are willing to pay more for each dollar of Google’s earnings relative to Apple amid optimism that Google, with more than 40 percent of the U.S. online-advertising market, will command even more of the $37.3 billion that businesses spend each year to reach Web audiences. [Note: Apple’s last-quarter profits exceeded this annual total.]

But I have to ask: Does anyone actually buy something as a result of seeing a Google ad? I, for one, don’t even notice them. Or perhaps it’s all subliminal, that I’m drawn to make my next car a Jaguar and my next vacation in the Bahamas. But there’s a problem for the advertiser. I will likely never buy another car; the 2nd-generation Prius has low miles and will surely outlast me. Nor do I like to travel, anywhere. The joke around here is that I’ve been to Bothell.

I do understand that it would be easy to join the crowd. I could invite companies to post their ads on my blog. They’d pay Google for the privilege, I guess, in the apparent hope that one out of every 10 readers (about the total thus far) would click on the ad then purchase a whatever.

As I said, I don’t get it. Blogging for free I get. Alms, anyone?

An “irreversible” trend

February 24, 2013 by davald

I finally got around to watching the “lost interview” with Steve Jobs. It was done in 1995, or about 10 years after Jobs was unceremoniously ousted from the company he had helped form, and a year before he rejoined Apple. That interview revealed or, more accurately, confirmed those qualities that made Jobs the most valuable CEO on the planet.

Jobs talked about “products,” and why some companies, like IBM and Xerox, were clueless. Of the latter, Jobs said that that its research units developed several revolutionary technologies that would have allowed the company to control the computer industry. Why didn’t they?

Jobs talked about ideas and execution. For IBM and HP and Xerox, creating a few successful products implied a successful “process” that could be simply replicated on new product development. But that was where they failed. For Jobs it was all about execution, and each “insanely great” idea required a unique collaboration between and among very bright people. That collaboration almost necessarily created friction, which, in Jobs’s mind, was a good thing, for it demonstrated both talent and a strong commitment to quality. The art was in enabling the quest for great products (“content”) to drive outcomes, the result of countless iterations of trying to squeeze the original ideas into a particular form factor.

Also, these clueless companies at one time enjoyed monopolies in their particular fields. With a handful of products dominating certain niches, the companies emphasized marketing and sales volume over R&D. In turn, those who were good at selling things within the companies ultimately led them.

Microsoft, whose success Jobs did not begrudge, was another company enjoying near-monopoly status with its OS and Office suite. So, who now leads Redmond? A sales guy. Jobs did criticize Microsoft for being “tasteless” and lacking “spirit.” Their offerings were merely “pedestrian.” Ouch.

As for Apple’s fortunes, keeping in mind that the interview was still two years away from his becoming the company’s CEO, Jobs said that Cupertino was on a downward trajectory to its eventual demise. He called the condition “irreversible.” When Jobs did re-assume the reins in 1997, Apple was facing bankruptcy in three months.

From 1997 to 2011, when he died, Jobs performed a miracle, judged against his 1995 pronouncement. He took the company from doom to dominance, creating the most valuable company in the world with a clutch of products that re-defined personal technology.

By the way, I find some irony in the fact that Apple charges to either rent or buy the “lost interview,” whereas Amazon’s “prime” customers can stream it for free, which is what I did. With further irony, Jobs predicted in 1995 that “the web” would emerge as the dominant force of commerce, with people buying billions of dollars worth or products via the Internet. Gosh, which company comes to mind? It’s not Apple, but the name begins with an ‘A.’

The robots did it

February 11, 2013 by davald

Why is American society so unequal? Some blame technology: robots and other electronic devices have reduced the need for human labor, which means an expanding surplus of people with nothing to do.

In a recent op-ed for the New York Times Harvard economist Greg Mankiw writes:

Over the last several decades, for the most part, the wages of workers without any higher education have stagnated, while the wages of those with advanced degrees have risen. The main forces driving these trends are technological change, which tends to increase the demand for skilled workers relative to unskilled workers, and, to a lesser extent, international trade.

But economist Dean Baker begs to differ:

Mankiw asserts as a fact that technology is responsible for the upward redistribution of income over the last three decades, but it is not clear that the evidence supports his story. After all technology had a much larger impact in increasing productivity in the decades from 1947 to 1973 yet workers shared in these gains more or less equally.

A chart may suffice.

What about productivity?

Hmmm. When worker productivity was at its highest, in the post-War decades, workers took a larger share of income.

As I recall, there was a lot of new technology in those days, especially at the consumer level, from wide-scale adoption of the telephone and television to cars and freeways and airplanes. (As a three-month-old infant I accompanied my mother on a very expensive voyage from New York to San Francisco. I’m told that the plane was a propeller-driven DC 9. That would have been in 1947. Fast forward to 1969, the year I graduated from Berkeley. I traveled by jet, I tell you, from San Francisco to Philadelphia. In that period our family switched from a rotary telephone on a party line to a touch-tone, single-line device. We purchased a color television, and transistor radios became ubiquitous. My first official job was working with business-class mainframe computers in New Jersey; there were none until the 60s. I could go on. Oh, and they put a man on the moon.)

Dean Baker offers some alternative explanations for rising inequality:

The villains that some of us would point to are anti-union measures by government and businesses that have weakened workers’ bargaining power, trade policy that was designed to put less-educated workers in competition with people in the developing world while largely protecting the most highly educated workers, patent and copyright policy that increased the rents pulled out of the economy for these monopolies, and macroeconomic policy that has led to more unemployment in the last three decades than in the early post-war period. High unemployment tends to disproportionately hit less educated workers, both by having more impact on their probability of being unemployed and reducing their wages.

Speaking of Comcast

January 28, 2013 by davald

I am embarrassed to admit that I pay Comcast a small fortune each month. It didn’t happen all at once. My bill began relatively small, then by increments grew, so that I now write a check for nearly $300 a month. The majority of the bill is television-related. I seem to be paying far too much money for literally dozens of channels I never watch or ever will.

Matthew Yglesias reminds us that bundling is a good thing. If we were to go à la carte, we’d wind up paying even more, even though we turn to only a small number of channels for our regular viewing.

But I’d really like to pay less for my phone, cable, and Internet. I know that the French get all three for less than $50 a month. The South Koreans and Japanese also pay far less than we do, and they enjoy download speeds many times faster than mine, and I’ve got a very fast connection.

What’s going on? Here’s Yglesias:

What would change the game here isn’t unbundling, it’s competition. If a typical neighborhood was served by a dozen different cable providers, then consumers could get a much better deal. But that’s the problem with the telecom market—there’s very little competition, so you end up getting screwed. But the bundling isn’t what’s screwing you [my hyperlink].

Gadget nightmares

January 28, 2013 by davald

I fall comfortably within the category of Old Fart. Yet, though I may not be as sharp as I once was and I occasionally forget where I put my glasses, I am no stranger to technology and electronic devices. My office has more gadgets than the flutters of an R.A. Dickey knuckleball.

I am also a stickler for getting all these gadgets to play nice with one another. And when they don’t, I get cranky and irritable as I obsess over possible causes and remedies for the disconnects.

A few weeks ago my home wi-fi stopped working. WTF? It was functioning just a moment before, then nada. I hadn’t done anything to disturb the elaborate setup. As I recall, I was listening to music being streamed from my computer to my Airplay-enabled receiver-cum-speakers.

So, I immediately descended from the state of normal to slightly unhinged, imagining all sorts of ghouls and goblins responsible for the collapse. I began my troubleshooting expedition, at first with some surprising equanimity. But when the standard solutions failed to restore the network, my mind eventually succumbed to rage as I fingered the usual suspects, starting with Comcast, the provider of phone, Internet, and television content.

I should briefly describe the devices. The cable modem was connected to my Apple Time Capsule’s WAN port. I ran another Ethernet cable from the TC to my MacPro (circa 2008). Two laser printers, one color, were connected to the TC. Now here’s the thing. I could access the Internet by plugging the cable modem directly to the MacPro via Ethernet. But the TC could not “talk” with the modem.

I powered down the modem, then restarted it. Nothing. I did the same with the TC. I performed resets and factory resets galore but to no avail. I called Comcast’s technical support. The technician said that he was seeing a “good” signal, so the problem must be on my end. I was told to go through the same procedures I just described. Negative function. Comcast would pay me a visit.

As it turned out, a coax connector had come loose in a junction box. A bit of tightening here and there by the field tech—service restored!

Fast forward to last Wednesday. I was minding my own business when I lost both the Internet and the wifi network. I went through all the steps I had done previously. Nothing worked. I contacted Apple’s support. The conclusion: must be the cable modem. I consult with Comcast again. Okay, maybe I need to swap out the modem, which required a visit to their store the next morning.

The new modem is a “gateway,” meaning it’s also a router with all the ports of my TC. But as an Apple aficionado I’m determined to get their products to operate effectively. I spend the better part of Thursday trying to get the gateway to work with the TC, before I give up. I had scheduled another visit from a Comcast technician for Friday morning.

There is both good and bad news to report. Since my building has built-in Ethernet, the technician was able to connect my Airport Express directly to the wired network, bypassing wireless altogether. (The AE feeds an Ethernet hub, from which I run cables to my Apple TV, receiver, and Blu-ray player.) The new setup consists of an Ethernet cable between the gateway and an Ethernet jack. The modem also provides a wifi signal, but only at 2.4GHz; the TC and the AE include a 5GHz channel. After some initial problems connecting my iOS devices to the new network, I changed the security from the default setting to WPA2 personal, which solved that problem.

The bad news is that we could never get the TC to work, although we succeeded in getting both of my Airport Expresses to play nice with the modem/router. Could it be that the TC was defective? It was only six months old.

I scheduled an appointment with the Genius Bar at the nearest Apple Store—my gadget mecca. Esra, the “genius,” at first got the TC to join the in-store network. But when she restarted the device, nothing. Well, the TC is still under warranty. Voila, I got a brand new Time Capsule, which retails for $300!

We knew that this story was not going to have a happy ending. When I got home I plugged in the new TC, following the directions, of course. It could not establish a network nor access the Internet. I got the same error messages as before.

Well, it’s not all bad. I do have a functioning network. I no longer have streaming problems, since the media devices are hard-wired to the gateway via Ethernet. My iOS devices automatically connect to the 2.4GHz network, albeit at slower speeds than I achieved using the 5GHz channel. My teacher-wife can access the Internet and print reams of paper for her students. What’s not to like?

It bothers me to have that brand new Time Capsule sitting idly in my closet. I keep the doors closed so I don’t have to see failure.

Sneakers

January 9, 2013 by davald

You may remember the movie, Sneakers, which starred Robert Redford and Ben Kingsley, initially as college students; think of Allen and Gates or merry pranksters Jobs and Woz. Kingsley’s character was caught hacking into what was then a nascent Internet to transfer money from “the bad guys” to “good” organizations. Redford’s character Martin, who was dispatched to fetch a pizza, observed the police and government officials apprehending his co-conspirator from the inside of his VW mini-van. So he left, evading arrest, then making his way to Canada, before returning to the states decades later under an alias. We see him as a middle-aged man running a security company engaged in trying to exploit companies’ computer systems, providing safeguards in the event they’re successful.

This is all subtext to the principal plot, which involves a secret black box, the mother of all code breakers. Former NSA agents “hire” Redford’s firm to steal the device. But Redford and his team grow suspicious, and we eventually discover that Kingsley’s character is behind the caper. He wants the box so as to “change the world” for the benefit of the masses—Marx in the digital age.

The movie entertains, as much because of the device’s plausible existence and its ramifications as the actors and script. If there isn’t such a black box, surely there are both “good” and “bad” guys working to design one.

Which brings me to this New York Times article. It seems that Iranian “hackers” have broken into financial firms’ computer systems.

The skill required to carry out attacks on this scale has convinced United States government officials and security researchers that they are the work of Iran, most likely in retaliation for economic sanctions and online attacks by the United States.

“There is no doubt within the U.S. government that Iran is behind these attacks,” said James A. Lewis, a former official in the State and Commerce Departments and a computer security expert at the Center for Strategic and International Studies in Washington.

The attackers were not after money, the paper reports. Rather, their objective was “disruption.”

We should appreciate the implications of cyber insecurity, given the near meltdown of the world’s financial centers following the collapse of the housing bubble. Money talks, big time. But we should also appreciate that the burgeoning financial sector no longer relies on dollars and cents; now it’s all about 1’s and 0’s, bits and bytes. Whoever controls the data controls the world.

The article ends on a chilling note:

The attackers said last week that they had no intention of halting their campaign. “Officials of American banks must expect our massive attacks,” they wrote. “From now on, none of the U.S. banks will be safe.”
