Grubered

November 19, 2011 by davald

I admit to being intrigued by John Gruber, who seems to be ubiquitous in the world of gadgetry, mostly those from Apple. His website, Daring Fireball, contains nuggets of technological insight that reflect an almost philosophical approach to software, hardware, and their interrelationship.

He’s also a big fan of typography, especially the font Helvetica. I share that appreciation. So did Steve Jobs, who wandered into a class on calligraphy at Reed College; that experience may have led, and most certainly contributed, to Jobs’s fascination with WYSIWYG: the Macintosh-cum-PageMaker-cum-laser printers.

Gruber recently posted on Isaacson’s biography of Steve Jobs. It’s not pretty. Gruber believes that Jobs chose the wrong writer, even though Isaacson’s tomes on Ben Franklin and Albert Einstein had been favorably reviewed. Nice company, I should think.

Gruber wonders, between the lines, “Who was Steve Jobs?” Or, to be more precise, “What did he bring to technology that made him sui generis?” Isaacson failed to address these fundamental inquiries, opines Gruber, probably because Isaacson is neither a techie nor an artiste, attitudes combined in the person of Jobs, who sought to intersect the humanities with technology.

The New Yorker‘s Malcolm Gladwell also gets Grubered. The author of Blink, Outliers, and other amazingly successful books, Gladwell called Jobs a “tweaker,” as opposed to “innovator.” Gruber won’t have any of this. If one wants an appropriate avatar for tweaker look no further than Bill Gates, tweaker extraordinaire, suggests Gruber.

I think Gruber is being a bit harsh on Isaacson, although Gladwell deserves general criticism for his rush to and obsession with connecting dots, however unwarranted. Isaacson emphasizes Jobs’s internalizing his father’s attention to detail. How things looked and felt and fit together mattered to Paul Jobs, and that sensitivity rubbed off on his son.

After several years of rather far-out experiences—including a sojourn to India, living in a commune, and episodes with LSD—Jobs started Apple Computer with his high school techie friend, Steve Wozniak. “Woz” knew motherboards, and circuits, and basic software. He was the quintessential geek who later obtained his engineering degree from Berkeley. That merging between the two Steves formed the basis for a new and very different computer company, named after the fruit of the commune’s orchards south of Portland.

Now, I have no idea how Steve did it. Was it the force of his personality? Did he see things that others missed? Was he obsessed, if not a bit wacko? I’d say “yes” to all. Isaacson reports these idiosyncrasies, those traits that made him unique—traits and their combination impossible to replicate in a single individual.

Jobs, knowing that his time on earth was prematurely ending, told Isaacson that he wanted his biography to explain to his children who he was and what he was up to during all those hours away from home. I believe that Isaacson captured both Jobs’s quirkiness and his unbridled passion for Apple and the products conceived at One Infinity Loop. I believe his children, having read the book, will understand their father as the genius, innovator, and, yes, tweaker he was. And that he “saw things differently.”

Here’s to the crazy ones.

One of Jobs’s legacies

November 7, 2011 by davald

Apple makes a lot of money. The chart below breaks down mobile phone industry profits. This past quarter Apple iPhone shares were but four percent of total unit sales; with the rollout of the iPhone 4s, Apple will likely be the top seller in the current quarter. Yet, Apple took over half of the profits!

Clearly Apple’s margins are much higher than those of Samsung, Nokia, et cetera. Those who purchase Apple products are willing to pay what turns out to be a small premium for greater value.

But there’s something else going on. Apple has left the rest of the electronics industry in the dust when it comes to manufacturing processes and supply chains. Apple’s costs are simply much lower than anyone else’s. The company builds a better product for less.

Those who own Apple stock can thank Steve once again. (I don’t, however, much to my wife’s chagrin.)

As for the end

November 2, 2011 by davald

I’ve just finished reading Isaacson’s biography of Steve Jobs. So I’ll probably post a few Jobs-related comments from time to time. Like right now.

In the “coda,” Isaacson asked Jobs, who was close to “achieving” death, about the afterlife, did it exist and whether or not there is a God. Jobs said on the question of a deity, “I’m about fifty-fifty…”

Then, after a period of silence and reflection, Jobs said: “On the other hand, perhaps it’s like an on-off switch. Click! And you’re gone.”

Jobs paused again before concluding, “Maybe that’s why I never liked to put on-off switches on Apple devices.”

No divisions, less is better

October 31, 2011 by davald

I’ve been reading Walter Isaacson’s biography of Steve Jobs. I’m just at the part where Apple is developing the iPod and iTunes to create a new music ecosystem. Why were Jobs and Apple able to pull this off and other companies, who had the requisite technology, unable to do so?

Jobs suggested that much of the problem with places like Sony, which arguably had all the tools in place (remember Walkman?), was that it was broken into divisions, each with its own profit-and-loss responsibilities. Thus, each division inevitably competed with the others, frustrating the collective effort necessary to put it all together. Apple, on the other hand, had only a single “division” and just one P&L.

But Apple also had Jobs. Key to his success, I think, was not only his exquisite and exhausting attention to detail. He understood that less is better. It was his ability to just say no that enabled Apple to concentrate on doing a few things extremely well.

Microsoft, as we know, charted a different course under the guise of freedom and choice. Bill Gates decided at the very beginning to make his money licensing the software to dozens of different hardware vendors.

When Apple was in the doldrums and at risk of having to shutter its doors, Gates and others strongly urged Apple to license its software to outside hardware manufacturers, just as Microsoft had done. And when Jobs was in the wilderness running both NeXT and Pixar, Apple’s then-CEO permitted a couple of companies to build Mac OS-compatible devices. Upon his return to Apple in 1997, Jobs quickly terminated the agreements.

Jobs believed strongly in closely integrating the software and hardware, unlike the Windows PC side. He reckoned that there was too much fragmentation under the Redmond regime. There were, and continue to be, so many problems with compatibility, as hardware vendors would incorporate different tweaks—and then all those third-party applications and utilities. It’s a wonder that PCs worked at all.

Nor was Jobs’s focus just on limiting the number of devices Apple would produce. The devices themselves were always on diets. Jobs’s creations had fewer features than PC counterparts. Just compare an HP laptop with a MacBook Air. The former is stodgy, ugly, assembled with screws, thick, heavy—but, my, it has a lot of ports and slots. Indeed, PC manufacturers seemed to suffer from “ports gap.” Jobs killed off the floppy drive and, more recently, the optical drive—neither of which will you find in the Airs. (Image from Apple)

As much as Jobs loved Apple and its products he did not hesitate to pull the plug on devices. Nor did he worry about cannibalizing his own products. For example, as iPhone sales skyrocket, iPod sales are down. No problem, since the iPhone includes the iPod functionality—by design. More people are buying iMacs than Mac Pros. Kill the latter.

Though Jobs would behave as a martinet more often than not, he also realized that good products require close collaboration amongst those with different, but complementary, skill sets. So Jobs insisted that the places he superintended be designed to facilitate accidental encounters.

Isaacson writes about Pixar’s being in need of new headquarters, having outgrown its old. Jobs purchased the abandoned Del Monte cannery in Emeryville, at which I happened to work during one college summer (a really crummy job, by the way). Always paying attention to design and function, Jobs ensured that employees would have to run into each other, even on trips to the bathrooms, of which there were only two, at opposite ends of the studio.

Jobs’s org chart differed remarkably from the conventional. Instead of an inverted tree, Jobs had fashioned a wheel. He was at the center surrounded closely by a small hub of direct reports. In most organizations, the branches become insular silos. Jobs would have none of that. (The following graphic from Fortune.

You may have seen the designs for Apple’s new headquarters in Cupertino, on property acquired, ironically enough, from HP. It reminded me of the space station hovering above the moon in 2001: A Space Odyssey. Again, employees, no matter what product they’re working on, will necessarily encounter one another, which yields ad hoc conversations that Jobs believed would stimulate innovative thinking via cross-pollination. (I could imagine Jobs sitting in the center of his new space station, sans shoes, eating apples and drinking Odwalla juice, pondering really cool products.)

Jobs also detested slide presentations inside the workplace; he reserved them for product rollouts, and he was a master at giving them. Jobs expected his leadership team to come to meetings fully prepared and ready to brainstorm. Jobs himself liked to think aloud using a whiteboard and markers. It was important for him to see things. He couldn’t be bothered with spreadsheets. If an employee had an idea for a product, he or she had better have a physical mockup available for Jobs to touch and feel.

It may sound Pollyannaish, but Jobs really did want to marry the arts with technology, and he believed that intuition was more important than intelligence. There are numerous examples of this preference in Isaacson’s book. One stands out so far. Jobs said that most computer vendors let the engineers determine how a machine is designed. The guts would come first, leaving those responsible for the containers to house the engineering contents. The result, thought Jobs, was a whole lot of ugly.

For Jobs, the look and feel and function by the eventual user were most important, qualities that he would intuit. The engineers would be tasked to devise the appropriate components to make them fit together seamlessly and attractively. That’s why Jobs made Jony Ive the second most powerful person at Apple; he is the award-winning industrial designer par excellence. In Ive’s studio was a machine that would convert computer drawings to foam models that could then be painted and even given heft with lead shots, if needed. And that’s where Jobs liked to spend a lot of his time, touching, feeling, holding the prototypes. The artist at work.

Apple, you will learn should you read the biography, is a much different place than any other organizations you may have worked, visited, or read about. As electronics companies try to ape Jobs’s devices, I’m sure that some of them will try to copy his management style and even his workplaces.

They will likely fail at this and produce, in Jobsian lingo, “nothing but shit.”

A worthy paean to Mr. Jobs

October 25, 2011 by davald

From Mr. Fry:

The quality I especially revered in him was his refusal to show contempt for his customers by fobbing them off with something that was “good enough”. Whether it was the packaging, the cabling, the use of screen space, the human interfaces, the colours, the flow, the feel, the graphical or textural features, everything had to be improved upon and improved upon until it was, to use the favourite phrase of the early Mac pioneers “insanely great”. It had to be so cool that you gasped. It had to feel good in the hand, look good to the eye and it had to change things. It changed things because it made users want to use the devices as they had never been used before. As I used to say of the Mac in the early days, “it makes me jump out of bed early to start work”. People may not think so but I’m as lazy as can be, and the creative, human-based implementation of technology in such a way as to encourage labour and thereby invigorate innovation and change is a remarkable achievement in so potentially dull a sector as computing.

Early adopter

October 12, 2011 by davald

It’s really a synonym for impatient. Can’t wait to get my hands on the newest and greatest of all things Apple. So, today is the big day, right? Cupertino releases iOS 5, a Lion update (which comes with an improved version of Safari), and new Apple TV software. The problem is, it seems that every Apple aficionado is equally impatient; we all had to have the few stuff—right now.

Well, there must be a gazillion of us, because it’s taking an eternity for the software to download. Would you believe three hours for the iOS 5?

Worse, I had started downloading the new iOS for my iPhone 4. It was taking a while, so I went for my walk, assuming that the download would be completed upon my return. No such luck. There was a problem restoring or backing up the phone. Try again, dummy.

Fortunately, I have a little bit of life outside the reality-distortion field. Like getting a haircut and now waiting for the Tigers and Rangers game to start, after a two-hour rain delay.

Did I say impatient?

Social media

October 11, 2011 by davald

In my previous life I was social. That is, I was busy being around lots of different people depending on the venue and purpose, from protesting U.S. involvement in Central America and the “Auschwitz of Puget Sound” to helping create liturgies and dancing crazy into the night.

Today, not so much. Indeed, save for my official duties as an elected official and a weekly lunch with a couple of “philosophers” I’m a hermit. Guess what? I like it.

Several months ago it was suggested that I join Facebook. I was reluctant, perhaps mostly because I didn’t understand the phenomenon—and I’m not sure that I do now. I did sign up, then after a short while, disappeared into my blogging and routine, which consists of reading, walking, dieting (Type 2), watching sports and a few TV programs, fixing meals, and cleaning house—but no Facebook. As I said, this suits me fine.

Yesterday Facebook finally agreed to Apple’s terms and conditions, making its app available for iOS. To those living in the darkness, that’s Apple-speak for the operating system that runs the iPhone, iPod Touch, and the iPad. I’m an inveterate gadget guy, possessing both the iPhone and the iPad, so I downloaded the free app, which, to be useful, means I had to rejoin the Facebook community(?).

Truth be told, I’d like to instigate a revolution, as long as I don’t have to get off my butt. Revolutions require a critical mass of people dedicated to change. Social networking, we’ve discovered with increased frequency, can help. Indeed, it may be crucial to organizing, delivering critical information to the masses. Occupy Wall Street. Battle Seattle. Overthrow dictators. Elect your favorite politician. Social media makes shit happen, or so it seems.

So I’ve rejoined Facebook, as much to try out the new app, as to start that revolution.

Now, everybody, raise your right fist. Higher.

Inevitable, yes, but…

October 6, 2011 by davald

…still a shock. Steve Jobs is dead.

I was sitting in front of the television watching the Phillies-Cardinals game with my iPad in my lap. A notice from MacWorld flashed on the display: “Steve Jobs has died.” I immediately went to the NY Times website. There it was under “breaking news.” Then to Apple’s website. Simple. Elegant. Final.

I had intended this morning to write about my memories of baseball. I’ll leave that to another day. What better occasion, now that Steve has succumbed to “Life’s best invention,” to share my Apple experiences, of which there are plenty.

It was in Bellevue that I first encountered an Apple product—the IIe. Clunky by today’s standards, but more polished than anything else in the storeroom. I was the owner of a dual-floppy (and I do mean floppy) CP/M machine, complete with a keyboard and a black-and-white monitor. Put in the system floppy. Voila, the ‘A’ prompt. Henceforth, nothing but keystrokes. Yet I did not purchase the IIe; a bit too expensive for me back then.

Fast forward to 1984. I’m in the Everett Mall, which then had a computer store. There it was: the Macintosh. You all remember the commercial directed by Ridley Scott. The 1984 Super Bowl brought the computer for the rest of us. I could not wait to get up close and personal. There on that tiny 9-inch screen was Microsoft Word. That’s right. Gates et al wrote for Apple before the PC. And it had a mouse! Type a few lines. Italicize this. Put these letters in bold. Copy, drag, and paste. Wow!

I finally bought my first Apple product two years later—the Macintosh Plus. I added a LaserWriter printer. Lots of money. But, hey, I was going into desktop publishing. Besides, I could write off the products as a business expense, which I did—legitimately. However, it was frustratingly difficult to draw accident scenes on it. (I consulted forensically on traffic accident reconstruction.) Slow with that tiny screen. But the printed product, after much painstaking work, looked neat, clean, and more professional than drafting old school, even if I did have to tape several letter-sized pages together. Also, I sold my IBM Selectric, since I could now do my reports and invoices on the Mac and LaserWriter. Heaven.

I have not stopped buying Apple products since that first Macintosh purchase. Here’s a list, which may not be exhaustive:

  • Macintosh II
  • PowerBook
  • Macintosh IIci
  • iMac (turquoise)
  • Newton
  • G4 desktop (PowerPC)
  • G5 desktop (PowerPC)
  • G4 laptop
  • MacPro (current)
  • MacBook Pro (passed on to my son)
  • iPad (first edition)
  • iPad 2
  • MacBook (for teacher-wife)
  • MacBook Air (for teacher-wife)
  • Time Capsule
  • Apple TV (1st and second editions)
  • iPod (80 GB)
  • iPod nano (for teacher-wife)
  • iPhone 3
  • iPhone 3s (for teacher-wife)
  • iPhone 4

(Note: If any of these get stolen, proof of my inventory is now in the clouds.)

During Apple’s dark days, between Steve Jobs’s tenures, the Marysville School District, where my wife teaches, decided to abandon Macs in favor of Wintel machines. I spoke against it at a public meeting, no doubt ineffectively, since passion ruled my mind. I warned the district that it would soon see a bloated IT staff and budget with technical problems that would overwhelm support personnel. Besides, I said, the cheap PCs would likely crash, break down, or just stop working altogether. I was right. To this day, unfortunately, the district has stuck with its unworkable and unmanageable PC-cum-Windows (still using XP) system, and teachers have to wait months for fixes. How much better for students and teachers alike if they could use MacBook Airs, iPads, Apple TVs connected to widescreen televisions—with everything connected wirelessly.

The PUD also jettisoned its mostly-Mac installed base a year or so before the school district. That decision—which probably made sense at the time given Apple’s downward trajectory—also led to much higher IT costs and a larger support staff. The utility is still using Windows XP. It’s familiar and most of the bugs have been worked out. There are a few isolated Macs in the communications department, which employs graphic designers.

Jobs never pushed his products to the enterprise. Instead, they are serving as Trojan Horses. Employees buy Apple products for their own use, then bring them to work. If they’re high enough in the food chain, they insist on IT’s accommodating their iPhones and iPads. Gradually, corporations are becoming more Apple friendly.

This includes the PUD, which is set to adopt iPhones rather than Android devices; iOS is more secure. This past week the board witnessed a demonstration of iPads, which will eventually be used by commissioners and upper management in lieu of printed binders. Of course, I was all over this, although my colleagues are a bit wary. The Trojan Horse has entered.

Apple products just work, an expression I’m sure that you’ve heard more than once. All of my old devices have either been sold or handed down to family and friends. They have not been tossed, because they still function—although I don’t remember what happened to the Newton.

While I am terribly saddened by the news of Jobs’s demise, I think Apple is just starting. While it’s the most valuable company on the planet, it will continue to be insanely great. Just look at all the patents the company has either registered or secured.

We ain’t seen nothing yet.
