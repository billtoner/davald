What’s in a number?

October 5, 2011 by davald

The number 5, to be precise. Apple, despite rampant speculation, did not release a fifth generation iPhone, at least not one ending in the suffix ‘5.’ This prompted pervasive “disappointment.” Why? Because pundits and fanboys had been led to believe—based only on speculation and rumor, mind you—that new CEO Tim Cook would unveil the iPhone 5.

Also, watchers were “underwhelmed” by Mr. Cook’s “performance.” He’s no Steve Jobs. Fancy that.

But what did Apple present yesterday in Cupertino? Quite a bit, actually.

No, it’s not an iPhone 5; it’s an iPhone 4S. Get it: the ‘s’ substituted for ‘5.’ Apple did not change the “form factor,” an evidently favorite expression of geeks and technologists. The iPhone 4S looks just like the iPhone 4: same dimensions, although the S weighs three grams more.

It’s what’s under the hood that many pundits have overlooked. Here’s a table from Apple Insider comparing the specs of the “old” and the “new” iPhones.

What should matter most to users is both usability and capability. Let’s peruse the key features of the S:

  • a new A5 processor (same as in iPad 2) that will double CPU performance;
  • that same processor will boost graphics capabilities seven-fold;
  • for a hefty sum, you can purchase the S with 64 GB of RAM, equivalent to the high-end iPad;
  • extended battery life;
  • a “world phone” that can run on either GSM (AT&T and Sprint and Europe) or CDMA (Verizon)—I suspect that Apple will save some production costs here;
  • a new and greatly improved camera, with more megapixels and much better lenses; it’s also faster and much easier to use than the current iPhone, which already takes excellent pictures;
  • full 1080 HD video;
  • the new iOS software, version 5 (see, you do get a ‘5’ after all);
  • iCloud;
  • iTunes Match (all your music in the cloud—i.e., on Apple servers—making songs available on all Mac devices);
  • streaming of music, photos, and video—from the cloud and to your Apple TV-connected devices;
  • automatic backup;
  • wireless set up and transfer (no need to plug the iOS device into a computer); and
  • Siri (“your humble personal assistant”).

This new iPhone, though not a ‘5,’ is vastly superior to anything on the market.

As for acknowledging the surge in Android-based phones (cheaper and inferior), Apple will allow you to buy the existing iPhone 4 for under a $100 and the 3GS (which was twice as fast as the 3) can be had for nothing. Yep. Free. It will run iOS 5.

Don’t weep for Apple. It’s still just getting started.

So, why exactly are we in Afghanistan?

October 4, 2011 by davald

Most, if not all, electronics, from iPhones to LED lightbulbs to X-ray machines use rare-earth elements. As the name suggests, the elements are not found in abundance. The US is 100% dependent on imported rare-earth elements for the manufacture of its devices. China happens to have 92% of the known world total. But guess where we’ve found a bunch more?

Yep. Afghanistan.

The October 2011 issue of Scientific American tells us:

Geologists say newfound deposits in the embattled country could fulfill the world’s desire for rare-earth and critical minerals and end opium’s local stranglehold in the process.

Gosh. So that’s why all the hardware and killing.

Oh, and those costs. Past $461 billion and counting.

Cupertino cupids

October 4, 2011 by davald

Apple’s top brass, sans Mr. Jobs, takes the stage this morning to “talk about iPhone.” Per usual, lots of speculation and rumors about what version 5 will look like and, more important, what’s inside. Of course, the product or products unveiled this morning will blow away the competition, such as it is.

One interesting tidbit is that Apple may also introduce less-spectacular phones for a lot less money. If true, I suspect that Mr. Cook et al recognize that, while it’s nice to extract half the profits from the entire phone industry, the less expensive Android units, manufactured by an array of different companies, are grabbing more and more market share. Application developers are necessarily gravitating toward Android, without, as yet, abandoning the more lucrative iOS ecosystem. For them many is better than few, even if they earn more revenue from iOS. Some have even speculated that Apple will give away an iPhone model and offset the loss via a percentage of wireless contracts from Sprint, AT&T, Verizon, and others.

Speaking of Sprint, the company has evidently agreed to spend $20 billion to acquire 30 million iPhone 5s over the next four years. That’s huge, and an astonishing admission of just how important the Apple units are to wireless vendors.

Finally, one of the rumors spreading across the blogosophere is that the new iPhone 5 will include Assistant, based on software developed by Siri, which Apple acquired in the spring of last year. One of Siri’s co-founders had this to say about the new software:

Apple’s ‘mainstreaming’ Artificial Intelligence in the form of a Virtual Personal Assistant is a groundbreaking event. I’d go so far as to say it is a World-Changing event.

But is the world ready to be changed? We’ll see. But Apple does make us fall in love with its gadgets.

The trouble with Macs

September 12, 2011 by davald

Quite simply, I crave them. Enough, as it turns out, to buy, but eventually return, three of them in the last couple of months.

You see, I, like millions of others around the globe, know a good thing when I touch, feel, and use it. There is nothing quite like an Apple product.

Yes, I have inhabited the dark side, when forced. What an awful experience. Today I can truly say that I’m Windows-cum-PC free. It’s all Apple all the time.

Okay, so why did I return the splendid gadgets. Well, I realized that I had no niche to fill in my daily life. I already have a Mac Pro with a marvelous screen and enough accessories to prompt the label “NORAD.” I also have the iPad 2, a beautiful device like no other. Then there is the iPhone 4. My wife has a MacBook Air, the smaller version and previous generation. Amazing. So what happened?

After checking out the many reviews of the latest MacBook Airs, I had to have one, up close and personal. As one reviewer put it: the best computer in the world; period. Perfect, said another. They’re right. But I really didn’t need a laptop, especially since I already have the iPad 2. If I want to create and otherwise produce on a computer, I have the inestimably capable Mac Pro (2008 version), with all its add-ons. Moreover, why would I walk down to the local coffee shop for free WI-FI, when I’ve got 61 Mbs in my downtown apartment? Oh, and I also have an espresso machine if I need the latte.

Ah, but wouldn’t you like an even larger display, like the 27-inch monitor for the latest and greatest iMac? Yeah, that’s the ticket. I’ll get a new iMac. Which I did. Beautiful. Gorgeous. Super fast. And huge. Maybe too huge. The smaller, 21.5-in. iMac might work. But I have an excellent Apple Cinema HD display, which is 23-inch. Just about the ideal size for my workspace. I returned the iMac, with a bit of sadness.

But that MBA is so alluring. So I ordered the top-of-the-line 13-inch model. What can I say? It really is a treasure. Jobs and Ive know what they’re doing.

Yep, I let that one go, too. Again, it’s not practical, right?

As for the money that’s apparently burning a hole in my pocket, there’s always the next iPhone and iPad. Or, the Mac Pro won’t last forever, will it?

Apple’s safe. I’m forever at its mercy.

My neighbor Steve Jobs

August 31, 2011 by davald

Okay, not my neighbor, but Lisen Stromberg’s. A very nice, personal note on Steve Jobs, a friendly family man. She writes:

From then on, when I saw him holding his executive meetings in our neighborhood, I didn’t hesitate to smile and say hi. Steve always returned the favor, proving he may be a genius, but he is also a good neighbor.

In time, things changed. The walks were less frequent, the gait slower, the smile not so ready. Earlier this year, when I saw Steve and his wife walking down our street holding hands, I knew something was different. Now, so does the rest of the world.

While Newsweek and the Wall Street Journal and CNET continue to drone on about the impact of the Steve Jobs era, I won’t be pondering the MacBook Air I write on or the iPhone I talk on. I will think of the day I saw him at his son’s high school graduation. There Steve stood, tears streaming down his cheeks, his smile wide and proud, as his son received his diploma and walked on into his own bright future, leaving behind a good man and a good father who can be sure of the rightness of this, perhaps his most important legacy of all.

I do have a nagging curiosity. Steve is a very wealthy guy. How much are reporters being paid these days?

Wishing for apples

August 30, 2011 by davald

Some time ago the Marysville School District jettisoned its Apple computers and switched to the dark side of cheap, largely unworkable, and frustratingly complicated Wintel machines. I predicted to a small body of citizens and staff that the district’s IT costs and personnel would mushroom immediately. As it happens, my prediction proved correct. Worse, now that the district is locked in to Redmond it is now decades behind in adopting newer, easier, and more robust technologies—the kind developed by Cupertino.

This past Sunday I accompanied my wife to her classroom in Marysville. Against the far wall were six Acer desktop machines all running Windows XP, a now-ancient operating system. Running from each computer were separate cables to connect the display, the mouse, and the keyboard. Another cord plugged into the electric socket. An Ethernet cable ran from the computer to an Ethernet hub, joined by the five other Ethernet cables. There was also a district-supplied laptop, designed to withstand an IED. It, too, had several cables connecting the laptop to a “doc-cam,” the Ethernet, and an electric socket. Did I say clutter?

Only two of the computers could connect to the Internet, including the laptop. I learned this after an eternity of waiting for the computers to come on. Did I say slow?

I didn’t bother to check the specs of the computers. Suffice it to say, they were vastly underpowered, no doubt possessing stingy quantities of RAM. With all computers on, the heat generated is almost unbearable. Did I say hot?

You may have read Froma Harrop’s recent column on Steve Jobs and Apple devices. She began by complaining about her previous PC experience—all bad; which drove her into the arms of Steve Jobs, figuratively speaking. She writes:

Apple has long worked under the solid assumption that consumers would pay more for sleek design, higher quality and superior service. Nothing it makes looks like it fell from a flying saucer.

Now let’s imagine my wife’s classroom equipped with all Apple products.

First off, there would be few cables. Instead, MacBook Airs and iPads would be connected wirelessly to the Internet, printers, district servers, and each other. You would find a large LCD television connected to an Apple TV. The iPads would be running iOS 5 to take full advantage of Apple’s Airplay feature.*

My wife would have an iPad, of course. Everything she did on her iPad, from slide shows, to math problems, to video would be instantly mirrored to the television via Apple TV.

And everything would “just work,” as Steve says.

Alas, my wife can only wish for such technology. School districts are in a world of financial hurt, with the state cutting education budgets each year. There is simply no money to make the switch. She and her colleagues are stuck with outmoded, misbehaving technology that is cumbersome, at a best, and nonfunctioning, for the most part.

By the way, I know that it would all “just work,” because I have all that Apple technology at home. It’s sweet.

*  Version 5 of the iOS will be released in a matter of weeks.

Crystal ball: 10 reasons that Apple will conquer

August 24, 2011 by davald

Now that HP is getting out of the PC business and has abandoned its tablet and WebOS coupled with Acer losing money on its own PC business and mediocre tablet initiatives, what happens to Apple? Could the world’s second most valuable company (first for a while) keep on truckin’? I say yes, and here’s why.

First, Apple is entering the Chinese market in a big way, opening up stores and signing agreements with telecoms there. China is now the world’s largest electronics market.

Second, Apple will be selling its new iPhone 5 via Sprint and T-Mobile. So the device will be available to the nation’s four biggest mobile networks. Recall that Apple started with AT&T on an exclusive basis.

Third, the new iPhone will be even better than the current iPhone 4 and likely be sold at the same retail price. And the iPhone 4 is the world’s most popular smartphone. (I’d add that it’s so far immune to malware, which is not the case with rival Android phones. This makes the Apple product more attractive to businesses.)

Fourth, the iPad 3, slated to ship early next year, will have a faster processor and a higher quality display (can you say retina?) than the iPad 2. Apple has cornered over 90 percent of the tablet market. With an even better product at the same price, and given the problems with would-be competitors’ devices, Apple will continue to sell multiple millions of iPads.

Fifth, no one has come close to replicating Apple’s app store, either in volume or in ease of use.

Sixth, Apple’s bricks-and-mortar stores are the envy of the entire retail world. It leads all retailers in dollars per square foot. And have you been in an Apple store lately? They’re packed and full of energy, and customers actually leave the stores with product in their hands. Apple continues to add new stores every week across the globe.

Seventh, Steve Jobs’s insistance on wedding hardware and software, long maligned in the industry, has proven to be a very winning strategy. As he says, “it just works.” There’s no “crapware.” There’s no fragmentation, as is the case with Android and Windows devices.

Eighth, with the release of iOS 5 next month, Apple devices will work even better and easier. I’m talking about iCloud and automatic, instant syncing of just about everything you do on the iMac, the iPhone, and the iPad. Take a picture, it’s pushed out to all your devices. Your music will be on iCloud and instantly available to all your devices. Write a letter or crunch some numbers on a spreadsheet—boom!—the file instantly appears everywhere, in all its versions.

Ninth, forget about a camera. The new iPhone will be the best you have because it’s always with you. Rumor has it that the new iPhone 5 will be equipped with an 8 megapixel lens, up from the current 5. Need to snap a photo or record a video? Use your iPhone. No need to carry around a camera.

Tenth, Apple has begun to make serious inroads into the enterprise market, and it’s not because Apple is actively selling to businesses. Rather, Apple products have become Trojan Horses; employees are asking if not demanding that their companies embrace the iPhone, the iPad, and even the iMac. Now that HP, the world’s largest PC seller, is getting out of that business, this widens Apple’s opening.

I’d add another factor. Let’s call it ‘cool.’ The marriage of design, materials, and engineering yields the best products. The new Mac Book Air, for example, as been called “the perfect computer.” The 13-inch model is already in short supply as demand skyrockets for the device. It’s fast, beautiful, and, of course, it just works.

UPDATE (Aug. 24, 2011):

OMG. Jobs just announced his resignation as CEO, effective immediately. He’s staying on as board chairman. Tim Cook will likely succeed him. My crystal ball tells me that Apple’s stock will fall sharply tomorrow. However, there’s too much good stuff going on in the company to detract from its progress. The stock price will rebound, for the reasons I mentioned above.

That said, I wish Steve Jobs all the best, although this is not a good sign, however much we expected the news.

I am Robot

August 4, 2011 by davald

As a long-time, dedicated, enthusiastic Apple fanboy, I regret this post. While I have had little need to contact Apple’s support services, I recently called the number—only to be presented with the voice of a cocky, machine-generated voice. I say ‘cocky’ because the thing at the other end assured me that “he” could understand common English vocabulary.

Well, after a few exchanges, where I’m certain that I yelled the words as if I were speaking to someone whose English was a second language, I gave up. Upon reflection, it occurs to me that I was too impatient, that I should have given the machine more time to help.

That said, I was typically delighted with Apple support, with a technician answering my call within seconds, then expertly walking me through the steps needed to fix my problem. Why did Jobs abandon real people for robots?

It’s ironic, in that Apple’s electronics are “made by hand.” Yes, there are hundreds of thousands of hands assembling iPhones and iPads and iPods and assorted Macs; and the people belonging to these hands live and work in slave-shop environments. But when it comes to helping Apple customers, who, let’s face it, tend to sip fine wines rather than slurp Coors Lite, Steve has dropped the ball.

Evidently, I’m not alone in my disappointment. A recent survey demonstrates that customers are far less than delighted with virtual customer support, delivered in artificial voice. Computerworld reports:

The primary cause of the tumble was a turnabout in customers’ opinion of the automated section of their calls to Apple. In the last year, the percentage of those who said they were very satisfied with the quality of Apple’s automation fell 13 points to 24%. That’s a new low for Apple in Vocalabs surveys, and a whopping 28 points off the peak of 52% a year-and-a-half ago.

Mr. Jobs, go live.
