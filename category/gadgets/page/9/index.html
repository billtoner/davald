Early adopter

October 12, 2011 by davald

It’s really a synonym for impatient. Can’t wait to get my hands on the newest and greatest of all things Apple. So, today is the big day, right? Cupertino releases iOS 5, a Lion update (which comes with an improved version of Safari), and new Apple TV software. The problem is, it seems that every Apple aficionado is equally impatient; we all had to have the few stuff—right now.

Well, there must be a gazillion of us, because it’s taking an eternity for the software to download. Would you believe three hours for the iOS 5?

Worse, I had started downloading the new iOS for my iPhone 4. It was taking a while, so I went for my walk, assuming that the download would be completed upon my return. No such luck. There was a problem restoring or backing up the phone. Try again, dummy.

Fortunately, I have a little bit of life outside the reality-distortion field. Like getting a haircut and now waiting for the Tigers and Rangers game to start, after a two-hour rain delay.

Did I say impatient?

Inevitable, yes, but…

October 6, 2011 by davald

…still a shock. Steve Jobs is dead.

I was sitting in front of the television watching the Phillies-Cardinals game with my iPad in my lap. A notice from MacWorld flashed on the display: “Steve Jobs has died.” I immediately went to the NY Times website. There it was under “breaking news.” Then to Apple’s website. Simple. Elegant. Final.

I had intended this morning to write about my memories of baseball. I’ll leave that to another day. What better occasion, now that Steve has succumbed to “Life’s best invention,” to share my Apple experiences, of which there are plenty.

It was in Bellevue that I first encountered an Apple product—the IIe. Clunky by today’s standards, but more polished than anything else in the storeroom. I was the owner of a dual-floppy (and I do mean floppy) CP/M machine, complete with a keyboard and a black-and-white monitor. Put in the system floppy. Voila, the ‘A’ prompt. Henceforth, nothing but keystrokes. Yet I did not purchase the IIe; a bit too expensive for me back then.

Fast forward to 1984. I’m in the Everett Mall, which then had a computer store. There it was: the Macintosh. You all remember the commercial directed by Ridley Scott. The 1984 Super Bowl brought the computer for the rest of us. I could not wait to get up close and personal. There on that tiny 9-inch screen was Microsoft Word. That’s right. Gates et al wrote for Apple before the PC. And it had a mouse! Type a few lines. Italicize this. Put these letters in bold. Copy, drag, and paste. Wow!

I finally bought my first Apple product two years later—the Macintosh Plus. I added a LaserWriter printer. Lots of money. But, hey, I was going into desktop publishing. Besides, I could write off the products as a business expense, which I did—legitimately. However, it was frustratingly difficult to draw accident scenes on it. (I consulted forensically on traffic accident reconstruction.) Slow with that tiny screen. But the printed product, after much painstaking work, looked neat, clean, and more professional than drafting old school, even if I did have to tape several letter-sized pages together. Also, I sold my IBM Selectric, since I could now do my reports and invoices on the Mac and LaserWriter. Heaven.

I have not stopped buying Apple products since that first Macintosh purchase. Here’s a list, which may not be exhaustive:

  • Macintosh II
  • PowerBook
  • Macintosh IIci
  • iMac (turquoise)
  • Newton
  • G4 desktop (PowerPC)
  • G5 desktop (PowerPC)
  • G4 laptop
  • MacPro (current)
  • MacBook Pro (passed on to my son)
  • iPad (first edition)
  • iPad 2
  • MacBook (for teacher-wife)
  • MacBook Air (for teacher-wife)
  • Time Capsule
  • Apple TV (1st and second editions)
  • iPod (80 GB)
  • iPod nano (for teacher-wife)
  • iPhone 3
  • iPhone 3s (for teacher-wife)
  • iPhone 4

(Note: If any of these get stolen, proof of my inventory is now in the clouds.)

During Apple’s dark days, between Steve Jobs’s tenures, the Marysville School District, where my wife teaches, decided to abandon Macs in favor of Wintel machines. I spoke against it at a public meeting, no doubt ineffectively, since passion ruled my mind. I warned the district that it would soon see a bloated IT staff and budget with technical problems that would overwhelm support personnel. Besides, I said, the cheap PCs would likely crash, break down, or just stop working altogether. I was right. To this day, unfortunately, the district has stuck with its unworkable and unmanageable PC-cum-Windows (still using XP) system, and teachers have to wait months for fixes. How much better for students and teachers alike if they could use MacBook Airs, iPads, Apple TVs connected to widescreen televisions—with everything connected wirelessly.

The PUD also jettisoned its mostly-Mac installed base a year or so before the school district. That decision—which probably made sense at the time given Apple’s downward trajectory—also led to much higher IT costs and a larger support staff. The utility is still using Windows XP. It’s familiar and most of the bugs have been worked out. There are a few isolated Macs in the communications department, which employs graphic designers.

Jobs never pushed his products to the enterprise. Instead, they are serving as Trojan Horses. Employees buy Apple products for their own use, then bring them to work. If they’re high enough in the food chain, they insist on IT’s accommodating their iPhones and iPads. Gradually, corporations are becoming more Apple friendly.

This includes the PUD, which is set to adopt iPhones rather than Android devices; iOS is more secure. This past week the board witnessed a demonstration of iPads, which will eventually be used by commissioners and upper management in lieu of printed binders. Of course, I was all over this, although my colleagues are a bit wary. The Trojan Horse has entered.

Apple products just work, an expression I’m sure that you’ve heard more than once. All of my old devices have either been sold or handed down to family and friends. They have not been tossed, because they still function—although I don’t remember what happened to the Newton.

While I am terribly saddened by the news of Jobs’s demise, I think Apple is just starting. While it’s the most valuable company on the planet, it will continue to be insanely great. Just look at all the patents the company has either registered or secured.

We ain’t seen nothing yet.

What’s in a number?

October 5, 2011 by davald

The number 5, to be precise. Apple, despite rampant speculation, did not release a fifth generation iPhone, at least not one ending in the suffix ‘5.’ This prompted pervasive “disappointment.” Why? Because pundits and fanboys had been led to believe—based only on speculation and rumor, mind you—that new CEO Tim Cook would unveil the iPhone 5.

Also, watchers were “underwhelmed” by Mr. Cook’s “performance.” He’s no Steve Jobs. Fancy that.

But what did Apple present yesterday in Cupertino? Quite a bit, actually.

No, it’s not an iPhone 5; it’s an iPhone 4S. Get it: the ‘s’ substituted for ‘5.’ Apple did not change the “form factor,” an evidently favorite expression of geeks and technologists. The iPhone 4S looks just like the iPhone 4: same dimensions, although the S weighs three grams more.

It’s what’s under the hood that many pundits have overlooked. Here’s a table from Apple Insider comparing the specs of the “old” and the “new” iPhones.

What should matter most to users is both usability and capability. Let’s peruse the key features of the S:

  • a new A5 processor (same as in iPad 2) that will double CPU performance;
  • that same processor will boost graphics capabilities seven-fold;
  • for a hefty sum, you can purchase the S with 64 GB of RAM, equivalent to the high-end iPad;
  • extended battery life;
  • a “world phone” that can run on either GSM (AT&T and Sprint and Europe) or CDMA (Verizon)—I suspect that Apple will save some production costs here;
  • a new and greatly improved camera, with more megapixels and much better lenses; it’s also faster and much easier to use than the current iPhone, which already takes excellent pictures;
  • full 1080 HD video;
  • the new iOS software, version 5 (see, you do get a ‘5’ after all);
  • iCloud;
  • iTunes Match (all your music in the cloud—i.e., on Apple servers—making songs available on all Mac devices);
  • streaming of music, photos, and video—from the cloud and to your Apple TV-connected devices;
  • automatic backup;
  • wireless set up and transfer (no need to plug the iOS device into a computer); and
  • Siri (“your humble personal assistant”).

This new iPhone, though not a ‘5,’ is vastly superior to anything on the market.

As for acknowledging the surge in Android-based phones (cheaper and inferior), Apple will allow you to buy the existing iPhone 4 for under a $100 and the 3GS (which was twice as fast as the 3) can be had for nothing. Yep. Free. It will run iOS 5.

Don’t weep for Apple. It’s still just getting started.

Cupertino cupids

October 4, 2011 by davald

Apple’s top brass, sans Mr. Jobs, takes the stage this morning to “talk about iPhone.” Per usual, lots of speculation and rumors about what version 5 will look like and, more important, what’s inside. Of course, the product or products unveiled this morning will blow away the competition, such as it is.

One interesting tidbit is that Apple may also introduce less-spectacular phones for a lot less money. If true, I suspect that Mr. Cook et al recognize that, while it’s nice to extract half the profits from the entire phone industry, the less expensive Android units, manufactured by an array of different companies, are grabbing more and more market share. Application developers are necessarily gravitating toward Android, without, as yet, abandoning the more lucrative iOS ecosystem. For them many is better than few, even if they earn more revenue from iOS. Some have even speculated that Apple will give away an iPhone model and offset the loss via a percentage of wireless contracts from Sprint, AT&T, Verizon, and others.

Speaking of Sprint, the company has evidently agreed to spend $20 billion to acquire 30 million iPhone 5s over the next four years. That’s huge, and an astonishing admission of just how important the Apple units are to wireless vendors.

Finally, one of the rumors spreading across the blogosophere is that the new iPhone 5 will include Assistant, based on software developed by Siri, which Apple acquired in the spring of last year. One of Siri’s co-founders had this to say about the new software:

Apple’s ‘mainstreaming’ Artificial Intelligence in the form of a Virtual Personal Assistant is a groundbreaking event. I’d go so far as to say it is a World-Changing event.

But is the world ready to be changed? We’ll see. But Apple does make us fall in love with its gadgets.

The trouble with Macs

September 12, 2011 by davald

Quite simply, I crave them. Enough, as it turns out, to buy, but eventually return, three of them in the last couple of months.

You see, I, like millions of others around the globe, know a good thing when I touch, feel, and use it. There is nothing quite like an Apple product.

Yes, I have inhabited the dark side, when forced. What an awful experience. Today I can truly say that I’m Windows-cum-PC free. It’s all Apple all the time.

Okay, so why did I return the splendid gadgets. Well, I realized that I had no niche to fill in my daily life. I already have a Mac Pro with a marvelous screen and enough accessories to prompt the label “NORAD.” I also have the iPad 2, a beautiful device like no other. Then there is the iPhone 4. My wife has a MacBook Air, the smaller version and previous generation. Amazing. So what happened?

After checking out the many reviews of the latest MacBook Airs, I had to have one, up close and personal. As one reviewer put it: the best computer in the world; period. Perfect, said another. They’re right. But I really didn’t need a laptop, especially since I already have the iPad 2. If I want to create and otherwise produce on a computer, I have the inestimably capable Mac Pro (2008 version), with all its add-ons. Moreover, why would I walk down to the local coffee shop for free WI-FI, when I’ve got 61 Mbs in my downtown apartment? Oh, and I also have an espresso machine if I need the latte.

Ah, but wouldn’t you like an even larger display, like the 27-inch monitor for the latest and greatest iMac? Yeah, that’s the ticket. I’ll get a new iMac. Which I did. Beautiful. Gorgeous. Super fast. And huge. Maybe too huge. The smaller, 21.5-in. iMac might work. But I have an excellent Apple Cinema HD display, which is 23-inch. Just about the ideal size for my workspace. I returned the iMac, with a bit of sadness.

But that MBA is so alluring. So I ordered the top-of-the-line 13-inch model. What can I say? It really is a treasure. Jobs and Ive know what they’re doing.

Yep, I let that one go, too. Again, it’s not practical, right?

As for the money that’s apparently burning a hole in my pocket, there’s always the next iPhone and iPad. Or, the Mac Pro won’t last forever, will it?

Apple’s safe. I’m forever at its mercy.

There’s blood on my iPad

May 23, 2011 by davald

I have been a devoted Apple consumer since the original Macintosh, with credit card receipts to prove it. Yes, I momentarily strayed to the dark side during Cupertino’s wilderness years, when Steve Jobs was in exile, but quickly returned after immediately experiencing the dreaded blue screen. I have not been tempted away since.

We Apple aficionados have gladly, obsessively paid premiums for our products, believing, with much evidence, that the Macintosh (just “Mac” today), iPod, iPhone, and now iPad are superior to their dark-side counterparts in every respect. And did I mention that we like things to work, with simple, efficient elegance?

I’ve known for some time that those magical products originate in mainland China, having tracked multiple FedEx shipments—from Taiwan, to Anchorage, to Seattle, to Bothell, then “out for delivery.” But I have been unwilling to encounter the ugly underbelly of Apple’s production system.

On the recommendation that I would laugh my head off, I accepted an invitation to see Michael Daisey’s one-man performance of “The Agony and Ecstasy of Steve Jobs.” Yes, I laughed and chuckled and chortled, appreciating the familiar vocabulary of “geekiness” and all things Cupertino. I even learned a few nuggets of Apple lore. But then I started to squirm, as did fellow audience members.

Daisey had contrived to enter the fortress of Foxconn in Shenzhen, China. What he witnessed and the facts he poured out last Saturday evening at the Seattle Rep scrubbed away the carefully burnished Apple sheen. Did you know?

  • The labor costs of an iPhone are 80 cents.
  • Foxconn manufactures or assembles 52 percent of all of the electronics in the world.
  • Foxconn’s Shenzhen plant employs over 400,000 workers.
  • The plant has 23 cafeterias, each built to hold 10,000 workers at a time.
  • The onsite dormitories’ rooms are about 10 by 12 and contain as many as 15 beds stacked vertically.
  • Many of the workers are in their early teens.
  • All of Apple’s products are assembled by hand; there are no robots.
  • Workers put in as many as 18 hours a day, often six or seven days a week.
  • Most are not paid overtime.
  • The Chinese government bans labor unions. Anyone suspected of belonging to one may spend the rest of his or her life in prison. (Karl Marx, where are you?)

For workers, having spent several years on an assembly line doing the same micro task over and over and over again, repetitive stress syndrome becomes the norm. Hands and joints grow gnarled and disfigured. When a worker can no longer perform the menial task, he or she is discarded, like a broken iPod. Ironically, in a terribly tragic way, workers assembling Apple’s products cannot afford to actually buy one, so they’ve never used an iPad, despite touching millions of them.

The Foxconn plant that recently exploded, killing several workers, was erected in 70 days to meet the burgeoning demand for the iPad2. Following a spate of suicides at their giant high-rise facilities, Foxconn installed nets around the top of the roofs. Daisey mockingly calls this “corporate responsibility.”

We Mac fans tend to be more liberal than PC devotees. I, for one, having gladly paid a premium for Jobs’s magical devices, wouldn’t hesitate to pay more to improve the working conditions of Foxconn. Or, perhaps Jobs would be content with a little less profit.

Consider that the iPhone claims just five percent of the mobile telephone market, but 20 percent of the revenue, and a whopping 55 percent of the profits. That’s obscene. The iPad has about three fourths of the tablet market and, no doubt, a huge lion’s share of the net revenues. Apple dominates the music scene, being the largest seller, even greater than Walmart.

Jobs sits on over $60 billion in cash, which has investors wondering what, if anything, he plans to do with all that money. Why not spend a little bit of that wad on the welfare of Chinese workers?

Apple could insist that Foxconn establish an 8-hour-day norm, abolish child labor, provide health care and social security, and a pay scale that would allow workers to make a decent living. The Chinese government won’t lift a finger.

Beijing conceived of a Special Economic Zone for Shenzhen, once a fishing village of 700 and now the third most populous city in the world. If you have electronics in your house, regardless of brand, there’s a good chance they were made in Shenzhen by nearly-slave labor, and very young, at that. No doubt profits are being siphoned off by the enriched party leaders in Beijing, as well as by Foxconn executives and Cupertino, of course.

Michael Daisey told his audience that we Americans made Shenzhen, insofar as we have demanded relatively cheap gadgets. Upon leaving the theater we were handed a piece of paper entitled “What happens next?” Daisey wrote, “That lies in your hands.” He asks at the bottom that we “tell others.”

This is just a monologue [he wrote]—a single voice telling a story of a single experience. But if I have opened the door for you, consider opening a door for others. We do not like to think about China and its implications in our country, but that silence can only exist if we are complicit with it. Talking about it, thinking about it when making purchasing decisions, and understanding it is not just symbolic. They are the first seeds of actual change. Do not be afraid to plant them.

Pass the shovel.

The iPad2

March 27, 2011 by davald

The few who know me appreciate that I’m an Apple addict, having acquired the original Macintosh, then never looking back to the PC world, except when forced to do so. There is no surprise that I adopted early the Apple laptops, desktops, and now the mobile devices, including the original iPhone 3G and now the iPhone 4. It never stops, of course. So now I’ve upgraded to the new iPad2, having sold the original iPad to Gazelle. The previous laptops and desktops have all been sent down the family tree, which is a nice way of justifying each new purchase.

So, now that I’ve had the newest tablet from Mr. Jobs, what are my initial thoughts over the last few days of ownership?

In a word, splendid.

The iPad2 is much faster. The display is brighter and smudge-resistant. The device is lighter and thinner. The new smart cover is ingenious. Did I say faster?

Of course, describing the unit is a poor substitute for actually using one, as 15 million customers of the original have so far discovered. There is no other device quite like it, given the design, power, and the apps. I have not missed my laptops.

The new iPad has cameras, both front and back. I can see the business community using the tablets for video conferencing or as an alternative to sitting across the table and incurring travel expenses. The video and camera features will be lost on me, however.

Yet, the iPad is not for everyone. For writing, it sucks. I chock that up to old age, since I’ve never gotten the hang of thumb-typing, which the young and hip have nailed. I don’t text. I write this, then, on an Apple wireless keyboard associated via Bluetooth with my Mac Pro. Overkill, I know.

I have to admit that Apple, in its zeal for thinness and lightness, has made some activities more difficult. The ports now sit along beveled edges, which requires some adjustments from the first iPad, where the ports were vertical, rather than angled, to the display. The volume and mute switches have sharper, uncomfortable edges. (By the way, you can convert the mute switch to a display mode lock under the settings menu, a welcome modification in the iOS update.) Controlling the volume via the external switches, then, has become an awkward affair. (I suspect that Jobs’s typical attention to detail was absent, because of his medical leave. Johnny Ive, where were you on this?)

But these are nitpicks. On the whole, the iPad, both the original and the new, are transformational devices. That is, the user will be doing things differently and, I predict, more efficiently and conveniently. Expect businesses to buy and use millions of these for a variety of purposes. It’s only a matter of getting these into different people’s hands. And that’s the key: you really have to experience the tablet to gain a sense of its potential.

I don’t believe the iPad is a fad, as some have suggested. Indeed, it starts a revolution, with apologies to Che.
