Oops, Mr. Daisey

March 20, 2012 by davald

I sat in the sold-out audience to be entertained by Michael Daisey, and entertained I was. He is a master story-teller. His subject for the evening was Steve Jobs and Apple, Inc. Titled The Agony and Ecstasy of Steve Jobs, the performance revealed the dark side to all those “insanely great” products emanating from Cupertino geniuses. I wrote about it here.

Well, as you’ve no doubt heard, Mr. Daisey played footloose with facts, embellishing for the most part, lying for the remainder. The revelations of deceit followed widespread praise for his work, including a recent link to Mr. Daisey’s website and monologue script by Robert Kuttner. It seems that Mr. Daisey made things up about working conditions at Foxconn, the principal assembler of iPhones and iPads.

Nevertheless, I suspect that the tens of thousands of workers who stand for hours in an assembly line are laboring under circumstances we’d find repellant: low wages, long days, repetitive tasks. Boring work, to be sure. But for these Chinese workers assembling gadgets beats the alternative, which is either toiling in the fields or doing nothing. Indeed, when Foxconn recently announced the need for more workers, thousands of eager youths joined a long queue outside its factory doors.

I believe Apple can and must do better. It was no doubt stung by the New York Times series on working conditions in southeast China’s “special economic zones.” Daisey, of course, didn’t help matters. Tim Cook, Apple’s CEO, responded by stepping up audits of its suppliers, denying the worst of the alleged abuses, and committing the company to improving its supply-chain conditions. Also, Foxconn has just boosted wages by as much as 25 percent. It’s adding “lifestyle services managers” to “ensure that the workers’ quality of life is fair and enjoyable within reason.”

Tim Cook is not Steve Jobs, in a good way, I think. The autocratic and mercurial Jobs likely gave not a whit for Chinese laborers. Nor was he obsessed about Apple’s environmental footprint, although he took steps to eliminate the more toxic elements from the company’s “post-PC” gadgets after Greenpeace gave Apple a thumbs down. Cook, however, strikes me as a genuinely decent man, concerned about both working conditions and the environment. (Apple’s new server farm in North Carolina will be powered by the country’s largest solar array.) While he’s no Bill Gates in this department, not long after he assumed the reins Tim Cook established a charitable giving program; the company will match employees’ contributions to charities of their choice.

For his part, Michael Daisey is sticking to his narrative, albeit with some recent modifications to reflect the backlash. He defends his work as a testament to the “broader truth.” Much of what he speaks about in his performance is true; workers toil under harsh conditions, they succumb to repetitive motion disease, they have even died on the job.

I wrote the following after attending Daisey’s Seattle performance.

Michael Daisey told his audience that we Americans made Shenzhen, insofar as we have demanded relatively cheap gadgets. Upon leaving the theater we were handed a piece of paper entitled “What happens next?” Daisey wrote, “That lies in your hands.” He asks at the bottom that we “tell others.”

This is just a monologue [he wrote]—a single voice telling a story of a single experience. But if I have opened the door for you, consider opening a door for others. We do not like to think about China and its implications in our country, but that silence can only exist if we are complicit with it. Talking about it, thinking about it when making purchasing decisions, and understanding it is not just symbolic. They are the first seeds of actual change. Do not be afraid to plant them.

Pass the shovel.

I see no reason yet to pronounce all is well with Apple and those who make its products. It can and should do more.

I was right, sort of

March 13, 2012 by davald

A huge obstacle for widespread adoption of the iPad-cum-iBooks Author ecosystem by public schools is the cost of the iPad, the least expensive of which will set you back $500. Many classrooms are equipped with televisions, perhaps even new flatscreen models. The Apple TV costs only $99, and facilitates mirroring the content of an iPad on a display, preferably an HDTV. Apple’s new content creator, iBooks Author, is free and is limited only by a person’s imagination and skills. But, again, it’s that darn iPad that prevents all this from happening in most school districts, especially those facing state budget cuts, a nearly universal phenomenon.

So I speculated a few months back that Apple may simultaneously lower the price of its iPad 2 as it reveals the latest version. Well, Apple did just that, knocking a $100 off the iPad 2, which is better than any other tablet out there, save for the “new iPad,” one of which will hit my front door sometime this Friday.

Is a $100 discount sufficient to squeeze iPads into school budgets? It probably helps, but I doubt that’s enough to open up superintendents’ wallets.

Suppose that Apple released a smaller iPad that was significantly cheaper than the current version’s 9.7-in. form factor. A Samsung official thinks that’s going to happen. Keep in mind that Samsung makes the iPad displays. The rumor is that the smaller unit will sport a 7.85-in. screen. If my math is correct, the smaller version’s display area would be roughly 65 percent as large as the current models.

How much might the smaller version cost? The linked article suggests somewhere between $250 and $300.

Again, if my math is correct, the iPhone’s screen has an area of 5.7 square inches. The rumored iPad’s display would still be five times larger than the iPhone’s, which means lots of room for content, and it would be easier to operate in tinier hands. This becomes far more attractive and affordable for public schools.

Stay tuned.

From Nick Bilton writing in the New York Times:

I prefer my iPhone over my television because it allows me to consume and create on the same device. I’m immersed in it.

If it were a TV, I could leave comments on YouTube clips, send Twitter messages in the middle of a show or movie, and most importantly, share the content I like, or dislike.

Instead, it will come down to apps and the software that ties them to the hardware. And as we have seen with the iPhone and iPad, Apple knows how to rattle sleepy industries.

Ok, I did it

March 8, 2012 by davald

Evidently I was one of the first to order the new iPad and Apple TV, as I’m scheduled to receive both on the first day of delivery, which is next Friday. As usual, I can’t wait to have a large “Retina” display in my hands. Watching full 1080p on my HDTV via the “hobby” should also prove “amazing.”

In watching yesterday’s Apple keynote I paid particular attention to the adjectives. I’m guessing that Steve Jobs impressed upon his followers the need to repeat words like ‘amazing,’ and ‘incredible,’ because if you heard them uttered once you heard them dozens of times; and while Tim Cook is no Steve Jobs (who is?), I thought he did well in his first post-Jobs presentation.

I used to own and use laptops. Now I’m in a “post-PC world,” another favored expression. It’s not that PCs are no longer useful. Rather, as Cook said, we consumers increasingly favor devices that are portable and wirelessly tethered via “the cloud”—in Apple’s case “iCloud.” (That said, I still do most of my blogging and other creative work—I’m using the expression literally not boastfully—on my reliable Mac Pro, circa 2008. I have yet to master the iPad’s virtual keyboard to the point that it replaces my physical Bluetooth Apple accessory.)

One of the drawbacks of the first two generations of iPads was the display. It works fine for video and photos, but not so much for text. The newer iPhones render type much more clearly. Now with a “Retina” display on the iPad, reading books and magazines and just about anything else with letters and numbers should be “incredible.”

Once received and put through their early paces, these “amazing” devices may warrant a report of initial reactions. Stay tuned.

Agglomeration

March 1, 2012 by davald

Not long before he died Steve Jobs and other Silicon Valley entrepreneurs had dinner with Pres. Obama. Obama wondered aloud how the U.S. might resurrect its manufacturing sector. In particular, why couldn’t Apple’s products be made in America.

Jobs replied that Apple’s Chinese suppliers employed 700,000 workers. This huge workforce required 30,000 engineers for support. There is no way, Jobs reportedly said, to cobble together that many technical people in the U.S. “If you could educate these engineers,” Jobs told Obama, “we could move more manufacturing plants here.” (Walter Isaacson’s biography of Steve Jobs, page 546)

One other selling point for China’s “free enterprise zones” is what economists call “agglomeration.” In the case of the iPhone, for example, all of the parts that must eventually be assembled are made in plants located in the same geographical area. It helps, of course, that China has a surfeit of qualified assemblers and technicians whose wages are much lower than those of would-be American counterparts. The Times:

Apple executives say that going overseas, at this point, is their only option. One former executive described how the company relied upon a Chinese factory to revamp iPhone manufacturing just weeks before the device was due on shelves. Apple had redesigned the iPhone’s screen at the last minute, forcing an assembly line overhaul. New screens began arriving at the plant near midnight [Jobs didn’t like the plastic screens because they too easily scratched. He persuaded Corning’s CEO to begin massive production of “gorilla glass” for the new iPhone. Corning built a factory next door to Foxconn.]

A foreman immediately roused 8,000 workers inside the company’s dormitories, according to the executive. Each employee was given a biscuit and a cup of tea, guided to a workstation and within half an hour started a 12-hour shift fitting glass screens into beveled frames. Within 96 hours, the plant was producing over 10,000 iPhones a day.

“The speed and flexibility is breathtaking,” the executive said. “There’s no American plant that can match that.”

In his blog this morning Paul Krugman talks about the “Motor City” and why it made economic sense to bail out Detroit. The automobile companies that received federal largesse indirectly employed many thousands of additional workers who supplied Chrysler, General Motors, et al. with the parts for their vehicles. Krugman:

Agglomeration economies exist because the whole is more than the sum of the parts — because the network of suppliers, the skills, the interchange of knowledge supported by a geographical industry concentration in turn gives firms in that industry concentration an advantage over firms elsewhere.

Boeing obviously didn’t get the memo when its leaders decided to globalize the manufacturing and assembling of its new 787 Dreamliner. To accommodate this vast network, Boeing persuaded Washington state’s legislature to subsidize the construction of a new port on Puget Sound to receive parts manufactured elsewhere. In other words, Washington taxpayers contributed to Boeing’s flawed experiment.

The Apple way, in contrast, takes advantage of “agglomeration economies.” While its devices are designed in Cupertino, they are manufactured and assembled in SE China. With the possible exception of Motor City, the U.S. lacks the necessary geographical nexus of skills and equipment to churn out thousands of electronic devices a day.

With respect to Boeing, I thought it made more sense to agglomerate all of the parts-making and assembly within a geographical network. The Everett site, as far as I know, was ideal.

If agglomeration works for Detroit and Apple, why not for Boeing?

Conway’s law

February 19, 2012 by davald

A long-time friend posted this comment in response to my brief essay on “building things“:

Boeing hardly deserves praise for a pro US jobs position. If it could bust the unions, it would. The 787 was one of the most ‘distributed’ projects to ever make the roadmap, creating a logistics nightmare for its project managers. Consequently, it was late and one can only hope the Sr. Management team understands the root cause. They might do well to read up on Conway’s law.

I admit to never having heard of Conway’s law, so I did the usual thing—checked Wikipedia. Here’s the link. I’ll give my layman’s summary, which may be terribly wrong.

The type of product producible by a firm is determined by both the architecture of that firm and its intra-architectural communications.

An example of Conway’s law in action is provided by Wikipedia:

A real life example: NASA’s Mars Climate Orbiter crashed because one team used United States customary units (e.g., inches, feet and pounds) while the other used metric units for a key spacecraft operation. This information was critical to the maneuvers required to place the spacecraft in the proper Mars orbit. “People sometimes make errors,” said Dr. Edward Weiler, NASA’s Associate Administrator for Space Science. “The problem here was not the error, it was the failure of NASA’s systems engineering, and the checks and balances in our processes to detect the error. That’s why we lost the spacecraft.”

I’m not sure that this example really explains my limited understanding of the “law.” My friend, who used to work at Boeing, so he knows a bit about the company from the inside, faulted management for farming out the various components of its new 787, which Obama found “cool” on his recent visit. Those of us who live in Puget Sound can attest to the many-monthed delay in getting the plane up to snuff. There was, indeed, a “logistics nightmare” for project managers attempting to coordinate globe-based manufacturing, transportation, and assembly. This “distributed” network proved to be a horribly conceived and poorly executed strategy.

This got me thinking about Apple, the world’s most valuable company. However Apple is structured and whatever its processes, it continues to put out amazing products that customers can’t wait to snap up. Why Apple and not, say, Microsoft or Samsung, or any one of dozens of other would-be competitors? Could Conway’s law help explain?

Apple is a notoriously secret company, but there have been glimpses into its operations from Walter Isaacsons’s biography of Steve Jobs and Inside Apple, by Adam Lachinsky. Both emphasize Jobs’s propensity to say no. The key to success is not only having the best available people but also identifying core competencies and focusing intently on just a few products.

There are only four major product lines at Cupertino: iPod, iPhone, iPad, and the Mac. In turn, these products use the best software, and all of the products are integrated via just two online ecosystems: iTunes Store and the App Store. We can now add a third, iCloud, which is emerging as the wireless ligament connecting everything.

Jobs told Isaacson that Sony, which had controlled most of the mobile music market with its Walkman, couldn’t produce the iPhone or even the iPod-cum-iTunes. Why not? There were too many divisions in Sony and each was jealous of and competitive with the other. By contrast, Apple was more like a series of concentric circles with Jobs at the hub. (source)

Here’s Isaacson:

Indeed, Sony provided a clear counterexample to Apple. It had a consumer electronics division that made sleek products and a music division with beloved artists (including Bob Dylan). But because each division tried to protect its own interests, the company as a whole never got its act together to produce an end-to-end service.

During Jobs’s years in the wilderness, tending to Pixar and NeXT, many outsiders urged Apple to license its operating system to hardware vendors, just as Microsoft does with Windows. For a while Apple did just that, under Gil Amelio, its then-CEO. When Jobs returned to the company he co-founded one of his first moves was to cancel the licensing deals. He also aborted the Newton, John Sculley’s favorite device, but widely lampooned in the press and in Doonesbury. Jobs realized before, then, and forever that the key to producing “insanely great products” is to carefully wed both hardware design and operating systems, and he would preside over the weddings.

Microsoft can’t do this, because the company is all about software; it has no hardware experience. Likewise Google develops the Android operating system, which it gives away to dozens of original equipment manufacturers, including Amazon, Samsung, and HTC. Each of these companies’ devices vary, often in significant ways, from screen size to the placement and use of buttons. I pity third-party developers who must figure out a way to get their applications to run satisfactorily on a myriad of different units.

There’s a link to an academic paper in the Wikipedia entry on Conway’s law. Three professors at the Harvard Business school sought to look more carefully at how products might “mirror” the organizations that produce them. They came up with this table to differentiate between organizations.

I’d say that Apple fits comfortably under “tightly-coupled.” But it could have become a different company altogether had Steve Wozniak had his way. Apple would have been “loosely-coupled,” “decentralized,” and “emergent.” It would also be a much, much poorer company and probably would not have survived the “Homebrew Computer Club” populated by geeks and nerds, Wozniak’s kind of people.

I suspect that Apple’s competitors are not so easily pigeonholed. They likely share more of the “tightly-coupled” traits, though Google and Facebook, at least in their beginnings, could be described as “loosely-coupled,” encouraging their employees to informally collaborate on “emergent” products. Lachinsky tells us that Apple, by contrast, is a very serious place; employees are expected to work long and hard once they enter the front door, and they have to pay for their own lunches.

When Isaacson asked Jobs to name his proudest achievement, Jobs didn’t mention the iPhone or the iPad. No, his proudest achievement was Apple, the company. He was so certain that this was so that he quietly established Apple University, internal to the organization.

In 2008 Jobs hired Joel Podolny, the dean of the Yale School of Management to build then superintend the “university.” Podolyny developed case studies of Apple’s product development, the great, the so-so, and the ugly. They are taught by members of Apple’s senior management. It was clearly Jobs’s intention to create a lasting legacy for people who “think different.”

When it was becoming clear that Jobs was not long for this world pundits and analysts wondered aloud if Apple would continue its success. How much of Apple was Steve Jobs himself? Or had he really succeeded in building a lasting legacy, comfortable that his successors would keep the Apple ball rolling?

While it’s still early, there appear to be no signs of the company’s abating. It’s stock price is now over $500. The new iPad 3 is expected within weeks. Apple will release a new Mac operating system every year just as it does now with iOS. This fall the company will reveal the iPhone 5. There are rumors of a new television that will function much differently than current examples, though how differently is presently the stuff of rumors.

Meanwhile, Apple’s would-be competitors, which could be the entire electronics industry, are busy copying Cupertino’s products in look and feel—Exhibit A, Samsung, the shameless target of numerous patent violations filed by Apple’s lawyers. But I doubt that they will produce devices and software and ecosystems that will threaten Apple’s juggernaut. The reason may indeed have something to do with Conway’s law.

You can’t do Apple unless you are Apple. And there’s only one Apple.

Some context on Apple sales

February 14, 2012 by davald

Speaking to a roomful of investors today, new Apple CEO, Tim Cook, put forth some sales numbers. I quote here from Apple Insider:

Asked about the demand ramp for iPad, which has sold 55 million units in 7 quarters, Cook said “this 55 million is something no one would have guessed, including us. To put it in context, it took us 22 years to sell 55 million Macs, it took around five years to sell [55] million iPods, and it took about 3 years to sell that many iPhones.”

Cook said “iPad stood on the shoulders of everything that came before it,” from iTunes to the Store to the iPhone. “It’s amazing how the product has captured” the attention of a variety of customers Cook said, noting “It’s the fastest adoption across a wide range that I’ve ever seen before.”

Apple’s on a roll. Its stock is at $509.46 and its market cap at $475 billion. The company likely has over $100 billion in cash, with no debt. Next month Apple is expected to unveil iPad 3, a dramatic improvement over its existing version, which ain’t all that bad.

Let the drooling begin

February 9, 2012 by davald

I bought the first version of the iPad. Then I bought the second. Early next month, according to the rumor mills, Apple will unveil the third version, which promises to be as big a leap forward as version 2 was to the original.

I spend a lot of time in front of computer displays. I’m typing this using a rather ancient Apple HD Cinema, 23-inch. It’s beautiful, in both form and function. Yet, text isn’t nearly as crisp and clear on the Cinema as it is on my iPhone 4, which I’ll replace with the 5 later this year. Nor is text all that nice on the iPad 2, though the device yields stunning color graphics. Indeed, I prefer to read books on the iPhone, because text is so clear on its Retina display.

So, in my humble opinion, the best feature on the soon-to-be-released iPad is a nearly Retina display. The current iPad sports 132 ppi (pixels per inch). The rumors have converged on a doubling of that to 264 ppi. That’s not quite the density of the iPhone (326 ppi), but text on the iPad 3 should be a quantum leap clearer and cleaner then on the iPad 2.

Of course, as Apple does, the next iPad will be faster, probably have longer battery life (although there’s hardly anything wrong with the 10 hours of the iPad 2), perhaps a better camera (although I don’t use either the front or back now), and maybe Siri, deemed quite popular on the iPhone 4S (which my wife has). Here’s the New York Times:

An Apple employee said that the version of the new iPad that is being tested inside the company is “essentially the same size and shape as the iPad 2,” with an improved and “truly amazing” screen. The tablet will include a faster processor, said the employee, who did not want to be identified because Apple is not fond of leaks.

If you’re asking, the answer is yes: my life is compartmentalized.
