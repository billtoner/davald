A catastrophic note

August 23, 2012 by davald

Elizabeth Kohlbert, a staff writer for The New Yorker, penned Field Notes from a Catastrophe: Man, Nature, and Climate Change. She had interviewed dozens of climate scientists who all painted a pretty pessimistic picture: unless people significantly curbed their greenhouse gas emissions, the planet would suffer a catastrophe. One such scientist, David Rind talked to her about water. She wrote:

He found that as carbon dioxide levels rose, the world would begin to experience more and more serious water shortages, starting near the equator and then spreading toward the poles. When he applied the [Palmer Drought Severity] index to GISS’s model [Goddard Institute for Space Studies] for doubled CO2, it showed that most of the continental United States would be suffering under severe drought conditions. [pp. 107-109]

Well, it seems that we’re already on that path. The New York Times reports today that drought now affects over 63 percent of the United States. And with drought comes diminishing water supplies. The Times:

The plunge in the water supply is mostly the result of a simple geological process. Without rain, aquifers deep beneath the ground have not been getting the water that seeps into the earth to replenish their supplies. In one part of northwest Indiana, Mr. Basch said, the groundwater level had dropped as much as 40 feet, or about eight times more than it normally does.

Compounding the problem for homeowners relying on smaller aquifers is the fact that municipalities and farmers who consume water on a much larger scale have been draining the groundwater supply at a faster rate this summer because of the drought.

There has been “a drop in the water table all over the state of Missouri, not just in one specific area,” Mr. Flynn said. “I’ve never seen anything like this in the almost 30 years I’ve been running the company.”

While Obama hasn’t done a damn thing about global warming, the Republicans actually deny that there’s a problem. Something about a hoax. Ironic, in a tragic sort of way, that those states impacted most by prolonged drought will cast their votes for those least likely to address the underlying cause of their water shortages.

I don’t think ignorance brings bliss. It will surely deliver catastrophe.

The big punch bowl

August 23, 2012 by davald

GOP presidential candidate, Mitt Romney, unveiled his “energy independence” plan, which, as one might guess, is simply “drill, baby, drill.” He would essentially streamline permitting processes for new oil exploration and broadly expand available drilling sites, including off-shore. Despite Obama’s enabling a rapid increase in domestic oil exploration and production, Romney said today, as quoted by the New York Times:

“The net net of all this, as you can see [from a chart that no one could see], is by 2020, we’re able to produce somewhere between 28 million barrels per day of oil and we won’t need to buy any oil from the Middle East or Venezuela or anywhere else where we don’t want to,” Mr. Romney said. [By the way, the New York Times reports that the U.S. produces 15 billion barrels of oil a day. The CIA estimates the total to be just under 10 million barrels a day.)

The petroleum market is truly global. I liken it to a giant punch bowl, with many spigots pouring oil in and many more straws drawing it out. The price of each output unit is determined the old-fashioned way: by supply and demand.

So, what would really happen if the U.S. increased domestic supply?

  • It would have negligible impacts on global aggregate supply and thus scant effect on market prices.
  • Oil is traded on a global scale. Exxon, for example, seeks the best deal on wholesale quantities from wherever then sells at retail based on a highest price the market will bear. If Exxon finds a better wholesale price elsewhere, it will likely keep domestic supplies in reserve until it makes economic sense to use them.*
  • If market prices rise, so do the revenues of the oil companies. Since revenue equals price times quantity, the greater the supply of oil the greater the revenues—at any given price.
  • Further environmental despoliation.
  • Increased greenhouse gases.
  • Undermining of conservation acquisition and renewable resource development.

Let’s look at a few charts. First, we’ll place U.S. production within the global context.

 

 

In percentage terms, here’s what it looks like:

 

 

What about consumption? The U.S. consumes 21 percent of the world total. (Note: Our population represents only 4.4 percent of the planet’s human inhabitants.)

 

Now let’s compare production with consumption.

Based on 2010 data the U.S. imports nearly 50 percent of the oil it consumes. From whom do we get our oil? (By the way, the data used in this post via the CIA World Factbook.)

In addition to illustrating the sources of our oil, the above chart reminds us that there is a vast global petroleum market. Since we must import so much of the oil we use, we spend an inordinate amount of our tax dollars on defending shipping lanes and both acquiring and protecting foreign supplies of petroleum (e.g., Iraq, Kuwait).

On a sour note, I conclude that neither Romney’s nor Obama’s energy strategies will make us energy independent or prevent a global warming catastrophe. Such is the state of politics in America.

Russell’s wisdom: why we fight

August 19, 2012 by davald

One essential primer is Bertrand Russell’s The Problems of Philosophy. Therein he provides a concise introduction to issues over which philosophers have long struggled. And if you have any familiarity with philosophy as an academic pursuit, you know that philosophers develop only tentative or provisional answers—such is the nature of the discipline. But if you’re patient, you’ll eventually uncover some kernels of wisdom, and Russell’s writings contain many.

For example, consider these words from the aforementioned book:

Material possessions can be taken by force and enjoyed by the robber. Spiritual possessions cannot be taken in this way. You may kill an artist or a thinker, but you cannot acquire his art or his thought. You may put a man to death because he loves his fellow-men, but you will not by so doing acquire the love which made his happiness. Force is impotent in such matters; it is only as regards material goods that it is effective. For this reason the men who believe in force are the men whose thoughts and desires are preoccupied with material goods.

Thus the conservative mind, since Republicans as a rule are obsessed with “material possessions.” What they have, they fear will be taken. What they lack, they strive to obtain—by almost any means. If they should rule, they will employ the nation’s considerable armaments to plunder the wealth of others. As for defending America’s fortunes, mostly owned by the privileged few, they will spare no expense.

They will not stop at the military or the police state. Their fear-cum-greed affects every fabric of our well-being.

In the hotly contested issue of educational reform, conservatives believe that students should learn those skills necessary to succeed in the acquisition of wealth or to tend to the care and feeding of the oligarchy. Critical thinking, the kind that would ultimately question status quo political arrangements, must be eliminated, since it poses a threat to the powerful. After all, the masses outnumber them.

Investing in the arts and music, except for the products of dead painters and composers, wastes dollars that should be earmarked for exploitation. Public dollars must never go toward self-expression or human development.*

On economic issues, merit determines all, and the rich possess a monopoly, since they made the rules and insist on punishing transgressors. At your workplace, you count for nothing, save for what you contribute to the wealth of your corporate benefactors. Drop any notion of unionism; Labor is evil, because it frustrates Capital’s unrelenting greed. Meanwhile, you are expected to work more hours and extract less from your effort.

 

 

Finally, the Rest of Us must stop worrying and learn to love:

  • decrepit school buildings
  • collapsing roads
  • urban ghettoes
  • ugly strip malls
  • pollution
  • global warming
  • no philosophy, no art, no music, no beauty (these are reserved for your masters, who are happy to pay dearly for them)
  • expensive and ineffective health care
  • mind-numbing TV reality shows
  • perpetual war
  • shit.

But take heart, your taxes are low.

Climate science deniers, which include nearly the entire Republican Party, would have us believe that the planet hasn’t warmed in a decade or more or, even if it has, the warming has nothing to do with increasing carbon emissions. Beyond that, whether or not the earth’s temperature is increasing is treated with a collective shrug.

But the evidence does not reward the deniers. Here’s just one example:

 

Climate scientists typically use the 1950-to-1981 period as a baseline. Variations from this “norm” are called ‘anomalies.’ We’ll note that with exception of those three decades, temperatures have steadily increased. Moreover, any positive anomaly represents warming. Rolling the dice

July 23, 2012 by davald

Paul Krugman’s op-ed this morning places James Hansen’s latest paper front and center. (You can download it here.) It’s about probabilities and climate change. Yes, it may be wrong to attribute any particular weather event, however extreme, to global warming. But, as Hansen et al. argue, the likelihood of more frequent events being the result of increased warming rises over time. Krugman:

How should we think about the relationship between climate change and day-to-day experience? Almost a quarter of a century ago James Hansen, the NASA scientist who did more than anyone to put climate change on the agenda, suggested the analogy of loaded dice. Imagine, he and his associates suggested, representing the probabilities of a hot, average or cold summer by historical standards as a die with two faces painted red, two white and two blue. By the early 21st century, they predicted, it would be as if four of the faces were red, one white and one blue. Hot summers would become much more frequent, but there would still be cold summers now and then.

The Hansen paper itself begins thusly:

“Climate dice”, describing the chance of unusually warm or cool seasons relative to climatology, have become progressively “loaded” in the past 30 years, coincident with rapid global warming. The distribution of seasonal mean temperature anomalies has shifted toward higher temperatures and the range of anomalies has increased. An important change is the emergence of a category of summertime extremely hot outliers, more than three standard deviations (σ) warmer than climatology. This hot extreme, which covered much less than 1% of Earth’s surface in the period of climatology, now typically covers about 10% of the land area. We conclude that extreme heat waves, such as that in Texas and Oklahoma in 2011 and Moscow in 2010, were “caused” by global warming, because their likelihood was negligible prior to the recent rapid global warming. We discuss practical implications of this substantial, growing climate change.

Here are some charts from the paper:

 

Without going into the details, note the rightward shift of the frequency distribution curves. The bright purple represents the 2001-2011 period; it is the furthest right. Also, we see that the curves become flatter over time. That is, the anomalies are more likely over the latest period; the first period, 1951 to 1961, saw less variability—the more pronounced peak with shorter tails. Here’s Hansen et al.:

An important point to note is the large shift of the probability distribution function toward the right in each successive decade in the past 30 years, the shift being even larger for land areas in summer (lower half of Fig. 4). Another point is the fact that the distribution becomes broader in recent decades.

The Midwest is suffering from prolonged heat and drought, making life generally miserable for its inhabitants but also wreaking untold havoc on corn crops. Plants may have to be plowed back into the soil if the rains don’t come and the air doesn’t cool. Here’s a sample of a Nebraska field (source):

Krugman:

Now, maybe this drought will break in time to avoid the worst. But there will be more events like this. Joseph Romm, the influential climate blogger, has coined the term “Dust-Bowlification” for the prospect of extended periods of extreme drought in formerly productive agricultural areas. He has been arguing for some time that this phenomenon, with its disastrous effects on food security, is likely to be the leading edge of damage from climate change, taking place over the next few decades; the drowning of Florida by rising sea levels and all that will come later.

The Seattle Times suggests that Midwest’s impending food disaster bodes well for Eastern Washington farmers, who have yet to face the weather problems of their counterparts in Nebraska. The map below confirms (source):

 

However, Washington State can’t possibly compensate for the expected loss of Nebraska corn production. Here’s a chart showing 2009 yields (source):

Will the current drought finally lead to serious climate action? History isn’t encouraging. The deniers will surely keep on denying, especially because conceding at this point that the science they’ve trashed was right all along would be to admit their own culpability for the looming disaster. And the public is all too likely to lose interest again the next time the die comes up white or blue.

Flipping

July 21, 2012 by davald

What do you know about “dynamical systems”? If you’re like me, probably nothing, save for what’s implicit in the terms. Writing for the New York Times, Scientific American executive editor Fred Guterl suggests that we take seriously the work of  Marten Scheffer, a biologist at Wageningen University in the Netherlands.

[He] grew up swimming in clear lowland ponds. In the 1980s, many of these ponds turned turbid. The plants would die, algae would cover the surface, and only bottom-feeding fish remained. The cause — fertilizer runoff from nearby farms — was well known, but even after you stopped the runoff, replanted the lilies and restocked the trout, the ponds would stay dark and scummy.

Mr. Scheffer solved this problem with a key insight: the ponds behaved according to a branch of mathematics called “dynamical systems,” which deals with sudden changes. Once you reach a tipping point, it’s very difficult to return things to how they used to be. It’s easy to roll a boulder off a cliff, for instance, but much harder to roll it back. Once the ponds turned turbid, it wasn’t enough to just replant and restock. You had get them back to their original, clear state.

We know that the earth is warming as a result of excessive greenhouse gas emissions, mostly from coal plants. The increased warmth is affecting the planet’s “dynamical systems,” with untold consequences. Among these systems are Greenland’s ice sheets and the West African monsoon. They are prone to reach tipping points. Once reached, the systems flip, almost as simply as on or off. If the monsoon fails to materialize, African agriculture suffers. If too much ice melts into the Northern Atlantic, sea levels inexorably rise around the globe, inundating coastal territory.

But unlike Mr. Scheffer’s lowland ponds, flipped climate systems cannot be restored. Indeed, because of feedback mechanisms, the effects magnify exponentially. Globally, once temperatures hit a certain threshold, some scientists place this at another two degrees Centigrade, a chain of large events is triggered, either sequentially or concurrently. For example, further warming will likely release huge volumes of carbon dioxide stored in now-frozen tundra. As the oceans warm they will discharge carbon dioxide now trapped deep below. They are surely becoming more acidic, posing imminent threats to corals and, therefore, the entire marine food chain.

It is the planet’s misfortune to have global warming politicized. There are even huge swaths of the electorate, including office-holders, who still deny the science of climate change and the effects of increased carbon dioxide emissions. Therefore, I seriously doubt that any significant steps will or can be taken to arrest the trajectory toward tipping points.

I imagine that some years down the road the impacts of global warming will so severe and widespread as to escape ignorance. By then, of course, it will be far too late to do anything other than adapt. Guess who will be best positioned to do so more effectively? Yep. The One-percent, otherwise known on these pages as “greedy bastards.”

Gad, what a legacy.

Soiling our own nest

June 19, 2012 by davald

Well, soiling is much too mild. We’re on a direct course of massive environmental destruction, the consequences of which we’ve yet to consider. I wrote a bit about this here. Now comes Media Lens with a more elaborate report on global warming, the artificial threshold of another two degrees centigrade, and the gargantuan role played by corporations, including mainstream media, in both pursuing the economic juggernaut and frustrating efforts to educate and mobilize the public. (There is much about this last point here.)

Media Lens asks what became of the green movement, launched some 50 years ago by Rachel Carson’s Silent Spring. Why are there no massive protests, as there were in the 60s and 70s, first to end the Vietnam War then to focus attention and action on preserving the environment, action that led to the EPA, the Clean Water and Clean Air laws, and a host of federal and state environmental protection measures?

Media Lens quotes Edward O. Wilson:

We’re destroying the rest of life in one century. We’ll be down to half the species of plants and animals by the end of the century if we keep at this rate.

Why aren’t you young people out protesting the mess that’s being made of the planet? Why are you not repeating what was done in the ‘60s? Why aren’t you in the streets? And what in the world has happened to the green movement that used to be on our minds and accompanied by outrage and high hopes? What went wrong?

Media Lens suggests that corporate media have changed the subject, constantly.

The trouble is that most of what the public hears about politics, including environmental issues, comes from the corporate media. This is a disaster for genuine democracy. As discussed in a recent alert, the media industry is made up of large profit-seeking corporations whose main task is to sell audiences to wealthy advertisers – also corporations, of course – on whom the media depend for a huge slice of their revenues. It’s blindingly obvious that the corporate media is literally not in the business of alerting humanity to the real risk of climate catastrophe and what needs to be done to avert it.

I also think there’s an issue of psychology that factors into the lack of protest. The Vietnam War was real and deadly, splattered across television screens and newspapers’ front pages. We were dying; hundreds of thousands of Southeast Asians were being slaughtered. Also, we young men at the time had a very good chance of joining in the macabre mayhem. (A college student, I received two deferments, then the draft gave new meaning to lucky.) The images of Love Canal and a river literally on fire inflamed the public, bringing outrage to the streets, culminating in Richard M. Nixon’s signing major federal legislation that actually worked to clean the air and water and protect endangered species. In the 60s we saw uniformed police officers fire-hosing young blacks non-violently protesting segregatioon southern streets. Ugly, systemic racism televised. So we protested by the millions, losing leaders to assassinations. Again, laws were passed, change happened, though not as quickly and as thoroughly as we had wished.

But a changing climate fails to deliver urgency. Sure, there is this or that violent storm, possibly the product of increased warming. It seems a bit hotter this year. More wildfires in California and droughts across the Southwest. Eroding shorelines of distant islands may bother some, yet not enough to force us to the streets.

Ironically, it is the scientists who are more alarmed than the public and certainly the politicians. Those who know most also worry the most. Meanwhile the largely ignorant populace is all too happy to have the subject changed.

Besides, a war against carbon? Corporations need carbon.

In praise of idleness

May 30, 2012 by davald

That was the title of one of philosopher Bertrand Russell’s essays. He didn’t have in mind just sitting on one’s butt. Rather, the hustle and bustle of modern living was depriving us of leisure, the opportunity to contemplate, to engage in the arts, to recreate.

We’re obsessed with growth and productivity. As it turns out, these two strategies will be our and the planet’s undoing. Besides, being less productive would allow more of us to work, and there are far too many without jobs.

Such ideas emerge from the keyboard of Tim Jackson, a professor at the University of Surrey. Writing for the New York Times, he tell us:

But the relentless drive for productivity may also have some natural limits. Ever-increasing productivity means that if our economies don’t continue to expand, we risk putting people out of work. If more is possible each passing year with each working hour, then either output has to increase or else there is less work to go around. Like it or not, we find ourselves hooked on growth.

He has an interesting suggestion. One that involves altering incentives.

[The transition to a more sustainable economy and lifestyle] demands careful attention to incentive structures — lower taxes on labor and higher taxes on resource consumption and pollution, for example. It calls for more than just lip service to concepts of patient-centered care and student-centered learning. It requires the dismantling of perverse productivity targets and a serious investment in skills and training. In short, avoiding the scourge of unemployment may have less to do with chasing after growth and more to do with building an economy of care, craft and culture. And in doing so, restoring the value of decent work to its rightful place at the heart of society.

I’m a product of Berkeley in the 60s. So I like what this man is saying. How about you?
