Not all that progressive

January 19, 2012 by davald

American Progress has done some arithmetic using publicly available sources. They produced this chart:

In 2010, according to the U.S. Census, the distribution of total income looked like this:

While this is hardly scientific, why not set the rate according to each quintile’s share of wealth? Or, we might take the average rate (in 2009 that was 17%) as a base, then set the marginal rate for all income above the average of adjusted gross income, which was $49,000 in 2009, for each quintile. Thus, if you were in the top quintile, your marginal rate on all income above $49,000 would be 50.2%.*

Several of the GOP presidential candidates have proposed tax reform strategies, including a flat tax on all income. The flat tax would certainly simplify matters, which is what Jerry Brown argued when he ran for the White House in 1992; he suggested 17%, as I recall, which happens to be the average tax in 2009 (see footnote). However, a flat tax yields hardship for the lower quintiles and a boon for higher-income individuals.

Suppose we assume a flat tax of 17% on all income. For someone making only $10,000, the tax obligation would be $1,700. If the income were $1,000,000, the tax would be $170,000. Under the existing tax structure, the rate is 10% on incomes below $17,000 (married with joint returns). For the millionaire, it’s a bit more complicated. I come up with a shade less than $320,000 that goes to the IRS, slightly different from the figure shown in the footnote, but nearly 90% more than under a flat tax.

After taxes, the person earning $10,000 has $9,000 left over. The millionaire still has $680,000 on which to live. Chances are the poorer individual spends every single penny of the $9,000; but the millionaire can pay all her expenses and still have money to save, invest, and play.

One may ask by what rationale should tax rates be progressive? (Or, in Ron Paul’s case, why should there be any federal taxes at all?) Good question. Here’s one answer:

The real justification for progressive taxation has nothing to do with compassion or fairness or funding the welfare roles. The rationale for progressive is the preservation of competitive markets in which people are not prevented from entering into a business and/or employment because of monopoly or oligarchical control by a few large “owners” of the productive means.  Nowhere is this more evident than in the “financial” sector.  It should be obvious that if corporate income in the billions is taxed much more heavily than corporate income in the millions then earnings per share will be better in smaller corporate entities. And if that is the case then we have a very elegant control on the size of corporations.

I would add to this that the situation that we’re in now, with increasing accumulation of wealth by one small sliver of society, leads ineluctably to mischief. The two great economic upheavals of the past 100 years followed huge increases in wealth by the One-percenters. They have so much money they literally don’t know what to do with it; so they start gambling. Yes, ladies and gentlemen, Mr. Keynes was right: Wall Street is nothing more than a casino. With so much money involved, their mistakes imperil the Rest of Us. We dodged a monumental bullet a couple of years ago. As bad as things are, they could have been much, much worse.

And one more thing, echoing Justice Brandeis: We can either have a democracy or concentrated wealth, but we can’t have both. We pretend to favor democracy. Yet we have allowed a tiny few to amass great fortunes. They operate by different rules than the Rest of Us, and those rules further enrich the powerful while eroding our ability to effect changes that benefit the many. Plutocracy wasn’t what the Founding Fathers had in mind. But that’s what we’ve got.

One way of limiting the power of the few is to tax the hell out of them, then put that money in service to the Rest of Us. Our schools are dilapidated and ill-supplied. The roads are in disrepair. We have no alternative transportation to speak of. Education is too costly. The planet is warming. (Though I write this in the midst of unusual snow showers.)

Time to resurrect genuine democracy. Except there’s all these damn Republicans in the way.

You want models?

January 13, 2012 by davald

Whether or not our political leaders take global warming seriously (and they’re not), global warming continues, mostly as a result of combusting coal. But there’s another specter threatening under seas and tundra: methane “burps.”

The climate researchers tending to the blog site Real Climate have given us an interactive model. We get to put in some numbers, push the calculate button, and, voilà, instant graphs.

CH4, methane, is an especially pernicious greenhouse gas, over 20 times more potent than CO2. Huge pockets of the gas are trapped under frozen tundra and cold seabeds. But as the climate warms, the tundra thaws—same with the oceans. The resulting burps promise further catastrophe.

Another punished good deed

December 30, 2011 by davald

Those of us not living under a rock these days can attest to the utter failure of Congress to act responsibly on climate change. Hell, an entire party rejects the science and many in their rank call global warming a “hoax,” perpetrated to attract government subsidies. Shame on Jim Hansen.

So, it is left to the states to tackle the problems. And that’s precisely what California is trying to do, boasting the nation’s best conservation programs and now trying to reduce the carbon content of transportation fuels. But a judge won’t let them. The San Francisco Chronicle:

A federal judge on Thursday blocked California from enforcing its first-in-the-nation mandate for cleaner, low-carbon fuels, saying the rules favor biofuels produced in the state.

Out-of-state entities, including biofuel producers, sued California charging that the law violates the Interstate Commerce clause by giving preference to in-state refiners.

California’s rules for fuels that comply with the law assign a 10 percent advantage to ethanol produced in the state. State officials said the preference was justified because the transportation of ethanol from other states produces climate-changing greenhouse gases, and because California producers have more access than those in the Midwest to low-emission hydroelectric and nuclear power.

Should this ruling stand (California may appeal to the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals) it may have repercussions in Washington state. In 2005 Olympia passed SB 5111, which provides a tax credit for solar equipment manufactured in the state.

One company making such equipment is Outback Power, which is located near the Arlington airport. Itek Energy is another.

I am unaware of challenges to the Washington law from, say, out-of-state manufacturers of solar equipment. The California judge’s ruling may spark one.

Stay tuned.

We’ll save lives, but will it stand?

December 22, 2011 by davald

E.P.A. head Lisa Jackson announced yesterday that electric-generating plants will have to substantially reduce mercury emissions and other toxins. The Times:

The new rule, unless blocked by Congress or the courts, will be the first time the federal government has enforced limits on mercury, arsenic, acid gases and other poisonous and carcinogenic chemicals emitted by the burning of fossil fuels.

Mercury is bad stuff. It kills thousands of people a year, although you won’t find ‘mercury’ on the toe tag. That it took 20 years to finally get the regulation in place attests to the strength of financial interests vis-a-vis clear scientific evidence that mercury is not good for your health.

The biggest source of mercury, however, is coal combustion. About half of the electricity produced in the U.S. comes from coal-burning plants. Although the new regulation will not force closure of the plants it will require utilities and independent power producers to install scrubbers and other devices to prevent the mercury from entering the atmosphere. Here’s Wikipedia:

Mercury is a highly toxic element commonly found in coal and municipal waste. Wet scrubbers are only effective for removal of soluble mercury species, such as oxidized mercury, Hg2+. Mercury vapor in its elemental form, Hg0, is insoluble in the scrubber slurry and not removed. Therefore, additional process of Hg0conversion is required to complete mercury capture. Usually addition of the halogens to the flue gas are used for this purpose. The type of coal burned as well as the presence of a selective catalytic reduction unit both affect the ratio of elemental to oxidized mercury in the flue gas and thus the degree to which the mercury is removed.

Burning coal, a relatively cheap way to generate electricity, will now get more expensive, though not so much as to render the plants economically inviable. Nevertheless, there are more than 600 coal plants in the United States and both their owners and endusers wield bipartisan political influence. So, it remains to be seen whether or not E.P.A.’s new regulation will survive.

Of course, for the good of the planet and its numerous species all coal plants should be terminated. It’s going to get too damn hot if they’re not.

Brown wants green, and so should we all

December 18, 2011 by davald

California governor Jerry Brown blasted global warming deniers in a recent all-day conference in San Francisco, called “Extreme risks and California’s future.”

“We have an entire political party who has a doctrine of absolute denial,” said Brown, who was wearing a green tie. “All the denial in the world doesn’t change the facts that greenhouse gases are building up. The main thing we have to deal with in climate change is the skepticism, the denial and the cultlike behavior of the political lemmings that would take us over the cliff.”

Golden State has 840 miles of coastline. Increased warming of the planet will melt glaciers and expand ocean volumes, threatening shoreline developments.

Max Auffhammer, an environmental economist at UC Berkeley, said if there is a 4-foot rise in sea levels by the end of the century, and then a storm surge, both the San Francisco and Oakland airports would be under water. So would parts of Silicon Valley, he said.

Then-governor Arnold Schwarzenegger signed into law the nation’s most ambitious program to curb greenhouse gas emissions. Other states have adopted energy resource portfolio requirements, including Washington with Initiative 937, which governs electric utilities; however, it doesn’t touch transportation, Washington’s biggest source of carbon dioxide emissions.

Despite such efforts, last year global CO2 emissions jumped by the highest amount on record, after a decline in 2009 owing to the recession. Because modern economies run on fossil fuels, economic growth yields higher emissions.

The challenge, which receives scant attention from our political leaders, is to grow the economy without adding more carbon dioxide to the atmosphere. Since coal is the most carbon-intensive fuel, any serious strategy must effect the closure of coal-fired power plants.

Washington has but one coal plant, in Centralia. It will closed by 2025, as directed by Olympia. But many states have dozens of coal plants, which supply almost all the electricity consumed by their citizens. Nationwide, coal plants represent nearly half of all electricity generation.

Sourcewatch.org has an interactive map with an inventory of each state’s coal plants. I clicked on Ohio.

Unlike Washington, states like Ohio lack significant hydropower resources. If not coal, what?

Natural gas-fired plants emit about half the CO2 of coal plants. If every coal plant could be and were converted to natural gas, carbon dioxide emissions would fall significantly. The question then becomes natural gas supply, the extraction of which also emits greenhouse gases.

Here’s a table comparing CO2 emissions by type of generation (source).

The Snohomish County PUD, of which I’m a board member, derives almost all of its electricity from hydro facilities, in particular those along the Columbia and Snake rivers. We have no carbon in our supply portfolio, save for what we purchase from the market pool, which is a small fraction of the total.

But this has much to do with geography, aided and abetted by the massive New Deal projects like Grand Coulee dam. Washington’s utilities will only get greener over time, because of the initiative.

But that’s not the case in Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Kentucky, Alabama, Texas, and the list goes on. If their coal plants were shut down, economies would collapse and the people would wilt in the summers and freeze in the winters.

The problem with carbon dioxide is that it doesn’t matter whence it’s emitted. Ohio carbons mix with all of the other emitted carbons in the atmosphere where they remain for more than a hundred years. So, we can be green in Washington and feel all the better for it. But we’ll suffer the consequences of climate change if others can’t find a viable path to green. Yet, one has to start somewhere, and since our national governments can’t commit to meaningful solutions it may be up to individual regions, states, counties, and cities—hoping, I guess, that a sufficient number of entities adopt effective programs to actually reduce carbon emissions.

It certainly doesn’t help that the two most powerful Republicans in Congress come from states heavily dependent on coal. Even if they were convinced that climate change is serious and mostly human-caused, neither could advocate the closing of their dirty plants should they wish to remain in office.

As you can tell, I am downright pessimistic on all this. There’s no political will. Besides, it’s probably too late to stabilize, let alone reverse, global warming.

Our descendants will somehow have to adapt to much harsher weather and flooded coastlines. There will be prolonged droughts in already dry regions and likely more precipitation in wet ones.

I foresee the day when the entire Southwest begins a mass exodus, after the wells run dry. To where will they run? The Pacific Northwest, of course.

Denial rises with greenhouse gases

December 16, 2011 by davald

There was a time in this country when the issue of global warming occupied center stage. We citizens vaguely understood the science of how carbon dioxide thickens the earth’s protective blanket thereby warming the planet. We wanted governments to do something about it. Some did. Conspicuously absent from the signature pages was the United States, the highest per-capita contributor of greenhouse gases. All told, there are more carbons in the atmosphere emitted by the U.S. than by any other nation. China is working hard to overtake us in that miserable regard.

But the modern industrial economy is literally fueled by oil, and a majority of America’s electricity is generated by combusting coal, with the highest carbon intensity. It should not surprise us that our vast military apparatus has much to do with preserving access to petroleum and protecting oil-shipping lanes. Without a reliable supply of oil, the modern economy collapses.

Extracting, refining, transporting, and purveying oil is big business. The companies involved in the petroleum industry would suffer mightily under an effective carbon-reduction scheme. So they’re working very hard and spending a lot of money to ensure that no such scheme materializes. And they’ve been amazingly successful at it.

We know that it’s fairly easy to influence public perception of world events and conditions. Want a war? Concoct an enemy. Want people to divert attention from climate change or question its validity? Fund think tanks, members of Congress, and media to create doubt about the science, minimize the risks of global warming, or simply to talk about something else.

Media Lens, a British meda watchdog, recently commented on the failure of the media to accurately report the scientific consensus on global warming, enlighten the public on the implications of climate change, and keep the issue where it belongs—front and center. Here’s Media Lens:

With the Kyoto Treaty due to expire in 2012, the so-called ‘international community’ has failed abysmally to fulfil its commitments to protect the planet. This should surprise no-one. As senior Nasa climate scientist James Hansen pointed out after the previous climate summit in Mexico in 2010, UN talks are ‘doomed to failure’ since they do not address the fundamental physical constraints of the Earth’s climate system and how to live within them.

Public concern about climate change continues to rise. According to the latest Eurobarometer opinion poll (October 2011), 68% of Europeans polled consider climate change a very serious problem (up from 64% in 2009). Altogether 89% see it as a serious problem (either ‘very serious’ or ‘fairly serious’). On a scale of 1 (least) to 10 (most), the seriousness of climate change is ranked at 7.4, against 7.1 in 2009.

Meanwhile, media interest in the subject has crashed. Dr. Robert J. Brulle of Drexel University describes a ‘collapse of any significant coverage of climate change in the [US] media. We know that 2010 was a record low year, and 2011 will probably look much the same. If the media doesn’t draw attention to the issue, public opinion will decline’.

Media Lens quotes a former journalist: ‘Capitalism is trampling on journalism.’ Of course, the trampling knows no bounds.

BBC produced a multi-part documentary called “Frozen Planet.” It’s narrated by David Attenborough. As the title suggests, it’s about the Arctic and Antarctic regions. In marketing the program for distribution BBC made the last segment optional. And you can guess what that dealt with: “the threat of climate change.” America’s public television network, PBS, declined to purchase the series’ final installment.

Media Lens interviewed James Hansen, the world’s leading authority on climate change. Here is an excerpt:

We then asked him to give a rough figure to indicate how far he felt governments had moved towards tackling climate change. Would he say that governments were 1%, 20%, 50%, 70%,… of the way there? We knew this was imprecise, but we wanted to get an idea of his gut feeling. He responded:

‘0%, because they are starting down a wrong track, requiring 1-2 decades to reset. “Goals” for emission reduction, cap-and-trade with offsets, while continuing to build more coal-fired power plants and developing unconventional fossil fuels is a disastrous path. It is meant to fool people, even themselves. A strategic approach would instead recognize the geophysical boundary conditions, specifically that coal emissions must be rapidly phased out.’

He added some disturbing analysis:

‘The fundamental economic requirement concerns the price of (cheap, subsidized) fossil fuels relative to alternatives (energy efficiency, renewables, and other carbon-free energies) — there must be a rising price on carbon emissions (a fee, at the coal/oil/gas source or port of entry). As that price rises and the competition ensues we would reach a point where alternatives suddenly take off and we move beyond the dirty fossil fuel era. The fear that this will in fact occur is what drives the fossil interests who have totally taken control of our governments’ actions.’

We’re doomed.

Low-carbon fuel standards and tar sands

December 15, 2011 by davald

Several states have either adopted or have proposed a low-carbon fuel standard, Washington being among the latter. The most comprehensive is California’s, whose goal is to reduce upstream carbon emissions, all the way to the extraction process. Transportation is the second largest source of carbon emissions, behind electricity generation (coal and natural gas).

Our neighbors to the north discovered a huge volume of oil trapped in Alberta tar sands. The process to separate the oil from the sands is every bit as pernicious as the ultimate release of vehicle carbon emissions. Big Oil and the Canadian government want to build a pipeline to pump the separated oil to the lower United States, where it would be refined for vehicle use. For those of us concerned with climate change the pipeline poses a very serious threat. It would facilitate the combustion of more carbons, which will be trapped by the atmosphere and contribute to planetary warming.

The extraction, transportation, and refinement of the Alberta oil requires a large capital investment. But the returns are even greater. Thus, there are significant political forces arrayed to fight carbon-reduction laws. Indeed, should a sufficient number of states adopt low-carbon fuel standards, the market for Canadian oil would be sharply curtailed or even disappear.

Salon.com has the story on how the Canadian embassy and U.S. lobbyists conspired to thwart low-carbon standards. The online magazine obtained hundreds of pages worth of emails chronicling the joint efforts.

When President Barack Obama decided in early November to delay a decision on TransCanada’s Keystone XL pipeline until after the next election, America’s environmental movement celebrated one of its biggest victories in recent memory. And no doubt the news came as a blow to Alberta’s tar sands industry, and to Canada’s oft-stated dream of becoming the next global energy superpower.

But behind activists’ jubilation lurked a somber reality, an untold story with much wider implications. The broader fight to reform Alberta’s tar sands, the one which actually stood a chance of breaking America’s addiction to the continent’s most polluting road fuel, has been quietly abandoned over the past several years. For that we can thank the planet’s richest oil companies and their Canadian government allies, who’ve together waged a stealthy war against President Obama’s climate change ambitions.

Adding insult to injury Canada’s recently re-elected conservative government just announced its withdrawal from the Kyoto Accords. Of course, the U.S. refused to become a signatory in the first place.

None of this bodes well for the future.

Irrational choice

December 10, 2011 by davald

You may share my bewilderment over so many of our fellow citizens choosing political candidates whose benefactors and ideologies betray positions and interests antithetical to those of the Rest of Us. No? Then feel free to skip this.

If one has been paying attention to the media and blogosphere since the collapse of the housing bubble and the emergent Great Recession one could not escape the sharp divide in rhetoric, diagnosis, and prescription. While consensus exists that the economy is not like it used to be, none exists beyond that trivial observation.

I have titled this blog “Irrational choice.” By that I mean deciding against one’s own interests, as opposed to a more literal connotation of unreasonable or illogical. Those who vote against their perceived self-interest no doubt possess reason and can wade through a syllogism. I might suggest that what’s going on is more subtle and yet perverse.

In his book Thinking, Fast and Slow, psychologist Daniel Kahneman tells us that most of what passes for cognition is instinctive and unconscious. He calls this “System 1.” Deliberation, inference, conscious mental behavior involves “System 2.” These do not stand for physical locations within our brain, but rather distinct processes. System 1 thinking (“fast”) can be affected by an infinite number of what Kahneman calls “priming effects.” (He didn’t coin the term.) The problem for our species is that System 1 dominates, even to the point that we “see” the world through prior influences and, especially, associations. Why? Kahneman suggests that System 1 mental behavior is easy, requiring less energy than System 2. Besides, it keeps us out of danger, since System 2 can’t react to warning signs quickly enough.

System 2 tends to be lazy and readily defers to System 1. It requires concentration, calories, and selectivity. We’ve got to block out stimuli in order to focus our attention. Kahneman gives the example of trying to calculate in one’s head the product of two numbers, 17 and 24. Unless we’re another “Rainman,” we’re forced to drop everything else to perform the mental arithmetic. System 2 would prefer (I’m being metaphorical here) to take the easy train, just because it is so much more intensive than System 1. It really doesn’t want to overrule our unconscious biases.

I’ll venture out onto a long intellectual limb here: the Republican mind operates almost exclusively in the System 1 realm; the Democratic mind avails itself of System 2 more frequently. Stepping out a bit further, Republicans make more irrational choices than Democrats.

Now, I’m not the first person to draw such an inference. Following the GOP landslide in 1994, Berkeley cognitive scientist George Lakoff was so anguished by voters’ electoral preferences that he decided to write a book. Published two years later, it bore the title Moral Politics. It initially carried the subtitle What Conservatives Know That Liberals Don’t. He replaced this in the second edition with How Liberals and Conservatives Think. (Thomas Frank, now the editor of Harper’s magazine, was also befuddled by the conservative victories, which required so many to vote against their interests. He wrote a book in response, What’s the Matter with Kansas?)

Lakoff set about to describe two broad categories of metaphors to distinguish between the conservative and the liberal, or progressive. The former falls under the strict father morality; the latter under nurturant parent.

The strict father is all about self-discipline, self-reliance, and tough love—I’m doing this or that to you, child, for your own good. It will make you stronger. Good behavior is rewarded; bad behavior is punished. If you fail, chances are it’s your own damn fault.

One also sees this constellation within the conservative Christian religion, at least as practiced in the U.S. There is a definite hierarchy. God, the father, is at the top. All must show obeisance to him, or else incur his wrath. The bible is a set of rules that must be followed.

Likewise, in the family unit the father/husband is the authoritative figure; everyone else defers to him, including the “submissive” wife. In turn, the father/husband is expected to provide for the family. Failure to do so, regardless of circumstances, is a sign of moral weakness.

If you’ve bought into this so far, you can appreciate that the conservative tends to view government as harmful, if not pernicious. The government, especially as provider of public services, contributes to moral weakness by creating dependencies, which lead to sloth and irresponsibility.

Quite opposite, the liberal is the product of nurturant parent morality. The child will be raised in a loving, caring, compassionate environment. Mistakes or misbehaviors are not signs of moral failing but part of normal development. Husband and wife are co-equals. Both are responsible for the child’s well-being.

If the liberal is religious, his or her god will be fair, just, sympathetic, forgiving. There is an emphasis on the New Covenant as described in the bible, rather than the Ten Commandments. The Sermon on the Mount looms large. Disagreements should be resolved by negotiation rather than fisticuffs. Peace is preferable to war.

Government, in the liberal mind, should provide help to those who are victims of circumstances, often outside their control. Government is about safety nets and collective insurance and less about punishment of wrongdoing. Taxes are judged necessary to provide important services, and tax rates should be progressive, so that those who have more pay higher rates.

Lakoff, like Kahneman, opines that most of our thinking is unconscious. We react instinctively, and we’re easily influenced by metaphors, as Lakoff argues. Indeed, that’s how we think—metaphorically. Lakoff discusses certain terms and phrases in our vocabulary and their service as metaphors in evaluating our experiences. For example, we associate ‘white’ with ‘good’ and ‘pure’ and ‘black’ with ‘bad’ and ‘dirty.’ ‘Taller’ is better than ‘shorter.’ Thus when someone says that he “looks up to someone” he is doing two things: elevating the admired individual while subordinating himself.

Both Ladoff and Kahneman attach much importance to associations, which are as strong as they are pervasive. The broad metaphorical categories described above yield relevant associations that affect how we view the world around us. Since System 1 is always “on” during our waking moments, we have a strong propensity to make snap judgments about nearly everything. (As Malcolm Gladwell put it, we decide in a “blink.”)

What I find perverse about all this is that the mental associations and the priming effect actually determine what we see—even after our judgments have been falsified by objective proof. Worse, there is some research that suggests that our prejudices are reinforced by the counterfactual evidence.

Consider the topic of global warming. Those who deny the science and its conclusions will do so in even stronger tones when confronted by more scientific evidence confirming that global warming is real and largely human-caused. That is to say, we cannot appeal to the denier’s reason, since his System 1 mode blocks out his System 2. The prejudices intensify.

Now let’s return to behaviors that undermine one’s self-interest. In the economic realm, most people belong to the 99-percenters. They have virtually nothing in common with the One-percenters, whether it’s accumulated wealth or the company kept. Most Republicans belong to the larger group. Yet, the Republican Party, as evinced by policies emanating from the House, not only ignore the interests of the 99-percenters; they eviscerate them. The GOP prefers to coddle the very wealthy, and stick it to everyone else.

That attitude becomes clearer with each proposal issuing from the White House. Most recently, the Republican-dominated House is holding hostage Obama’s proposals: extended unemployment benefits, the payroll tax cut, and about $20 billion in funding for the new health care law. The New York Times:

House Republicans moved Friday toward a showdown with President Obama over the Keystone XL oil pipeline, which they included in their bill to extend tax relief for 160 million American workers through 2012.

Republicans said the pipeline would create jobs and had support from dozens of Democrats in Congress. And they welcomed the opportunity to split two Democratic constituencies: labor union members who stand to benefit from work on the pipeline and environmentalists, who oppose the project.

The House Republican bill, scheduled for a vote next week on the House floor, would deal with three urgent issues: extending a payroll tax cut scheduled to expire next month; continuing jobless benefits for the long-term unemployed; and averting a 27 percent cut in Medicare payments to doctors that would otherwise occur on Jan. 1.

The bill, unveiled Friday, would also block certain air pollution rules and eliminate more than $20 billion of spending planned under Mr. Obama’s new health care law.

The House Republicans, and all current GOP presidential candidates, favor policies that benefit extremely wealthy citizens at the expense of the Rest of Us, including most conservatives. So why do so many Republicans support policies adverse to their own interests?

It may have everything to do with Lakoff’s strict father morality, which is dominated by System 1 cognition. Financial success, the conservatives hold, deserves praise and honor rather than opprobrium. The rich should be worshiped rather than vilified. They have earned their money through discipline, self-reliance, and dint of hard work—or so the myth goes.

Thus, in the conservative mind, the Occupy Wall Street crowd are infidels; they would seek to subtract from the vast fortunes of the wealthy. By this System 1-thinking, extracting more taxes out of the rich violates the mores of the strict father metaphor. Likewise, using tax dollars to fund “entitlements” is equally pernicious to the conservative mind, since government benefits reward the weak and, therefore, the morally degenerate.

Irrational, to be sure, but nevertheless understandable within the above-described context. It would follow, then, that no attempt to demonstrate the folly of undermining one’s self-interests can succeed. More likely, such efforts only harden the resolve.

The liberal Rest of Us waste precious time and resources engaging the conservative in argument. There is a battle going on, and it’s time to forcibly press the nurturant parent. Here’s Lakoff:

Conservatives have, to a significant extent, come together on the question of their central values. Progressives [and liberals] must also unite, if they are to have a chance of holding their own against the conservative juggernaut. I think this issue to bring progressives together should be this most central of all issues—raising children to become responsible, empathetic adults.
