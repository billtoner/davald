Demonizing

September 12, 2012 by davald

A recent post prompted the following comment:

attacking the rich makes me very uncomfortable, and sadly that has become the theme of many discussions these days. also, most speeches at the RNC and DNC tried to celebrate poverty and struggle…Is this the right way forward? Comparisons that demonize an accomplishment lead to divisions and ill- feelings.

I admit to watching not a second of either convention, so I can’t speak to their content and whether or not there was an attempt “to celebrate poverty and struggle,” something I doubt would emanate from party officials on either side of the political aisle—at least uttered in earnest tones. Indeed, my readings of recent statements by Ryan et al. is that the poor are essentially “moochers” and ne’re-do-wells. Such an attitude is evidently permissible to those who are rendered “uncomfortable” by attacks on the rich.

But here’s the point: if there are “divisions” and “ill-feelings” amongst the populace, and there certainly are, they are not the result of the successful rich being demonized. Rather, and as I’ve tried to make plain on numerous occasions, it is the rich themselves who have created the disharmony and disparities.

This is not merely an idle opinion on my part; many have chronicled the concerted efforts to extract ever-larger rents from the Rest of Us, mostly through the best Congress money can buy. I have in mind recent works by Joseph Stiglitz, John Cassidy, Dean Baker, and others. The Great Divergence, in other words, was neither an accident nor the inevitable outcome of “the market.”

That said, does engaging in “class warfare,” as the Right accuses, improve the chances of attaining a more humane society, one less marked by extremes of both wealth and poverty? I have no idea.

I am reminded, however, of a Catholic nun who told me many years ago that the first step toward achieving social justice was “to name the evil.” One such evil, she continued, was the growing gap between the Haves and the Have-nots. To ignore the glaring inequality was to undermine the Gospel, in her opinion.

I have long since abandoned religion and my Catholic heritage. Yet, the nun’s advice  strikes me as both reasonable and necessary.

So, I shall continue to rail against the excesses of wealth and privilege, especially as they deprive the Rest of Us of dignity and economic security. If that makes some “uncomfortable,” so be it.

Striking teachers

September 12, 2012 by davald

Chicago teachers are on strike.

The New York Times editors condemn the educators and, in particular, the union head for stubbornness and audacity. Salary should not be an issue, say the editors, since Chicago’s teachers earn about as much as their New York City counterparts, at about $75,000 a year. Nor should the teachers be resisting Mayor Emmanuel’s effort to impose a state-mandated requirement that evaluations be based in part on student test scores. Worst of all, add the editors, a strike hurts children. Ouch.

On these pages I have been singing the praises of Finnish education reform, which emphasizes teacher quality—not after teachers have been hired, such is the U.S. focus, but before they enter the classroom. The evidence strongly suggests that the Finnish Way has succeeded to such an extent that Finland’s students now score highest in international tests. Moreover, Finnish teachers enjoy as much, if not more, respect than other professionals.

In America, the reform movement is also centered on teachers and their presumably recalcitrant and reactionary unions. But, as I’ve said, we go about it ass backwards. Predictably, this terribly flawed strategy, from No Child Left Behind to Race to the Top, causes rancor, bitterness, anxiety, and, in the end, fails to improve our public schools. Meanwhile, legislators slash education funding, deny troubled students much needed social services, and allow, if not enable, widespread poverty absent in Finland and elsewhere.

Against the Times editors I offer this perspective from Freddie deBoer, writing for Balloon Juice. Do teachers make too much? deBoer:

The discussion about the Chicago strike is filled with people parsing the numbers about how much teachers make and whether it is too little or too much or just right. But having that conversation is conceding exactly the point we shouldn’t concede. It assumes that, for public employees and no one else, there is a certain amount that is just too much for the job. If you run a sex toy factory and make $250K a year, you’re a lion of capitalism. If you drive a city bus and you claw your way up to $38K a year, well, you’re a lucky ducky who has got to be put in his or her place.

deBoer argues that one cannot be pro-education and anti-teacher. This falls under the well, duh category. Think about it for a moment. Where do students go when they arrive at school each day? The classroom. And whom do they see already there and interact with? Their teacher. Formal education is all about the critical interface between that classroom teacher and her pupils. Period. Whether students are rich or poor, at grade level or below, hungry or well-fed, handicapped or healthy—the teacher is expected to improve academic performance.

For anyone who has not taught school, and, I dare say, that’s nearly all of us, you have little, if any, idea of what is involved in dealing with twenty or more children of disparate needs and dispositions. I’ve said elsewhere that teaching is the most challenging occupation on the planet. None of us would survive minutes let alone months in a classroom, especially at the elementary level.

I have the good fortune of listening to my wife share with me her daily experiences teaching nearly two dozen students. Most of them are poor, products of broken homes, and many are either Native or Hispanic. They are parsecs removed from “the top,” and their intellectual journeys will be slow, frustrating, and incredibly difficult. Yet, despite the obvious impediments, my wife delights in her charges, struggles to close their academic gaps, and provides physical and emotional security where none exists outside the classroom. I sit and marvel.

Yet my wife and her colleagues are begrudged their modest wages and benefits, even as we turn a blind eye to the incomes of athletes, CEOs, and hedge-fund managers who do no more for society than score touchdowns and purchase second and third homes. The latter earn their dollars in accordance with “the market.”

Teachers, on the other hand, work for “the government.” And that, I guess, is the problem.

Converging on teachers

September 5, 2012 by davald

Having spent some time trying to teach adolescents I can attest to the challenges confronting those who would presume to educate our nation’s youth. Each classroom presents its own unique problems, among them the broad disparity in skills, curiosity, and self-discipline. Nevertheless, teaching has become a thankless endeavor, as reformers—always the reformers—seek to blame teachers for poor student performance and everything else.

Writing for Salon.org, Paul Tough argues that such focus is not only wrong but counterproductive, given persistently high poverty in America. He offers a dubious syllogism to explain the reformers’ logic:

First, scores on achievement tests in school correlate strongly with life outcomes, no matter what a student’s background. Second, children in low-income homes did much worse on achievement tests than children in middle-income and high-income homes. And third, certain schools, using a very different model than traditional public schools, were able to substantially raise the achievement-test scores of low-income children. The conclusion: if we could replicate on a big, national scale the accomplishments of those schools, we could make a huge dent in poverty’s impact on children’s success.

Against this argument Tough cites research linking poverty with arrested intellectual and, I would add, emotional development. Simply put, poor kids struggle with learning to a much higher degree than privileged pupils. Thus, placing the onus on teachers for reducing or eliminating poverty is doomed to failure.

Tough asks why no one talks about poverty anymore. It used to be front and center in the national dialogue, especially during the Kennedy and Johnson administrations. The Republicans mention the poor only to blame them for their own misfortunes. Tough:

Where the typical conservative argument on poverty falls short is that it often stops right there: Character matters … and that’s it. There’s not much society can do until poor people shape up and somehow develop better character. In the meantime, the rest of us are off the hook. We can lecture poor people, and we can punish them if they don’t behave the way we tell them to, but that’s where our responsibility ends.

Democrats have joined their conservative counterparts in pushing reform by accountability, embracing “value-added” performance criteria, as if developing better teachers were only a matter of data. Collect it. Analyze it. Act on it. Today it’s “race to the top,” which exacerbates the flawed approaches in No Child Left Behind.

But, again, both political parties and a host of educational theorists and pundits completely ignore the elephant in the room: poverty. The percentage of Americans who are poor reached 15.1 in 2010 and is expected to be almost 16 percent this year. These are the highest levels since the early 60s.

 

Several years ago I painstakingly combed through data from the Washington State Office of the Superintendent of Public Instruction, or OSPI for short. I correlated the results of standardized test scores (it was called the WASL back then) with the percentage of students receiving free or reduced lunch, a proxy for poverty. The conclusion was obvious: the higher the poverty rate the lower the test scores.

I compared two schools: Shoultes Elementary in Marysville, Wash., and Mercer Island Elementary. Here is a chart showing fourth grade math scores on the WASL:

 

Now let’s look at poverty rates, again using the free-or-reduced lunch as proxy:

 

What a glaring contrast. Mercer Island’s poverty rate is so low as to barely register on the graph, whereas the Marysville school’s is above fifty percent as of 2010.

It would seem that the answer to improving academic performance is to make kids wealthier. But reformers mistakenly put it backwards: wealth follows higher test scores. Author Tough:

So when we’re looking for root causes of poverty-related underachievement, why do we tend to focus on the wrong culprits and ignore the ones that science tells us do the most damage?

Tough suggests that the science is difficult to understand and not widely publicized. He also believes that we’re uncomfortable talking about parenting, and that good parenting skills tend to be more prevalent in wealthier households and neighborhoods. Finally, Tough agrees that character matters. However, he adds, those traits that lend themselves to higher academic performance (e.g., perseverance and optimism) are teachable themselves and not the product of genetic code.

As I’ve written about Finland’s education system, Finnish students with special needs—and nearly all students have them—receive special attention, including free meals, free health care, and targeted intervention. It helps, of course, that the Finns are dedicated to social justice and generous welfare for all. Less than four percent of the population lives in poverty, although, again, being poor does not deny Finns access to health care or a quality education.

Diane Ravitch, who was a big proponent of the NCLB law, did an about-face. She now condemns it and Obama’s Race to the Top. She writes:

Here’s an interesting contrast: We claim to be preparing students for global competitiveness, and we reward mastery of basic skills. Our guiding principles: Competition, accountability, and choice. Finland has this singular goal: to develop the humanity of each child. Isn’t that a shocking goal? Their guiding principles: equity, creativity, and prosperity.

Finland rightly deserves attention today as a nation that treats its children as a precious resource and that honors the adults who make education their passion and their career. Someday, I hope, we will recognize the failure of the behaviorist approach now in vogue; someday we will see that our current “reforms” are appropriate for the industrial era of the early 20th century, not for the needs of the 21st century. When that day arrives, we will understand the deep wisdom of Finland, with its love for children and its respect for educators, and we will be grateful that there is a successful alternative to our own failed model.

Hmm. What a novel idea: “develop the humanity of each child.” Education as if children mattered.

Debt, moochers and beyond

September 2, 2012 by davald

For those in need of some guidance, the Republican Party believes that it can fool most voters most of the time. That was the general theme of its recently concluded convention, which formally established Mitt Romney and Paul Ryan as the GOP torchbearers heading into the November elections. Oh, and the party put forth a whopper of a platform amidst treacly rhetoric appealing to our baser instincts. Okay, nothing new here; and I’m sure that we’ll get more of the same this week from the Democrats.

But back to the Republicans. A recurring motif, which may serve to define the conservative agenda, is a perpetual rant against debt. That’s a topic about which I’ve written often, if not also extensively. Should we be concerned?

Well, we can check in with the Japanese. That country’s debt-to-GDP ratio is up to 233 percent! Has Japan departed civilization? Do its people suffer mightily?

To be sure, Japan’s poverty rate continues upwards, which is not a good thing, especially if you’re poor. On the other hand, Japan’s unemployment rate remains low, certainly against ours.

 

Gad. Even at its peak, the rate was just five percent. If we value work, and Republicans assuredly do, the Japanese have figured out a way to keep people employed—notwithstanding rising debt. So, it seems that high debt and low unemployment can coexist. But don’t tell the Republicans.

The Teapublicans, as one cartoon put it, would have you believe that the answer to all problems, foreign and domestic, is reducing the federal debt. Since debt is the difference between revenues and expenditures, shouldn’t higher taxes be on the table? What? Are you crazy? Raising taxes will inhibit our job creators! No, the solution to the debt is, and always is, to cut spending. What to cut?

The Republicans hope that you’re not too keen of mind when they get around to discussing a shrunken federal government. First off, they want to sharply increase military spending. Why, of course. After all, we currently give the Pentagon more money than the rest of the world spends on defense. Why stop now?

After lavishing the Army, Navy, and Air Force with more weapons and sophisticated war tools, the Republicans have to cut from the other half of discretionary spending. This is where the party reveals its true colors.

If you are poor or near-poor (about fifty percent of us, according to the U.S. Census), you will feel much pain and further misery under a Republican regime. It doesn’t help if your skin happens to be less-than-white.

But the Republicans aren’t about to couch their prescriptions in overtly racist or class-based language. No, they speak of “entitlements.” That is the mother of all euphemisms, and here they get at nearly everyone who is not both rich and white.

My dictionary defines ‘entitlement’ as ‘the fact of having a right to something,’ or ‘the amount to which a person has a right.’ But despite the definitions, the term has taken on a decidedly derisive tone, meaning something quite opposite. There is no mention of right. Indeed, when uttered by conservatives, entitlement is used to suggest that the recipient is unworthy, undeserving. In less guarded moments, Republicans may refer to recipients as “moochers.”

As I near retirement age, I expect to receive Social Security and Medicare benefits. After all, I have been paying taxes my entire adult life. I feel “entitled”—by definition.

The modern-day Republican detests government and all of its programs, save for defense and criminal justice. Even though they depend on governments at all levels, they cannot admit it, for fear of undermining their Randian ideology, which places individualism and self-reliance über alles. (Ironic that Rand applied for those entitlements.) If government is “the problem,” than its programs are equally so.

I’ve mentioned before that raising the debt is a bipartisan activity. Even Ronald Reagan presided over expanding deficits. G.W. Bush, notwithstanding his frugal rhetoric to the contrary, squandered an inherited surplus, then left his successor with gobs of debt. The point here is that Republicans talk but they don’t act.

However, this time could be different. I can surely imagine a solid Republican regime finally making good on their fiscal promises. What’s to stop them, should they control all branches of the federal government? This GOP has demonstrated that it would gladly throw the Rest of Us under a bus in pursuit of ideological purity. Why let a few million poor people of color stand in their way?

Deploying this attitude will most certainly force an increase in another major part of federal, state, and local governments: criminal justice. Further oppressing the Rest of Us will necessitate more cops, judges, jails, and prisons.

A small price to pay for the Fourth Reich.

Back to debt

September 1, 2012 by davald

A few posts back I wrote of debt, both public and private, and the Great Divergence, which began in the late 70s then rose exponentially. That would be the growing divide between the very rich and the Rest of Us. I suggested that the chasm may have some relationship with the increasing financialization of the economy, and that the financial sector is all about debt being imposed by the wealthy on everyone else.

We often hear Republicans decry this heaping mound of debt as a burden on our children and grandchildren. What some of them and all their principal benefactors conveniently omit is that their financial fortunes depend increasingly on this accumulating debt.

We need look no further than the housing bubble to appreciate the implicit Ponzi scheme at work, as real estate companies, Wall Street traders, hedge-fund managers, and giant insurance companies desperately tried to slake their thirst for more unwitting mortgage holders. In the months just before the bubble’s collapse, one needed neither income nor downpayment to acquire keys to the front door of a “brand new home.” After all, G.W. Bush declared that we should be an “ownership society”; renting is for schmucks.

I’m afraid that we’re in for a lot more bubbles down the road, since the U.S. economy no longer builds things. Rather, we finance what amounts to giant piles of shit; it really doesn’t matter what, if anything, serves as the underlying “asset.” It could be the price of blue cheese or the presumed value of pet rocks.

Before leaving, I should acknowledge that this is all quite bipartisan these days. Both major political parties, whether or not intentional, facilitated the economy’s move away from middle-class jobs and toward Wall Street and million-dollar salaries. Regulations so carefully constructed during the New Deal were chipped away, coinciding with the Great Divergence. Taxes were lowered on the already privileged, reducing federal receipts and hastening debt. Having erected the financial edifice, national politicians, like Willie Sutton, naturally turn toward the modern-day barons in search of campaign funds.

Human capital

August 25, 2012 by davald

Capital, as we know, belongs to a privileged few, the Haves and Have-mores—otherwise known as the One-percenters. The Rest of Us scramble and struggle to make ends meet, while being forced to constantly look up to rich men’s butts. But, please, we don’t have to like the emanating shit.

Charles Blow, writing for the New York Times, tells us that “America is in trouble.” He’s thinking about the future, and at present course, that future belongs to China and India. Why? Because those nations (and others, especially central and northern European) are investing heavily in their children’s education and well-being, while America is content to let most of our young people do without. The privileged few made it, then pulled up the ladder of success, relegating the Have-nots to lives of misery and hopelessness. That we long ago surrendered to this perverse order and now embrace it evinces a Stockholm Syndrome second to none.

So I return to education. Blow cites the results of a recent study (pdf) that compares and contrasts investments made by different countries in their children’s present and future. America, as I said above and elsewhere, dropped the ball, which it had only loosely carried for generations. We shortchange our children and thus their and, collectively, our future, whereas China, India, and the Nordic countries, in particular, understand that enhancing human capital makes all the difference—across numerous fronts, including international competitiveness.

Here’s an excerpt from that study that strikes a familiar note with readers:

A consequence of unequal education funding is that the least experienced and prepared teachers receive the lowest pay and teach the most disadvantaged students. And the teaching corps itself is weak. America’s teachers have strong academic credentials, yet with no improvement on national or international student assessment in decades it’s clear that many teachers struggle to help their students learn.

This may be due, in part to the fact that high school students who choose to enter undergraduate programs for education have SAT scores on average in the bottom third of all students tested. That stands in sharp contrast to nations with impressive student results, such as Finland, which successfully recruit teachers from their top high school graduates.

The report reaffirms upfront the importance of teacher quality in properly reforming America’s education system. Despite the emphasis of No Child Left Behind on mandating that only “highly qualified” teachers teach, there has been no significant increase in students’ academic performance. We might conclude, correctly, I believe, that we still don’t get it when it comes to selecting then preparing our nation’s best and brightest educators. I submit, based on the Finnish experience, that focusing on this crucial ingredient and acting accordingly will enhance human capital.

Sadly, not only do we fail at delivering the best teachers to our public school classrooms, we deprive our children of those social and economic benefits enjoyed by their counterparts in Finland, Sweden, et al. Worse, America is trending toward increased privatization of education, marked by charter schools and class-based segregation—all in the name of competition and choice. The report:

Economists studying the root causes of economic growth increasingly stress that investments in early childhood development and learning are especially critical for the human capital development needed in a strong, competitive economy. That’s because prenatal care and experiences from birth through early childhood affect children’s physical and brain development—and therefore the cognitive, social, and emotional development that will carry them through their lives.

Though we bang our “We’re Number One” drum loudly and repeatedly, with no evident reason, we ignore a glaring societal defect: too many of us are poor. Nor do we seem at all concerned about diminishing poverty. Once poor in America you’ll likely stay that way; and the same holds if you’re born rich. How do we stack up against other countries on the inconvenient poverty metric (OECD, 2005 data)?

All of the above countries, including the U.S., of course, have market economies, whose outcomes relegate substantial portions of their populations to poverty. That’s before taxes and transfers. The Nordic countries, especially, re-distribute national wealth to their “losers,” in the form of economic security, education, and social welfare benefits. We, on the other hand, take less from the rich, the “job creators” (not), therefore depriving everyone else of a decent life.

John Lennon imagined a better world. In America, that’s all we can do—evidently.

Don’t enable “the moochers”

August 24, 2012 by davald

In his column this morning Paul Krugman continues with his criticisms of Paul Ryan, who, despite all evidence to the contrary, is being put forth as an intellectual of sorts, one whose views should be taken seriously, even if you don’t happen to agree with all of them. How he rose to this vaulted position within the GOP is the subject of Ryan Lizza’s piece in The New Yorker, which I heartily commend to my dear readers. Here’s Krugman:

But he is deadly serious about cutting taxes on the rich and slashing aid to the poor, very much in line with [Ayn] Rand’s worship of the successful and contempt for “moochers.”

This last point is important. In pushing for draconian cuts in Medicaid, food stamps and other programs that aid the needy, Mr. Ryan isn’t just looking for ways to save money. He’s also, quite explicitly, trying to make life harder for the poor — for their own good. In March, explaining his cuts in aid for the unfortunate, he declared, “We don’t want to turn the safety net into a hammock that lulls able-bodied people into lives of dependency and complacency, that drains them of their will and their incentive to make the most of their lives.”

So, all those previously able-bodied adults who lost their jobs because of Wall Street shenanigans and the housing bubble collapse suddenly became dependent and complacent, preferring to while away their hours atop a hammock than combing the streets in proper desperation looking for the economy’s crumbs, sprinkled down from above by our nation’s “job creators”? Sure, you’ll buy that, won’t you?

Of course, this Randian notion of self-reliance tinged with abject fear of destitution requires an important ingredient: that the wonderful entrepreneurial geniuses so admired by Ryan et al. have already created more than enough jobs to go around. Work is simply an application away. Why, then, are you sitting on your butt?

 

See that sharp drop in the ratio beginning in 2008-2009? It depicts a booming market for hammocks—oh, and a lot more laggards.

 

Once again, the skyrocketing climb in the average duration of unemployment has nothing to do with lack of jobs. It’s all about too many people abruptly losing their desire for work as they enjoy the luxury of living on the dole. In Ryan’s world, these benighted fools could be busy transforming themselves into so many John Galts or Mitt Romneys. All they need are some tough love, a strong distaste for failure, and adoption of all those qualities that make men (forget about women) great.

You see, unemployment has everything to do with inappropriate incentives and a complete lack of values possessed by successful white males. Denying would-be workers assistance is the first step in opening the gates to unfounded riches.

Only in America.

Finnish lessons

August 22, 2012 by davald

My post “After vs. before” generated numerous comments. I should reply to a few.

One commenter suggested that Finland is a “monoracial” society, more homogeneous than America’s. Along this line another commenter assumed that Finland’s largely white population possessed a higher IQ than people of color, both here and in Finland. (I won’t touch this, by the way.)

In his book Finnish Lessons, Pasi Sahlberg addresses Finland’s homogeneity. It’s changing. Ever since Finland joined the Eurozone the number of foreign-born residents has skyrocketed, as the chart below illustrates (data from Sahlberg):

 

In 2010, 248,135 of Finland’s residents were foreign-born, accounting for 4.7 percent of the country’s total population. Sahlberg tells us that only a small percentage of this cohort obtain formal citizenship given the government’s requirement that a candidate must be “proficient” in one of Finland’s three domestic languages: Finnish, Swedish, and Sami. As he says, very few people outside of Scandinavia speak them.

Nevertheless, despite the increase in immigrants, Sahlberg emphasizes that Finland maintains its commitment to educate all. Here’s Sahlberg:

The Finnish education system follows the principle of inclusiveness regarding the treatment of students with differing characteristics and needs. Students are placed in regular schools unless there is a specific reason to do otherwise. Therefore, in a typical Finnish classroom, one finds teachers teaching different abilities, interests, and ethnicities, often with the help of assistant teachers. The increased diversity in Finnish schools also suggests that variance in student performance within schools may increase. Cultural heterogeneity in Finnish society would suggest that variance in student learning among schools may become wider. However…a very high overall student performance in science, mathematics, and reading is evenly distributed throughout schools across Finland [pg. 69].

To be sure, Finland’s test scores declined from 2006 to 2009, though only slightly, and they were still far above the OECD average. Could this be the result of increasing immigration? I can’t say. However, Sahlberg writes, “Immigrant students in Finland scored on average 50 points higher than their peers in other countries.”

We should return to Sahlberg’s essential point. Finnish schools were not always superior. Indeed, a principal motivator for reform was mediocre performance on international tests. Finland’s leaders deigned to change things. What they accomplished, in my judgment, is truly remarkable. That even foreign-born students improve academic performance after being taught in Finnish schools suggests that the country’s educational system deserves study by U.S. educational and political leaders.

Now there is one factor that adversely impacts both teaching and learning: poverty. Fortunately for Finns, there is very little of it in their country. That’s hardly the case in the U.S. Here’s a chart comparing poverty rates and scores on the 2009 math portion of PISA, the international test administered under the auspices of the OECD.

 

We’re number one in poverty, not so much in academics.

One commenter averred that the U.S. education system deserves more respect. After all, we set about educating an immigrant nation (though our predecessors relegated the native population to a life of misery and poverty). Children from dozens of disparate countries wound up in our public school classrooms. They spoke different languages and enjoyed different cultural traditions. Yet, many, if not most, received a more-than-adequate education, as manifest in the socio-economic attainment of individuals possessed of a broad variety of surnames.

The commenter focused on high school graduation rates, which have risen dramatically over time. He writes:

However, there are 193 countries represented in the UN, putting the United States High School graduation rate in the top 5.69% of all the nations that are members of the UN. That means 94.31% of the Earth’s countries have lower high school graduation rates.

I’ll assume his numbers are correct. Should we take comfort in the knowledge that American secondary students graduate at a higher rate than, say, Ethiopia’s or Haiti’s? Or would we prefer to compare ourselves to Canada or Denmark or Finland? We can even shuck the comparisons and ask, Do we not want out schools to be better than before?

Let me return to the homogeneity argument before closing. Do less diverse societies perform better on the international tests?

The data offer little to support an affirmative answer. Certainly most nations are both poor and homogeneous. I have in mind the countries of Africa and Central and South America. But let’s take a look at other Scandinavian countries, whose populations, unsurprisingly, are almost exclusively Nordic. If homogeneity explained academic success, then we would expect to find a correlation.

What we find is that, if we exclude Finland, there does not seem to be a significant relationship between the percentage of foreign-born and test scores.

That Finland is such a strong outlier suggests—are you ready?—a superior educational system.
