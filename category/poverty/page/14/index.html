Poverty’s correlations: mobile homes

August 18, 2012 by davald

G.W. Bush pronounced America an “ownership society.” We are to own rather than rent our abodes. So he set about facilitating precisely that goal, with disastrous consequences. Something about a housing bubble. Yet, while home ownership rates remain high, above half the population, many of us rent either by choice or necessity. (Source for chart: U.S. Census Bureau)

But what kind of dwelling units? As it happens, the poorer the state the higher the rate of mobile home use.

 

In reality, of course, there is nothing mobile about mobile homes. They are delivered to a site, often in a “park,” where they sit—for decades if not generations. Nor are their occupants enjoying the fruits of social mobility, which has declined in the U.S. over time. Moreover, it’s far more difficult for the relatively poor to become relatively rich in America than it is in, say, Nordic countries (source; the lower the correlation—y-axis—the greater the mobility).

 

American mythology reigns supreme.

Poverty and blackness

August 15, 2012 by davald

A few posts back I commented on skin color as a salient marker for determining “us” vs. “them.” I wrote:

We are a sorting species. Broadly speaking we divide among “us” versus “them.” What salient features will we employ? Skin color works well, all too well. It serves as a first approximation for identifying in-group and out-group. Further, we are particularly keen on class-based hierarchies. In America the wealth spectrum spreads wide, with most money concentrated in a few hands. Very little, we’ve learned, trickles down to the Rest of Us.

Republicans, the party of Lincoln, have adopted policies that keep darker skinned people subjugated to their white “superiors.” Mississippi, our nation’s poorest state, despite having the largest percentage of African Americans, remains under the control of whites for the most part, and they will keep it that way.

Their success requires only that they continue to screw the poor, and in Mississippi, especially, blacks are likely to live in poverty. That convenient correlation makes it easy for dominant whites to keep blacks “in their place,” as Khalil Gibran Mohammed. The mechanism of choice is the criminal justice system.

 

Mississippi stands out in the above graph. It’s the diamond at the upper right. (There are exceptions to the trend. Maryland, represented by the most upper left diamond, has a low poverty rate but a high percentage of African Americans, about 30 percent.)

Inequality’s correlations: murder

August 13, 2012 by davald

Americans love their guns, or at least an alien would draw that conclusion from a casual glance at television news. Perversely, the affection increases immediately following the latest slaughter by some arsenal-ladened psychopath, previously described as bright, quiet, but a bit of a loner. No one would have suspected, and all that rot. But it happened, nevertheless, after acquiring a small-army’s worth of weapons and ammunition. As has been said often enough, there is no such thing as bad publicity. So when the crazies massacre, the National Rifle Association can count on adding members. The more atrocious and horrifying the accounts, the better.

Louisiana, home to the French Quarter and crayfish, enjoys a peculiar status: people there shoot each other—all the time. Bang. You’re dead. Next. It’s also a state of relatively high income inequality, though not as high as New York’s.

There are a few interesting takeaways from the above chart. New York, for example, has the highest Gini index but a “modest” murder rate, somewhere in the middle of the pack.

The states with the lowest murder rates also have the lowest level of inequality. Fancy that. So what might explain the correlation?

In their book The Spirit Level researchers Richard Wilkinson and Kate Picket emphasize the correlation. As to possible causation they quote a few psychiatrists. One, James Gilligan of Harvard Medical School, suggests that violent acts “are attempts to ward off or eliminate the feeling of shame and humiliation—a feeling that is painful, and can even be intolerable and overwhelming—and replace it with its opposite, the feeling of pride.” He found after interviewing murderers that they acted out of “perceived threats…to pride.”

The researchers also cite the work of Margo Wilson and Martin Daly who wrote a book entitled Homicide. You may find the following a bit off-putting, but here goes, as quoted by Wilkinson and Picket:

…they use statistical, anthropological and historical data to show how young men have strong incentives to achieve and maintain as high a social status as the can—because their success in sexual competition depends on status.

We should not be surprised that more unequal societies, or states, increase “the stakes in the competition for status.” The more pronounced the income differentials the more susceptible young men are to “perceived threats” to their image.

Whether or not you subscribe to such hypotheses there is no question that the correlation between inequality and violence is quite strong. Here’s a chart from The Spirit Level on international comparisons:

Gosh, look at the U.S. We are to the other countries as Louisiana is to the other 49 states, though even more so. Look at the lower left of the chart. Note the Nordic countries and Japan, all having much more equal societies than the U.S. and also much lower homicide rates.

Ah, but it’s nice to be number one, isn’t it?

Inequality’s correlations—blackness

August 13, 2012 by davald

America, for all its collective nationalistic hubris, is a troubled nation. Indeed, it sucks, big time. And the confirming evidence abounds.

One metric that stands out is our country’s wholesale embrace of inequality. The rich are different, and the Rest of Us not only worship them, we coddle, coddle, and coddle some more. In return the wealthy demand that heads they win and tails we lose; no matter the vagaries, they are either rewarded or protected—by the Rest of Us, who are left to suffer in countless ways, from a failed public education system to increasingly impoverished habitats.

But such masochism is as perversely unhealthy as it is preventable. Overcoming the imbalance compels us to abandon our pervasive Stockholm syndrome. Don’t worship the rich; unshackle their chains then beat the shit out of them. (What am I smoking?)

A few evenings ago I watched Bill Moyers’s interview of Khalil Gibran Mohammed, who heads up the Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture. Moyers asked Mohammed for his reaction to New York mayor Bloomber’s policy of “stop and frisk.” As an historian Mohammed provided context, tracing this latest initiative to slavery and lingering identification. By using the salient trait of blackness generations of white political leaders have successfully controlled African American populations. Mohammed:

It tells us that it’s an old and enduring form of surveillance and racial control. So if we think about the moment immediately following the Civil War, there was the invention of something called the Black Codes in every southern state. And those codes were intended to use the criminal justice system to restrict the freedom and mobility of black people.

And if you crossed any line that they prescribed, you could be sold back to your former slave owner, not as a slave, but as a prisoner to work off your fine after an auction where you were resold to the highest bidder. It tells you something about the invention of the criminal justice system as a repressive tool to keep Black people in their place, from the very moment where 95 percent of the Black population became free. And it’s still with us. It’s still with us, because ultimately, as a social problem, crime has become like it was in the Jim Crow South, a mechanism to control black people’s movement in cities.

Note the phrase “the invention of the criminal justice system as a repressive tool to keep Black people in their place.” Mohammed contrasts the fate of white immigrants with their darker counterparts. Italians, Russians, Scandinavians, and so on became “white” on our census rolls. They retained their surnames while melting into the great Caucasian pot. Not so Blacks.

Having been denied equal opportunity, what happens to Blacks, especially young males? They cannot find jobs. They cannot live where they choose. They are perpetually branded by their color. Well, they go to prison after resorting to crime. If one can’t meet one’s needs honestly, desperation dictates larceny or worse.

Well-meaning politicians have suggested that we stop talking about race and focus on poverty. After all, Blacks are more likely to be poor than whites, as this table confirms:

 

I don’t disagree, actually. We should be concerned about poverty in America—and elsewhere. Yet, Mohammed’s historical context for our modern criminal justice system, one that disproportionately imprisons Blacks more than any other race, should at least force us to ask why this is so. Poverty’s effects yield anti-social behavior. But in America especially color affixes socioeconomic status. Blackness equals poverty, and that association stubbornly persists.

So, we put them in jail (source).

 

We are a sorting species. Broadly speaking we divide among “us” versus “them.” What salient features will we employ? Skin color works well, all too well. It serves as a first approximation for identifying in-group and out-group. Further, we are particularly keen on class-based hierarchies. In America the wealth spectrum spreads wide, with most money concentrated in a few hands. Very little, we’ve learned, trickles down to the Rest of Us.

Unfortunately, we seem to enjoy the divisions. We like our rich and detest our poor. We aspire to be the former and live in horror of becoming the latter. Color, again, helps.

Blackness, more so than “brownness,” allows non-wealthy whites to feel relieved knowing that they can never become Black and all that blackness signifies, from poverty to desperation-driven criminality. Meanwhile, they can fantasize about their economic and political captors.

Sick.

Two veils

August 13, 2012 by davald

If you could play Founding Father for a moment, what kind of society, or government, would you design? More important, what set of assumptions would you make about human nature and what processes might you follow to arrive at your intended intellectual destination?

One American philosopher, joining the ranks of noteworthy predecessors (including Hume, Locke, and Rousseau), devoted much of his academic life to this challenge. The result: A Theory of Justice, perhaps the most celebrated 20th century work on political philosophy. The author: John Rawls.

Philosophy, I might add from personal experience, is hard. One reason is that it requires you to think deeply and radically about topics that most of us treat summarily, if at all. Justice? Fairness? Meh. John Rawls expended the energy and time to address these moral issues straight on. And how he came to his conclusions is every bit as fascinating as the conclusions themselves. But appreciating his journey and insights demands extraordinary focus and, I hastily add, an ability to suspend judgment and bias.

In today’s Stone essay, philosopher Benjamin Hale reminds us of Rawls’s Herculean triumph. He contrasts the late philosopher’s “veil of ignorance” with his own neologism: “veil of opulence.” Hale:

The idea behind the veil of ignorance is relatively simple: to force us to think outside of our parochial personal concerns in order that we consider others. What Rawls saw clearly is that it is not easy for us to put ourselves in the position of others. We tend to think about others always from our own personal vantage; we tend to equate another person’s predicament with our own. Imagining what it must be like to be poor, for instance, we import presumptions about available resources, talents and opportunities — encouraging, say, the homeless to pull themselves up by their bootstraps and to just get a job, any job, as if getting a job is as simple as filling out an application. Meanwhile, we give little thought to how challenging this can be for those who suffer from chronic illnesses or disabling conditions. What Rawls also saw clearly was that other classic principles of justice, like the golden rule or mutual benevolence, are subject to distortion precisely because we tend to do this.

Americans, in particular, approach the fundamental challenge in an entirely different fashion, behind a “veil of opulence.” Instead of exploring an “original position” in which we are ignorant of our own circumstances, we imagine ourselves to be the next Bill Gates or a rich hedge-fund manager. We replace ignorance with aspiration. If the goal is to become that wealthy individual, how might we go about achieving it? Moreover, if we assume that we are privileged, how do we answer questions about efficient or equitable taxation? Would I prefer a smaller government, one less intrusive and, therefore, less capable of impeding my personal aggrandizement? What would I think of the poor, the infirm, the “lesser mortals” who demand much but contribute little?

Hale suggests that the two competing veils serve as alternate prisms through which to view political ideologies, in general, and particular policy initiatives, in particular:

Those who don the veil of opulence may imagine themselves to be fantastically wealthy movie stars or extremely successful business entrepreneurs. They vote and set policies according to this fantasy. “If I were such and such a wealthy person,” they ask, “how would I feel about giving X percentage of my income, or Y real dollars per year, to pay for services that I will never see nor use?” We see this repeatedly in our tax policy discussions, and we have just seen the latest instance of it in the Tax Policy Center’s comparison of President Obama’s tax plan versus Mitt Romney’s tax plan. “He’s asking you to pay more so that people like him can pay less,” Obama said last week, “so that people like me pay less.” Last Monday he drove the point even harder, saying that Romney’s plan is like “Robin Hood in reverse.” And certainly, Romney’s selection on Saturday of Paul Ryan as his running mate will keep this issue in the forefront of our political discourse.

I could add yet another veil, the “veil of destitution.” Can we see ourselves as homeless, impoverished, without hope? In our society, with so much insularity and division, we may never encounter a “street person.” Our daily routines may take us from a suburban enclave to the downtown office. We can choose where to go and how to spend our time, completely avoiding contact with those less fortunate. How could we possibly develop sympathy outside experience? And since we don’t have to observe the plight of the indigent, why even have them appear on our intellectual radar screens?

Hale concludes:

The question of fairness has widespread application throughout our political discourse. It affects taxation, health care, education, social safety nets and so on. The veil of opulence would have us screen for fairness by asking what the most fortunate among us are willing to bear. The veil of ignorance would have us screen for fairness by asking what any of us would be willing to bear, if it were the case that we, or the ones we love, might be born into difficult circumstances or, despite our hard work, blindsided by misfortune. Society is in place to correct for the injustices of the universe, to ensure that our lives can run smoothly despite the stuff that is far out of our control: not to hand us what we need, but to give us the opportunity to pursue life, liberty and happiness. The veil of ignorance helps us see that. The veil of opulence keeps us in the dark.

Republicans, by the way, and especially their most recent incarnation, are generally selfish bastards who operate behind the “veil of opulence.” They may not be all One-percenters, but they certainly aspire to be one. As I recall Archie Bunker saying, taxing the rich removes his incentive to one day join their ranks.

Only in America.

A follow-up on geographical determinism

July 26, 2012 by davald

Yesterday I reprised my angst and bewilderment over America’s redness. One reader expressed his opinion on why so many of our fellow citizens choose to align with the Republican Party when it’s quite obvious that the GOP has nothing to offer them. He wrote:

I wonder how much the views of rural areas I [are] influenced by the inundation of conservative radio which in many places is the only programming you can receive out there.

Otherwise, perhaps the leanings to the right have to do with this kind of “individualism” many in the mid-west and rural areas have about themselves. Their perceptions are rooted in that and the line sold by the GOP runs right in line with that. Unfortunately, given the relatively lower income levels many in those areas live within, they are persuaded by the GOP that the Democrats are the antithesis of that perception so they vote conservative. I’d say it also has to do a lot with the religion. So many of the smaller communities’s centers are focused on the church.

Religion clearly demarcates red from blue, since those in the Midwest and South are far more religious than their coastal counterparts. And religion has a tendency to blind its adherents to what’s going on around them. Besides, it drives them to Fox News, which exhibits no regard for the truth.

So, why do people who live amongst cropland go to church while Seattleites drink coffee at the nearest Starbuck’s? Is church the physical hub of social networking, whereas urban-dwellers prefer pubs and cafés and restaurants and theaters and cinemas and city parks? I mean, what does one do in a corn field?

But back to the misalignment of interests. The New York Times editors remind us that the Republican-controlled House will surely vote down the recently passed Senate bill to extend the Bush-era tax cuts, except for the top two percent of households. The editors:

The vote, however, exposes the true priorities of the Republicans. No Senate Republican agreed to support the middle-class tax cut by itself because they insisted that the rich get one, too. (Actually, the rich would have gotten a tax cut on their first quarter-million of income, but, apparently, that wasn’t enough.) Forty-four Republicans (and one Democrat) voted for an alternative bill that would give wildly generous estate tax breaks to a few of the richest American heirs at a cost of $119 billion to the deficit.

And those 44 Republicans also voted to raise taxes on 13 million low- and moderate-income working families. Though it seems unbelievable on a day when Republicans tried to be so generous to their wealthiest contributors, they voted for a bill that would end the child tax credit for nine million families that make less than $13,300, costing some of the nation’s most struggling households $854 a year. Another four million families would be affected by the Republican vote to reduce both the earned income tax credit and the middle-class credit for college tuition.

How many citizens who call corn “neighbor” benefit from the Republican alternative, one that fleeces the poor and further coddles the rich?

Only in America.

Happiness

July 9, 2012 by davald

The New York Times features a couple of recent pieces on happiness. One, written by Arthur Brooks, president of the American Enterprise Institute, argues that conservatives are happier than liberals, then offers some purported explanations. Another, written by Elizabeth Dunn and Michael Norton, suggests that money equals happiness—but only up to a point.

While surveys do show that conservatives are more likely to say that they are happy with their lives than liberals, and Brooks mentions and links to a few, he doesn’t really offer more than correlations. For example, a higher percentage of conservatives than liberals are married. So, does marriage convey happiness? If so, why do so many end in divorce? Perhaps it’s a timing issue. Ask newlyweds if they’re happy, and they’ll probably say yes, enthusiastically so. Come back a few years later with the same question, and you’ll likely get a different answer. Nor does it follow that single people are incapable of being happy. George Clooney, the world’s most famous bachelor, strikes me as being one happy camper, for reasons I need not provide, because they’re all too obvious. (Wikipedia)

Conservatives, to no one’s surprise, are more religious than liberals. People who regularly attend church services report being happier than those who don’t. Does religion make you happy? Or is there some third factor that may explain both conservatism and religiosity?  I suspect that it’s the latter, though I have no candidates at the moment.

The U.S. Catholic Bishops during Reagan’s regime wrote several drafts of an encyclical on the economy. In one draft they wrote that no one should be happy when so many are poor. This phrase did not survive the final version, no doubt because happy conservative Catholics lobbied the clerics to remove it. But it might have proved interesting had it remained. Imagine parish priests looking down from the lectern at their congregations mentally recording those with smiles on their faces. After all, there were lots of poor people back then and far more today.

Citing literature, Brooks tells us that extremists on both ends of the political spectrum are happier than their moderate counterparts.  Yet, there are surely exceptions, some profound. Hitler was not a happy man. He was bitter, resentful, and of course, vindictive. And he was certainly extreme, wouldn’t you say? Does this man look happy? (Wikipedia)

Karl Marx was married with children, but he was also poor. We must agree that he was part of the radical, as in extreme, left; he was one of its architects, after all. When he wasn’t reading and writing at the London Library, he could hardly have been happy with what awaited him at home, a cramped, damp apartment with little food and too many mouths. However, he seemed quite fond of Jenny, his long-suffering wife. He did manage a slight grin in this photo (Wikipedia) taken the year before he died:

As it happens, unhappiness is strongly correlated with poverty, which should be no mystery. Dunn and Norton write:

…But it turns out there is a measurable connection between income and happiness; not surprisingly, people with a comfortable living standard are happier than people living in poverty.

It would seem to follow that having more money would make one even happier. That’s true, but only up to a point, and that point is about $75,000 of household income. The authors:

The catch is that additional income doesn’t buy us any additional happiness on a typical day once we reach that comfortable standard. The magic number that defines this “comfortable standard” varies across individuals and countries, but in the United States, it seems to fall somewhere around $75,000. Using Gallup data collected from almost half a million Americans, researchers at Princeton found that higher household incomes were associated with better moods on a daily basis — but the beneficial effects of money tapered off entirely after the $75,000 mark.

The title of Dunn and Norton’s essay is “Don’t indulge. Be happy.” The two psychologists, using research they and others have done, make the case that under-indulgence leads to greater appreciation for the things we consume. While they don’t use the term, they’re talking about diminishing marginal utility—the 10th hotdog is not nearly as enjoyable as the first, assuming you like hotdogs at all.

What I found most interesting about their article is that we are happier if we spend money on someone else rather than ourselves. The psychologists:

Imagine walking down the street to work and being approached by our student Lara Aknin, who hands you an envelope. You open the envelope and find $20 and a slip of paper, which tells you to spend the cash on something for yourself by the end of the day. Sounds like a pretty sweet deal. Now imagine instead that the slip of paper told you to spend the cash on someone else. Being generous is nice, sure, but would using the money to benefit someone else actually make you happier than buying yourself the belt, DVD or apps you’ve been dying to get?

Yes, and it’s not even close. When we follow up with people who receive cash from us, those whom we told to spend on others report greater happiness than those told to spend on themselves. And in countries from Canada to India to South Africa, we find that people are happier when they spend money on others rather than on themselves.

If we follow the logic of the above we might arrive at this conclusion: Strive to make at least $75,000 a year; if you should make more, then spend the rest on someone else.

You’ll be very happy.
