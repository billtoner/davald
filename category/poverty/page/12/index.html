But by all means, worry about the deficit

December 19, 2012 by davald

Amidst all the furore over the fiscal cliff and budget deficits we tend to lose sight of how large a toll the Great Recession inflicted on the Rest of Us. The unemployment rate threatens to stay high above post-WWII averages. Millions of Americans struggle on a fraction of what they used to make. The New York Times brings the suffering home, focusing in this article on the plight of a young Seattleite.

Duane Taylor was studying the humanities in community college and living in his own place when he lost his job in a round of layoffs. Then he found, and lost, a second job. And a third.

Now, with what he calls “lowered standards” and a tenuous new position at a Jack in the Box restaurant, Mr. Taylor, 24, does not make enough to rent an apartment or share one. He sleeps on a mat in a homeless shelter, except when his sister lets him crash on her couch.

“At any time I could lose my job, my security,” said Mr. Taylor, explaining how he was always the last hired and the first fired. “I’d like to be able to support myself. That’s my only goal.”

Conservatives like Mitt Romney relish blaming Mr. Taylor and his cohorts for lacking the values necessary to become less reliant on “the government,” which, Romney intoned, is paid for by successful rich people. Mr. Taylor is a “moocher,” undeserving of assistance and, certainly, our sympathy.

Yet, clearly Mr. Taylor is trying—desperately so. After all, he says, his only goal is “to be able to support myself.”

I just read somewhere that a survey of the major newspapers reveals that articles on the debt and deficit outnumbered articles on joblessness by over four to one. Those headlines matter, because we intellectually defenseless Americans draw our opinions from them. If the papers are all about the federal governments fiscal imbalance, then that’s the problem, by gosh.

Those with a conscience, consider:

In Washington, Lance Fuller, a 26-year-old with a degree in journalism, spent the end of last month packing up a one-bedroom apartment he can no longer afford after being laid off. Mr. Fuller said he had been unable to keep a job for more than eight months since graduating from the University of Florida in 2010.

“Thankfully, I have a girlfriend who is willing to let me stay with her until I get back on my feet again,” said Mr. Fuller, who writes a blog, Voices of a Lost Generation. “It’s really hard for people in my generation not to feel completely defeated by this economy.”

Inequality is real

December 14, 2012 by davald

As more states join the attack on public sector workers we would do well to consider just how unequal U.S. society is. While you’re at it, consider the strong correlation between union participation rates and Gini index: the higher the former the lower the latter.

First, this chart based on OECD 2009 data. It shows the ratio of incomes between the top 10 percent and bottom 90 percent. Look at the U.S. We’re 27th on a list of 30, and that’s not a good thing, unless you’re rich and you don’t mind everyone else struggling.

As for the correlation between unions and inequality, look at the following chart:

The U.S. is represented by the black diamond in the upper right left. Three Scandinavian countries appear at the lower right: Sweden, Denmark, and Finland.

To be sure, correlation does not equal causation. Yet, intuitively, the stronger unions are (say, the ability to influence wages and benefits at firms), the more likely income differentials will be smaller—ceteris paribus.

Politics and the poor

December 4, 2012 by davald

The Republicans’ counter to Obama’s fiscal proposal , while short on details, essentially sticks to a recurring theme: coddle the rich, screw the Rest of Us. Here’s the New York Times‘ editorial board:

It would cut $1.2 trillion in spending, half of which would come from Medicare, Medicaid, and other health programs, including an increase in the Medicare eligibility age to 67.

So far, Obama has resisted the familiar tendency to compromise, though that could change, which would not bode well for the poor and much of the steadily shrinking middle class. The economic divide, what I call ‘the Great Divergence,’ has gotten only wider over recent decades, much to the evident delight of conservatives. They would continue their own brand of terrorism, impoverishing more while further enhancing the fortunes of the rich.

The percentage of Americans who live below 125 percent of the poverty threshold remans stubbornly high. Were it not for social insurance spending (Social Security, Medicare, and Medicaid) the rate would be even higher.

We’ll note that spending has actually declined since 2007. Here’s a chart showing the decrease.

Yet, Republicans want to spend even less. Their rationale, as far as I can tell, is that they do not want to increase the budget deficit. So why not raise taxes to generate more revenue? Here is where the moral values argument enters the picture.

When Mitt Romney told a gathering of über wealthy that nearly half the population would vote for Obama, because they are dependent on government handouts, the message was obvious to the assembled few, who have taken to the view that the current administration scolds rather than praises them. Without nary a wink and a nod Romney told audiences during the campaign that he wanted more Americans to share in his prosperity; and they would have that opportunity if only Washington would get out of their way. Remove the safety net (a term used by Ronald Reagan) and millions of Americans would begin to ape the virtues and appetites that made Romney et al. rich.

After rigging the economy to their advantage, the wealthy blame the poor for their plight. Rich.

Charter schools

October 23, 2012 by davald

Washington voters are asked to decide whether or not the state will finally have charter schools, after several failed efforts to make them a reality. I’ll get to the point. I’m opposed.

Before I discuss my reasons for opposing the ballot measure, I need to say a few words about the miserable state of public education. In a word, it sucks. For several decades now—and this is especially true in my home state of California—school budgets have been squeezed; so much so that the teachers and administrators can’t possibly do their proper jobs. Educators lack resources, time, and high-quality raw materials—as in learning-ready children.

Against this awful status a bunch of very rich people want to stick the camel’s nose inside the educational tent. This latest effort, which the Seattle Times‘ editors describe as “thoughtful and limited,” possesses neither trait. (Also, I find it odd to praise something because it’s “limited.” We didn’t mind the torture, because it stopped.)

Here are a few caveats to voters.

  • The devil will be in the details, and the details will emerge only in submissions to either the yet-to-be-established “charter school commission,” which will authorize and superintend some charter schools, or existing local school boards. The applicant, a non-profit organization, will submit a plan that includes an overview of the proposed school and a financial roadmap. Should either the commission or the school board authorize the charter school, the non-profit applicant will be awarded an initial contract of six years.
  • The initiative’s advocates stress “flexibility.” Therein lies the rub. Each charter school board will have the authority under its own contract to “hire, manage, and discharge any charter school employee.” Not surprisingly, there is no mention of unions or labor protections. We can understand the Washington Education Association’s opposition.
  • While the advocates seem to go out of their way to emphasize “at-risk students” and that the charter schools will be open to all, there is one provision that stands out. It’s under Section 203 and reads in part:

“[A charter school board may] solicit, accept, and administer for the benefit of the charter school and its students, gifts, grants, and donations from individuals or public or private entities…[except religious groups].”

Hmmm.

In the brochure that I received in today’s mailbox I see that the effort is being financed by Bill Gates, Alice Walton (of the Walmart family), Mike and Jackie Bezos (Amazon), and Nick Hanauer, a local venture capitalist who earned a degree in, gulp, philosophy. He was an early, major investor in Amazon. Why would such wealthy individuals want public charter schools, when their own children can and do go elsewhere?

Bill Gates, we know, has got his money in a lot of projects, with education being just one. He attended private school as a child then dropped out of Harvard. His wife seems to have pushed him into foundation work, and, to his credit, he’s donated billions of dollars to apparently worthwhile efforts, in particular to the eradication of diseases. I’m going to assume here that charter schools is his latest do-good foray into education, having pushed small schools for several years, with absolutely zero results.* I’ll even concede for argument’s sake that the other wealthy benefactors have a noble basis for pushing charter schools. They may even care about the fate of at-risk children, many of whom no doubt shop with their parents at Walmart stores.

But what we don’t hear from these individuals is the necessity of fully funding public schools via a progressive tax system. All children deserve better. Yet they get the shaft, because too many of us deprive state and local governments of the revenues they need to properly educate students.

One more thing, as readers of this blog will recognize, the focus of educational reform in the U.S. is grossly misplaced. The charter schools measure joins a host of other initiatives that emphasize after-the-fact solutions to improving learning. Research does show that quality teaching is vital. So let’s start producing quality educators before they enter the classroom.

Moreover, and equally familiar, is that our society has fallen criminally short of providing an array of public services that ensure that children grow up safe, secure, and capable of learning. Too many suffer from food insecurity, inadequate shelter, and lack of health care.

This charter school measure is just one more distraction from what’s really needed. Worse, if it passes, the artificial limit of 40 total charter schools (and no more than eight new ones per year), will surely be eliminated.

Consider that troubling provision about gifts, grants, and donations. Gates et al. are free to lavish all their generosity on charter schools to their liking, while allowing conventional schools to starve. Will we not see one more manifestation of Haves and Havenots, this time in our public school system?

And those gifts, grants, and donations will come with strings attached. Do it my way, or risk losing my “generosity.”

There is nothing public about this at all.

Vote no on Initiative 1240!

_________

*  In principle I don’t like the idea of someone having so much influence on public agendas. Cash-strapped school boards, in particular, have a difficult time saying no to Microsoft and, now, the Gates Foundation.

Certainty for the Rest of Us

September 25, 2012 by davald

It has become fashionable during these times of economic malaise to blame the problems on certainty, or, more precisely, its lack. Invariably the uncertainty has almost everything to do with wealthy investors sitting on the sidelines waiting for, well, certainty. We are to understand that they want assurances that their taxes will remain low, that regulations will diminish, that labor unions will remain few in number and weak in power, and that politicians in both camps will do their bidding. In short, they want it all, and feel hurt should their paid minions fail to deliver or, as Paul Krugman suggests, others should “look funny at them.”

But uncertainty afflicts us all—the rich, the poor, and in between. Those out of work would like the certainty of a job, especially one paying a livable wage with modest benefits. Those contemplating retirement relish certainty of income from pensions or financial instruments. Firms across the nation would like the certainty of demand: before they build or make something, they must have confidence that people will buy their products.

In Europe these days exists a tug-of-war between those who preach and practice austerity (that presumably heretofore profligate governments must tighten their belts so as to induce “certainty” among investors) and those who suffer the consequences of these measures. As I write, events in Spain, a country particularly hard hit by its own housing-bubble collapse, exhibit the pain, the misery, and the frustration of prolonged unemployment—if not precipitated by, then assuredly exacerbated by that government’s austerity measures, imposed as a condition for capital infusion from the continent’s core nations, notably Germany. The New York Times:

The pressures facing the government of Prime Minister Mariano Rajoy mounted on several fronts on Tuesday, as thousands of demonstrators besieged Parliament and Spain’s two largest regions took steps that underscored their deepening economic troubles and displeasure with his austerity plans.

 

The thousands who have formed a ring around the Spanish parliament claim that they have no intention of occupying the building. Rather, they wish “to show that ‘democracy has been kidnapped ‘and needs to be saved from the hands of inept Spanish politicians,” according to the Times.

Greece, as we know, has its own problems, including a rising unemployment rate. Protests there have become commonplace.

 

Let’s think about these graphs for a moment. The unemployment rate for both countries is above 20 percent. During our Great Depression the official unemployment rate hit 25 percent, which, based on their trajectories, both Greece and Spain will soon surpass.

 

But we’re doing much better today than during the Great Depression or compared with Greece and Spain, as this chart suggests:

 

 

Upon observing these charts should we be thinking about a “new normal” for the United States, one marked by persistent high unemployment and economic stagnation? Consider that when the unemployment rate tends toward a fifth of workers, people take to the streets demanding that their governments do something, anything to provide them with jobs. We didn’t come close to reaching 20 percent following the collapse of the housing bubble and the Wall Street meltdown. One can argue that the bailout and the stimulus package prevented it, and certainly there are those who believe that Congress should have done much more to bring down the unemployment rate to pre-recession levels. But there it is—the rate is stuck at around eight percent nationwide, with no signs of moving significantly in either direction.

Meanwhile, of course, those with lots of money are making even more of it, with record-high profits and accumulating financial portfolios. I don’t pretend to understand how the few continue to amass great fortunes while the Rest of Us struggle to keep afloat. But I suspect that this new normal of stubbornly high—but not too high—unemployment keeps enough people modestly satisfied with their situations, not rich but not poor. In turn, their demands for increased wages and benefits remain stifled. Most important to those who have money, low inflation persists. We should add an historical note: the Right’s longtime contempt for labor unions paid dividends, as union participation rates continue to plummet, thus eliminating what John K. Galbraith called a “countervailing force” against the excesses of capital.

 

Economic output has been declining on a year-to-year basis for decades.

 

So, one might be tempted to forget about all this certainty nonsense for the One-percenters. They’re doing just fine, thank you.

We’re getting poorer

September 24, 2012 by davald

That’s what the U.S. Census data (pdf) show. We’re also becoming more unequal, as the Gini index rose year over year. Some details:

  • inflation-adjusted median household income fell by 1.3 percent, from $51,144 in 2010 to $50,502 in 2011
  • the nation’s Gini index rose 0.48 percent, from 0.469 to 0.475
  • Washington state’s changes were more modest for both income and inequality
  • Washington’s 2011 median household income in 2011 was $56,835, which is 12.5 percent above the national average of $50, 502

From the St. Louis Federal Reserve we get this chart:

 

 

The scales are different, which accounts for the apparent higher volatility of the unemployment rate—until we reach the Great Recession, when the difference between potential and actual GDP skyrocketed by almost $800 billion.

Real per capita disposable income took a hit following the collapse of the housing bubble. It’s sputtering along since.

 

The economy, according to Krugman and others, suffers from inadequate demand, no doubt because we don’t have as much money as we used to.

 

So far, it seems that the two political parties are standing on the sidelines, though they are holding firm to boosting the fortunes of the rich as they let the Rest of Us slide.

Stepping in it

September 19, 2012 by davald

Mitt Romney has a very large shoe he likes to shove into his very large—and always open—mouth.

The Internet is abuzz with Romney’s remarks uttered in the presence of wealthy benefactors. I need not reprise them here, other than to point out the obvious: while his shoe is otherwise occupied, he’s blowing all kinds of crazy flatulence out of his ass.

Romney essentially believes that half of Americans aren’t properly so; they lack the self-reliance and perseverance of successful people like, well, Romney. Maureen Dowd, writing for the New York Times:

It’s literally rich: Willard, born on third base and acting self-made, whining to the rich about what a great deal in life the poor have.

We thought Romney was secretly moderate, but it turns out that he’s secretly cruel, a social Darwinist just like his running mate.

Half of Americans, Romney intoned, don’t pay income taxes and depend on government handouts. Let’s say that his assumption is correct. Does it follow that the recipients are “moochers,” in Paul Ryan’s argot? That if only these people would get out of their hammocks and ape the values of David Brooks they’d join the One-percenters, then say goodbye to Medicare, Medicaid, unemployment benefits, and low-income credits.

But not so fast. Matthew Yglesias reminds us that some very wealthy people benefit handsomely from direct government programs. Take copyright laws. They have enabled a single man to become the richest of them all. Yglesias has Bill Gates in mind.

He links to here for information on who else benefits from U.S. “entitlements.” Some highlights, or lowlights, depending on your viewpoint:

  • three-fourths of those receiving benefits are the aged or the disabled
  • a plurality of government benefits go toward the middle class, though woefully shrunk since the Great Divergence
  • mortgage-interest deductions (a government program) benefit the rich more than the relative Have-nots
  • indeed, the rich, like Mitt Romney, have far more opportunities to avoid paying taxes while enhancing their capital gains—because of government laws and codes

And Romney-the-rhetorical-fart-monger will get about half the votes cast in November.

Only in America.

As depressing as they come

September 17, 2012 by davald

While the collapse of the housing bubble was bad, very bad for millions of us who lost homes to foreclosure, it was a perfect opportunity for those with extra money to exploit misfortune. The following excerpt from Thomas Edsall’s piece in today’s New York Times:

At the height of the housing collapse, Tarver saw an opportunity. “This moment right now,” she told Desmond, “it’s going to create a lot of millionaires. You know, if you have money right now, you can profit from other people’s failures. … I’m catching the properties. I’m catching ‘em.”

Edsall ends with these terribly depressing paragraphs, which serve as a follow up to my previous post:

This dependence on moneyed interests effectively precludes exploitation as a theme for either major party to develop. These sources of campaign cash would dry up if they became the target of policies or positions they found threatening.

Even as polarization poses more sharply defined choices to the voter, pressing issues remain off limits. Poverty and hunger have been dropped from the agenda. The range of policy and electoral choices remains confined to what fits comfortably into a world of muted ethical concern, a world in which moral relativism has permeated society not so much from the bottom up, as from the top down.

The unshackling of moneyed interests — in the name of first amendment rights — from restraints on campaign contributions has, in fact, constrained the free speech of the disadvantaged. It empowers those whose goal is to hinder consumer-protection legislation, to forestall more progressive tax rates and to quash populist insurgencies.

This skewing of the odds in favor of the rich comes at a time when the Democratic Party is already inhibited by accusations that it likes to foment “class warfare” and to play “the race card.” The result has been a relentless shift of the political center from left to right. The two most recent Democratic presidents, Bill Clinton and Barack Obama, have pursued agendas well within this limited terrain. There is little reason to believe that Obama, if he wins in November, will feel empowered to push out much further into territory the Democrats have virtually abandoned.

Taxes and death spirals

September 12, 2012 by davald

Mayor Bloomberg says that Congress should get on with it and just pass the Simpson-Bowles recommendations, even though the committee’s report failed to gain sufficient support from its members. The mayor also believes that lower taxes help businesses.

Well, let’s see. I’ll try a little thought experiment.

Businessman A, who makes Widget X, believes that he could become more competitive if his company’s tax burden were less. To be sure, his costs would decline, all other things being equal, which would free dollars to, say, hire more workers to boost production, even though aggregate demand remains weak.

Businessman B also makes Widget X. If A gets a tax break, so will he. So, both A and B will have no comparative advantage against the other—again, all other things being equal.

Now we could introduce Businessman Chang. He’s Chinese, of course, and he also makes Widget X. Both A and B find it difficult to compete with Chang. So they push for lower taxes in hopes of taking some market share from Chang. A does not better B, nor B, A, but A and B, with lower taxes, better Chang—to limited extent and only temporarily.

Chan has a government that builds all the infrastructure he needs, from high-speed rail shipping, to modern factories, to adequate schools that train his workers. But A and B, because their taxes have been lowered—along with every other business person in America—have lousy infrastructures. The roads are congested or crumbling. Railways are clogged and unable to accommodate high-speed trains. Nor do the would-be workers have the requisite skills to improve production, because the schools are underfunded.

Both A and B press for even lower taxes to compensate for Chang’s advantages. All levels of government comply with their request. But the governments, in turn, have less and less tax revenues to provide those things that Chang takes for granted. Schools, roads, and other transportation options continue to fall apart. Nevertheless, A and B complain about the lousy infrastructure and the failing schools, as they keep lobbying their government representatives to do more with less and, of course, to further lower their taxes.

A and B have created their own death spiral. The more they clamor for and receive tax breaks, the more difficult it is for them to compete against Chang.

But A and B are idiots, of course. They never see beyond the tip of their noses. Indeed, they shout and scream that “they built it” and that they are “job creators” answerable to no one—if only government would reduce their tax burdens and stay out of their way.

For their parts, the different levels of government have no choice. The elected representatives depend on A and B for their campaign donations. Nor do they have any money to get in A and B’s way. They can’t pay for schools and roads and railways. And the governments can no longer invest in research to spur technological innovation.

Mr. Chang’s future looks bright. Not so much Mr. A and B’s.

Why so many poor?

September 12, 2012 by davald

Keeping with my naming-the-evil theme, I pause to ask the fundamental question above. The U.S., as we know from writing and reading this blog, has a disproportionately high percentage of its people living in poverty—relative to other industrial democracies. Why?

Some argue that Americans don’t have the requisite skills to land and maintain higher paying jobs. Okay, even if true, and I’m not sure that it is, again, why? Why do so many Americans lack the “right stuff” to earn decent incomes?

Well, they have only high school degrees, which pay less than those jobs requiring a college education. Then why do so many fail to advance beyond the secondary level?

It’s because they lack the proper attitudes or, as some would put it, “character.” I’m thinking here of David Brooks or Charles Murray. If only more of us had self-discipline, curiosity, and the desire to get ahead, then more of us would go on to college then earn more money.

To repeat, why? Why don’t more of us have those traits?

Well, it’s the parents’ fault. They don’t provide for their children, they don’t enrich with language or the right experiences.

Again, why? Why are so many parents lacking, assuming the rationales for the sake of argument?

How far back should we go? To the amoeba?

Such an infinite regression, and I can see how easy this one is, helps no one. At each step the finger is pointed at this or that culprit. But just as we can’t hardly blame the first cell, what good does it do to fault the child or his parent?

For that matter, what do we gain by criticizing the teacher or public education?

How can we end poverty, should that be our goal? There are at least two schools of thought: we either directly eliminate poverty or we invest more in education so that tomorrow’s students succeed where there predecessors fell short. Or, we do both.

I’ve written before that poverty itself interferes with learning. There are both psychological and physical assaults on a poor child that prevent normal development—deemed necessary for proper learning. So taking the second approach may be like pushing a string.

Better, it seems, to focus on poverty itself, while boosting education investments. That would entail far greater provisioning of social services, including adequate nutrition, health care, shelter, and overall economic and emotional security.

Oh, gosh, you’re talking socialism. Fie on you.
