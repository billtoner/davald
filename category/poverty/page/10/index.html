Growing separation

July 8, 2013 by davald

I imagine that some of us are either blind to or inured to inequality. I attribute some of this to ghettoization and self-selection of coteries, walling off from our everyday experiences the sharp differences that abound in the main. Thus, we may not see poverty simply because we’re not exposed to it. Wealth, on the other hand, is ostentatious and seeks notice, typically in cultural motifs via standard media.

Such thoughts were triggered by reading this essay in the New York Times about “statusization,” a term coined by the author, James Atlas. He writes about a recent plane trip:

This stark class division should come as no surprise: what’s happening in the clouds mirrors what’s happening on the ground. Statusization — to coin a useful term — is ubiquitous, no matter what your altitude. While you’re in your hospital bed spooning up red Jell-O, a patient in a private suite is enjoying strawberries and cream. On your way to a Chase A.T.M., you notice a silver plaque declaring the existence within of Private Client Services. This man has a box seat at a Yankees game; that man has a skybox. And the skybox isn’t the limit: high overhead, the 1 percent fly first class; the .1 percent fly Netjets; the .01 fly their own planes. Why should it be any different up above from down below?

The U.S. was not always so divided, especially during the post-war decades of the 50s and 60s, when the middle class was large and stable. The trend since the mid to late 70s, what I call the Great Divergence, can be described rather accurately as the rich becoming richer and the poor poorer—and more plentiful. The rising Gini index illustrates the phenomenon.

 

My dystopian vision is that the accumulation of wealth in the hands of the few, while certainly not new or rare, portends severe societal problems now and especially in the future. Aside from the apparent fact that inequality is bad for our individual and collective health, the Great Divergence leads almost ineluctably to separate and, of course, unequal societies within the same country. We will become one nation only in times of war, and that artificial unity will mask the continuing exploitation of what Naomi Klein calls “shocks” by the rich against the Rest of Us. Moreover, even during relatively ordinary times the accompanying consolidation of political power by monied interests guarantees the socioeconomic imbalance, as the needs of the impoverished many succumb to the appetites of the privileged few.

The Times‘ essayist suggests that individuating flying protocols serve as a microcosm of trends writ large:

In its efforts to provide “high-value customers” with a “private, intimate, exclusive” experience, the airline hands out all kinds of cool stuff, from pajamas and a duvet to “branded dermatologic products.” The “in-flight wine tasting” offers five vintage labels. And you’re always plugged in: behind each seat is a wall of electronic outlets to recharge one’s gadgets, in order to ensure “life uninterrupted.” (What a nightmare.)

At the end of my tour, I walked down the aisle past business (less gadgetry, but still a lot), past economy plus (more legroom), and to the vast acreage of economy, row upon row of empty seats that, in a few hours, would be filled with squalling babies, muscled guys in Bubba Gump T-shirts, girls in flamingo-pink tank tops, obese armrest hoggers, divorce lawyers jabbering on their cellphones before takeoff, women in spectacles reading “Wolf Hall,” Hasids, Pakistanis, Japanese — the melting pot of American coach.

At least we got out of steerage. Or did we?

Infectious sclerosis [u]

June 21, 2013 by davald

According to a recent Gallup Poll, only ten percent of Americans view Congress favorably. That’s the lowest score in Gallup’s history for any U.S. institution. Writing for the New York Times, Charles Blow suggests that the principal reason for the low opinion, that legislators fiddle while the country metaphorically burns, misses the point. Republicans are pursuing a deliberate strategy of stubborn refusal to adopt legislation of import.

Although Democrats have a majority in the Senate, a fact more reflective of the nation as a whole, minority Republicans nevertheless exploit arcane and convoluted rules to scuttle bills and executive appointments. The House, as we know, is much worse, since conservatives dominate, pushing legislative sclerosis. Republicans, now more than ever, combine obstructionism with breathtaking ignorance. Should they ever prevail, god help us all.

But they do prevail in many states, causing much unnecessarily cruel suffering. Republicans as a rule loathe poor people of color. They do not hesitate to balance their budgets on the backs of those less fortunate. Thus, they will happily increase tax subsidies to woo or retain corporations as they gut social services, including Medicaid.

Here in Washington state, though Democrats control the executive branch and the house, and enjoy a nominal majority in the senate, a strange hybrid breed of political animal, the self-described Road Kill Caucus, holds Olympia hostage. These are Democrats in name only, who essentially switched sides just before the current session to cede power to Republicans.

So what has the senate done? Yep. They have threatened to shut down the government. Now, where have we seen this before?

The Poopy Party, insanely loony, has seized the opportunity to turn ideology into practice, not by appealing to the citizenry, which largely rejects their philosophy, but by thwarting the will of the majority. After all, they generally don’t like government in the first place, so they make sure that it doesn’t work. Splendid.

Still waiting for the revolution

June 6, 2013 by davald

How bad do things have to get before the people start saying no en masse? Economic problems are most acute in Europe, where even Germany, the presumed success story, boasts growth of only 0.3 percent. Hooray. Meanwhile, the unemployment rates in France, Spain, and elsewhere are well above double digits, with no signs of relief.

Europe is hardly a stranger to revolt. We know what the French did to their monarchy when told to eat cake. The entire continent took to the streets in 1848. Western democracies seemed to have had enough a hundred years later, when protests were as common as flies in August. Yet, lassitude in the face of mediocrity has become the norm.

The Times:

Nearly six years after the financial crisis in the United States spread across the Atlantic, plunging Europe into recession and, in some places, desperate depression, “good” is relative. Economic figures that would be considered disastrous elsewhere are being held up by many politicians and policy makers as really not so bad at all — the first tender shoots of a recovery that is out there somewhere.

The wealthy are back to making lots of money, despite the dismal economic news. Perhaps the Rest of Us are truly superfluous. If that is to change, we’re going to have to make it so.

The editors of Monthly Review are encouraged, citing Occupy Wall Street, the pushback by Wisconsin public sector workers, and the resistance of Chicago school teachers to Rahm Emanuel’s dictates. They write:

One thing that these three rebellions had in common is the growing awareness that economic and political power in the United States is firmly in the hands of a tiny minority of fantastically wealthy individuals whose avarice knows no bounds. These titans of finance want to eviscerate working men and women, making them as insecure as possible and totally dependent on the dog-eat-dog logic of the marketplace, while at the same time converting any and all aspects of life into opportunities for capital accumulation.

I seem to recall someone urging: “Workers of the world unite! You have nothing to lose but your chains.”

Stark relief

June 6, 2013 by davald

The New York Times editors expose the inequities between and within states. Wisconsin, led by its stubbornly conservative firebrand Scott Walker, has refused to extend Medicaid, apparently a thumb-to-nose gesture at the Affordable Care Act, if not also to Obama himself. Meanwhile, next door, Minnesota lawmakers voted just the opposite, realizing that their state will save money via Medicaid.

In their opinion the editors write:

What’s really going on, of course, is that state Republicans have made poor people the victims of their ideological resistance to President Obama and his health care law.

That infection has spread to 25 states, mostly with Republican leaders, that have said no to the Medicaid expansion or are resisting it. As Robert Pear of The Times recently reported, more than half of all people without health insurance live in states that are not planning to expand coverage. Many of those states already do a terrible job of covering the poor and have no interest in changing their ways, no matter the financial incentive.

Readers of these pages know that I despise Republicans and their ideology, though I remain baffled by why so many subscribe to the idiocy, mendacity, and mean spiritedness that marks if not also governs the GOP. Or, maybe that’s the point. Our nation is home to bastards who care about their lily-white selves and not a whit for the fortunes of others, whom they are all too anxious to throw under the bus then run over again and again.

So, while I share the editors’ sentiment, I doubt their optimism:

The one virtue of a patchwork is that better examples are not far away. When residents begin to realize the grass is much greener on the other side of the state line, budget-slashing lawmakers will be under pressure to either change their ways or change jobs.

The race is to the rich

April 29, 2013 by davald

Americans appear to be preternaturally ignorant. We just don’t do very well with facts and figures. Much of this syndrome can be blamed on our equally strong tendency to adhere fast to bullshit theories about human nature and religion, which render us impervious to reason and reality.

Unfortunately, the ignorance is widespread, though it’s most evident among our politicians, who will do or say anything to obtain your stupid vote. And, yes, I’m suggesting that we look in the mirror.

This episode of my periodic rant concerns a spate of research confirming two obvious features of American society, in particular. First, the rich continue to get richer. Second, the children of the wealthy do much better academically than the offspring of the Rest of Us.

I’ve posted extensively on the wealth gap, so no need to restate my position here. Just perform a search of my site with the words “wealth gap.” I’ve also commented on the second point, about the growing educational achievement divide between children of the rich versus everyone else.

Recently I wrote about “educational apartheid.” Before that I commented on the  rigidity of achievement gaps—once rich or once poor, always so. Research suggests that money spent on schools to close those gaps may be wasted.

Today I came across this article written by a Stanford professor for the New York Times. Sean Reardon tells us:

Here’s a fact that may not surprise you: the children of the rich perform better in school, on average, than children from middle-class or poor families. Students growing up in richer families have better grades and higher standardized test scores, on average, than poorer students; they also have higher rates of participation in extracurricular activities and school leadership positions, higher graduation rates and higher rates of college enrollment and completion.

Professor Reardon chides all of us for blaming the schools and the teachers therein for academic failures. The achievement gap increases by only ten percent from kindergarten to high school between the children of the rich and the children of the Rest of Us . The disparity, which is huge, already exists before five-year-olds start school.

Moreover, test scores of poor and middle class students have actually risen over time. What is so remarkable is the rapid improvement of rich kids, and it has everything to do with enrichment, which begins at birth and continues throughout a wealthy child’s educational development.

Liberals like Barack Obama deceive themselves by insisting that the focus of reform should be on creating better schools. Wrong. Here’s Reardon:

The more we do to ensure that all children have similar cognitively stimulating early childhood experiences, the less we will have to worry about failing schools. This in turn will enable us to let our schools focus on teaching the skills — how to solve complex problems, how to think critically and how to collaborate — essential to a growing economy and a lively democracy.

What gets me angry is how far away we are as a society from recognizing this important truth. We don’t build a more egalitarian society through education. Rather, we increase educational achievement by fashioning a more egalitarian society. Indeed, children of impoverished homes actually have difficulty learning in the first place.

Yet, as the New York Times editors exclaimed, we’re “heading the wrong way” by slashing funding for public services. To our point, and as Professor Reardon urges, government dollars should be aimed at reducing inequality and providing enrichment for all children, not just those of the wealthy.

Writing for the New York Times, Floyd Norris opines that we are “starving the beast” as conservatives have long desired. But in so doing, we’re depriving our children of both educational and economic opportunities over their lifetimes. Norris:

In the 1950s, government spending fell quickly after the Korean War ended. In the second quarter of 1956, the total government gross domestic product was $91.4 billion, at an annual rate. That was 0.4 percent lower than the $91.8 billion rate three years earlier, in the third quarter of 1953.

Notions on high and low finance.

It would be almost 60 years before another such decline was recorded.

The G.D.P. report released Friday states the total government part of G.D.P. – federal, state and local – came to $3.0306 trillion in the first quarter of this year. That is 0.01 percent below the $3.0309 trillion recorded four years earlier.

Those are nominal figures, not adjusted for inflation (as are the figures in the chart below). On a real basis, the decline was 6.5 percent.

His chart:

 

When are we going to wise up, folks? We can—and must—do better.

Working poor

April 12, 2013 by davald

The Bureau of Labor Statistics has a new report (pdf) on America’s working poor. To be quick about it, there are lots of them. Here’s a chart on “primary families,” which are defined as families with at least one person working at least half a year.

What the graph shows is the percentage of primary families who are poor despite a member’s working. So, in 2011 8.3 percent of those families live in poverty, up from 6.4 in 2007, just before the Great Recession. Among all persons the percentage of working poor in 2011 was seven, down slightly from  2010.

What’s going on? BLS takes a stab and comes up with a partial list. Among the culprits are the proliferation of part-time jobs and, more important, low wages. A good proxy for both would be so-called burger-flippers. Not only do they work less than full time, they don’t make much money, the minimum wage or perhaps a bit more.

It is a sad testament to our political culture that the needs of the poor go unmentioned let alone mentioned. Certainly the Republicans aren’t about to address the problem. They consistently resist raising the minimum wage and always champion the desires of employers over their past, present, and future employees. So it’s quite easy to jettison workers and deny them both benefits and job security.

While most modern nations have a labor party, America doesn’t. We have the Democrats, who seem just as eager to screw unions as their conservative counterparts, and it’s unions that brought us the eight-hour day, the end of child labor, the minimum wage, and a host of other benefits that are as niggardly as they are taken for granted.

Infuriating Obama

April 11, 2013 by davald

The G.O.P. would prefer that the poor and infirm get lost, as in out of sight out of mind. Their existence serves as a constant reminder that the great American experiment fails many, if not most, of the country’s inhabitants. So we are never surprised by the Republicans’ gleeful efforts to screw them even more.

What surprises, unfortunately, is Barack Obama’s willingness to play Clinton. As the slippery one from Arkansas did with African Americans and welfare recipients Obama now does to the elderly, most of whom rely on Social Security benefits to get by in their retirement years. Shameful and disgusting, I say.

The New York Times editors weigh in on Obama’s proposed budget. They write:

President Obama knew full well that many Democrats and liberals would be sharply critical of his decision to propose reducing the Social Security cost-of-living adjustment, one of the centerpieces of his 2014 budget, which was released on Wednesday. In fact, he was counting on it. He wanted to show that he was willing to antagonize his supporters to get a budget compromise, putting Republicans on the spot to do the same.

That sounds an awful like Slick Willy. We may have thought we were voting for someone with higher moral standards. We were wrong. The editors:

The Social Security proposal remains a bad idea, and, as this page has explained, it could hurt vulnerable retirees and stymie better ideas to improve the system, like raising the wage cap subject to the payroll tax. But it seems unlikely to happen if Mr. Obama holds to his demand for more revenues in exchange, given the Republican intransigence. For now, it has served its purpose — no one will be able to accuse Mr. Obama of refusing to touch entitlements, and no one can credit Republicans for being at all serious about a deficit-reduction compromise.

Playing politics on the backs of old people. I’m personally offended, since I’m officially old now.

Yet, I’ve long given up hoping that Obama will present different stripes. FDR’s would do, and it was Obama who encouraged us to think that he would be invoking the New Deal president in his approach to the economy, nearly destroyed under Bush II. Alas, he’s chosen a different predecessor, the one who “did not have sexual relations with that woman”—though he most certainly did.

Obama prefers the dissembler, the deceitful one over the president who dared welcome the wrath of Wall Street. Our first African American president welcomes their money as he screws the Rest of Us. All too familiar, I say.

Once poor, (almost) always poor

April 3, 2013 by davald

We don’t like to read that message. But the research is strongly suggesting that in America, especially, upward mobility is a fiction, now more than ever.

Yet we learn that it’s nearly impossible to break the trend by investing more in education, the way we do it in the U.S. Here, we put far more of our education dollars in college than at other levels. But if we do so with the expectation that more children will graduate with degrees then go on to earn decent livings, we would be mistaken. Indeed, we’re mostly wasting money.

These depressing thoughts come by way of an article in the New York Times. All this Race to the Top and No Child Left Behind hoopla, however well-intentioned, distracts us from focusing on the underlying problem: poor kids perform much worse in school than their richer peers, and the gap either stays constant or, more likely, grows over time.

So, where should we be spending our dollars? The research cited tells us to invest in early childhood programs. We’d get a far bigger bang for the buck.

American students from prosperous backgrounds scored on average 110 points higher on reading tests than disadvantaged students, about the same disparity that exists between the average scores in the United States and Tunisia. It is perhaps the main reason income inequality in the United States is passed down the generations at a much higher rate than in most advanced nations.

That’s a scandal, considering how much the government spends on education: about 5.5 percent of the nation’s economic output in total, from preschool through college.

And it suggests that the angry, worried debate over how to improve the nation’s mediocre education — pitting the teachers’ unions and the advocates of more money for public schools against the champions of school vouchers and standardized tests — is missing the most important part: infants and toddlers.

Research by Mr. [James] Heckman and others confirms that investment in the early education of disadvantaged children pays extremely high returns down the road. It improves not only their cognitive abilities but also crucial behavioral traits like sociability, motivation and self-esteem.

We can, and should, do better.
