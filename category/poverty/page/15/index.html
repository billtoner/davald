A geography of Why?

July 3, 2012 by davald

I came across this map in today’s New York Times. It depicts the blue states, the red states, and those judged a toss up.

My big question, which would have a very long, complicated answer, is Why? Why do we divide up this way, with the West Coast and Northeast being solidly blue and so much red elsewhere? What is about living close to the Pacific that makes us or transforms us into liberal Democrats? Likewise, what is so different about living in the middle states that a majority of people there vote for Republicans?

There are more and larger cities in the blue states than in the red. Does urbanity make us blue? Does living far from your neighbors make you red?

In today’s political climate, the reds are strongly and extremely so. The extremism is much less pronounced in the blue states. After all, being a liberal or a Democrat gets you nowhere near to being a socialist or social democrat, both popular in Europe.

To be sure, I don’t get out much. I loathe traveling. But over my six-plus decades I’ve been to New York, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania as an adult. A previous occupation took me to Alaska on a couple of occasions. I used to regularly drive to California via Oregon to visit family. I’ve also been to Nevada, and after a very long bus trip I found myself in Colorado to play baseball for an extended weekend. But you see all those red states up there? I’ve never been to one of them, save for Sarah Palin’s. So I have no personal observations that could help me answer those why-questions.

Is there something about farming that lends itself to conservatism. There’s plenty of agriculture east of the Cascades, and all of those legislative and congressional districts are represented by Republicans, with just a few exceptions. But why would farmers dislike liberal programs such as Social Security, Medicare, and public education? Farmers are hardly independent of government; they collect all sorts of taxpayer subsidies. They get cheap water from the Columbia River and cheap electricity, too, from public utility districts.

As it happens, there is a strong correlation between per capita income and state color. The poorer states are disproportionately red. The richer states are disproportionately blue. These correlations extend to education; the poorer and redder states are also less educated, with some exceptions.

Urbanization is increasing across the planet. You get a feel for this in viewing this interactive graphic. Does this trend suggest that we’ll become more liberal over time? There’s a downside, however. Poverty is increasing at a faster rate in the cities than in rural areas. Think slums. Which factor is more explanatory of political viewpoints: urbanity or income?

If you feel so inclined, post your opinion on this via comments. Maybe there’s a scholar out there who actually knows a thing or two about why red or blue.

Austerity’s victors

June 20, 2012 by davald

While the U.S. unemployment rate remains stubbornly high by historical standards, it’s not nearly as high as the rates in Spain, Greece, Italy, and the other peripheral countries of the Eurozone. As national governments receive fewer revenues occasioned by the economic downturn, and as the number of citizens needing public assistance rises, something’s got to give. The increasing anxiety and desperation of suffering people manifests themselves in political polarization and, worse, a turn to radicalism.

In Greece, which is at the bottom of the Euro pit, another round of elections resulted in a center-right coalition claiming victory, however narrow. Its leaders are willing to let its citizens take it in the shorts to comply with German austerity conditions, meaning lower wages, fewer benefits, and slashed government payrolls.

But a very far right political party, Golden Dawn, gained 18 seats in the new parliament, after receiving a scant 0.23 percent of the vote in 2009, insufficient to garner representation. The party’s campaign theme: “Let’s rid this country of the stench.” It proposes to deport one million immigrants, bar the immigration of Muslims, and bury land mines along the Turkish border to deter would-be immigrants. It would also halt the austerity measures. For The Socialist Worker, Shaun Harkin writes:

That Golden Dawn was able to maintain its newfound support is an indication of the depth of political polarization in Greece: the crisis is so acute and the situation so volatile and uncertain that this scourge of humanity will continue to pose a serious threat as long as the situation drags on.

Golden Dawn, of course, has been emboldened, viewing its vote as an endorsement of its message and violent attacks on immigrants. Taking advantage of the new media attention, party leader Nikolaos Michaloliakos boldly took the opportunity to spew a denial of the Holocaust in May, claiming, “There were no ovens–this is a lie…There were no gas chambers either.”

During the twilight of the Weimar Republic in Germany, similar austerity measures were underway. They were the price the country was forced to pay by England, America, et al. for “starting” the first world war. (The causes were a bit more complicated than that, of course.)

In his book The Economic Consequences of the Peace, written in 1919, John Maynard Keynes issued a sharp warning to the victors:

Moved by insane delusion and reckless self-regard, the German people overturned the foundations on which we all lived and built. But the spokesmen of the French and British peoples have run the risk of completing the ruin, which Germany began, by a Peace which, if it is carried into effect, must impair yet further, when it might have restored, the delicate, complicated organization, already shaken and broken by war, through which alone the European peoples can employ themselves and live.

His fair warning went unheeded, nor has his ghost fared particularly well in current circumstances. Keynes provides insights into the conditions that led to war and why Germany could not withstand the harsh reparations. Here’s a particular interesting observation that resonates today following the Great Recession:

Europe was so organized socially and economically as to secure the maximum accumulation of capital. While there was some continuous improvement in the daily conditions of life of the mass of the population, Society was so framed as to throw a great part of the increased income into the control of the class least likely to consume it. The new rich of the nineteenth century were not brought up to large expenditures, and preferred the power which investment gave them to the pleasures of immediate consumption. In fact, it was precisely the inequality of the distribution of wealth which made possible those vast accumulations of fixed wealth and of capital improvements which distinguished that age from all others [original emphasis].

Sound familiar? We’re repeating history’s mistakes with predictable consequences. Keynes cites one particular provision of the Reparations Commission that contributed to Germany’s post-war economic deterioration:

There remains a third provision more sweeping than either of the above, neither of which affects German interests in neutral countries. The Reparation Commission is empowered up to May 1, 1921, to demand payment up to $5,000,000,000 in such manner as they may fix, “whether in gold, commodities, ships, securities or otherwise.” This provision has the effect of intrusting to the Reparation Commission for the period in question dictatorial powers over all German property of every description whatever.

The onerous measures imposed on Germany radicalized the German electorate. Economist Christian Stögbauer of the University of Munich published a paper (pdf) wherein he associates the push to polar opposites with the crisis of the Great Depression exacerbated by the penalties imposed by the Reparations Commission. He writes:

The most obvious evidence for the economic crisis in Germany was the increase of unemployment from about 1.1 million in May 1928 to about 6.1 million in February 1932. Unemployment decreased only slightly in 1932 and stayed at a very high level. In January 1933 the number of people on the dole again passed the 6 million threshhold (Institut für Konjunkturforschung, 1933, p. 15). Over the same period, the percentage of unemployed people receiving insurance benefits fell from 83.2 % to 37.8 % (Adamy and Steffen, 1982). The unemployed (and their families), whose unemployment benefits had expired, had no choice other than to rely on public relief, which provided a bare minimum level of subsistence. Coincidentally with the increase in unemployment, wages were drastically cut back and partial lay-offs also increased dramatically. As a result, weekly nominal per capita income diminished from about 31 Reichsmarks in 1928 to about 25 Reichsmarks in 1933. However, because of unmistakable deflation, real income increased by about 5 %. The agricultural sector, the single largest employer with 30.5 % of the labor force, faced drastically declining prices, especially for dairy products and livestock whose price decreased by over 50 % from 1928 to 1933. The result was a debt crisis and numerous foreclosures (Brustein, 1996, p. 66). Although unemployment figures were much lower in agriculture – especially in regions with small farms – hidden unemployment was substantial. Regional variations in the extent of indebtedness were also very high, partly because large grain farms with high wages bills and social security obligations were concentrated in the East.

He includes this chart (NSDAP stands for National Socialist German Workers Party, or Nazis):

We’ll note that as incomes fell and unemployment rates rose, the fortunes of Hitler advanced. Here’s a chart showing the electoral results of the latter years of the Weimar Republic:

Until the Sept. 1930 elections, the Nazis were a no-show. But look at how quickly their power rose in subsequent votes. Although the communist party’s support also increased, it pales in comparison. (I have not included all of the parties, of which there were as many as 40 over the Weimar period.)

Hitler also had a thing about “stench.” In his case he was referring to Jews (and communists and gypsies). We know about his “final solution.”

Although the Socialist Party prevailed in the recent French elections, the fascist National Front garnered nearly 18 percent of the popular vote. In Hungary, a very far right party, Fidesz, secured two-thirds of that country’s parliamentary seats. The New York Times:

Democracy here is dying not with a single giant blow but with many small cuts, critics say, through the legal processes of Parliament that add up to a slow-motion coup. And in its drift toward authoritarian government, aided by popular disaffection with political gridlock and a public focused mainly on economic hardship, Hungary stands as a potentially troubling bellwether for other, struggling Eastern European countries with weak traditions of democratic government.

If you’re not getting a bit upset by this stuff so far, think of what could happen should the Euro collapse and the zone disintegrate into its status quo ante. The problems of Greece could easily spread to all of Europe, save, perhaps, Germany and the Nordic countries. That means widespread high unemployment, declining wages, diminished benefits, and sharply reduced public services. Could long-standing animosities resurface with horrific results?

Europe had always been a continent at war with itself. After the devastation of WWII, countries banded together so as to put their sordid pasts behind them. With the best of intentions they negotiated a common currency and eliminated (most) trade barriers between them. Things worked reasonably well until the Great Recession. Then the defects of a common currency without a common fiscal union painfully revealed themselves. The whole experiment may collapse.

In its wake will be the conditions that have already led to political extremism and polarization, even in a more solid France, but notably in Greece and Hungary. Spain, we will recall, was also under fascist rule, both during and after WWII. It could return.

We would do well to keep in mind that even Hitler, for all of his dastard ideology and actions, was legitimately elected by the German people. If enough of us begin to break under pressure, we can go apeshit.

After all, we’re busy electing our own brand of right-wing ideologues. They’re called ‘Republicans.’

Meritocracy and government

June 7, 2012 by davald

Michels’s grim conclusion was that it was impossible for any party, no matter its belief system, to bring about democracy in practice. Oligarchy was inevitable. For any kind of institution with a democratic base to consolidate the legitimacy it needs to exist, it must have an organization that delegates tasks. The rank and file will not have the time, energy, wherewithal or inclination to participate in the many, often minute decisions necessary to keep the institution functioning. In fact, effectiveness, Michels argues convincingly, requires that these tasks be delegated to a small group of people with enough power to make decisions of consequence for the entire membership. Over time, this bureaucracy becomes a kind of permanent, full-time cadre of leadership. “Without wishing it,” Michels says, there grows up a great “gulf which divides the leaders from the masses.” The leaders now control the tools with which to manipulate the opinion of the masses and subvert the organization’s democratic process. “Thus the leaders, who were at first no more than the executive organs of the collective, will soon emancipate themselves from the mass and become independent of its control.”

— excerpted in The Nation

Hayes is talking about Robert Michels, a German social theorist, who, born of wealth, nevertheless embarked on a political journey, moving further and further left. In each case he found that organization leads ineluctably to oligarchy, rule by a few.

In our own political systems, more properly described as representative government, we ostensibly select those we prefer to govern us. Hayes goes on to say about our current Congress, especially, that we are increasingly represented by elites beholden to monied interests, from which many, if not most, of them spring.

Hayes begins this lengthy excerpt chronicling his own experience as a young boy scoring well enough on a three-hour exam to gain entrance to Manhattan’s most elite educational institution, Hunter College High School, free to those few who attend. The only criterion for admittance is the score on a once-administered three-hour exam. As pure a meritocracy as we have in America. Or, at least it was. Over time, Hayes reports, New York City’s wealthy denizens invested in private tutors and test-taking classes for their young children. And the preparation and investments worked, so that the demographics of the school changed over time, becoming whiter and richer.

Meritocracy, Hayes suggests from his reading of Michels, requires adherence to two principles: difference and mobility. The first assumes that we have vastly different capabilities, which should be not only acknowledged but embraced. The second principle, mobility, assumes that, though we are different, we should all have equal opportunity to compete for the more attractive positions.

I’ll step in here with some comments. The above sounds an awful like conventional conservative thinking: the role of government is to ensure equal opportunity, but also to recognize that the better should prosper and the worse should suffer. After all, that’s what markets are all about. Scarce goods are distributed on the basis of merit rather than, as liberals would argue, to ensure fairness. Hayes has his own take:

But this ideal, appealing as it may be, runs up against the reality of what I’ll call the Iron Law of Meritocracy. The Iron Law of Meritocracy states that eventually the inequality produced by a meritocratic system will grow large enough to subvert the mechanisms of mobility. Unequal outcomes make equal opportunity impossible. The Principle of Difference will come to overwhelm the Principle of Mobility. Those who are able to climb up the ladder will find ways to pull it up after them, or to selectively lower it down to allow their friends, allies and kin to scramble up. In other words: “Who says meritocracy says oligarchy.”

America, for better or worse, and I believe strongly that it’s the latter, is enthrall to “The Iron Law of Meritocracy.” Born rich, you’re likely to stay that way. On the other end of the scale, you should have picked more successful parents.

In such a pseudo-merit system, those who are first when the metaphorical race begins are far more likely to finish first. They will have taken every advantage to ensure that once rich they will leave the world rich. Hayes:

But the Iron Law of Meritocracy makes a different prediction: that societies ordered around the meritocratic ideal will produce inequality without the attendant mobility. Indeed, over time, a society will become more unequal and less mobile as those who ascend its heights create means of preserving and defending their privilege and find ways to pass it on across generations. And this, as it turns out, is a pretty spot-on description of the trajectory of the American economy since the mid-1970s.

Notice the dates. They are too familiar to readers of this blog. They signal the start of the Great Divergence, a term that is both apt and increasingly popular. It coincides most sharply with Ronald Reagan’s occupation of the Oval Office.

While I doubt that he was the architect of the Great Divergence he certainly allowed it to happen, giving it a big nudge, certainly, with the wholesale firing of the air-traffic controllers. He also spoke the language of “meritocracy,” singing the praises of the private sector and its inbred social Darwinism while defaming government as “the problem” rather than the solution.

It has become all too fashionable to denigrate government as did Reagan. Conservatives would go further than crusade against it; they spend their waking hours conjuring up new ways to give effect to their attitude. Government, they say, must be put on a diet. It must be denied revenues, because its expenditures frustrate the “Iron Law of Meritocracy,” nurturing the undeserved at the expense of the privileged.

I hope that you see what’s at stake here, especially in light to what’s been happening in conservative-controlled states like Wisconsin and Ohio, where public employees are under siege. Government, for all its ills, is about the only mechanism available to rein in the extremes, rendering both the rich and poor less so.

Okay, this sounds like socialism! Put down your swords for a moment, wipe off the spittle, and allow yourself to be mentally transported to northern Europe. If you don’t like government doing things on behalf of the Rest of Us, you’ll hate Finland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, et al. Their governments are huge in comparison to the U.S.’s And what do their citizens get in return? Free education. Free health care. Scant poverty. Far less inequality. Higher living standards.

Well, yes, education and health care are not exactly “free.” The citizens of these lands pay higher taxes for these benefits, in most cases willingly if not enthusiastically.

But the Nordic people largely embrace the exchange: higher taxes and larger government for those things we Americans increasingly expect the private sector to provide, in accordance with the “Iron Law of Meritocracy.”

Mark my words. The more we support, either by action or inaction, the shrinking of government, the more the Rest of Us will suffer and the more wealth the already wealthy will siphon off from a protracted, sputtering economy.

Having issued this caveat, I quickly offer another. There are many obvious reasons why we should distrust our government, though not necessarily the concept of government. Congress is the home of “institutional corruption.” And as Hayes points out, regulatory capture is rife in D.C., with federal agencies serving as the source of future employees for the firms they are supposed to regulate.

I’ve got to believe that the Founding Fathers, Abe Lincoln, and certainly Teddy Roosevelt would be aghast at what has happened to the federal government, especially the deluge of billionaire dollars ensuring that the Rest of Us get screwed as the “malefactors of great wealth” extract greater shares of GDP. They would be appalled by Citizens United, which brought unbridled bribery into the open and onto the pages of the U.S. Code.

But in particular for those who cobbled together the Constitution, they would be horrified by how the very concept of democracy as they conceived it has been greatly sullied beyond their recognition. Of course, they wanted no part of monarchy, and none of the associated pomp and circumstance. And they also preferred a limited, less intrusive government than the one they had endured under King George. And they also believed in the entrepreneurial spirit, about fashioning a respectable life through dint of labor and discipline.

However, the virtues of self-reliance and self-actualization cannot be practiced today by the Rest of Us. Those attitudes are reserved for the extremely privileged, for the most part, and unavailable to the masses. We’ve also lost our sense of innovation and imagination, if we take economist Tyler Cowen’s word. With the Great Divergence we may have entered a protracted state of stagnation.

You see, handing everything over to the plutocracy has been bad for business. The plutocrats know no limits. If left to their own appetites and devices, they will cause the whole damn thing to come crashing down on the Rest of Us. After all, they came awfully close to doing that just a few short years ago. They must be constrained for their—and our—own good.

Who regulates, or at least is supposed to? Government, preferably one whose agents are themselves virtuous.

Who can lift the poor? Government.

Who can restrain the malefactors? Government.

Who can provide education, health care, and guaranteed retirement income? Government.

Who can enable scientific research? Government.

But that kind of government, now just a mental figment, can be realized only by a citizenry committed to those achievements. Yet, it’s proved to be far too easy to manufacture the consent of the governed. Actually, we’re pretty damn cheap.

Why do we collectively put up with this? Is the challenge too large? Have we already lost? Did we ever struggle in service to the Rest of Us?

I guess that’s why some people feel the need to occupy something. Doing something—anything—is better than nothing. Lean out your window and scream that you’ve had enough.

We’ve recently witnessed real revolutions. After suffering a long train of autocratic abuse, citizens of the Middle East erupted, literally taking to the streets then occupying them. Something or perhaps several things sparked the collective outrage.

What might be the spark that sets off the Rest of Us? How low do we have to go before we scream “enough”?

Or, horror of horror, perhaps most Americans are quite comfortable with the growing inequality, the decline in public education, the erosion of our public infrastructure. Perhaps they have other things to do than to arrest these developments. Maybe, just maybe, they like not only oligarchy but the plutocrats who run it.

Spark. Where are you?

As to the rant charge. Guilty, with no regrets. Yes, I have known and worked with Republicans, and a few have actually been very fine individuals, but their ideology sucks.

Now I find it quite a leap to imply that “u-bam-a” (what’s that all about, anyway?) is a tax-and-spend socialist. He’s nothing of a sort, as this morning’s AP article makes clear. If Obama is a socialist, he’s a very bad one.

I have no explanation as to the different attitudes toward socialism from one side of the pond to the other.

But there may be no stronger epithet in America than “socialist.” It really gets people stirred up, causing them to blabber and spit. And I just don’t get it, since there are so few to be found here, and only one in Congress out of 535. That’s 0.19 percent. You fear this?

Back to my anti-Republicanism, I am mostly concerned about the growing inequality in America and the breakdown of society, which are related. Democrats are more inclined to share this concern; Republicans embrace the divisions.

But, truth be told, neither political party really seems to give a rat’s rectum for the Rest of Us. The Democrats, who used to have a solid Labor core, are now beholden to Wall Street—as the Republicans have been, gleefully so, for a century or more.

And speaking of Labor, in his column this morning Joe Nocera writes about Timothy Noah’s latest book, The Great Divergence, wherein Noah attempts to account for its causes. One of the factors that has changed remarkably since the 50s and even more so beginning in Reagan’s initial term is the decline in union participation rates. Nocera quotes Noah on this: “Draw one line on a graph charting the decline in union membership, then superimpose a second line charting the decline in middle-class income share,” writes Noah, “and you will find that the two lines are nearly identical.”

The above chart, from OECD data, illustrates the strong inverse correlation between union participation rates and income inequality (Gini coefficient). I’ve sorted the countries by union participation rates. Sweden’s rate is 82.2 percent; it’s Gini index is .25 (the lower the number the lower the inequality). The U.S. has the highest Gini index and also the lowest union participation rate.

Republicans, as a rule, loathe unions. The Democrats have gradually distanced themselves from Labor. Unlike other democracies, in America there is no party of Labor. Nocera:

But if liberals really want to reverse income inequality, they should think seriously about rejoining labor’s side.

You’ll have to hurry, though. Labor’s numbers are shrinking rapidly. Wait too long and you won’t find any.

Taxes

May 21, 2012 by davald

Republicans, as a rule, loathe taxes. Some, like Ron Paul, would prefer to eliminate the IRS, which would have the effect of eliminating federal taxes, I suppose. Supreme Court Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes reportedly said in a 1934 speech that “taxes are the price we pay for a civilized society.” It would seem, then, that Republicans have no use for the latter, and it shows, given their rabid temperament—which is simply mean.

I have a decidedly different view of government, one more aspirational than descriptive. I believe that we deserve a government that works for the Rest of Us rather than as a tool for the filthy rich. As it stands, Congress is a cesspool of corruption, completely beholden to its wealthy benefactors.

Yet, if we were to have a government that Abraham Lincoln talked about, you know “of the people” and all that, taxes would have to rise on the rich, certainly, and perhaps a few quintiles below. Those taxes, however, would be used to fund what “the people” want.

But I’m going to eliminate Republicans from “the people,” since they don’t like government, any government, to begin with. That leaves progressives and liberals, the kind of people who long for a “kinder, gentler” nation. We, the real Americans (how’s that?) would gladly pay our taxes knowing that they would be used in furtherance of that goal.

So, speaking of taxes, I offer a few charts below.

The first two are based on historical data provided by the White House, one showing tax receipts from individuals and corporations. The second shows each as a percentage of the gross domestic product.

GDP actually fell in nominal dollars between 2008 and 2009, while tax rates remained constant. That helps explain the last spike in corporate receipts (red) and also in individual income taxes.

Corporate taxes as a percent of GDP have steadily declined since 1952, widening the gap with individual income taxes.  Individual income taxes reached their peak in 2000 (as a percentage of GDP) then fell sharply as a result of Bush II’s tax cuts.

Individual income taxes, as a percentage of GDP, remained fairly constant from the early 1950s to the middle and late 90s, when Clinton’s tax increases became effective. Moreover, I would expect that during that period the distribution of the income taxes changed with the marginal tax rate.

Medieval

May 18, 2012 by davald

A friend, and fellow ex-Catholic, linked me to the video on this site, Catholics Called to Witness. It is indeed slick, in a production sort of way, and appeals not to one’s reason but to our limbic system. That is, it’s all about imagery and music. Therefore, it will surely be effective in calling Catholics to November’s voting booths to cast ballots that reflect the Church’s “values,” which are “marriage,” “life,” and “freedom.”

The video, which is positively medieval in tone and visual expression, with a blacksmith pounding out letters that are eventually assembled to spell out the “values,” emphasizes that marriage must be “reinforced” rather than “redefined.” Abortion, of course, must be prohibited in all circumstances. Compelling the Church’s institutions to provide health care insurance that covers contraception is anathema.

Conspicuously absent from this call to arms is any mention of the Church’s social gospel, the teachings found in the Sermon on the Mount, for example. Thus, we hear or see nothing about the plight of the poor and near-poor, roughly half our population, or that 40 million Americans lack health insurance, or the plutocracy that makes paupers of the Rest of Us, or the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan, or the thousands on death rows awaiting lethal injection. Nothing. Nada. Nichts.

The conspicuous absence is all in the name of our Lord. Damn the Church.

A kinder, gentler nation

May 16, 2012 by davald

That phrase of George H.W. Bush, surprisingly, describes my aspiration for this country. We’ve got a long way to go.

A friend occasionally scolds me for being “progressive,” which he equates with utopianism. The world is what it is, and there’s really nothing anyone can do about it. That may very well be true, but I choose to believe that things could be—and should be—different.

Suppose you join me for a bit as I describe that aspiration. I’ll just lay out a list.

  • free health care for everyone, regardless of circumstances
  • free education, from pre-school through graduate school
  • a squeezing from both the top and the bottom, so that the rich aren’t so rich and the poor aren’t so poor
  • generous, even lavish support for the arts, in schools and in communities
  • clean water
  • clean air
  • immaculate and well-maintained sidewalks and streets
  • human-scale architecture that both calms and uplifts
  • no death penalty
  • rare and always safe abortions
  • great, not just adequate, schools, with beautiful, clean, and efficient buildings, well-paid and well-trained teachers, ample supplies, state-of-the-art technology
  • a national pension fund that provides a secure and adequate retirement for every worker
  • livable-wage jobs for anyone who needs work
  • truly livable, walkable cities and towns
  • state-of-the-art public transportation systems readily available to all, with an emphasis on convenience, comfort, and aesthetics
  • no guns or bullets
  • safe and high-quality food at affordable prices
  • attractive and eminently usable “third places” (home and work being the other two places), where people congregate to eat and socialize
  • beautiful, proximate, and citizen-friendly public buildings
  • no Fox News
  • no coal plants
  • no Tea Party
  • no Rush Limbaugh
  • a military with the sole objective of only defending our borders against foreign enemies, and no more
  • no Grover Norquist
  • no Harley Davidsons in downtowns
  • a completely different government based on and fueled by ideas; no politics of dirt (i.e., real estate); a unicameral parliament; no judicial review

I should think that in such a country, people would be less anxious, less worried, less greedy, and less angry. They would me more tolerant, more relaxed, more educated, more social, more civil, and more reasonable. In short, we would become a kinder, gentler nation.

What’s wrong with that?

Why so many poor?

May 11, 2012 by davald

I suspect that every time people gather to recite the Pledge of Allegiance or sing the National Anthem they implicitly believe that they exist in the greatest country on earth. So, I’ll stick with this motif for a moment to pose some questions.

I begin with a fundamental question: If this country is so great, why are so many of us poor?

According to recent estimates, about half of all Americans are poor or near-poor. One out of every two Americans lives in poverty. Whatever the causes, the mere fact that half of us are struggling to survive should sow doubts about our greatness.

If this country is so great, why are so many of us lacking jobs?

If this country is so great, why do we coddle the rich at the expense of everyone else?

If this country is so great, why does our education system suck?

If this country is so great, why do we spend so much on health care while leaving more than 40 million of us without any insurance at all?

The Republican majority in the House passed a budget yesterday. If it became law, the poor would be punished further. Here’s the New York Times editorial on the subject:

For more than a year, House Republicans have energetically worked to demolish vital social programs that have made this country both stronger and fairer over the last half-century. At the same time, they have insisted on preserving bloated military spending and unjustifiably low tax rates for the rich. That effort reached a nadir on Thursday when the House voted to prevent $55 billion in automatic cuts imposed on the Pentagon as part of last year’s debt-ceiling deal, choosing instead to make all those cuts, and much more, from domestic programs.

The paper’s editors entitle their essay “The Human Cost of Ideology.” They go on to enumerate the toll the Republicans would exact on our most vulnerable citizens. The Democratic minority offered a counter proposal, which was summarily dismissed by the Republicans. The editors:

House Democrats offered an alternative bill that would replace the $109 billion sequester by raising taxes on the wealthy, ending oil company tax loopholes and cutting farm subsidies, but it was rejected. Republicans are determined to protect millionaires and defense contractors, no matter the costs to the country.

“…with liberty and justice for all.” Really?
