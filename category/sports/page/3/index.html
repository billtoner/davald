McCarver has a point

October 20, 2013 by davald

Tim McCarver caught the great Bob Gibson, who loathed being pulled from a game. In his day a starting pitcher was expected to finish, so it was not unusual for the best pitchers to record a high number of complete games.

In last night’s final ALCS game between the Tigers and the Red Sox, McCarver opined that managers and front office folks should not become “prisoners to pitch counts.” Pay attention, but letting numbers dictate is “just wrong.”

I agree, and perhaps Tigers’ starter Max Scherzer does too. He’d thrown 110 pitches into the seventh inning and allowed a couple of batters to reach base. The Tigers were ahead 2-1. But manager Jim Leyland yanked him in favor of the bullpen. Disaster struck, with Shane Victorino launching a curve ball over the Green Monster for a grand slam.

I’m old enough to remember the best game ever: the Giants’s Juan Marichal dueling the venerable Warren Spahn over 15 scoreless innings. Willie Mays ended the game in the bottom of the 16th with a walk-off homerun. Both pitchers must have tossed more than 200 balls toward the plate.

Let pitchers pitch. Forget about the count.

Closer to home

September 6, 2013 by davald

At least with respect to education policy I have advocated a Finnish approach. I also prefer governments and economies committed to greater equality than the U.S. system.

Yet, Matthew Yglesias offers a different perspective, one that I hadn’t considered. He cites statistics from the greater Boston area, suggesting that more Americans would be better off if they aped Bostonians rather than, say, the Danes. He writes:

Consider the Boston-Quincy-Cambridge Metropolitan Statistical Area of the United States. Greater Boston contains about the same number of people as Denmark. And in Purchasing Power Parity terms it’s richer than Denmark. There is inequality in Greater Boston, but prosperity is broadly shared—median household income was $68,515 in 2010. That’s 25 percent higher than the American average. The 10th percentile of Greater Boston workers earns $11.05 an hour. That’s 28 percent higher than the American average. Massachusetts has a universal healthcare system, and Massachusetts’ public school system outperforms every European country’s schools (some Asian countries do better).

Bostonians certainly have a better baseball team than Seattleites do.

Following mediocrity

September 2, 2013 by davald

I know. I must have a screw loose. I still watch the Mariners on TV. Pathetic. But, hey, I was drafted by the New York Mets, another team lounging in mediocrity.

Larry Stone says it all in this post. Writing for the Seattle Times he reminds us that the M’s have had losing seasons in 8 of their last 10. The glory days are such ancient history that few can even remember, though the owners dutifully trot out the living legends from that period. There aren’t that many hanging around, however.

So, we look forward to the Seahawks and the Huskies as baseball fades into the dreary sunset. Heck. Did you know that over 67,000 people showed up to watch the Sounders play soccer the other day. Amazing.

The insanity will continue. Bet on it. Why? Because the Mariners continue to make buckets of money from television. The owners don’t give a damn about winning. They don’t have to. Stone:

It’s especially sad considering it wasn’t that long ago the Mariners owned this town. From 1995 through 2001, when they made the playoffs four times and fielded a consistently charismatic team, the Mariners worked their way to the forefront of fan loyalty and affection. It peaked with an MLB-best 3,540,482 spectators in 2002 — an astonishing 92.8 percent of capacity over 81 games.

That goodwill was squandered long ago, however. Those fans who haven’t yet drifted off into apathy are consumed by frustration.

Intentional hitting

August 21, 2013 by davald

In light of Ryan Dempster’s hitting Alex Rodriguez, my sister inquired if I’d ever deliberately thrown at a batter. Well, yes, I replied, and I remember the event quite well.

We’ll have to journey back many, many years—about 50, to be more precise. The Pittsburg H.S. (Calif.) baseball team has invaded our home diamond (Mt. Diablo), and I’m on the mound. My opponent was doing much too well for my tastes, as I was losing the battle (I lost only two games my high school career and both were against Pittsburg).

I had a grudge against the opposing pitcher going back to CYO basketball days a few years before. I thought he was a cocky jerk who played as if he were some superstar. He wasn’t, being a pudgy little guy.

So the guy comes to the plate, and I’m determined to hit him in his pitching arm. I succeeded. But the damn guy shook it off to continue, notching the victory.

Aside from that sorry event, I don’t think I threw another pitch in my career with the intention of hitting a batter. No fines. No suspensions.

Catcher’s interference

August 20, 2013 by davald

Several times this season I’ve observed a hitter swing his bat at the catcher’s glove while ostensibly trying to make contact with the ball. In one instance, the bat knocked the mitt off the catcher’s hand, which was then exposed to a 90-mph fast ball. Ouch.

Last night Jacoby Ellsbury of the Redsox swung his bat into Buster Posey’s glove. The ESPN commenters suggested, to my surprise, that the move was intentional. The penalty, after all, is an error on the catcher, no at-bat by the hitter, and an award of first base. Not bad. Ellsbury is a repeat offender, the commenters reported.

Here’s my two cents on the subject, and it has much to do with the plate umpires’ inability to call the low strike. Invariably, they call pitches in the lower part of the zone (as revealed by the “tracer”) a ball. In turn, the umpires are heavily influenced by where and how the catcher receives the pitch.

It is this subtle influence that induces the catcher to position his glove closer to the plate so that the ball is caught a bit higher above the ground. The reason for this is clear. If a pitch crosses the plate at the low end of the zone, it will normally hit the catcher’s glove at or near the dirt, affecting the umpire’s judgment. This is especially true on overhand curve balls and down-breaking sliders.

Coaches and hitters know this, too. And since most batters place their back foot at or even beyond the rear end of the batter’s box, it’s relatively easy to widen the swing’s arc so as to hit the catcher’s glove.

Last night, Aaron Boone, one of the commenters, said that the catcher needed to adjust by pulling further back. The effect of this, of course, is to reduce the likelihood of the umpire’s calling the low strike, for the aforementioned reasons.

As a former pitcher, I don’t like this at all. I would propose to eliminate the umpire’s judgment completely by relying on the electronic tracers in place at all ball parks. They not only accurately record the pitch’s location, they also provide the velocity and trajectory. Moreover, the result is instantaneous.

Okay, purists. Have a go.
