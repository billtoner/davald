Torture vs. masochism

July 26, 2011 by davald

I grew up with the S.F. Giants. I remember vividly sitting on the couch, playing hooky from school, watching the seventh game of the 1962 World Series. There were two outs in the bottom of the ninth against the Yankees, with runners in scoring position and Willie McCovey at the plate. Then the famous line drive and the Peanuts cartoon. If only the ball had been a foot higher. But Bobby Richardson snared the searing spheroid, ending the longest series in history (rain delays and postponements in both cities).

After moving to Washington state in 1973, I continued to follow both the Giants and the Oakland A’s. Despite a quirky, outlandish owner, the A’s managed to string together three consecutive World Series titles. But the Giants, not so well. The Bay Bridge series in 1989 was marred by the major earthquake, and the Giants were soundly defeated–swept, actually—by their counterparts from “there’s no there there.”

Then last year, the glorious. The Giants finally won the World Series, after 56 years of wandering in the baseball wilderness. But it was torture. Close, nail-biting games during the final stretch of the season then throughout the playoffs. Opponents, however, learned to fear the beard. So, no matter how perilous the journey, San Francisco fans gladly endured the torture for the end result.

But what can we say about Mariners’ fans? There is no endurable torture, since the outcome is almost always the same: a loss. No, if you follow the home team, as do I, you must engage in a perverse form of masochism, knowing that futility-squared produces the same miserable result. During this record-breaking losing skein, the M’s have discovered that it’s become nearly impossible to combine good pitching and good hitting; indeed, on this recent devastating road trip they’ve had neither.

What to do?

Managers come and managers go, yet nothing changes. Players come and players go, yet nothing changes. General managers come and general managers go, yet nothing changes. The only constant in the organization is the absentee ownership and the CEO.

Let’s speculate. I’ll assume that the owners keep the team, although I don’t know why anyone would want it. The general manager is in charge of acquiring and jettisoning the manager and is mostly responsible for the roster of players. Jack Z. has performed poorly, by almost any measure. Nail-chewing Eric Wedge, as feisty as they come, has all but exhausted his rhetorical arrows. Continually chiding the players to “get tougher” doesn’t seem to work. Now he’s shaved off his Fu Manchu in a feeble attempt to relieve the clubhouse pressure. The tune has shifted from tough to what-the-heck. But sticking with this bunch of players and coaches would only guarantee misery.

Jack Z., believe it or not, says he’s not actively shopping for deals. His major problem: none of the players, with perhaps three or four exceptions, will command value in the market. What’s a Figgins worth? Same for Jack Wilson. Yet, they’re doing no one, including themselves, any good playing for the hapless M’s. There may be young players with “potential,” that elusive quality that may never be realized. Trade the future?

I’ll go out on a limb. I bet that there will be movement, since the season is hopelessly lost. I also bet that whoever leaves Seattle will feel as if they’d escaped purgatory—and they will prosper in different environs. That’s what former Mariners do.

The next few days before the July 31 deadline should be interesting. The masochism is a given.

Tying the futility record

July 24, 2011 by davald

Today the Mariners have the splendid opportunity to set another team record. This one for consecutive losses. Their total at the moment is 13 14. If they fall again to the Red Sox this afternoon, they beat the record. Glory.

A week ago, the club gathered a handful of players from the 2001 roster. That team set a record: most wins in a season. Since then, Seattle’s fortunes have plummeted, with little hope of and, strangely, not too much interest in improving. The M’s enjoy sole ownership of last place in the West, 14.5 games behind division-leading Texas. To listen to their general manager, so what? A Seattle Times headline says it all.

Those who read the sports page are accustomed to a certain lingo not found elsewhere in the English language. For your consideration, General Manager Jack Z. (good luck with that spelling) offers this:

 “I’m trying to accumulate talent. We’re doing that throughout the organization. From the first day I got here, I said we had to accumulate talent. If we can do something that helps us add talent as we move forward, in the right situation, I’m ready to go.”

Thus far, Jack Z. seems to have done a pretty poor job. The M’s once again sport the worst-hitting team in baseball, scoring the fewest runs. But who’s worrying? Not Jack. Not the M’s front office. Not the owners.

Last month, when the M’s were within striking distance of the division lead, there were calls to add at least one more “bat.” Jack Z. implied that acquiring a “bat” was no easy proposition. He told the Times‘ Larry Stone:

“So many clubs were figuring out what they were and where they were at. Quite frankly, a lot of clubs would like one more bat, but no one seemed to come up with one. That big bat doesn’t seem to be out there at a reasonable cost. It’s not as easy as saying, ‘We need a bat. Let’s go get a bat.’ Great. Who’s giving one up? Lots of clubs who are doing well are looking for a bat.”

Well, guess who got a bat? The world champion S.F. Giants, needing to replace all-star Freddie Sanchez, who suffered a season-ending injury, landed Astros’ second baseman Jeff Keppinger. His career batting average is .283. With the Reds in 2007 he hit .332 with a .400 on-base percentage. He’s now hitting .302.

Yes, the Giants can afford to spend money. Their payroll ranks 8th in the league at around $112 million, and they’ve sold out every home game. On-field success does that, I guess. The Mariners, interestingly enough, are right in the middle of the pack, with an annual payroll of $86 million. (See data here.) The Giants’ revenues are about average for the league. The Mariners’ are just a few million dollars below, more than enough to acquire another “bat.”

Nor are the M’s shy about spending money? Can you say ‘Chone Figgins’? Can you say ‘Milton Bradley’? Can you say ‘Carlos Silva’?

Me thinks, in the end, that the entire organization is in denial. I offer this final quote from Jack Z.:

“Doggone it, we’re going to get this thing turned around. And it’s going to get turned around quicker than people think.”

Don’t hold your breath.

UPDATE (July 25, 2011):

Well, as expected, the M’s set a new franchise record for consecutive losses. But it only looks better: today they face the Yankees. If I were a betting man, my money is on the Bronx Bombers and three more Mariner losses. Yet, nothing in the news as I write about selling, buying, or trading players. Are we stuck with this miserable bunch for the rest of the year? Or maybe Wedge’s shave will do the trick. Is that Jack Z.’s solution to the quick turnaround? Might I suggest better hitters and pitchers?

Yes, it is insane, but let’s keep doing it anyway

July 18, 2011 by davald

The manager of the hapless Mariners has allowed more than once that his players are practicing on-the-field insanity, because they try the same thing over and over expecting a different result. I might suggest that Mr. Wedge take a look in the mirror.

Following yet another Mariners’ loss (nine in a row as of this writing), the manager repeated his admonition to players: get tougher. The Seattle Times quoted him thusly:

“Yeah, it’s been a tough stretch,” he said. “But what we’ve built hasn’t changed. Where it’s changed drastically, obviously, is in the standings. That’s a tough pill to swallow, no doubt about it.

“Ultimately, you have to believe good things lie ahead. As tough as it is right now, we’ll get this thing turned around. It has to start by winning a ballgame, and then you work to get better than that. … What makes it painful for everyone, it’s just been so drastic offensively. We’ve been playing good baseball; we just haven’t been hitting at all. That’s why it sticks out so bad.”

Further on in the article the writer equates Wedges’s lineup changes to rearranging the deck chairs on the Titanic. An apt metaphor, me thinks; this ship is sinking very fast. The tragedy is that nothing that either Wedge or management does works. It really doesn’t matter who is inserted when or where,  the outcome is the same—a thorough trouncing.

One comment overheard during a recent game was that just as winning can be infectious so can losing. Players come to the ballpark expecting to be beaten. The negative attitude is palpable, even to television viewers. Contrast the facial expressions and physical demeanor of the M’s with those of, say, the Red Sox. The latter look determined with an expectation that they’ll figure out a way to win the game at hand. The Mariners appear nervous, dazed, confused, anxious, and dispirited. They are angry with themselves, just as their manager seems to be chewing on nails—the kind you hammer into wood.

Perhaps this bunch needs a different kind of leader, one who is more avuncular, forgiving, relaxed, and encouraging. These players know that they can do better, because they have a résumé to prove it.

I’m suggesting that the players’ problems may lie between their ears and not between the foul lines. The pressure builds with each loss and the manager presses the lid down even harder. An explosion is imminent. We saw it last year in the dugout when Chone Figgins and then-manager Don Wakamatsu nearly came to blows.

On the other hand, with the exception of Lou Pinella, no Mariners skipper has recorded a winning season. Here is the sorry history:

 

Might this dismal record suggest that the real problem lies upstairs with the owners and the corporate heads? As with the players, it really doesn’t seem to matter who is managing, again with the exception of Sweet Lou. Moreover, the styles of these different managers varies from the apparent calm-and-collected (e.g., John McLaren) to the fierce-and-fiery (e.g., Wedge).

Maybe, just maybe, it’s all about the talent in the batter’s box (we have good pitching and defense). These guys just can’t hit the ball, which is the minimum requirement for offense.

I have a very selfish reason for following baseball in general and the M’s in particular. On occasion I need to take my mind off the awful stuff we call ‘reality.’

What now, Mariners?

July 14, 2011 by davald

With the second half of the season underway, what can or should the Mariners do? If they honestly believe that they are still in the hunt, they’ll likely make different choices as we near the July 31 trading deadline than if they are convinced that yet another season is headed into the toilet.

As of this writing, the team is 7.5 games back of division-leading Texas, which has eked out a one-game lead over the closing Angels, who began the season mired in last place. In order to compete in the West, the Mariners will have to beat both of these teams, which is a tall order, indeed. We need remember that just before the All-Star break the Halos swept the Mariners in a four-game series.

We should also keep in mind that the M’s have the worst hitting team in baseball, and this dismal fact is on display almost every game. The Angels beat the M’s with the long ball, and Texas has even more firepower. Despite being among the best, the Mariner hurlers make mistakes from time to time. Texas and Anaheim exploit those errors with four-bangers. In reverse, the M’s either let the center-cut fastball slip harmlessly into the catcher’s mitt or they manage a weak out. Yep, these Mariners just can’t hit worth a darn.

But, we hear, there is potential. There’s always potential. Greg Halman and Dustin Ackley show early promise, with both hitting above .300. Then it’s downhill from there. Perennial all-star Ichiro Suzuki is batting a career-low .270 and may not record 200 hits for the first time in the bigs. Justin Smoak lingers at 12 homeruns, tied with catcher Miguel Olivo for the team lead. Yet their averages are .229 and .223, respectively. Gold-glover Gutierrez has shown some recent spark at the plate, but is still far below the Mendoza line at .187. Brendan Ryan, after a superlative May, has seen his average plummet; he’s hitting .252. Old reliable Adam Kennedy is doing slightly better at .259.

Because the Mariners have a topnotch pitching staff most of their games are close. However, when the opposition averages more runs per nine innings than you, chances are you’ll lose more games than you’ll win.

I’m going out on a very short limb to suggest that the Mariners are unlikely to be challenging either Texas or Anaheim in September. So, if I were the general manager, here is what I’d do:

  1. Jettison Chone Figgins and Jack Cust. In eight years with the Angels, Figgins batted 0.291, with an on-base percentage of .363. So far with the Mariners, Figgins is hitting an anemic 0.238, with an OBP of .308. When the Figgins deal was first announced, I said “kudos” to GM Jack Zduriencik. Who would have thought that Figgins would just collapse upon moving to Seattle. (Second thought, how many players have plunged into the performance black hole we natives call ‘the Emerald City’?) As for Cust, over his career he’s struck out 39 percent of the time he comes to the plate. (Figgins’ number is 15 percent.) His 162-game seasonal average for home runs is 26; this year he’s hit just three.
  2. Jack Wilson is another acquisition I initially supported, with good reason. Seven years ago he made the All-Star team as a shortstop, batting .308. His lifetime fielding percentage is .977. (Current shortstop Brendan Ryan is a hair better at .979.) But with Wilson’s myriad injuries and placing himself indefinitely in manager Eric Wedge’s doghouse for volunteering to quit in the middle of a game for “the team’s sake,” Wilson’s value to the M’s is nearly zero.

Here’s the problem, which you can anticipate: the Mariners won’t get very much for these players, unless there really is something to the black-hole phenomenon, in which case, once these players leave town they’ll blossom again. Try that for a negotiating tactic; actually sell Seattle as the nonpareil talent-sucking vortex. But I jest.

The players that would fetch big money or quality players are pitchers you’d need to compete, if not now then eventually. What a dilemma: weaken the pitching staff in the hope of landing some bats.

Speaking of bats, Safeco Field might as well have force fields for fences. Balls don’t fly away; they die in outfielders’ gloves. This is not Texas or Anaheim. So, I would focus on acquiring contact hitters with higher OBPs and more productive outs. The stadium nearly dictates a station-to-station approach. Forget about the long ball. (Which is why I thought Figgins would be a good fit. Oh, well.)

The Mariners have a pretty good reputation for developing talent in their farm system. And it’s interesting to note that their AAA team, the Tacoma Rainiers, has almost a team makeup the reverse of the M’s: good hitting, poor pitching. Just look at these stats. Five players are hitting .335 or above. Kyle Seager was leading the team at .455 before he was brought up a couple of weeks ago. Several on this list have been with the M’s, only to fail miserably. Yet, as soon as they return to Tacoma, they resume hitting the cover off the ball. The difference has to be the quality of pitching in the majors vs. the minors.

Now, are these Tacoma hitters really that good so that the only thing they lack to succeed in the bigs is experience hitting top-caliber pitching? To a fault, perhaps, I think the Mariners’ brass has been patient with the likes of Michael Saunders, Matt Tuiososopo, and Ryan Langerhans. None did well up here. Mike Wilson didn’t get a fair shake this year; he’s hitting .345 with the Rainiers.

At the risk of condemnation, I’m going to wade into dangerous territory. It’s about Ichiro. Both his hitting and his fielding have suffered this year, and his batting average is trending down. While this shouldn’t surprise us, after all he’s 37, it brings up an obvious question: Could another team realize more value from Ichiro than the M’s?

What gives me pause, is that Ichiro is still quite popular in Seattle, for good reason. Also, the team’s Japanese ownership, I gather, would never consent to trading him. These qualities, however, combined with declining skills create an obstacle. There are only three outfield positions, and the Mariners appear to have some real natural talent waiting to fill those slots. Should management conclude that the team can’t compete for the top rung, then it would seem appropriate to bring up the hitters from Tacoma to gain experience against major-league pitching.

But what about their hitting instructor, Chris Chambliss? Last year, during the team’s 101-loss season, the hitting coach was fired. Will Chambliss also be sacrificed? He could certainly hit as a long-time major leaguer, finishing with a career average of .279. (He hit a home run off me when he was at UCLA and I at Cal.) Hitting and coaching don’t always go together, however.

Charlie Lau is considered one of the best hitting instructors in major league history, yet his career average was only .255. One characteristic of the “Charlie Lau swing” is removing the top hand from the bat after contact with the ball. Ted Williams scoffed at this method, and the Splendid Splinter was not only one of the top five hitters to ever play the game he was perhaps the most earnest student of hitting, even after he’d long hung up his cleats.

I mention these two individuals to suggest that hitting has many theories and few good practitioners. Moreover, it does not follow that those who have excelled at the plate can succeed as instructors—or vice versa.

It’s obvious, though, that whatever Chambliss is telling and showing these young talents isn’t translating. One of the M’s’ problems is that they quickly fall behind in the count, letting the best pitches (which could be “mistakes”) go by for called strikes. Also, lacking plate discipline, the now-behind hitters easily fall victim to the down-and-out slider. How many times have we seen Olivo, or Peguero, or Gutierrez swing wildly at a pitch far off the plate or in the dirt?

As a former pitcher I can assure the reader how important it is to get ahead in the count. The fast-ball first-pitch strike is strategy number one. So why don’t the Mariner hitters realize this? Why do they let so many good pitches go? If Chambliss is telling his charges to be “more aggressive,” it isn’t sticking.

Enough of this. The M’s aren’t going anywhere. Let’s trade or sell the deadwood, keep the pitching, and bring up the bats from Tacoma. But I’d also recruit a different hitting coach. Sorry, Chris. Unfortunately, Charlie Lau died a long time ago. So did Ted Williams.

Maybe there’s someone down in Tacoma.

Playing in Arizona

July 12, 2011 by davald

Dave Zirin tells us that he will refuse to watch tonight’s All Star game on television. But if he lived within a day’s drive of Phoenix, he’d show up to protest. Why?

Well, unless you’ve been living under a rock during the last year or so you are aware of that state’s draconian anti-immigrant law, the one that’s currently being challenged in the courts. After the bill was signed into law by Arizona’s governor, Zirin tried to draw attention to the xenophobic racists of Arizona. Zirin:

Over the last year, civil rights organizations, politicians, sportswriters and baseball players have asked Major League Baseball Commissioner Bud Selig to move Tuesday’s 2011 All-Star Game out of Arizona. He chose not to listen and now I choose not to watch.

Some players—quite some time ago—said that they would refuse a trip to Phoenix as a statement of solidarity with the Hispanic population, the target of the anti-immigrant law. Among them were Adrian Gonzalez and Heath Bell. At the time of their announcement they were teammates in San Diego. Now Gonzalez is playing with Boston and leading the American League in batting. Both Gonzalez and Bell were selected for the All Star game. Yet, last night there was Gonzalez participating in the home run derby and Bell chumming it up with ESPN panelists.

Since the law’s enactment groups have filed legal challenges. Indeed, the US Department of Justice sought an injunction. Just before the law was to take effect, a court blocked its implementation. Arizona has been unable to overcome the court’s decision for the most part, although parts of the law are still in effect.

The players association initially condemned the law, but has since retreated. Gonzalez, perhaps the most outspoken of the players last year, has evidently changed his mind. A few civil rights groups have indicated that they will protest outside the stadium tonight.

In the months following Arizona’s law other states have passed even worse pieces of anti-immigrant legislation. Alabama, always known for its dedication to civil rights, has the most egregious such law in the land. Evidently that state’s legislators weren’t at all rattled by threatened boycotts.

Ugly in America.

The art and science of hitting

June 28, 2011 by davald

In all my years of playing baseball, from Babe Ruth League to Pac-8 (yes, I’m old), I managed to hit just one home run, and that was in the rarified air of Lake Tahoe as a semi-pro pitcher. I also hit a double at Berkeley’s Evans Field. It was a spitball at which I swung blindly and got lucky. I’ve never had a first base coach wave me to second before. Yet, despite my miseries at the plate, I think of myself as a student of hitting, discerning the better swings from the bad.

And the best place to view the bad is in front of my television set watching the Mariners.

Take last night’s game, for example. All of the M’s struck out at least once, totaling 13 for the evening. So many swings. So many misses. Among the wild swishing, however, were a few quality batting styles. Well, maybe just one.

That belongs to rookie Dustin Ackley. Even the amiably clueless Mike Blowers noticed the salient characteristic of Ackley’s swing: his bat stays in the zone longer.

You’ll appreciate that most batters, whether weak or powerful, possess magnificent uppercuts, with Adam Kennedy, Jack Cust, and Carlos Peguero having the worst. But Kennedy has been swinging long enough in the majors to develop a discerning eye and some plate discipline. He recorded just one hit against the Braves, but that was a homerun. An uppercut reduces the amount of time the bat is “in the zone” because of disparate trajectories: the pitch versus the swing.

A typical batter moves his lead foot a bit, then plants it firmly in the soil. He then rotates his hips followed by his upper torso. The swing, then, is completed against a stiff front leg. Where most batters go wrong is in the plane of their shoulders, swinging as if they were playing golf. The front shoulder flies up and away as the back shoulder dips far below.

Dustin Ackley, on the other hand, keeps his shoulders on roughly the same plane. He also does one other thing that helps him maintain a more level swing: he bends his front (right) knee rather than keeping it rigid as most players do.

The Splendid Splinter, otherwise known as Ted Williams, was the quintessential professor of hitting, studying and talking about it until the day he died. He reasoned that the proper swing should have a slight uppercut. Why? Because the flight of the pitched ball was slightly downward as gravity took its toll. Now let’s look at his swing:

We see that slight uppercut as his bat connects with the ball. But in his follow through we’ll note that the bat finishes on a level plane, as are his shoulders. There is no pronounced dip in his swing. He also keeps both hands on the bat from beginning to end.

I have no idea, of course, if batting coach Chris Chambliss is trying to get the Mariners to adopt a more level swing—in the manner of Ted Williams or Dustin Ackley. I do know that he could hit. He smacked a homerun off of me in the bottom of the 12th inning in a game between Cal and UCLA played at the old Veterans Field in LA.

Riggleman wriggles out

June 24, 2011 by davald

There are only so many major league manager positions—30, to be exact. Nor is holding one a path to job security; tenures are short, with rare exceptions.

Yesterday, former Mariners manager Jim Riggleman quit as skipper of the Washington Nationals, the hottest team in baseball, before its last game with the Mariners. He didn’t wait until after the Nationals had finished their sweep of the M’s.

He was pissed, especially so after the team’s upper management gave a five-year contract extension to general manager Mike Rizzo, who was Riggleman’s direct superior in the organization. Riggleman, with some justification, felt that he deserved the same kind of commitment that Rizzo received. He was tired of being the Nationals’ “interim” manager.

Now these things just don’t happen in baseball, or perhaps in any other major sport. There aren’t enough hands and fingers to count the number of people who aspire to have Riggleman’s job, whether or not interim. Riggleman, of all people, surely knows that, given his checkered history managing and coaching in the big leagues.

“Jim told me pregame today that if we wouldn’t pick up his option, then he wouldn’t get on the team bus today,” Rizzo said. “I felt that the time wasn’t right for me to pick up the option … You can’t make that decision in a knee-jerk reaction. It’s too big of a decision.”

Riggleman has been working on one-year deals since replacing Manny Acta in July 2009. He was being paid $600,000 this year and the Nationals held a team option for 2012 at $600,000.

Would you manage a baseball team for $600,000?

If Vargas can do it, why not Fister?

June 22, 2011 by davald

I delighted in watching the Mariners put runs on the board last night, starting with two in the first inning. Going into the ninth the M’s held a comfortable (commanding?) 5-to-1 lead.

Doug Fister had yielded but three hits and the one run, with fewer than 100 pitches. Yet, manager Eric Wedge, unlike with Vargas the game previous, decided to give closer Brandon League some work in the National’s last at bat. He recorded two outs and one run when former Mariner Mike Morse shot a line drive into League’s calf.

David Pauley replaced League, promptly giving up a single. Two men on and two outs, when rookie catcher Wilson Ramos crushed a three-run walk-off homer to sink the M’s.

In baseball’s inimitable vocabulary, Wedge pronounced that the team had been “kicked in the teeth,” then added that shit happens, or words to that effect. Did he regret not letting Fister complete the game, as Vargas had before him? Evidently not asked; therefore not answered.

League, by the way, is out indefinitely with a deep contusion.

I say, stop coddling the pitchers. If they’re doing well, let them continue, regardless of the damn pitch count.

Speaking of pitch counts, we can always rely on commentator Mike Blowers to tell us how many pitches have been tossed. Otherwise, he sticks with the obvious, adding little to overall viewer experience. Why can’t they clone the Giants’ broadcasters Kuiper and Krakow? They’re excellent. The best, in my book.

Patience, discipline, and strategy trump pure athleticism

June 13, 2011 by davald

Despite detesting most things Texan, with a particular grudge against the city of Dallas for hosting both the assassination of JFK and the Cowboys, I found myself rooting for the Mavericks in this NBA finals. Perhaps it was their irrepressible owner, Mark Cuban, who voted against moving the Sonics to the oil fields of Oklahoma. But I think my affection for Dirk and the two Jasons, long-suffering bridesmaids, sealed the deal.

As one commenter put it, the Mavs aren’t fast, they’re not quick, they’re not all that big, and they don’t jump. There are no superhuman LeBrons and D-Wades, who can turn every move into a YouTube highlight. But they win.

The world got to see a different kind of freak. Dirk Nowitzki. A German-born 7-footer with a deft shooting touch and a unique penchant for taking shots off one foot while leaning backwards. He cannot contort, explode, or jam. He simply makes baskets. Foul him at your peril, because free throws are automatic.

Jason Kidd, from Cal, I have to add, brings steadiness, superb court awareness, and tenacious, anticipatory defense—packaged in a 38-year-old body. Oh, and he can hit the three’s. Jason, the Jet, Terry, was born and raised in Seattle. He, too, can shoot, with a remarkable ability to pull up quickly for the jump shot. He scored 27 in last night’s game, leading all scorers.

So, one brief moment in time I cheered for a bunch of Texans. It’s not likely to happen again.

Less Figgieness

May 31, 2011 by davald

Despite the insouciant smile, Mariners’ third baseman Chone Figgins is no doubt churning inside. He’s got the numbers to prove it.

A typical at-bat:

  • first pitch fastball down the middle for a called strike
  • off-speed pitch for another called strike
  • a swing and a miss on either a fastball out of the zone or a breaking ball in the dirt.

This gets old.

But his few supporters tell us that he’s still the best glove at that position in the club’s organization. Yet, he muffed a sure double-play grounder yesterday, almost costing the M’s another game. Fortunately, the pitching, for the most part, has done phenomenally well.

The emerging star of the lineup is Brendan Ryan, known more for his glove and psychological quirks. He’s now hitting the ball with consistency, batting over .500 this past week. Mercifully, manager Eric Wedge dropped Figgins to the 8th slot, promoting Ryan to the second, Figgins’s normal place in the order.

I routinely study the Tacoma Rainiers’s box scores. Dustin Ackley is finally demonstrating his batting prowess, inching up toward the magical .300. I’ve not seen him play defense, but he’s a quick learner and hard worker, so I imagine it’s just a matter of some polishing before he’s called up.

Italian-born Alex Liddi has hit .300 for a season; he’s currently at .276 with 9 homeruns. Again, I can’t vouch for his fielding. Matt Mangini was sharing third-base duties with Liddi, but is now listed at first base, with Matt Tuiasosopo. Mangini hits about as well as Liddi.

Both Mike Carp and Josh Bard have batted over .300, with Carp leading the club with 14 round-trippers. While a first baseman by trade, Carp has been tried in the outfield, but is now listed as the DH. Bard, we know, is a capable catcher, having been with the parent club much of last year.

I sense that there will be some movement over the next few weeks. Jack Wilson can’t be satisfied with his part-time role at second base, since he’s used to playing shortstop full time. Will management try to trade him? What about Figgins, who has a very lucrative contract?

If I were the GM, I’d try the following:

  • send Michael Saunders back to the Rainiers so that he can learn how to hit; like Figgins, he lets too many good pitches go by and swings at the bad ones;
  • as high as I was on Carlos Peguero, he’s become a human helicopter at the plate, creating a mighty wind with each empty swing; I’d send him back down to learn the strike zone and, more important, to start his swing earlier without prematurely opening up his front shoulder;
  • bring Ackley up to share second base with Adam Kennedy, the off-season acquisition I truly loved, and I have not been disappointed; Wilson, as much as I liked his becoming an M, would benefit from new environs;
  • Figgins’s problems appear to be mental more than physical, like Milton Bradley before him; he, too, might welcome new settings, which would leave room for Liddi.

These past few games I had opportunity to view the Yankees at the plate. There’s a reason their games last longer than the rest. Almost all their hitters exercise plate discipline. And when they see a pitch to hit, they swing hard, though not overly so, to bash solid line drives or four-baggers. I would not want to face them. I’m hoping that the Mariner hitters are watching films of Cano, Teixeira, and Granderson.

On the other hand, the Mariners are playing extremely well of late, winning more than losing. They’re within striking distance of first place. They can afford to play host to Figgins and Wilson and Peguero and Saunders. They are solid up the middle, especially with the return of Gutierrez in center. I’ve always liked Miguel Olivo behind the plate. He doesn’t have to hit to be valuable (notice how he frames the close pitches to receive favorable calls from the umps), yet he’s contributed much to the M’s current offensive performance.

We’ll see. How nice it is to be a fan again.
