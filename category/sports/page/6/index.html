Speaking of legacies…

March 29, 2013 by davald

Via a Frank Deford piece on NPR the other day I was introduced to a Brittney Griner, who plays center for Baylor’s women’s basketball team. Deford said that she was the best female player in history. I then saw a couple of YouTube clips to see why. She dunks with ease, clearly dominating the game. In one of those clips arriving at the arena were none other than George and Laura Bush. They were cheered.

But why?

Matthew Yglesias comments on work done by Linda Bilmes of Harvard University. She estimates the combined costs of the Iraq and Afghanistan wars to be between four and six trillion dollars, making them “the most expensive wars in US history.” (Yglesias includes a link to Bilmes’s paper.) Oh, and most of those costs have yet to be paid.

Love all this conservative fiscal discipline. It’s all bullshit, of course.

Sacramento vs. Seattle and the exclusivity clause

March 1, 2013 by davald

I can fully appreciate Kevin Johnson’s urgent attempts to keep the Kings in Sacramento. After all, he’s the mayor of the capital city and a former NBA all-star, then known simply as “KJ.” So, he’s been scampering behind the scenes to woo deep pockets to mount a counter-offer to the Kings’ majority owners, the Maloof family.

However, I see a couple of almost insurmountable hurdles for Johnson. First, been there and done that. There have been several failed efforts to build a new arena in Sacramento. No dice, with the Maloofs evidently reneging on its initial commitment to put millions into a deal.

Second, and this may be the nail on the coffin lid, the purchase and sale agreement between Chris Hansen’s group and the Maloofs contains a non-compete clause. From the Seattle Times:

The [Sacremento] Bee further reported that the “Maloofs have an exclusive agreement with the Seattle group that prohibits them from entering into negotiations with anyone else offering a competing or counter offer.” Another offer could only be made if the sale to the Hansen-Ballmer group falls through.

In exchange for this provision, the Hansen group paid the Maloofs $30 million, none of it refundable.

The NBA owners will vote on the Hansen proposal this month. Given the checkered history of the Maloofs in Sacramento, combined with solid, well-financed agreements between Hansen, Seattle, and King County (including, of course, money for an arena south of Safeco Field), I’m betting that the Kings will be playing at Key Arena for the next couple of years until the new complex is completed.

An obligation to others

February 25, 2013 by davald

Dave Zirin interviewed Houston Rockets rookie Royce White. He sat out the first half of the season to protest the team’s treatment of his mental health issues, according to Zirin. He’s back playing now. But he’s surely not the typical athlete, given his outspoken views on the economic structure. For example:

Here’s the deal, we all understand the dynamic of the few having control of most of the resources and the money and power and it’s a brilliant system that they set up. And I take my hat off from an intellectual standpoint; the system in itself is so brilliant. But the reality is that for 98%, the quality of life that we endure is so tumultuous, and it’s so drama-filled, and the messages we receive are so drama-filled and so tumultuous that it’s no surprise to anybody in the medical world why it is that we’re experiencing what I believe to be a mental health epidemic. And, not to say that it’s just starting now, because it’s been in effect for a long time we just now are finding words to call it [what it is]. But it’s in full effect and it will continue to grow until we start to care for one another. I think that mental health represents one of the greatest examples of the need for obligation, because with mental health, you don’t just stay in your lane. Somebody else’s issues become your issues and you have to be more conscious of how you interact with someone else, as opposed to just thinking about yourself.

Though I’ve been on this planet for more than half a century, I do not often think about what some call “the system.” For my purposes here “the system” could serve as a proxy for what ails us. Its effects are pervasive and pernicious, making life difficult for the Rest of Us.

The other day it occurred to me that ubiquitous commercials, media content, our affection for gadgets—the broader culture—are subtle and not-so-subtle ingredients and manifestations of a “system” that works on behalf of “the few [who have] control of most of the resources and the money and power.” Participation in this culture requires means and a proper attitude.

As I’ve recounted many times on this blog, the gap between the Haves and Have-nots continues to widen, especially in America. Thus, even if we worship the ducat and all it bestows (the proper attitude) we cannot possibly expect to commingle with “the few.” We are shut out, by default, even as we are bombarded with the images and effects of the dominant culture.

Royce White speaks of our obligations to others. That attitude is conspicuously absent from the culture of the few. In that culture Darwin reigns supreme and loosely translates into “I am successful because I am fittest.” Therefore, the Rest of Us are failures, otherwise known as “moochers” and “takers,” depending on “the government” to redistribute the wealth of the few to the pockets of the many, all underserving.

Yet, just as the culture of the few is known by “ingredients and manifestations” so is the culture of the many. In both cases, the few define the terms.

If 10 people were selected from the Rest of Us and placed in a room to have a conversation, they might eventually get around to describing their conditions, their plights, and how they are poor and sick and food insecure. Some would share their difficulties obtaining work or holding on to a job through a series of layoffs. Others would report on the myriad obstacles preventing their getting from A to B on a daily basis. In general, I should imagine, the collective descriptions reveal despair and defeat. Against the dominant culture of the few they are failures, one and all.

The rich, if we’ve learned anything, deny that they have obligations to others. Moreover, whatever they have, they earned on their own. They are self-reliant, prudent, and persistent—characteristics of the proper attitude. It is thus easy for the few to define the Rest of Us by what we are not. Indeed, we are counter-cultural.

The imaginary group in the room describes absence. They lack self-reliance, prudence, and persistence. They are not healthy. They have little money. Sufficient food remains elusive. They have no heat in their houses nor quiet in the evenings. They are Have-nots.

What they do have in abundance is need, with no one obliged to address them. If we in the room feel an empathy-based obligation to others we lack the means to act. That’s because the means are in control of “the few.”

It would seem, then, that the trick is to put means in service to the many. Now how is that going to happen?

I don’t have ready answers, at least none that could be effective in the short term. Revolutions are so messy, and there’s no guarantee that what displaces “the system” would be any better than the status quo. I would suggest, however, that we start down whatever path by shedding the culture of the few.

First and foremost we might abandon the notion that our predicaments are our own damn fault. While we can appreciate that a single poor person can be blamed or his particular plight, how to explain that half the population are either poor or near-poor? Do over 150 million Americans struggle to make ends meet because they simply lack the right attitudes? How can a country’s leaders proclaim us Number One with so many so lacking? I suggest that there’s something else afoot.

If we still believe in the dignity of the individual person, regardless of circumstances, then their needs must be met, and those needs take precedence over the wants of the few. Since we live in scarcity (not all appetites can be sated), attending to the Have-nots necessarily demands redistribution of existing resources; we cannot grow our way to equality, or even approach it.

Yes, the rich will object. They always do. As I said, they generally feel no obligation to the Rest of Us. But if current trends persist, inequality will become so large as to create a very restless public, and that threatens the wealth and comfort of the few; the conditions wrought by heightening disparity may eventually render the few unfit for survival.

First and goal…

February 4, 2013 by davald

…from the seven-yard line, no less. A little run, then three errant passes to the same receiver. Nada. The game essentially over.

The problem with really close games, and yesterday’s Super Bowl was one of them, is that they invite lots of second-guessing and finger-pointing. Both activities fall under the counterfactual category. If (only) this had happened, then that would have occurred. If only Akers had kicked the ball higher, then Jones doesn’t tie the record for return touchdowns. If only the refs had called holding, then the Niners would have gotten another four downs, though not more precious time. If only Kaepernick had more games under his belt, then he would have made better decisions. We could go on, of course.

San Francisco was participating in its sixth Super Bowl, having won the five previous. One interesting stat: Kaepernick’s interception was the first by a 49er quarterback in the series. Guess which quarterback this year had the highest rating? Would you believe Alex Smith? If the Jets have any front-office talent left, they’ll snag him as fast as a Tebow arm flash.

My beloved Niners lost. I’m sure that they’ll be back, just as they overcame last year’s crushing defeat in the NFC championship game against the eventual Super Bowl winners. However, my allegiance has shifted north to the Seahawks. After all, I’ve lived in Puget Sound a lot longer than I lived in the Bay Area.

But, damn it. I hate to watch Super Bowls. The plays themselves can be measured in seconds. From kickoff to finale, the clock ticked off the better part of four hours. Man was my butt sore. My eyeballs didn’t fare much better. And can any company produce an interesting commercial. Why pay millions for lame? Several even grossed me out. Yuck.

Roiling NBA

February 1, 2013 by davald

The players’ union of the National Basketball Association has just canned its head, Billy Hunter. Details to follow. But I have some guesses, and they were precipitated by the recent trade of Rudy Gay from the Grizzlies to the Raptors.

In the pre-game show former NBA great Kenny “the Jet” Smith spoke disapprovingly of “financial-based” deals, of which the Gay trade seemed to be Exhibit Number One. Gay had been leading Memphis in scoring and was close to double digits in rebounds. However, he had signed a lucrative contract a year ago, making him a liability to the team’s new owner.

The collective bargaining agreement (CBA) negotiated during last year’s lock out includes some discomforting provisions, many having to do with the so-called ‘luxury tax.’ If a team’s payroll exceeds a cap set by the CBA that team will have to pay a penalty, the tax, to the league, which then redistributes some or most of the dollars to other teams, presumably to foster league parity.

Memphis is sitting in fourth place in the Western Conference, due in no small part to the performance of Gay. Without him or his replacements, who were getting “physicals,” the Grizzlies fell behind early to the Oklahoma City Thunder, who never looked back. Gay, at six-foot-eight, would have guarded Kevin Durant, the league’s top scorer. In his absence, Grizzlies coach Lionel Hollins designated a much smaller player to defend in Gay’s stead. It didn’t work, of course. Will Memphis be able to finish the season with a playoff berth? We’ll see.

During their pre-game chatter TNT’s resident experts, the aforementioned Smith along with Shaq and Chuck, predicted that no team would be able to retain a big three. The cap would force many if not all teams to jettison a franchise player. They had in mind Miami, with its highly paid and successful triumvirate of James, Wade, and Bosh. One will likely have to go as their contracts come up for renewal. In the alternative, a team’s owner would have to fork over millions of dollars in cap-related penalties.

I suspect that sooner rather than later the ramifications of the CBA, especially the luxury-tax rules, will manifest in reduced salaries, which would be anathema to the union. As likely, fewer teams will be able to hold on to marquee players; they may afford two but not three or more. If players prefer money over championships, they’ll go wherever the dollars take them. Or very wealthy owners who value rings will rule, electing to eat the tax in exchange for title opportunities.

There is, of course, another possibility, and not a bad one for us fans. The league’s elites will become more evenly distributed, making nearly every team competitive. It’s just that we can’t expect too many dynasties in the future.

And one more thing. The most valuable person in each organization will almost certainly be the general manager. Whoever can exploit the byzantine rules of the newest CBA may be able to write his own ticket.

The players, for good reason, believe that the NBA is their league, whether or not true. Hunter—and I’m only guessing, remember—may not have seen the fine print of the agreement, the effects of which are just now beginning to sink in. If he did and kept quiet, no wonder he’s out.

I wouldn’t try to take any of this to a bank. Idle speculation, all of this, which could turn out to be cow dung.

Looks like cow dung.

Speaking of Comcast

January 28, 2013 by davald

I am embarrassed to admit that I pay Comcast a small fortune each month. It didn’t happen all at once. My bill began relatively small, then by increments grew, so that I now write a check for nearly $300 a month. The majority of the bill is television-related. I seem to be paying far too much money for literally dozens of channels I never watch or ever will.

Matthew Yglesias reminds us that bundling is a good thing. If we were to go à la carte, we’d wind up paying even more, even though we turn to only a small number of channels for our regular viewing.

But I’d really like to pay less for my phone, cable, and Internet. I know that the French get all three for less than $50 a month. The South Koreans and Japanese also pay far less than we do, and they enjoy download speeds many times faster than mine, and I’ve got a very fast connection.

What’s going on? Here’s Yglesias:

What would change the game here isn’t unbundling, it’s competition. If a typical neighborhood was served by a dozen different cable providers, then consumers could get a much better deal. But that’s the problem with the telecom market—there’s very little competition, so you end up getting screwed. But the bundling isn’t what’s screwing you [my hyperlink].

Sonics return

January 23, 2013 by davald

Let’s face it. Things suck, a recurring theme on these pages, which I won’t recount here. So, what’s a person to do? One could try to start a movement for change, and good luck with that. Really. One could try to point out the obvious, a recent pastime of mine. Or you can get mad and angry and say that you’re not going to take it anymore.

There is a channel for relief, however. And that is sports. Why? Because they don’t mean a damn thing, unless you’re Dave Zirin, who reminds us on the pages of The Nation magazine that your favorite team is really just a business, a big one, certainly, and that owners of professional franchises are likely the same people who regularly stiff the Rest of Us in their everyday lives. But what’s the fun in pondering that?

Despite the economic doldrums, or maybe because of them, we embrace the ups and downs of our local Seahawks and Mariners, especially the former, a team that defied the odds with a brilliant young quarterback at the helm, delivering week after week of excitement and, more important, victories. We’re not used to that. Just look at the Mariners.

Soon, perhaps, we may add to our list a basketball franchise now known as the Sacramento Kings, formerly of Kansas City, Cincinnati, and Rochester. If the basketball gods, otherwise known as NBA owners, decide to approve the sale, that team will begin playing first at Key Arena, the stadium much maligned and fingered as the cause of the Sonics departure to Oklahoma City, where they became the Thunder, although I think their name should really have something to do with oil. After a couple of years and a few hurdles overcome the re-christened Sonics will play in Sodo, or “south of the dome,” although there is no longer any dome, since the Kingdome was blown up to make room for the Mariners and Seahawks. The new arena will have to wow the NBA board and the league’s napoleonic commissioner we call ‘Stern.’ As revealed, the architectural plans promise a world-class venue. Since it will be in Seattle, eco-friendly it must be.*

My wife asked an obvious question. If Chris Hansen, Steve Ballmer, et al. want a basketball team in Seattle, why don’t they just create one rather than buy an existing team? I gently replied that the NBA—like the NFL and MLB—is a cartel. The owners control the supply of teams, so as to extract ever-higher rents from media and fans. They artificially limit the number of franchises, with the blessing of Congress and the courts.

We are in the final stretch of a two-year process, with the requisite dots getting connected. But Sacramento mayor Kevin Johnson, one of my favorite NBA guards and a fellow Cal alumnus, vows to prevent the Kings from moving up I-5. He successfully thwarted a previous effort to relocate the franchise to Anaheim. Is he just trying to avoid embarrassment? After all, how would it look for a former NBA all-star to “lose” the city’s basketball team, its only major league band?

As for the cartel, I’m sure that its members would love to have a franchise back in Seattle and the Puget Sound, a major media market and home to some very wealthy people with sufficient bucks to support several major league teams. Now that a new arena appears close to being approved and constructed, the previous stumbling block (Key Arena, judged inferior and inadequate) evaporates.

For us sports fans, after the excitement of pilfering a franchise wears off and the first shovel is thrust into the dirt, we will confront a lousy basketball team in its current configuration. The original Sonics brought Seattle’s only major league championship, if we count only men. That was way back in 1979, before the Kings’ players were born. Sacramento has won just 16 games  against 26 losses. That means an awful lot of work, beginning with the front office. Its first challenge: How to maximize the considerable potential of DeMarcus Cousins, the team’s center?

Well. This could be fun. Or the deal falls through. But there are a lot of other bad teams out there with lousy attendance. Sacramento may be only one of several options available to Hansen and his group.

Nice distraction, though.

A few brain farts short of victory

January 14, 2013 by davald

Puget Sound came to a freezing halt yesterday morning. Between the hours of 10 and 1:30 p.m., sports fans and their suffering others sat before television screens anxiously riveted to the fortunes of the Seattle Seahawks. In the end, and I mean the very bitter end, hopes were dashed, quickly replaced by thoughts of next year.

Pete Carroll’s decisions during the game disqualify him for coach of the year, or anything else. His only claim to fame this season was selecting Russell Wilson as his starting quarterback, after the team signed Aaron Rodgers’s backup to a multi-million-dollar contract. Were it not for the rookie QB at the helm, the Seahawks may have been scrambling to win the cellar, a tough competition among two other hopefuls, St. Louis and Arizona.

As for the decisions, what, pray tell, was he thinking? Perhaps that was the problem. When careful thought was required, Carroll suffered brain farts. It’s fourth-and-one in the red zone. Behind by two scores, the coach elected to “go for it.” Except that his team wasn’t ready, with a mismatch at the line. So, rather than take the sure three points from a field goal, the Hawks ran the ball and actually lost a yard. The Falcons took possession then marched up field for a score, putting Seattle down 20-0. Oh, oh. Had Carroll done the right and obvious thing, the Seahawks, as it turns out, would have won the game, perhaps handily.

Then at the end of the first half, with his team trailing 27-7, Carroll took his last time out, denying the Hawks yet another opportunity to put points on the board. Into the locker room they went, much further behind than in last week’s game against the Redskins. But with Wilson, no opponent’s lead is safe.

I can only imagine what D.C.’s fans were going through in that second half, as different from the first as Bill Walsh is from Pete Carroll. Russell Wilson proved once again that he deserves the Rookie-of-the-Year award, if not also MVP. He marched and threw and ran, taking big chunks out of the Redskins’s margin. In our brief story, we are setting things up for one last brain fart.

Miraculously, the Seahawks score a touchdown, achieving a tie. The extra point is good. Seattle is ahead by a single point with only 31 seconds to go in the game! Victory in hand, right?

Let’s keep in mind that the Seattle defense sucked in the first half then held the Redskins to nada in the second. But that last brain fart is looming as time ticks away.

First Carroll elects to kick the ball deep, allowing a runback that consumed almost 30 yards in just six seconds. A completed pass puts the Redskins into Seattle territory. On their last play from scrimmage, the Redskins would throw to the most obvious man on earth. That would be fellow former-Bear Tony Gonzalez, the planet’s best tight end—ever. He caught the ball, against a linebacker! No contest; a poor coaching decision not to have a safety also covering the future Hall-of-Fame inductee. Time out.

Out trots the Redskins’s place kicker. Now we witness the last episode of mental flatulence. Carroll would choose to call a timeout himself. You know, to “ice” the kicker. Silly stuff. The ball was snapped anyway, and, guess what? The ensuing kick sailed wide right. But Washington gets a do-over. The next try would arch its way through the middle of the goal posts. Game over. The Seahawks lose 30-28.

Damn.
