The jump

October 20, 2011 by davald

Last time I wrote about my mediocre early days playing baseball, beginning in elementary school and carrying forward through Babe Ruth League. Despite floundering as a budding teenager, I nevertheless made the local American Legion team. I did not let my initial failures cloud my enthusiasm for baseball in general and pitching in particular. It helped, of course, that I grew more than half a foot between the ages of 14 and 16. I could now throw the ball hard enough to strike out just about any one I faced.

Yet, I did suffer a setback, owing more to unwarranted cockiness than a momentary decline in physical ability. Coming off a splendid summer season playing American Legion, I felt that, as a junior, I was a shoo-in for the varsity high school team. I got lazy. Following a series of absolutely lousy pre-season starts, Coach Bordi, who had played on the Cal baseball team just a few years before, confronted me. I don’t remember what he said, other than that he was sending me down to junior varsity, where I had played as a sophomore. That demotion, dear reader, immediately changed my attitude—and for the better.

Humiliated, I was determined to work my way back to varsity as soon as possible. And did I work. I put in extra running, pushups, sit-ups, and developed a laser-like concentration on the mound. The investment paid out handsome dividends. Every game yielded nearly the same results: shutouts and double-digit K’s. However, Bordi did not relent, keeping me on the JV team far too long for my tastes. Finally, with the season nearing its close, I was invited back to the varsity squad. I picked up where I had left off in JV, winning all three of my outings.

I must have been impressive, since I was recruited to join Concord’s Fiztpatrick Colts semi-pro team. Just the year or so before my arrival, both Willie Stargell and Joe Morgan had played for the team. The competition was keen and the talent as good as it gets for high school and college athletes. I did well, though I was only 17 then. The scouts must have noticed, as I was selected to play winter ball in the Peninsula League for a team sponsored by the Cleveland Indians.

Our games were played in San Francisco, San Jose, Redwood City, and other Bay Area cities I can’t recall. Our coach was the regional scout for the Indians. Other teams were sponsored by the Giants, the Yankees, and so on. Again, the players were mostly college level along with a few like me still in high school. Our uniforms were hand-me-downs from the major league teams.

I was picked to start the first game for us. Second in the rotation was Dick Tidrow, who would go on to pitch in the majors and earn the nickname “Dirt,” for his swarthy complexion; he could do shaving commercials as a teenager. He’s now with the S.F. Giants front office. Unfortunately, I threw out my back in the middle of the game. I would not pitch again for that team, the injury was that severe and prolonged.

Fortunately I recovered in time for the next high school season. I vowed not to let myself become overconfident. I would take nothing for granted. In the preseason I tossed a no-hitter.

The next week I took the mound again. I had a no-hitter through two outs in the last inning. I threw a curve ball to the batter on a two-strike count. He swung and missed, for strike three. However, our catcher was unable to block the ball as it hit the ground in front of him then shot between his legs to the backstop a few feet behind him. The batter, of course, realized what had happened and headed for first base. But the catcher, with the ball ricocheting back to him, threw the ball far over our first baseman’s head. The batter was safe. I could still notch a consecutive no-hitter. I just had to get the next guy out. Well, he lined a clean single to left. In disgust I slammed my glove into the dirt, which brought an angry Coach Bordi to the mound. He was quite miffed at my behavior. I got out of the inning and wound up with a one-hit shutout, but next day’s newspaper headlines made a big deal out of the mitt-tossing.

Speaking of catchers, in my next installment of “Glory Days,” I’ll tell you about the best catcher I have ever seen—better than any in the majors, including, I dare say, Johnny Bench and Pudge Rodriguez. How can I say this? Stay tuned.

As promised…

October 12, 2011 by davald

I didn’t make Little League. I waited until my last year of eligibility because I felt unqualified. That is, I sucked.

I should have known this while playing inter-mural baseball at Concord’s Hillcrest Elementary, now defunct. Our coach, who doubled as a fifth-grade teacher and P.E. instructor, put me in right field, that area of the diamond reserved for the worst player on the team. I fit the part. As I recall I went the entire abbreviated season without getting a hit. Indeed, I don’t think I even made contact, with two exceptions.

We were playing another school, which housed a former classmate on whom all boys had a crush. The coach, no doubt as frustrated with my ineptitude as I, suggested that I choke up on the bat—really choke up. As I strode to the plate my hands were glued halfway up the wooden stick. The pitcher threw. I swung mightily.

I had never felt that sensation before, contact between a bat and a ball. And did I make contact. The ball soared far off into the distance. Only problem, it was foul by a fairway’s width. But that majestic fly ball brought oohs and ahs from the sparse crowd of parents and siblings. And yes, the former classmate was sitting in the stands. On the next pitch, same result and more crowd buzz. The third pitch induced a whiff. Now that was familiar.

Despite these obvious shortcomings, my life in baseball was just beginning. I tried out for Babe Ruth, the league sandwiched between Little League and American Legion. Something must have happened to change my fortunes. I may have gotten a bit taller or stronger. The next-door lady, a former softball player in her own right, had told me that she could no longer catch me; I was throwing too hard. Hmm.

Although I made one of the Babe Ruth teams, my performance declined from the first season to the third. I pitched mostly, with one disastrous stint at second base, where I managed in one game to either miss everything hit to me or when I accidentally caught a ground ball threw it several feet over the first baseman’s glove. I knew then that if I was to continue playing baseball it would have to be as a pitcher.

After Babe Ruth I continued sprouting upwards; we had the markings on the wall to prove it. I tried out for American Legion. Surprise. I made it. Here was real talent, almost everyone on that team went on to play college ball.

I’ll share only one recollection from those Legion days because, well, it features me. We had made the playoffs and were slated to face our nemeses from Richmond. Coach Snider gave me the ball, signaling that I would be the starter. Before I took the mound, he warned me not to throw the ball over the outside of the plate to the opponent’s cleanup hitter.

I often had trouble getting through the first couple of innings. Nerves? Struggle to find release point? I recall two of the first three batters reaching base. Then up comes Mr. Cleanup. Huge, with a neck as big as his thighs. Well, you can guess what happened. I tossed one over the outside of the plate. I’ve never seen a ball travel so far and so quickly. It not only left the yard. It sailed over the Concord Armory just beyond the fence. Of course, this prompted a rather hasty visit from the coach, who spat out something like, “What did I tell you!” But he didn’t pull me.

For reasons I can’t explain, I went on to shut Richmond out the rest of the game, which we won 15 to 3. I recorded 16 strikeouts and headlines in the next day’s local paper.

Cool.

I’ll continue these “glory days” in future installments.

Nolan Ryan and me

October 11, 2011 by davald

The cameras provided several shots of an earnest, fretting Nolan Ryan, sitting in the front row with his wife and, at least through the first game of the American League Championship Series, Laura Bush accompanied by her husband. You know, what’s his name.

At any rate, I thought of announcing a reunion. This reunion would re-unite Nolan and me.

You see, in 1965 both of us had completed our high school careers. Baseball held its first draft that year. The Mets drafted Nolan Ryan and, yes, me.

Of course, that’s about all that we have in common. He went on to set the major league record for strikeouts and no-hitters. He now serves as the Rangers president and co-owner. He’s in the Hall of Fame.

As for me, I’m writing this.

By the way, Nolan was drafted in the 12th round, overall the 295th player selected. I was a bit further back, number 762 in the 35th round. You can see a list of the Mets’ first draft here.

Ichiro

September 28, 2011 by davald

The New York Times has a nice piece on Ichiro. His streak of 200-hit seasons has ended, unless he can somehow manage to get 16 hits in tonight’s last game.

It doesn’t seem that long ago that he joined the Mariners for their one miracle year, in which they tied the record for most wins in a season—116. Ichiro batted .350, racking up 242 hits, mostly singles—stats that earned both the Rookie of the Year and MVP awards.  This year, his eleventh, finds his average down to .272, the first time he’s hit below .300.

Ichiro will be 38 next year, yet he’s stolen 40 bases. He keeps himself fit, and he still has remarkable flexibility and durability. Could he turn things around next year? Probably.

But one thing is certain. I seriously doubt that Ichiro will become any team’s batting coach. His approach at the plate defies baseball wisdom, from Ted Williams to Charlie Lau. No young player should or would try to copy Ichiro’s swing. It’s better suited for cricket than baseball.

The problem with Timmy

September 21, 2011 by davald

As a life-long Giants fan and now a resident of Puget Sound I followed with much interest the young career of ex-Husky Tim Lincecum, the quirky phenom from Renton. How could such a slight frame produce such velocity?

After earning two consecutive Cy Young awards, Lincecum’s performances have declined; and he’s still only 27 years old. In those two miracle years Lincecum routinely threw his fastballs in the mid- to high-90s. Last night against the Dodgers, a game he lost, his velocity was in the low-90s, rarely reaching 92 mph. (Note: I did not watch the entire game.) He pitched seven innings, gave up eight hits, including a solo home run, walked three and struck out five. It’s the last two numbers that concern me.

Let’s look at Lincecum’s strikeout-to-walk ration, beginning in his first full year in the majors.

Notice the trend. It’s downward, and steadily so. His walks per nine-innings have increased, while his strikeouts per game have fallen.

When I watch Lincecum these days his pitches seem to be all over the place, evincing a lack of concentration. When he misses the plate, he does so badly. His motion, which engendered awe, has now become overly jerky; lots of wasted energy. He acknowledged as much several weeks ago when he temporarily abandoned his windup in favor of the stretch, even with no runners on base, in an attempt to gain more balance in his delivery.

When Lincecum was racking up all those strikeouts (he led the league for three consecutive years) he did so with a mid-90s fastball thrown more proximate to the plate, which, I think, induced more swings at his splitter/changeup, a pitch he started at the middle of the strike zone but by the time the batter had started his swing, the ball darted down and either to the left or to the right, depending on the pressure he applied to the baseball. Now, because his just-average fastball often ends up a foot or more out of the zone, hitters ignore his off-speed pitches. At least that’s my take.

Wikipedia describes Lincecum’s pitch repertoire:

Lincecum throws a four seam fastball at 91–94 mph, but mostly uses a two seam fastball grip which he throws around 90–93 mph for more sinking movement to get more ground balls. This pitch has little lateral movement, due to his overhand delivery and the speed at which the pitch is thrown. He has a big breaking curveball that is thrown at a range of 77–81 mph and breaks away from a right-handed hitter. Lincecum uses a changeup that he grips similar to a splitter with sinking two-seam action. His changeup appears similar to his fastball for the first 30 feet, but then dives down sharply tailing away from a left handed batter, with a difference of 10 mph or more in velocity from his fastball (82–85 mph). The majority of his strikeouts are recorded with this pitch. Lincecum also has a hard slider that breaks down and away from a right-handed hitter at a speed slightly higher than his changeup (84–88 mph). With his power fastball and strong secondary pitches, he has established himself as one of the elite pitchers in the game.

Now, Lincecum doesn’t need to throw his fastball in the mid- to upper-90s to be effective. But he does need to regain better control of his pitches. And by control I don’t mean merely throwing strikes. I’m talking Maddux-like control, alternating both the horizontal and vertical planes.

With the Giants’ playoff chances dwindling (they’re five-and-a-half games behind the leading Diamondbacks and four-and-a-half back in the wildcard race with eight games to go) I offer a suggestion to Lincecum. During the off-season adjust that once-magical delivery to achieve greater physical balance and, thus, more control over your pitches. Also, train your body to throw all your pitches precisely where you want them to go. And the only way to train the body is through repetition.

Actually, that unsolicited advice applies to all pitchers. Attention, Mariners.

That extra twist

September 15, 2011 by davald

The Mariners’ Jason Vargas pitched a gem last night. It was his second superb outing in a row, after a string of big-time losses. What’s the difference?

Well, it seems that Vargas has twisted his torso a bit more toward second base, pausing a bit, then driving toward the plate. His fast ball has picked up some velocity as a result, and it appears that batters are having a more difficult time picking up the ball. Combined with excellent location of his pitches, Vargas has actually dominated the hitters—for two games, anyway.

I can relate to the twist.

I got the nod many, many years ago to face Santa Clara at Cal’s Evans Field. Santa Clara always had a good baseball team, stocked with hitting and pitching. The afternoon lacked an auspicious beginning. No matter what I threw it was shellacked. Fortunately, the line drives were either foul or right at Cal fielders.

Now I had a rather bad habit of experimenting with my delivery, often depending on what pitcher I had watched on TV. On that afternoon, realizing that nothing was working, it occurred to me in the third inning, as I recall, to adopt the motion of a pitching mate, who happened to be left handed, just like Vargas. Mike Baldwin, the teammate, had an exaggerated twist and pause, which yielded remarkable results. Why not copy?

Remember, this is Berkeley. I was immediately transported into “the zone” after aping Baldwin’s delivery. I went from whack to whiff. Santa Clara could no longer hit my stuff. Fast ball? Swing-and-a-miss. Curve ball? Forget about it.

Perhaps it’s like fishing reports, but I believe that I completed the game with a one-hit shutout. I doubt that there are any extant records to discredit my recollection. You’ll just have to trust me on this.

The Mariners’ bizarre dealings with Wilson

September 1, 2011 by davald

Yesterday the M’s traded Jack Wilson to the Atlanta Braves. His short stint with Seattle must have been as frustrating to him as it was to management.

He was hitting only .249, the same average he had last year for the Mariners. Despite having been an All-Star shortstop with the 2004 Pirates, Wilson was told at the beginning of the year to play second base—if he wanted to play at all.

It seems that after he entered Eric Wedge’s dog house several months ago (Wilson took himself out of a game for fielding errors) he never escaped. Then there were the injuries, the latest being a bruised heel sustained by landing on first base too hard—I guess.

At any rate, Wilson lost his starting job to rookie Dustin Ackley. Now he will get a new opportunity with an organization used to success.

By the way, this is what GM Jack Zduriencik had to say upon acquiring Wilson a couple of years ago:

“What we acquired here is a very talented defensive shortstop,” Mariners general manager Jack Zduriencik said of Wilson. “He set several records of error-less games. He’s a feisty player. I think you’re going to like watching this guy play.

“In my conversation with him, I thought he’d be a little disappointed in leaving an organization that he basically grew up in … but he was very excited. He was like, ‘Wow, I know you guys are in a pennant race. This is exciting.’ I told him how much we wanted him.”

Evidently, Wilson didn’t feel the love. Oh, and we long since stopped talking about pennants around here.

A few comments about pitching

August 23, 2011 by davald

In one of my previous lives I pitched baseballs. And when I wasn’t physically throwing a baseball I was practicing before a large mirror we had in the living room. I’d also watch as many baseball games as possible, paying particular attention to the style and delivery of the pitchers. For me, baseball was all about the contest between the batter and the pitcher, and I always rooted for the latter. Even now, when I’m watching games on TV, I study the pitcher.

I just got around to finishing Michael Lewis’s Moneyball, wherein he chronicles the application of statistics to the evaluation of baseball players. He focused on the Oakland Athletics, who played ball just a short drive from his Berkeley home. His fascinating book is all about the A’s’ general manager, Billy Bean, and his entourage of nerds. Their joint task: locate the diamonds in the rough; players that contributed more to the outcome of games than would be obvious to even seasoned veterans. It was all in the numbers.

On offense, the goal is to score the most runs. The defense, of course, is supposed to allow the fewest runs. So, what metrics play the most important roles in accomplishing both objectives? That’s what Billy and his small team of number-crunchers figured out. Unlike the huge markets of the Yankees and the Red Sox, who could afford to pay lavish salaries for the best conventionally evaluated talent, the A’s were a rather sorry bunch. Attendance was always mediocre, even during the string of three consecutive world championships. Bean et al. had to produce a winning ball club with a very limited payroll.

The players Bean assembled on the field would not impress. There were no obvious stars like a Barry Bonds or a Greg Maddux. They may not have been fast afoot, powerful at the plate, or possessed of a blazing fastball. But as a team, they were collective overachievers. Bean managed to win ball games, despite a lack of superstars and a New York bank account.

Since I’m most interested in pitching, I wondered what would be the key performance metrics by which to assess a pitcher’s ability. The usual stats are ERA (earned run average), wins-vs-losses, and, more recently, walks and hits per inning pitched (WHIP). But there are confounding variables that influence these measurements. Can the defense catch and throw the baseball? As we Mariner fans know, winning games depends a great deal on how well your offense can score runs. (To their credit, the folks responsible for selecting the Cy Young Award winner looked beyond this metric to give the prize last year to Felix Hernandez.)

It seems to me that there are three stats over which the pitcher has the most control. These are: walks, home runs, and strike outs. If a batted ball clears the fence, the defense could not have prevented it. Even factoring in the variations in umpires’ strike zones, over a season such preferences would tend to be neutralized. Thus, bases on balls and strike outs were mostly about the pitcher.

I came up with a simple formula. Opponents score runs only after rounding the bases then touching the plate. A walk puts a batter a fourth of the way to home. But a home run puts a number on the board. It’s worth four walks, then. If the pitcher strikes out a batter, that out is all about the pitcher; he’s prevented the hitter from reaching base while racking up one of the three outs necessary to complete an inning.

As it happens, Major League Baseball provides us with a trove of statistics on its website. I copied a page of stats to a spreadsheet, then added one more column to reflect the aforementioned formula.* I then sorted the pitchers by this last statistic. (Note: I did this a couple of weeks ago.)

We should not be surprised to see Roy Halladay at the top of the list. When I performed the calculation, he had tossed 175+ innings and given up only nine home runs while walking just 22 and striking out 163. I was surprised, however, to see Brandon Morrow in fifth place. His record has been spotty to date, with a high ERA. But as we look more closely at the key metrics, we see that he’d allowed just nine home runs while striking out 139 in just under 120 innings pitched.

There are some diamonds in this list, Morrow one of them, as it turns out. They do not command, yet, the wallets of Halladay, Lee, and Sabathia.

Billy Bean, at least in the Moneyball years, acquired these statistical anomalies and paid them low salaries. When their contracts were due to expire and others noticed how well they were doing, Bean would trade or sell them—rather than pay them their new market-based wages. That was by default, I guess, since he never had the money.

*  WHRSOIP for [BB+ (4*HR)-SO]/IP

We interrupt this program

August 7, 2011 by davald

Ok. Life is not all doom and gloom. Tough to say that, though.*

For a welcome respite, visit Ken Levine’s blog. Well written, he occasionally does radio for the Mariners, and injects humor, which is in short supply these days. Unless, of course, you can ape George Carlin, in which case all this crazy shit is just a long-running play in the Theater of the Absurd. I especially liked this post on a recent visit to Seattle. A sample:

Missed by one day the streaker who ran across the outfield during a game (and it wasn’t even “70s Night”). He spent two nights in jail, faces multiple charges, and a scout from the Pittsburgh Pirates, impressed with his speed, wants to sign him. Actually, there were several cretins who ran out onto the field that night. One was a military man (now spending the rest of his life cleaning urinals with a toothbrush), and the other buffoon was scheduled to get married the next day. “Uh, honey, any chance we could move the venue from your church to the city drunk tank?”

Going for the new record

July 27, 2011 by davald

The M’s are at 17 and still diligently pursuing both the American League and Major League records for most consecutive losses in a season. The former is held by the 1988 Baltimore Orioles (21); the latter by the really hapless 1961 Philadelphia Phillies (23). Only four to go to match the Orioles. Tying the Phillies is a long shot, but I have faith in the M’s.
