Up and down

August 16, 2012 by davald

King Felix (Hernandez) tossed a perfect game, escaping a long drive to deep right center field, snared awkwardly by the M’s Eric Thames, a recent acquisition. Mix in a couple of above-average fielding stops, and Mr. Hernandez did the rest, striking out a dozen.  Imagine: 27 batters up and 27 batters down. Perfect, or as the Seattle Times headlined it, “Pitcher Perfect.” All this couldn’t happen to a nicer guy, who has stated frequently and unequivocally that he wishes to stay in Seattle surrounded by family and teammates. Novel.

About 900 miles down the road in San Francisco my beloved Giants caught a punch in their collective gut. All-Star MVP Melky Cabrera was suspended immediately for 50 games, effectively the rest of the year. He tested positive for steroids. Great. Now that’s a big hole for the orange and black, as Cabrera was hitting well over .300 and leading the National League in hits.

Tyler Kepner, writing for the New York Times, was none too kind:

Nobody truly knows if Cabrera is an All-Star in peak physical condition, or the doughy bodied, rather ordinary player he was for the Yankees and the Atlanta Braves.

Ouch.

Meanwhile, “The Freak” lost another, yielding home runs on terribly fat pitches delivered center cut, as they say. Either figure out a way to throw the ball in the upper-90s again or get serious about controlling your pitches.

About that baseball experiment

July 14, 2012 by davald

A few posts ago I proposed that major league baseball conduct an experiment, beginning in spring training. Dispense with umpires and rely exclusively on television cameras, video replay (including slo-mo), and computer technology to instantly “call” balls and strikes and every other play on the field requiring judgment. Why? Because the umpires’ failures threaten to undermine the sport’s integrity.

Case in point. Last night the Mariners had fallen behind the Texas Rangers 3-0. The M’s had eked out only four hits and had posed no threat of scoring the entire game. We enter the bottom of the ninth with Seattle at bat. A ground ball dribbles through the infield. Then another. A run scores, making it 3-1. The Mariners eventually load the bases against All-Star closer Joe Nathan. There’s only one out. Michael Saunders hits another grounder. This one takes a bad hop then bounces off the glove of Texas first baseman Michael Young. The ball squirts up the right field foul line. One runner scores. It’s now 3-2. Justin Smoak, who had been on second when the play started, could easily have scored. But third base coach Jeff Datz, the tallest man on the field, couldn’t see just how far Saunders’s ball had traveled away from Young. So he holds up Smoak. Saunders, assuming that Smoak surely would have scored, rounded first to head for second. But Jaso, who had started at first when Saunders hit the ball, had to hold up at second, because Smoak had stopped at third. Saunders is caught in a pickle and tagged out. Two outs.

Up steps to the place the struggling Dustin Ackley. Well, “struggling” doesn’t quite describe his offensive decline. He’s on a trajectory to drop below the Mendoza line and join the ranks of Brendan Ryan and Chone Figgins, who sport embarrassingly low averages. He’s got the tying run at third and the winning run at second—again, with two outs. First base is open, but why put Ackley on? Why, indeed?

The Rangers elect to pitch to the Mariners’ second baseman. In his previous three at-bats he’s connected. One was a deep fly ball to center field, which was caught near the fence. But no hits, per usual.

Nathan throws a fastball a good four inches off the plate. The umpire—did I say umpire?—calls it a strike! The second pitch, only a slight bit closer to the zone, is also called a strike. Now Ackley is in the worst possible count, 0-and-2. He fouls off the next pitch. Then I think he took one or more pitches for a ball.

As a former pitcher I’ve come to appreciate batters’ weaknesses. Almost all of them have “sweet” spots, which the pitchers should avoid. Most young players, though, have more weak than sweet spots, and the former are quickly identified by the opposition. This is especially true in Ackley’s case.

Ackley, for those who haven’t watched him play, stands completely erect in the batter’s box with the bat held high. His stance is decidedly open, with his front foot (right) a foot or more behind his back foot. As the ball approaches, Ackley strides quite a distance toward the pitcher, partially closing his stance. As a result, his eye level drops significantly between start to finish. Given that the pitch is on a downward trajectory, Ackley’s eye-level change puts him at a disadvantage before he even makes contact.

This year I’ve noticed a loop in Ackley’s swing. Last season, when he hit a respectable .273, Ackley stayed longer in a horizontal plane, notwithstanding the eye-level drop. As a result, he hit a lot of line drives, spraying the ball to all fields. In 2012, the looped swing yields more fly balls and pop-ups.

One more thing. Ackley doesn’t follow the pitch “into the catcher’s glove.” I put quotes around that phrase because it’s physically impossible to follow a ball’s entire flight. A batter can’t see the ball hit the bat, nor can he see it smash into the catcher’s mitt. However, a good hitter will try, nevertheless; and the longer a hitter can keep his eyes on the incoming pitch, the better he can “see the ball,” and the more accurate he can be making contact. In Ackley’s case, it’s as if he stops looking at the ball long before it reaches the plate. (Chone Figgins is an exception. He watches nearly every pitch as long as he can. One problem. A batter can’t get a hit without swinging the bat.)

So, Ackley is behind in the count with “ducks on the pond.” To be sure, most left-handed power hitters have a loop in their swings. Their favorite spot is low and inside. But because of Ackley’s poor combination of erect stance and eye-level drop, his vulnerable areas include low and inside—among other spots, especially the high fastball. What will Nathan throw?

As I watched last night, feeling the tension that could end with a walk-off winning hit, I had this sense of dread that Ackley was going to strike out. And as a former pitcher I guessed that the next pitch from Nathan would be low and inside. Since the Rangers’ closer has a nasty slider, I guessed that too.

Sure enough. Here comes that pitch, and Ackley swings and misses. It was not even close.

Like many M’s fans (well, I guess there couldn’t be that many), I had hoped for some shake-up changes during the All-Star break. I thought that Ackley and others needed to work on fixing their weaknesses. Perhaps there would be a trade or two. I was kidding myself. This organization is strange.

That strangeness was exhibited by manager Eric Wedge’s comments on Thursday, the day before the second half commenced. Instead of calling his players “dumb,” as he did not too long ago, or threatening wholesale changes, including numerous demotions, his tenor abruptly changed. In so many words, he announced that things would become more relaxed and less tense. And, we’re going to stick with the current crop. (This may be tantamount to saying that the organization has raised the white flag, surrendering this season to the opposition, as they’ve done so many times before.)

Using Einstein’s definition, the club is also collectively insane. The Mariner players exhibited mediocrity writ large for more than half a season. Surely that’s enough time to manifest one’s talents. If Ackley et al. haven’t figured things out after 300 at-bats, why believe that another 300 will produce success?

I’m inclined to the view that it’s altogether possible, if not likely, that these guys just don’t have the natural skills to perform well in the bigs. Management can keep pushing them onto the field game after game, but there’s absolutely no reason to suspect a turnaround; we’ll see more of the same.

On the other hand, there is much evidence to suggest that Seattle is baseball’s incubator. Because the club finishes near or at the bottom at every year’s end, it drafts high. Those presumably talented players they select then make their way through the M’s system, which evidently sports better-than-average coaches who know how to hone skills. At some point in the players’ development, whether or not they are called up to the majors, M’s management sells or trades the incubated talent. From the Seattle club, many such players then blossom into unquestionable stars. Here’s a brief list of those who “got away,” deliberately so:

  • Adam Jones (All-Star centerfielder for the Baltimore Orioles, traded in exchange for Eric Bedard, now with Pittsburgh)
  • Mike Morse (All-Star outfielder for the Washington Nationals, traded for Ryan Langerhans)
  • Doug Fister (he threw a two-hitter last night for Detroit; the M’s got Caspar Wells, who may prove to be a find, and Charlie Furbush, a niche left-handed pitcher who’s done well so far)
  • Bryan Lahair (he’s now an All-Star first baseman for the Chicago Cubs; the M’s gave up on him)
  • David Ortiz (you know all about him)

What each of the above players have in common is that they became stars only after leaving the Mariners. Now, did the Mariners’ brass have some idea that these players had enormous potential? If they did, is it possible that the club’s owners desired to dispatch them because they refused to pay them what they would eventually demand? Also, had they let their talents unfold on the parent club, trading them away would have repulsed what few fans the Mariners have, though it’s bad enough to see these players succeed elsewhere. If they didn’t recognize the talent and potential, then they’re incompetent, in which case they should join the other 13 million unemployed Americans.

I hope that I’m wrong on both possibilities and that this bunch of ne’er-do-wells defies history and actually rekindles the Spirt of 2001, when the Mariners tied the major league record for most wins in a season. That was the time of Sweet Lou and Edgar and Boonie and…well, the list goes on.

But back to that experiment. What might have happened to Ackley had the umpire made the correct calls? He would have been ahead at 2-0, the batter’s best count. His odds of being successful then rise dramatically.

We’ll never know, of course. The umpire blew it. We at home watching on TV knew that he blew it, because we had “the tracer” to see exactly where Nathan’s pitches landed.

So, why do we rely on gross imperfection to decide the fates of games and players and teams? Why, indeed?

Instant replay and baseball

July 11, 2012 by davald

Baseball resists calls for modernization. The purists proclaim the integrity and history of the game. Introducing technology to the sport will only degrade the experience. So, the MLB has only gradually acknowledged the utility of instant replay, allowing umpires beginning three years ago to review tapes to determine whether or not a ball left the park or hit the wall. Even that limited use of technology amounts to a painful interruption of play, since the squad of umpires repairs to some room under the stands to see what really happened.

I’ve been around baseball for a very long time, watching the few televised games in my youth, then playing at ascending levels through college. I, too, appreciate its rich history of talent, records, and statistics.

Yet, I feel the game is under threat by the league’s reticence to confirm or refute umpire calls, from balls and strikes to balks and steals. We television viewers know what really happens on the field, because we can see every pitch and every play, both live and by instant replay, including “slo-mo” video.

With that technology comes embarrassing exposure of human failings. Umpires blow calls, sometimes spectacularly so. And those calls can mean the difference between becoming world champions or the losing team.

The New York Times as weighed in a bit. Its article cites one of those blown calls in the 1985 World Series. The Kansas City Royals beat the St. Louis Cardinals in seven games, but only after an umpire got it wrong. In this case, the umpire was Don Denkinger.

Denkinger was the first-base umpire for Game 6 of the 1985 World Series between the Royals and the St. Louis Cardinals. He missed a crucial call at first base that sparked a ninth-inning comeback for the Royals, who won the championship the next night. It was probably the most significant missed call in baseball history.

Every person watching television that game knew that the call was blown. But not the umpires, who are required to make instant calls on split-second events. They are human. All too human.

So, I propose the following. It will surely rankle the traditionalists.

We’ll start with a spring-training game. There will be no umpires on the field. Instead, all actions on the field will be immediately captured by multiple television cameras. All balls and strikes will be “called” in accordance with pitch trackers, which are able to record speed, rotation, and, most important, location.

Every play on the diamond will be instantly “called” by televisions-cum-computers. I emphasize ‘instantly.’ There is no need for deliberation, second thoughts, video review. Just as we television viewers “know” what’s going on the moment it happens, and are granted several follow-up replays to make sure, the players and benches and fans in the seats will “know” as well.

The human element, which is merely a euphemism for getting things wrong from time to time, will be eliminated. All focus will be on the players and their competition.

Despite resistance to such a proposal, I believe it inevitable.

Why sports?

July 8, 2012 by davald

The linguist-philosopher-political observer Noam Chomsky has talked about his days in high school where he was expected to attend football games. At some point, probably rather quickly, he concluded: what’s the point? Why root for one’s home team and against the opponent? After all, he had no idea who was on the team, nor did he have any interest in either the plays or the outcome. So he stopped attending, returning only once to a sporting event; he accompanied his grandson to Fenway.

I, on the other hand, seem to have a preternatural affinity for sports, though not all of them. In rank, I place baseball at the top, followed by basketball and football. Mark Twain had it right about golf: a waste of a splendid walk. Soccer and hockey are 99 percent to and fro and a fraction of a percent actually scoring. As I write, I think there’s a tennis match going on at a place called Wimbledon.

Yet, I often ask myself after watching several innings of the Mariners on television, WTF? Why am I sitting here on a rare gorgeous day in the Northwest feigning interest in Ichiro or King Felix or the new model of M’s bats—they have holes in them? I’m witnessing mediocrity writ large, punctuated by a few moments of success. At the finish of nearly every game, manager Eric Wedge can be counted on to scold his troops. He even said that they were getting “dumber.” More recently, he’s talked about major changes, including sending former media darlings down to Tacoma to work on this and that.

Again, why do I care about any of this? I could be curing cancer or running the world, if only I’d cease my addiction to sports. I exaggerate.

I’ve got to end this so I can watch the game on TBS then the Mariners facing the A’s. King Felix is pitching in front of, what, 5,000 people?

Now those few fans in the Oakland Coliseum seats are the ones we have to worry about. They’ve paid over $50 bucks a piece, spent an almost equal amount on food and beverages, only to have to endure the guy who constantly blows that god-awful horn in the left field bleachers. At least the weather’s always nice in the Bay Area. Worst of all are the idiots who sit for hours in St. Louis cheering for their Cardinals in 100-degree heat.

Come to think of it, there’s no way that a sports fan could cure cancer if only he’d turn off the television. There’s just not enough between the ears.

Baseball’s org chart

July 2, 2012 by davald

Most large firms have an organizational hierarchy. There is typically one person at the top, the CEO, followed by a first tier of vice presidents, then successive layers of managers and supervisors. While the CEO is accountable for the overall performance of the company, the first and second tiers have the day-to-day responsibility for ensuring that things get done in a manner consistent with the CEO’s directives and board policies, the latter being generally vague—usually something like “make us a lot of money.” If a division or department fails to perform adequately, its leader is shown the door.

Can or should baseball teams be run like firms? Let’s consider the Chicago White Sox. They currently lead the Central Division of the American League. The team’s manager is Bobby Ventura. He has never managed at any level in his life. Yet, his team is doing well.

Before a recent televised game one of the sportscasters interviewed Ventura about his management style. Ventura said something to the effect that each of his coaches is accountable for a designated facet of the game. Whether or not the team pitches well is the responsibility of the pitching coach. Likewise, getting hits and scoring runs falls under the purview of the batting coach (sometimes called the hitting instructor). There are infield and outfield coaches, bullpen coaches, and even a bench coach, the manager’s right-hand man. In a chat to his team during spring training, Ventura told the players and coaches that they are jointly responsible for performance. If there is an issue with hitting, it is the batting coach’s responsibility along with the hitters’. The same with pitching. Ventura implied a chain of command, with problems better dealt with at the appropriate level. He concluded by saying that if he had to deal directly with an issue, rather than his lieutenants, the player should be worried.

If baseball teams (or any other sports team, for that matter) were operated like firms, with the layers of accountability, the respective coach would be on the hook for on-the-field performance. Yet, very few coaches get the axe; its the manager whose job is at stake.

Which brings me to the Mariners.

The entire team is hitting below the Mendoza Line at Safeco Field. In the just-completed series with the Red Sox, which the M’s somehow managed a split, the team scored just five runs and recorded 20 hits. This was not unusual. Perhaps unusual is that the top three hitters in terms of batting average are pitchers. There is no position player threatening to hit .300. Ichiro’s average of .271 leads the team. The every-day first baseman, Justin Smoak, is batting just above .200. Brendan Ryan, the team’s shortstop, sports a .182 average. You read that right.

While “The Safe” is a pitcher’s dream, visiting teams routinely golf balls into the seats. The M’s team leader in home runs, home and away, is Smoak, with 11. Kyle Seager, the freshman third baseman is next with 10. In 38 home games, the team has hit just 18 home runs at home. They’ve hit over 50 home runs on the road. Every player, with the exception of shortstop Ryan hits for a higher average in away games, and three are batting over .300 in games played anywhere else but Safeco Field.

Let’s run through a few more stats.

As I watch the Mariners I see that too many of their hitters take far too many pitches. Invariably, they fall behind in the count. On 252 occasions the M’s have reached an 0-2 count, and on 131 of those at-bats they have struck out, or 52 percent of the time.

Derek Jeter, the Yankees shortstop, loves to swing at the first pitch he sees, and with good reason. The pitcher desperately strives to get ahead in the count, so he will typically throw a first-pitch fastball close to if not at the middle of the strike zone. Jeter has made a nice career hitting early in the count. How about the Mariners?

On 317 at-bats the M’s have a collective .344 average swinging at the first pitch. When the count goes to 0-1, they hit .296. At 0-2 the team’s average plummets to .079.

Well, I’ll put the counts and averages in a table to make it easier for the reader (data from MLB.com):

 

Fifty percent of the time the Mariners are in counts with at least two strikes. But look at what happens when they swing at the first pitch, which is 11 percent of the time. They record 17 percent of their hits.

Only two percent of the time do Mariners face 2-0 counts, considered the best time to swing, because the pitcher is loath to add another ball to the count. Yet, even here, the Mariners obtain just three percent of their hits. Another hitter’s count is 3-1. Just three percent of the time do M’s batters reach that point, although they get five percent of their hits in this count.

 

The M’s do best in 0-0, 1-0, and 3-1 counts. So, what’s wrong?

If the Mariners and their coaches are familiar with these publicly available stats, and they certainly should be, then the batters ought to approach the plate with the intention of swinging on the first pitch, which, again, is almost always a fastball and within the strike zone. Far too often they let the count get into the pitcher’s favor, and when that happens they suck—horribly. Look the righthand side of the chart. High frequency and low batting average.

The Mariners batting coach is Chris Chambliss. I don’t know much about him, except to recall a walk-off home run he hit off me during college. He usually sits or stands placidly in the dugout, and removed from the manager, Eric Wedge. More likely, it is the pitching coach who stands at Wedge’s elbow. Is there significance in this arrangement? Mariner pitchers are doing well; the hitters, terribly.

If Chambliss were working at a private firm with the responsibility for his division’s or department’s success, he’d be long fired by now. But this is baseball, which plays by a different set of organizational rules.

To be sure, there’s always a question of talent. Maybe Mariner batters would perform miserably under any batting coach or playing for any other team.

But Dustin Ackley, a standout college player who batted over .400 in his senior year, hit very well at every level up to the majors. He’s obviously doing things wrong now, as his average descends. He, too, takes far too many pitches. Let’s look at his average in each of the counts.

In only three counts does Ackley hit better than .300, with his highest on a 0-1 count. The most frequent count for him is at 2-2, about one in every five at-bats. Thirteen percent of his plate appearances result in both 3-2 and 1-2, for a combined 26% of the total. Only three percent of the time does he get a 2-0 count, batting only .250. Ackley, for the year thus far, is hitting only .239. He does lead the team in walks, with 35. But he also leads the team in strikeouts with 69. He has one sacrifice fly and no sacrifice bunts.

I focus on Ackley because I believe that he is a proxy for the team’s problems. Moreover, he does have talent, as was demonstrated before this year. His declining performance has to be attributable in part to Chambliss, the hitting instructor.

Another proxy for batting talent is Ichiro. His batting average has fallen over the last few years, though I’m sure that his mechanics and routine are the same now as they were ten years ago. Also, I doubt seriously that he spends time with Chambliss or any previous batting coach; Ichiro’s style is sui generis, and probably immune to coaching. His decline in performance coincides with his rising age, though he still leads the club.

As for the other players, the veterans have all done much better with their prior clubs. I offer as exhibits Chone Figgins (a lifetime BA of .291 before the M’s) and Brendan Ryan, whose average fell by 33 percentage points; he’s now hitting .182. They displayed talent before they got here, and have hit bottom once they put on the M’s uniform. Could their talent have suddenly dropped off the charts, or should we be paying more attention to the coaching?

One final comment on all this (trivial) stuff. In a firm, excuses don’t count. Either your division thrives, in which case the VP stays, or it fails, in which case the VP departs. Why or how questions do not concern the CEO. It’s performance that counts. Period.

Obscure but amazing

June 21, 2012 by davald

My son sent me an email recently about a couple of items he saw on television. One was on the Cincinnati Reds’ Joey Votto; the other on the NY Mets’ R.A. Dickey. I’ll do them in order.

Votto is the 28-year-old All Star first baseman for the Reds. He’s been in the majors since 2007. His lifetime batting average is .318. Only once, during his sophomore year, did he fail to reach .300; he hit only .297. So, here’s the amazing, but obscure, fact about Votto. He’s hit only one foul ball into the right or left field stands—in his entire career! What does that mean?

Votto strives to hit every pitch back through the middle. He doesn’t try to pull or go inside-out. Rather, he meets the ball with his bat nearly perpendicular to the pitch’s flight path. If he gets just a piece of the ball, chances are, overwhelmingly so, that the ball will go either straight down in front of him or to or over the backstop. His style of hitting, which I would heartedly recommend to any young ballplayer, is the polar opposite of, say, Ichiro Suzuki, the master of fending off one pitch or another by slapping it foul and into the stands—or the dugout, or the on-deck circle, or any other place but fair.

My wish for all the Mariners batters: emulate Votto.

Now on to R.A. Dickey. We used to have him, you know, here in Seattle. Now he toils for the team that drafted me. He became the first Mets pitcher to record two consecutive one-hit shutouts, barely missing a no-hitter in the first game, when third baseman David Wright could have been charged an error, although a very tough one, to be sure. Dickey, who is 37, throws a knuckleball, almost exclusively. And what a knuckler it is. The amazing thing about his is that it may rotate only once after it leaves his hand and crosses the plate. My son saw a video demonstrating this fact.

As a former pitcher I can attest to this pitch, since all of us throwers, at one time or another, experimented with the knuckle ball. Like any good fisher person talking about the one that got away, mine was spectacular. Ask any poor schmuck who had occasion to catch me in the bullpen; one did so without his protective cup, and he was sorry that he’d forgotten it that day.

The amazing thing about my knuckleball is that the only time I used it in a game was in an exhibition between the Cal Bears and the Oakland A’s in the Coliseum before a massive crowd of 2,000, or just under the A’s average attendance in most years.

I was scheduled to start the next day against Santa Clara, always a good baseball team, so I would pitch just one half-inning. Now most players, I should think, would view this as a lifetime opportunity, going against a major league team, the same team that in a few short seasons would record three consecutive World Series championships. But I was a head case who always struggled to be in the here and now while on the mound. My mind was elsewhere. And when I was physically elsewhere, my mind was back on the mound. Go figure.

During my warmup, I tried the knuckleball. It was working splendidly, dancing like a small school of fish. It was my turn to take the hill to face Rick Monday, Tom Reynolds, and, I think, Joe Rudi. Monday was the first player ever drafted, going number one in the 1965 debut draft. I was back aways—closer to 700, but I forget.

I started Monday out with my knuckleball. It was knuckling, all right. But no where near the plate. I could tell that Monday was miffed. The A’s on their day off would prefer to be anywhere but playing a bunch of college kids. Each Oakland batter came to the plate ready to tear the cover off the ball. Monday wanted nothing more than to have me throw the ball down the middle. After throwing three consecutive balls, all knucklers, I delivered a straight one down the middle. Every Oakland hitter had the green light that evening. So Monday swung mightily, connecting on the sweet spot. His one-hop line drive nearly drove our diminutive second baseman into the right field wall. But he caught it, stumbled a bit, then threw to first in plenty of time. One out.

I dispensed with the knuckleball for the next two hitters. I retired both on popups, as I recall. A curve ball got Reynolds. Don’t know what pitch did it with Rudi, or whoever it was.

Now, back to misery.

The X-factor

June 18, 2012 by davald

It seems that nearly every final series hinges on an X-factor, someone relatively obscure who defies his own historical stats to unexpectedly propel his team to victory. I think of Cody Ross in the Giants’ amazing run in 2010, culminating in a World Series championship.

In this year’s NBA finals, the usual suspects have performed at their predicted high levels, from Durant and Westbrook for the Thunder to James and Wade on the Heat. But look who’s become the difference.

I’m speaking of Shane Battier, often overlooked, though appreciated by those who really know basketball. The New York Times noticed in 2009, featuring the “no-stats” Battier in a major piece.

His defense, as always, is top-notch. It’s those three-pointers! Pity the poor Thunder who must devise clever ways to limit the damage caused by King James and D-Wade, both superlative athletes who can score in countless ways. But you didn’t count on Mr. Battier waiting in the wings. Clog the middle to prevent the inevitable penetrations of the Heat stars, only to have the ball kicked out to that wiry, six-foot-eight Battier. Smooth as silk, he arches the ball from behind the 3-point line. Swish. Not once. Not twice. Nearly every time he shoots the trey he drains it.

There are two ways for the Thunder to still pull this one out, trailing now two games to one. James Harden has to start making his shots. He knocked in only two out of ten last night. The other is to hope that Battier returns to the mean and starts missing all those long-range bombs.

Go, Sonics—er, Thunder.
