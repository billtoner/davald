Judge Redden

November 30, 2011 by davald

The Seattle Times‘ editors showered praise on retiring federal judge James Redden. Among the accolades:

U.S. District Judge James Redden has been a good and demanding steward of the Endangered Species Act and the protection of Columbia River wild salmon.

It depends.

To be sure, he rejected several biological opinions he thought flawed, mostly for not doing enough to preserve salmon, a Northwest icon and food.

Here’s the strange part. The salmon were listed as “endangered,” so urgent, critical steps were made necessary to make them less so. To what end? Well, to be eaten.

Remember, save for habitués of Tennessee hollers, we don’t serve Spotted Owl for Thanksgiving dinner. It, too, is an endangered species, and a whole lot of lumber firms and their workers were mad as hell when the courts declared huge swaths of forest off limits to logging.

Efforts to save salmon qua food have not been cheap. For Bonneville Power customers the costs are in the billions of dollars. If one were to factor those costs into the price of fish, salmon would also be off limits for the Rest of Us—we simply couldn’t afford them. But those costs are borne by electricity consumers in the form of higher rates.

I’m not here to argue the merits of saving fish for our diets. I only wish to point out that preservation hasn’t been free.

Salmon for dinner? Enjoy.

Rise in federal spending

November 27, 2011 by davald

Paul Krugman is frustrated. No matter how frequently and thoroughly he debunks would-be critics, he’s repeatedly barraged with the same pseudo arguments. This morning is no exception.

The topic in this case is the increase in federal spending, and how Obama is behaving like a conventional liberal—expanding the size of government. Krugman has taken this on again and again. So he does today.

Indeed, we should agree that federal spending as a percent of GDP has risen since Obama assumed office. But what are the numbers?

Well, part of the answer is that the ratio is up because the denominator is down. According to CBO estimates, in fiscal 2010 the economy operated about 7 percent below potential. This means that even if what the government was doing hadn’t changed, the federal spending share of GDP would have risen by 1.4 percentage points.

Krugman then breaks down the 1.4 figure spending increase. As sentient beings should understand, there is a recession going on for a huge slice of the population; and it’s not the One-percenters. Over several decades “those liberals” have established public safety nets to help those who need it, mostly the poor, the aged, and veterans. We should not be surprised, then, that spending has increased for unemployment benefits, Medicare  and Medicaid, and, since we’ve got wars going on that tend to injure military men and women, veterans’ health care and benefits.

Under “Other,” Krugman identifies stimulus money and an increase in the use of Medicare and Social Security by the Baby Boomers.

Krugman concludes:

What isn’t there, no way, nohow, is a massive expansion of government, which is a figment of the right wing’s imagination.

I’ll segue here to the PUD’s experience with its rate-discount programs. The utility offers lower rates to qualified low-income customers, including seniors. The tiers are 20, 40, and 60 percent, depending on income. We’ve discovered the rather obvious relationship between the unemployment rate and the number of customers receiving benefits under the programs. (We also provide similar discounts to water customers, whose numbers are a fraction of electric customers.)

But we can see a huge jump over the last couple of years, reaching an all-time high of 15,346 as of October 2011. The corresponding budget for these programs has also risen, as we would expect.

Meanwhile, Washington’s legislature convenes tomorrow for a special session to wrestle with the newest revenue forecast, which is not pretty. As I mentioned in a previous post, the overwhelming majority of the state’s budget goes to education and just slightly less to human services, the programs that assist our at-risk citizens, whose ranks have swelled with the recession.

Unfortunately, albeit predictably, the Republicans assert that the revenue shortfall must be covered by cuts alone—no revenue increases. Olympia Democrats, as in D.C., will push for a combination of cuts and new revenues. The Herald warns that things could get “ugly” next week. The Seattle Times, as is its wont, pushes the legislators to show “courage.” WTF.

Can’t win for losing

November 7, 2011 by davald

The Snohomish County Public Utility District board adopted a far-reaching, progressive integrated resource plan. It has two major components: achieve as much cost-effective conservation as practicable; and supplement the conservation with renewable resources, preferably in our own backyard.

The rationale for the policy has multiple ingredients:

  • acknowledge climate change and the need to reduce the utility’s carbon footprint;
  • comply with the mandates of I-937;
  • minimize financial risk (e.g., acquire both supply and demand-side resources in small chunks so as to mirror customer growth);
  • reduce reliance on regional transmission, which is costly and increasingly problematic (e.g., integration of intermittent resources like wind); and
  • preserve—and enhance when possible—the area’s environmental attributes and indigenous species.

The PUD has the highest percentage of wind energy in its supply portfolio of any regional utility. However, because of the problems of integrating wind resources (the wind doesn’t always blow when we want it to), the utility will not be acquiring more wind power in the foreseeable future. Rather, to meet continuing load growth and comply with the state’s resource portfolio standard the PUD is focused on harnessing local resources. We’ve identified four:

  • solar;
  • tidal;
  • geothermal; and
  • small, low-impact hydro.

Just recently the PUD received a 45-year license renewal of the Jackson Hydroelectric Project. This involved a decade-long process culminating in agreements with an array of stakeholders, including the Tulalip Tribes, non-governmental organizations, and state and federal agencies. But the utility went over and above the various legal requirements. We’ve enhanced fish habitats, improved river flows, and expanded recreational opportunities.

Over the past several months the PUD has restored and greatly improved the existing Woods Creek and Young’s Creek small hydro projects. In so doing, we have left the sites in better shape than when we initially acquired them. Of critical importance the PUD actually enhanced fish habitat and survival.

The PUD is investigating at least three more small hydro projects, two of which we acquired from Puget Sound Energy. The largest is Sunset Falls, which, if built, will provide 30 megawatts of capacity. The utility filed for a preliminary permit from the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission to investigate the feasibility of constructing and operating the facility. There have been two community meetings thus far, which gave citizens an opportunity to see what the utility has in mind, ask questions of staff, and voice their concerns. (You can view related documents here.)

While it may seem self-serving on my part, since I am on the PUD board, I can say without reservation that the utility will not force this project on anyone. Indeed, we do whatever is necessary to ensure that all interests are not only respected but incorporated in the design, construction, and operating of this or any other facility.

Despite our being only in the exploratory mode, some people have registered strong objections. For our part, the utility will go out of its way to determine their legitimacy.

A few individuals seem to be ideologically opposed to anyone building any new dams or diversions anywhere. To those I would suggest taking a step back to examine these small projects within a larger context. Keep in mind that the utility has a statutory obligation to meet the electricity needs of people living and working in Snohomish County and Camano Island. I’ve already mentioned that the PUD is pursuing all cost-effective conservation. I’ve also mentioned the challenges we face transmitting electricity from distant resources. Also, there are opportunities within our own territory that deserve exploration. The utility has no fossil-fuel resources in its own supply portfolio now or ever. But the energy that we need beyond conservation has to come from somewhere.

Snohomish County is blessed with natural features that can be exploited in environmentally sensitive ways. We’ve got the Sound to the west, so it makes sense to investigate tidal power potential, which the utility is doing. The Cascades sit above the “ring of fire.” Our preliminary investigation has revealed a promising site for geothermal exploration and, if feasible, development. Geothermal, by the way, is an ideal resource: it’s renewable and firm—that is, it runs 24/7.

It also rains here. That combination of falling water and mountains yields hydropower potential.

The utility desires small, incremental projects that pose no danger to fish or wildlife. The PUD will not build a dam or diversion that in any way threatens or interferes with the viability of anadromous fish species. Indeed, it is the utility’s goal to enhance the survivability of indigenous species as we construct and operate facilities.

So, I was taken aback by a letter that appeared in this morning’s Herald. The author wrote:

PUD officials misleadingly compare the Sunset Falls project to their recently completed Young’s Creek dam and proclaim to Herald readers that “the project’s location above the falls would not affect spawning salmon.” Sounds good, except the facts are that salmon and steelhead are trapped by the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife and released upstream where they thrive in the 100 miles of pristine spawning habitat above the falls. The PUD’s public deception about this is very disturbing.

How is the PUD misleading people? In the event the Sunset Falls project is built, fish would continue to be trapped and released upstream where they will continue to “thrive in the 100 miles of pristine spawning habitat above the falls.” And as I said above, the PUD will most certainly enhance fish populations should the project proceed.

We can’t make this stuff up

September 2, 2011 by davald

I loathe the credit agencies. First, they consistently fail to give the PUD its highest ratings, when our ability to pay debt service is as pure as the new fallen snow. No, I’m not just biased. The PUD will pay its bills. Period. Even if we have to raise rates to do so.

Second, the agencies bestowed AAA ratings on Enron! Pass me (another) beer. The “smartest guys in the room” turned out to be a bunch of sophisticated crooks. I know. They shafted the PUD, and we’re the ones responsible for exposing their shenanigans.

Third, the agencies make their money on the very companies they rate. “Say, S&P, I’d really like to have your highest rating. But if you’re shy about that, I’ll take my business elsewhere—and do I have business.”

Fourth, the folks who rate seem to be clueless, in a very dumb way. Can you say “subprime mortgages”? Consider this:

From Bloomberg: “S&P is poised to provide AAA grades to 59 percent of Springleaf Mortgage Loan Trust 2011-1, a set of bonds tied to $497 million lent to homeowners with below-average credit scores and almost no equity in their properties.”

The folks at Talking Points Memo ran this story just now. We know that S&P downgraded the federal government. And what happened? Treasury rates plunged. Why? Smart investors know that U.S. government bonds are the safest place to put their dollars. If any of them took S&P seriously, they’d run for the German Hügel.

The rating agencies, in my humble opinion, are a sham. They provide no valuable service.

But they’re part of the game, a game I’d like the PUD to abandon. We don’t need the agencies. Perspicacious investors know all about the PUD, even if Moody’s et al. don’t know what the hell they’re doing.

Paying to save

June 21, 2011 by davald

Last night I had the honor of presenting the Marysville School District with a check for some $82,000. The district had cooperated with the PUD to design a more energy-efficient high school. The utility offered incentives to the district for the installation of better lighting and other devices. As I said to the school board, doing so saves money and the environment.

But before I gave my remarks I sat in the audience listening to several people making their case to the directors to save programs and rescind the transfer of an evidently beloved high school teacher.

The teacher, it seemed, had been involuntarily transferred from one school to another. A half-dozen or more of his students, sharply dressed, attended on his behalf. One from their ranks spoke admirably of the teacher, giving the rest of us in the audience reason to question the wisdom of the district’s personnel decision. I gathered that the transfer was a matter of numbers and seniority.

And the pain on display over program cuts is all about numbers. The district’s finance director presented a slide showing declining revenues from the state. He pointed out that the district faced another budget shortfall, this one over $3 million. Since the district cannot simply impose taxes, fees, or rates at its discretion, the only way to close the gap is by cutting further.

The district has already been forced to slash music programs, job counselors, physical education, among other cuts. Teachers have received layoff notices. The state budget calls for a reduction in the salaries of teachers and principals. District employees will be asked to pay more out of pocket for health and welfare benefits.

So, I could not help prefacing my prepared remarks with a sympathetic statement: “I don’t envy your challenge. With so many deserving demands on ever-dwindling resources it’s a shame that the school board lacks the same authority as the PUD board.”

We all clamor for more and better education. But we straitjacket school districts, denying them the resources to fulfill our wishes. If learning is so damn important, and I certainly believe that it is, then why do we routinely underfund it?

Were school districts governed like PUDs, we’d have topnotch educational programs. I guarantee it.

See how they run

June 11, 2011 by davald

The Herald gave us a list this morning of people who have filed for various elected positions throughout the county. Few seats are unchallenged; there are lots of folks who aspire to hold office, even one or two who have run and lost, again and again.

Why do people choose to launch campaigns, some more costly than others, to sit behind a dais and vote on x, y, and z? How will they make a difference and on whose behalf?

I have stood for political office three times: once as a failed candidate for the Marysville city council; the last two as PUD commissioner, both successful. In the Marysville race I tried to offer a distinct alternative to the incumbent, who was also a former county council member. I did not care for how city government, and its citizens, were being exploited for the benefit of the real estate industry, which has long had its way in that city.

As for the PUD, I said in 2002 that I was a “reluctant” candidate. I was drafted by a small group of people familiar with the utility and me. That first time there were five of us vying for one seat, including the incumbent, who failed to survive the primary. Things were bad at the utility following the West Coast energy debacle, engineered in large part by the spectacularly corrupt Enron. So, 2002 was an opportune occasion to run for the PUD board.

Last time, in 2008, the utility—I do not hesitate to proclaim—was in very good shape, under the able leadership of a new general manager. Yet that didn’t stop someone from filing against me, in the last hours of the final day. As it turned out, the challenger, when pressed as to why he was running, allowed that he didn’t want my name to appear alone on the ballot. Huh? He had no sense of urgency, and why should he? There was really nothing wrong with the PUD that warranted a major adjustment—as had been the case in 2002. During the campaign I mostly scratched my head, wondering why an individual, who had shown absolutely no interest in the utility before his filing, would put thousands of his own dollars into a feeble attempt to unseat yours truly. Of course, he had every right to do so; my position on the board was not inherited. But his challenge seemed to be a waste of time, money, and effort. I spent $175 and won re-election handily—on the strength of a revitalized organization.

You’ll notice that one of my colleagues on the PUD board has filed for county council. I have no idea why, but, again, she has every right to do so. Perhaps she wants to fry bigger fish. And, should she win, her wages from holding office will more than triple.

Jackie Minchew is running again for Everett city council. His heart is certainly in the right place, and we agree on many issues, especially with respect to the environment and energy policy. But Jackie risks becoming the Harold Stassen of local politics. Maybe the fourth time will be the charm, if I’ve counted correctly. As an Everett resident, I’ll surely vote for him—again.

Before I leave, a few words about the county executive race. I’m sure that Mike Hope, who is challenging incumbent Aaron Reardon, suggested to voters when he ran for his Olympia seat that he belonged in state government and would better serve the citizens of his district than the incumbent, Liz Loomis, whom he defeated in an extremely close race. Now it appears that he will make similar claims about the county, about how he belongs in the executive’s seat, and that the people from the Sound to the Cascades will benefit from his running the government. Don’t hold your breath. The acrimony has already started, judging by the accompanying Herald article:

“I think the executive’s race is going to be particularly interesting,” said Bill Phillips, chairman of the Snohomish County Democratic Party. “It’s going to be Mike Hope’s rhetoric versus Aaron Reardon’s record of accomplishment.”

His GOP counterpart couldn’t disagree more.

“There’s been more people than normal standing up here because, frankly, there’s a lot of people who are tired of where the country is at and want to step up and do something about it,” said Bill Cooper, the Republican Party’s Snohomish County chairman.

I have written elsewhere on what I perceive to be the county’s dysfunctional governance model. There are five independently elected council members and an independently elected executive. How is that working? Despite Democrats in the majority on the council and Reardon’s being a nominal Democrat, the council and the executive have publicly clashed on more than one occasion. Mike Hope is a Republican. Should the council remain Democratic, and Hope should prevail (what a campaign slogan, by the way), what can we expect to see beginning in January? Smooth or rough waters? I’m guessing the latter.

At any rate, we have dozens of people with aspirations, which is not a bad thing. I remain too cynical to expect the kind of results we deserve from our public offices, with one notable exception, of course.

Contrasting cycles

June 8, 2011 by davald

When it comes to combatting climate change, our political system is ill-equipped; it has much to do with time-cycles.

Yesterday the PUD board and staff were treated to a presentation by Mike Davis, who holds the mouthful-title of “Associate Laboratory Director of the Energy and Environment Directorate of Pacific Northwest National Laboratories.” (Since his slide presentation is now a public record, I invite you to take a look.) I want to focus on one of his slides, in particular.

Notice how the graphic is divided, with “special interests” on the left and “special outcomes” on the right. On the left we find “news media,” “earnings reports,” and “political elections.” Each of these has time-cycles, very short with respect to the news media, quarterly with respect to company balance sheets, and between two and six years with respect to elections.

On the right Davis places “science and technology,” “energy infrastructure,” and “climate.” To develop innovative devices and protocols can take up to ten years, or so. Establishing an energy infrastructure requires even more time. But the “climate” can best be appreciated in century-long time scales. We know, for example, that carbon dioxide molecules last at least a hundred in the atmosphere.

We can understand that the solutions (“special outcomes”) on the right demand sustainable and durable policies extending far beyond the timeframes of the news, earning reports, and elections. Yet, the rules and incentives that exist on the left side of the chart prevent those long range solutions.

Davis, who has spent much time in China, spoke of recent policy prescriptions coming out of Beijing. The government leaders, all technically sharp, Davis adds, produce five-year plans consisting of just a half-dozen pages. The desired outcomes and the persons accountable for achieving them are clearly defined. But the plans don’t spell out how the outcomes are to be achieved, with the exception of a few boundary conditions; that’s up to those responsible for solving the identified problems.

Davis contrasts this simple procedure with Congress’s. We have become inured to the documents (laws) running thousands of pages. Each stack of paper incorporates the desires of a myriad of special interests, who spend an inordinate sum of money lobbying and electing their favorite senators and representatives. But after a bill survives the news-cum-special interests-cum-election cycles and is eventually signed by the president, there is no pretext to durability. There is nothing to bind the policy over successive elections.

Yet that durability is precisely what’s required to meet the challenges of climate change. Ironically, the Chinese may be better suited to accomplishing the “special outcomes” than Americans.

What a tradeoff: democracy and more greenhouse gas emissions or hybrid socialism/capitalism and climate solutions.

That said, China has huge environmental challenges, including rising emissions, that may overwhelm even its streamlined governance model.

But given our contrasting cycles, I wouldn’t bet on the US getting serious about climate change any time soon. Sorry, children.

The weeping and wailing over public salaries

May 20, 2011 by davald

Should public employees receive lower wages than their private counterparts, even though they perform essentially the same tasks? Judging by the flaming reactions to both the Herald and KOMO TV stories on PUD General Manager Steve Klein’s compensation, the evident answer is yes. But why?

In my defense of the PUD board’s decision to provide Steve with a retention bonus, I said that his proven track record made him very attractive to utility head-hunters and that his skills and experience were second to none in the industry. In my television interview I even went so far as to suggest that Steve was the best utility manager in the country, whether public or private. (Okay, I concede that may have been a bit hyperbolic, since I am unfamiliar with the performances of Steve’s counterparts outside the Northwest region. But I can’t imagine anyone else possessing his skill set.)

To be sure the KOMO feature was a genuine hit piece, making no pretext to being fair and balanced. It led with “Snohomish Co. PUD exec paid more than the gov., AG combined.” True, but so what? Should viewers infer that KOMO believes that the governor and attorney general’s salary is adequate or appropriate and that anything higher is simply too much? Should KOMO have distinguished between elected officials and appointed CEOs? The station mentioned only in passing that the new CEO of Puget Sound Energy, an investor-owned utility, made $500,000, but that was before she was selected to succeed the outgoing CEO, Steve Reynolds, whose 2009 compensation was $8 million, according to the Herald. Although serving more customers, PSE’s business, and the challenges it faces, are virtually the same as the PUD’s. Yet, I would argue that Steve is far more valuable to the customers of the PUD than Mr. Reynolds was to either the customers or shareholders of the IOU.

Most of what I told KOMO was chopped out. I told the reporter that the public squawks at public employee salaries but turns a blind eye to the multi-million-dollar compensation packages in the private sector. Some hedge fund managers draw salaries of a billion dollars a year. And these are some of the people who nearly brought down the global economy, for which they received federal bailouts, only to resume their wanton ways once people’s attentions were again distracted.

Why does the public evidently believe that teachers should make no more than about $50,000 a year? We say that education is of paramount importance, yet deny schools adequate resources and expect their employees to produce academic miracles. I know, because I’m married to a public school teacher, the most difficult job on the planet, given all the institutional constraints and pressures.

One rationale for underpaying government workers is that people freely choose to work in the public sector. Nobody is forcing them to take low-paying jobs. True enough.

It is an American pastime to rail against “the government.” The Tea Party folks, of course, are over the top; in their eyes, no government equals good government. Yet, we all depend on public services rendered by the harshly criticized government agencies. From transportation to education to environmental protection, governments provide goods and services without which our lives would be even more dismal.

Nevertheless, we consistently underfund our governments, expecting superior performance from employees paid below-market wages. We demand our cake and to eat it too, or, loosely translated, something for nothing. I never cease to marvel at what public school teachers accomplish with limited resources, heightened pressures to perform, and exhaustingly long hours.

But let me suggest another way of thinking about all this. Suppose we started acknowledging all those essential public goods and services. Moreover, and this is the important part, suppose further that we actually adopted the novel attitude that we deserve superior performance from government agencies. Instead of governments being viewed as necessary evils, they would become indispensable to enhance the general welfare.

We would not settle for dilapidated school buildings. We would not put up with expensive and environmentally harmful transportation systems. We would demand clean air, water, and soils. We would expect health care to be a right rather than a privilege. We would insist on livable-wage jobs for anyone who was willing and able to work. We would expect adequate pensions upon completion of our working lives.

If you think that the private sector is going to deliver on these demands and expectations across the board, then you live in the right country, the US of A, and you’ve been duped—big time. The market is not concerned with your well-being. It’s too busy obsessing with profits; and increasingly those profits are being siphoned off by just one percent of the population.

Top ten percent

By the way, the share of the wealth enjoyed by the rest of us has remained stagnant and low.

Meanwhile, and this simply astounds me, the rest of us refuse to subject our privileged few to more progressive tax rates. We had our chance last year to impose an income tax on those making more than $250,000, which, I dare say, includes none of you readers. The tax would have affected only the income above the threshold and the revenues collected would have funded public education. In Congress, the Republicans and far too many Democrats want to preserve the Bush tax cuts, which have contributed the lion’s share of our current deficit—along with the two wars.

So, our current politics of resentment is misplaced. We should not be denigrating public officials for receiving modest salaries while ignoring the accumulating fortunes of a privileged few. Rather, we should expect our governments to work for us and, certainly, provide them with sufficient resources, including competitive salaries, to do their proper jobs—serving the people.

The rich have got you buffaloed. Stop them, before it’s too late.

PUD GM compensation: from there to here

May 19, 2011 by davald

I have no doubt that the front-page article in this morning’s Herald about PUD General Manager Steve Klein’s compensation boost will generate much negative reaction. I’ve been there, done that. So, what’s different about today’s PUD and its general manager than past years’, when I strenuously objected to higher management salaries? Good question, Dave. First, a bit of background may be in order.

In my previous life at the PUD I served as Commissioner Chuck Moon’s policy aide. He had proposed to raise the general manager’s salary to what I believed to be an outrageous amount. If the board concurred, which it eventually did, the general manager would be making more than any other public employee, save state university personnel. Chuck told me that he was “paying the position” rather than the person. Herein lies a difference between then and now. Not long after increasing the GM’s salary, the board was forced to terminate him for both incompetence and other matters I need not discuss. The current PUD board is definitely paying the person and not necessarily the position, although the compensation package should be sufficient to attract future GMs; the board’s policy is to “pay market wages” with respect to all PUD positions.

In my years at the PUD I have worked with a half-dozen different general managers, going back to Charlie Earl (who now superintends the state’s community college system). The GM’s tenure during the last two decades was brief and, with few exceptions, tumultuous. I was not terribly excited about any of them. Before I arrived on the board in 2003, the commission had hired long-time Everett mayor Ed Hansen to replace a general manager who left town under a cloud. This was following the Enron scandal. The commission desired two things: to restore fiscal integrity and attain some level of leadership stability. Hansen performed those tasks well. When he retired five years ago, the PUD was in much better shape than when he started.

Then along came Steve Klein.

I had never believed in the “great man” principle, that a single individual could make a significant, positive difference in an organization. After all, I had not witnessed it at the PUD across several general managers. But, and I have no hesitation to admit this, Steve Klein stands out, far and above the rest. His brief bio on the utility’s web page doesn’t do him credit. His abilities extend beyond his resume, which is ideal for a utility general manager; he combines technical knowledge and expertise with a lengthy track record of superior management experience. Steve brings a set of intangibles, difficult to quantify, to produce remarkable results to any challenge.

The Herald article, which I penned a couple of weeks ago, began with the paper’s leader, describing the PUD as a “well-oiled machine.”

There’s a good reason for the headline: the PUD really is a well-oiled machine, and I attribute that description to a single individual: Steve Klein. And that opinion is not just my own. (During a break at our last board meeting, a prominent member of the community approached me to say that he had appreciated the article. He then turned in the direction of Steve Klein and said that the PUD would not be in as good a shape as it is without “him,” namely Steve Klein.) A week ago, the Snohomish County Business Journal, published by the Herald, named Steve Klein its executive of the year. Keep in mind, that the award did not go to the head of a private utility, or to Boeing, or to Kimberly Clark, or to the CEOs of any number of businesses operating in Snohomish County. The award went to the general manager of a public entity—the PUD.

Is a single individual “worth” the money that the board is giving him? Let’s put it this way. Steve Klein brings more value to the PUD than, say, Alex Rodriguez brings the New York Yankees. And what does A-Rod make? Over thirty million dollars a year, or about 90 times as much as Steve.

Okay, you say, that’s an unfair comparison. A better discussion, perhaps, would attempt to answer some counterfactuals. I’ll suggest two:

  1. What compensation could Steve Klein command elsewhere?
  2. Could the PUD entice a similarly qualified individual to run the utility if the GM salary were no more than the governor’s? (One commenter to the article on Steve Klein’s salary openly suggested that Tim Eyman launch an initiative that would place a ceiling on the salaries of all public employees at the level of the governor’s. By the way, that commenter was one of five opponents I faced in my 2002 campaign for the board.)

The CEO of Puget Sound Energy, a private utility, recently retired. The Herald reported that he made $8 million in 2009. His replacement made half a million dollars last year, before her promotion. None of us should be surprised if her salary is at least doubled as she takes over the reins of the IOU. Would PSE have wanted to hire Steve Klein to replace the outgoing CEO? In my judgment, they would have been fools not to have given him the job—were Steve willing to depart the PUD. If PSE’s CEO is “worth” nearly $10 million a year, then certainly Steve Klein brings as much value, and probably a whole lot more.

That said, by all appearances Steve Klein derives much satisfaction running the PUD, which at least partially explains why he chose to remain rather than leave for richer environs. I’ll offer a bit of speculation.

First off, and as I emphasized in my Herald piece, there is very little bureaucracy at the PUD, which consists of just three elected governors (the board) and the general manager. For someone who has great ideas and is anxious to realize them, there is perhaps no better place than the PUD. Steve has broad latitude and discretion, because he has demonstrated over his initial five years an unparalleled capacity for both keeping the lights on and pushing the energy-resource envelope.

Steve has spent all of his career in the public sector, which suggests that he believes in those inherent principles that distinguish public from private organizations. As a public utility, the PUD cannot extract profits for shareholders, although the PUD maintains healthy reserves to ensure fiscal prudence. Nor, as I’ve said, do public utility managers make private-sector salaries like those of Puget Sound Energy. I would argue that PUD ratepayers are getting quite a deal: probably the best utility executive in the region at public-level compensation.

So, what might happen if the board were to cap the GM’s salary? Suppose the commissioners passed a resolution that prohibited any PUD employee from making more than, say, the governor.

Governor Christine Gregoire receives about $167,000 a year. While that salary places her at the top of state’s elected public officials, it’s below that of the Seattle Port Commission’s CEO. And we should note the “elected” part. For whatever reason, we citizens balk at permitting private-sector salaries for our elected officials. As a PUD commissioner, my annual salary, including per diems, is about $30,000, an amount set by Olympia. County Council members make over $100,000 a year.

But Steve Klein is not an elected official. He is the PUD’s chief executive officer. PUD customers deserve to have their utility run as well as possible. A very good CEO makes that happen. A very good board of elected officials can’t, since we commissioners lack the requisite skills and experience.

Back to our question, what kind of GM could the PUD attract if his or her salary capped at the governor’s? What could we get for $167,000 a year? Unless the board just got lucky, I’d say that the best and the brightest would give the PUD wide berth. Why? Just look at the market for utility executives.

Carl Mycoff, whom the PUD board retained a bit over five years ago to help recruit Ed Hansen’s replacement, was mentioned and quoted in the Herald article.

Klein’s salary is among the low end for executives among comparable public utility districts nationwide, said Carl Mycoff, co-owner of the utility executive search firm Mycoff, Fry, and Prouse near Denver.

…

“The only reason Klein stays there is because he likes that kind of utility,” Mycoff said. “He could make more money elsewhere in a heartbeat with his skills.”

There is an adage: you get what you pay for. We three commissioners want top talent at the PUD, led by a superior general manager. We’ve got one in Steve Klein, and we’re willing to pay for it.
