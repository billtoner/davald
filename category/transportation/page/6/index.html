Legislative herding

February 4, 2013 by davald

Legislators sponsor bills that prescribe certain behaviors. For example, when dealing with the environment or global warming, representatives propose legislation designed to induce the acquisition of renewable and efficiency resources over the bad stuff—anything with carbon or soot or toxins. I will assume that such bills result from the best of intentions. However, there is a simpler, more effective approach.

Let’s focus on climate change. If one is serious about preventing or mitigating global warming, then, by all means, dramatically reduce, if not eliminate, the combustion of carbon. A little bit of atmospheric carbon dioxide is a good thing; too much will certainly destroy the planet, one species at a time.

Economists have known for some time now, probably for centuries and before ‘economist’ joined the dictionary, that raising the price of a good or service will reduce demand for the item. If we want less carbon, we need to make it more expensive.

There are many ways to do this. But surely the simplest and most effective is by taxing it. 

But legislators in Olympia and D.C. can hardly even say the word ‘tax,’ let alone establish one. Instead, they attempt to herd us all into metaphorical pastures.

The Snohomish County PUD, of which I am a board member, tracks legislation introduced in both Washington state and Washington, D.C.. Typically, we commissioners receive updates, which are ranked by topic and priority. The latest Olympia update was sent at the end of last week. I’ll mention a few legislative titles:

  • Modifying the renewable energy and cost recovery program
  • Regarding net metering of electricity
  • Regarding the use of an energy storage facility to meet annual targets under RCW…
  • Requiring integrated resource plans developed by electric utilities include an assessment of energy storage systems

Implicit in these bills is an underlying concern for climate change. But they are all prescriptive or proscriptive, another way of “herding” utilities, in this case. So, we at the PUD pay attention on behalf of our customers. If we believe that a bill’s passage will harm ratepayers, we speak against and otherwise try to modify or even “kill” it. On the other hand, some legislation we deem beneficial, in which case we actively support. In most instances the PUD “monitors” or perhaps expresses “concern.” Since legislators are in session for months at a time, the process is long and occasionally tedious. At the end, dozens, if not hundreds, of bills “die,” with only a handful landing on the governor’s desk for signature.

Suppose the legislators imposed a carbon tax. Oregon has one. California established a cap-and-trade regime (a form of taxation). Ireland recently instituted a stiff carbon tax. Wherever taxes or caps have been imposed, consumption of carbon has fallen. What would happen if Olympia did?

Rather quickly, if international examples attest, those subject to a tax would take steps to avoid them. Electric utilities would pursue non-carbon strategies. These would include more conservation and renewable resources. If the tax were high enough, more utilities would have an incentive to pursue such initiatives as energy storage, which has proven costly, given its infancy. (The noble Snohomish County PUD does all this good stuff in the absence of a tax. We board members recognize that carbon will become more expensive; it’s just a matter of time. As always, the PUD is ahead of the curve.)

However, utilities do not hold a monopoly on innovation. A carbon tax would encourage utilities to serve as a conduit between third parties (the entrepreneurs and innovators) and their customers, who, after all, would ultimately pay the tax in the form of higher electricity rates. The higher the tax, the wider the conduit and the greater the opportunity.

Another thing would happen. Legislators could busy themselves with other pressing matters, like education and transportation, confident that the tax would do the herding, like Adam Smith’s “invisible hand.” 

If any legislators are reading this, why not start the conversation among your colleagues? Hey. We’re only trying to save the earth.

Building less

January 14, 2013 by davald

Fewer people employed in construction. No surprise there, given the collapse of the housing bubble and the severe overhang of both stock and debt. We would also expect spending to be down in this sector as well.

Consider this excerpt from a recent study (pdf) on infrastructure spending:

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The goal of this report is to understand the short- and long-term effects of public infrastructure spending on the U.S. economy, as well as to contribute new suggestions towards alternative financing of future road construction.

Estimated Short-Run Effects

  •  In the short-run, a dollar spent on infrastructure construction produces roughly double the initial spending in ultimate economic output.
  •  The biggest effects of infrastructure spending occur in the manufacturing and business services sectors.
  • In better economic times, spending on infrastructure construction generates a larger return. Yet even in a recession, the overall effects of initial spending still double output as they ripple through the economy.

Estimated Long-Run Effects

  • Over a twenty-year period, generalized ‘public investment’ generates an accumulated $3.21 of economic activity per $1.00 spent.
  •  Over twenty years, investing $1.00 in highways and streets returns approximately $0.35 in tax revenue to federal and state/local governments, of which $0.23 specifically accrues at the federal level.
  • Over twenty years, investing $1.00 in sewer systems and water infrastructure returns a full $2.03 in tax revenue to federal and state/local governments, of which $1.35 specifically accrues at the federal level.

Despite the need for and desirability of spending on infrastructure, Republicans resist any and all fiscal stimulus. They say, in their defense, that they just don’t like government debt. Nor do they care much for government at all, save for the jobs they hold within it.

But look at the above numbers. Every dollar the government spends on building roads or schools, for example, adds two dollars to GDP. A stronger economy yields higher government revenues, thereby reducing deficits. Since Washington can borrow money at essentially zero cost to fund infrastructure improvements, we seem to have a no-brainer.

All of which suggests that Republicans lack more than a brain.

Neglect

December 9, 2012 by davald

The next time you hear someone proclaim America (is) first, consider the state of our nation’s public infrastructures. You will quickly disabuse yourself of sharing the hoary sentiment.

The New York Times editors provide some measure of how bad things are with the country’s roads, sewer systems, and other public works. We are behind trillions of dollars in making systems safe and reliable, including the electric grid.

Yet, the conservatives have been so successful in demonizing government and its obligations that the Obama administration and dozens of Democratic governors dare not present a realistic proposal to fix the nation. For example:

When the president asked recently for a modest $50 billion for transportation improvements in the “fiscal cliff” talks, Republicans literally laughed out loud. There will be no stimulus in any deal, said Representative Bill Shuster of Pennsylvania, the incoming chairman of the Transportation and Infrastructure Committee.

The amount, the editors said, would amount to only a “modest downpayment” on what’s really needed, more than $2 trillion just to restore public transportation to working order. Forget about high-speed rail or state-of-the art schools.

The scenic route

November 28, 2012 by davald

I have travelled the Sounder train just once, a lovely trip from Everett to Seattle, then back again later that day. The passenger compartment was spotless, newish, and comfortable. The route takes one along Puget Sound, up close and personal, with the Olympic Mountains forming a gorgeous backdrop. I thought, if I were commuting this would be the way to go.

Evidently not that many people agree. Despite the horrible congestion along I-5, no matter when, commuters still favor their cars, and this is a problem. Traveling by automobile burns excess carbons and wastes countless hours sitting idly in a barely moving goo—and cars and gas are expensive. Yet, taking the train may be worse.

As Bill Sheets has been reporting in the Everett Herald, the Burlington Northern-Santa Fe line suffers from scores of mudslides, the result of combining lots of rain and impermeable hilltop surfaces with steep slopes adjacent to the tracks. Sheets:

The hillsides along the tracks between Everett and Seattle are the worst slide area for trains in Western Washington, said David Smelser, a high-speed rail program manager for the state Department of Transportation.

The problem, he said, isn’t just the slopes — it’s the runoff from developed areas above the slopes along the route.

Oh, I should point out that the Everett-to-Seattle journey is anything but “high-speed.” It is relaxing and, as I said, lovely, provided the sun is out and the landscape visible.

The more fundamental problem with Sounder is the route. First, it doesn’t actually go through or connect population centers. Second, the rails are owned by BNSF, which has priority. Thus, the commuter train runs only a few times in the early morning and late afternoon. Outside those narrow windows, you’ll have to take a bus or hitch a ride if you’re stuck in Seattle and need to get home sooner.

On these pages I’ve consistently railed against our collective stupidity. It is displayed front and center on public transportation. In a word, it sucks, if it exists at all.

A few years ago I looked at vehicle miles travelled. Here’s a chart showing the number of gallons of gasoline consumed annually from 1983 to 2008, assuming 24 mpg. (According to this site, the average was 23.1 in 1980 and 24.7 in 2004.)

The good news: consumption began tapering off in 2006 and 2007. The bad news: there’s still a lot of gasoline being consumed by driving cars and trucks.

What about carbon emissions?

Burning carbons is a bad idea, as anyone even vaguely familiar with the science of climate change can appreciate. Washington state, given its high percentage of hydroelectric power, emits less carbon dioxide than coal-dependent states. So the transportation sector leads the way in carbon emissions (2010 EPA data).

Initiative 937 requires electric utilities that serve more than 25,000 customers to gradually increase the amount of renewables in their supply portfolios as they maximize their conservation potential. But we were already a “green” state, with most of the electricity generated through hydroelectric facilities. We’re becoming even greener, as utilities add thousands of megawatts of wind energy. Still, the state has not seriously addressed its transportation sector.

The simplest way to do this is to get people out of their cars and trucks and into trains. But the Sounder route will not do it. Here’s a rough illustration of the path (white line along the water to the left).

The population centers rim I-5 and not the water. So, why didn’t the powers that be select a train-dedicated corridor somewhat parallel to the freeway?

Money.

We can imagine how expensive, not to mention disruptive, it would be to acquire the real estate. Years of sprawling development have gobbled up the most desirable locations to build tracks.

Yet, and here’s the tragedy, it would be far less expensive had Puget Sound Transit purchased the proper path, built the line, then ran dozens of trains each day at all hours—than the aggregate costs of so many thousands of motorists buying their own private vehicles and the fuel to run them. But we’re wedded to individual solutions while disdaining public strategies that would be cheaper and far more convenient.

In a few years those of us still kicking will finally have an epiphany. We’ll realize that we’ve really screwed up in our obsession with the automobile. But it will be too late, far too late, to do anything. If only we had acted sooner, most will be force to confess.

Quite the legacy.

Return on investment [u]

November 8, 2012 by davald

I happened to catch an episode of Rick Steves’s travel program. He was in Denmark, showing us the sights and sounds and people. One comment stood out, at least for me. The Danes, Steves suggested, willingly pay high taxes, but, and this is the interesting part, they demand a return on their investment. So far, that calculus seems to work, and Danish people enjoy splendid public services, including universal health care and transportation.

This got me thinking. We Americans generally loathe taxes, and we believe that ours are too high. Never mind that they are among the lowest of the industrial democracies. With few exceptions, we record our antipathy via the ballot box, as Washingtonians did again, passing the latest Tim Eyman super-majority tax requirement for Olympia. Otherwise, we vote Republican.

Could contempt for taxes be a consequence of poor return? When we look about us we see bridges and highways in disrepair. Our school buildings are falling apart. We have no well-functioning passenger trains to speak of. Our cities are generally dirty, because we don’t hire people to clean them. And everyone knows that our public education system doesn’t work; provide your own check list of dissatisfaction, whether it be the teachers’ fault or crowded classrooms or misguided curricula.

In our heads we may judge tax revenues adequate, since they’re already too high. So when considering the lousy state of public services, we instinctively resist giving the bastards any more money, which will surely be wasted or otherwise spent foolishly.

Consider this possibility. Imagine that there is a critical threshold of taxing, below which we get only mediocrity combined with persistent loathing of taxes, and above which we begin to realize societal value, the kind of value expected by the Scandinavians but rarely experienced here in America. How high could we go? (source of data below)

Emanuel Saez, French-born but now a professor of economics at Berkeley, wrote a provocative paper with his longtime collaborator, Thomas Piketty, arguing that marginal tax rates could be raised significantly (up to 80 percent) without adversely affecting economic growth.

The authors believe that extreme inequality is problematic in itself, thus warranting greater wealth distribution via a more progressive tax code. But for our purposes here, the extra revenues derived from higher tax rates would go to schools, transportation, health care, and other public services that the private sector is unable or unwilling to provide.

Of course, there is no way that Congress would even attempt to increase tax rates, and they’d surely run for the hills at the first mention of Scandinavia. Thus, we are consigned to mediocrity.

But at least we know that it’s possible to have a much better public sector and that citizens are evidently willing to pay for it. Just not in America—yet. Wait a minute, we used to pay much higher taxes and had good schools and roads. Ah, those were the days.

Inequality is good for you. Not. [u]

October 26, 2012 by davald

Okay, that’s not the explicit message the rich convey, but it’s close. Without our plutocrats the Rest of Us would be in even worse shape. You buy that, don’t you?

There should be no question about America’s inequality. It’s high and getting higher. Nor should there be any question that the extremely wealthy, the top 0.01 percent of households, extract the bulk of income from the economy. Let’s revisit some data (from Emmanuel Saez).

But despite the wealth gap, the Rest of Us haven’t fared so well. We see this clearly in the following chart:

 

Notice that flat line at the bottom. That’s the Rest of Us. And, yes, the average income of the top 0.01 percent surpassed $7 $19 million before the Great Recession. In 2010 it fell back to a piddly $5.9 $16.3 million. How can they possibly manage? Oh, and these numbers do not include capital gains, which comprise most of the super rich’s income.

Keep in mind that these charts reflect inflation-adjusted dollars. Going back to 1917, the earliest data year, the income of the bottom 90 percent has hardly budged.

Nobel Prize-winning economist Joseph Stiglitz tells New York Times readers that inequality actually stunts economic growth. He also reminds us that social and economic mobility in the U.S. is the lowest of all advanced industrial democracies. Then Prof. Stigltiz sets out to debunk five essential myths promulgated by Romney, the Republicans as a whole, and the capitalist class. All of the following are false:

  1. America is a land of opportunity.
  2. Trickle-down economics works.
  3. The rich are “job creators.”
  4. The costs of reducing inequality are so great that “we would be killing the goose that lays the golden egg” should we try to raise the incomes of the bottom 90 percent and lower them at the top.
  5. Because markets are efficient and self-regulating the government should not meddle in the economy.

In a recent Harper’s essay (subscription required) Jeffrey Madrick emphasizes the importance of raising taxes, especially on the wealthy. Americans pay about 26 percent of their income on taxes of all forms. The OECD average is 38 percent. Yet higher tax rates do not impede growth. Madrick:

…the high-tax economies grow about as fast as ours does, sometimes faster. Prosperous Denmark, Norway, and Sweden have tax rates well above 40 percent.

Suppose Americans were to pay the OECD average. Madrick says that over 10 years tax revenues would increase by $16 to $17 trillion. The Budget Control Act, which, if allowed to trigger, would reduce the deficit by only $1.5 trillion over the same period.

Now here’s a statement that’s sure to rankle the Randians. “Better government, not weaker government, is the path to prosperity.” The whacky Tea Partiers would prefer no government, except for a bloated defense and a larger criminal justice system. But if we are to make the investments necessary to restore the economy and, I dare say, “compete” with other countries—or at least improve our trade balance—then our governments need more dollars. Here’s Madrick:

Politicians and ideologues are playing a cruel game by keeping serious tax increases off the table, but it is especially hypocritical to do so in the name of fiscal responsibility. America’s budget problem is a revenue problem, not a spending problem. The current national conversation about tax hikes is a fine example of political deference to the rich and powerful. It is not good economics.

So, ladies and gentlemen, our first order of business is to raise taxes. The rich will do just fine, by the way, and the Rest of Us will benefit from a solvent Social Security system, better transportation, greener energy (Madrick recommends a carbon tax), and world-class schools.

Unless, of course, you really enjoy misery.

The peace dividend

October 17, 2012 by davald

With the collapse of the Berlin Wall and then the Soviet Union there was much talk of a “peace dividend.” The dollars presumably earmarked to defend against “the communist menace” would now be available to build schools, roads, and, well, a better society. It didn’t happen, of course. The financial interests tied to guns and bombs quickly dashed any notions of diverting Pentagon dollars to improving American lives.

But we see from the above chart that defense spending did stabilize after Clinton took office, or just after Russia reentered our vocabulary. Then along came 9/11 and two wars. Military brass and their benefactors drooled.

This next chart tracks annual changes in defense spending from the previous year. We’ll note sharp increases during G.W. Bush’s initial term and another spike just before he left office. Spending has dropped on a year-over-year basis since Obama became president.

 

The point here, of course, is that Congress always seems to find a way to maintain a bloated defense budget, regardless of circumstances. As for the underlying rationale, it makes one wonder.

During last night’s presidential fisticuffs Obama said that he would use dollars devoted to prosecuting the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan to fund education and infrastructure, which he opined were necessary to reinvigorate the national economy.

Both the Everett Herald and the Seattle Times ran an AP “fact check” on last night’s debate. Here is a relevant excerpt:

OBAMA: “Let’s take the money that we’ve been spending on war over the last decade to rebuild America, roads, bridges, schools. We do those things, not only is your future going to be bright, but America’s future is going to be bright as well.”

THE FACTS: What Obama didn’t mention is that much of the money that has been paying for the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan was borrowed. In fact, the government borrows nearly 40 cents for every dollar it spends. Thus using money that had been earmarked for wars to build schools and infrastructure would involve even more borrowing, adding to the federal deficit.

Like most of the Hempstead blather, promises of a new peace dividend will not be realized.

Washington state governor

October 16, 2012 by davald

Rumor has it that Democratic gubernatorial candidate Jay Inslee pales in side-by-side comparisons with his opponent, Republican Rob McKenna. Whether he’s being interviewed by editorial boards or various interest groups Inslee appears weak, ill-prepared, and unimaginative. McKenna, on the other hand, comes across as bright, energetic, and quick.

Now, as I said, I’m relying only on vague rumors here. While I have met Inslee and seen McKenna only on television, I have no personal experience for rendering the above judgments. I’m in the same position as most voters: dumb and dumber.

Yet, we have a problem in this either-or proposition based on personality only. When it comes to governor, for example, the position for which Inslee and McKenna are vying, there would seem to be two important questions: (1) What would each candidate want to do if elected? (2) What could each candidate do?

We should remember candidate Obama, with his rather bold set of promises, versus President Obama. While the latter has his share of accomplishments, they are but mere shadows of the hopes and dreams he encouraged and facilitated before assuming office. He disappointed many of his supporters, in large part because his opposition desired to achieve that effect, blocking legislative initiatives designed to advance his goals and objectives.

As a Republican, McKenna would surely wish to adopt the austerity regimen championed by his party’s faithful. That is, he would endeavor to cut government spending and lower taxes, all the while proclaiming that these two strategies—and perhaps these alone—would make us all prosper.

In the Voters’ Pamphlet McKenna rails against the state’s “burdensome tax system and redundant regulations,” which, he implies, impede economic growth. He “knows the private sector creates jobs, not government.”

Certainly the recession took a major toll on public-sector employment. State and local governments have continued to shed jobs, including teachers.

Moreover, the percentage of the total workforce employed by Washington state governments has also declined.

It seems difficult to argue that “bloated” governments are the problem, the reason that the economy is lagging. What about taxes?

People and businesses buying things and services represents nearly half of all tax revenue. However, when people lose their jobs, their wages are cut, and firms contract or shut down retail sales fall, which means that the state receives fewer dollars to fund schools (about 40 percent of expenditures) and other social services. Taxes on businesses (B&O) deliver much less revenue to the state, suggesting that they have less effect than McKenna suggests.

What McKenna doesn’t or can’t grasp is that the economy—whether national, state, or local—depends on those retail purchases. And it doesn’t matter whether people are working in the public or private sector. It matters that they have a job with sufficient income to buy food, shelter, clothing, and so on.

Moreover, governments can create jobs, provided they have adequate revenues. They can employ teachers, health care and transportation workers, and technicians to monitor and protect the environment. In turn, those public employees will spend their money, generating taxes.

Inslee, according to the Voters’ Pamphlet, has a “mission”: “to create a stronger and growing economy for Washington.” He would do so by emphasizing “innovations” and “revolutions” in technology and aerospace (read Boing). Inslee has long advocated renewable resources and would surely direct public resources to facilitate their development.

I’m going to reveal a bias here. I am not very excited about Inslee. He lacks administrative experience, most of all. Nor do I care for his government-based approach to renewable resource development; I would prefer a carbon tax, then let the innovators innovate.

Yet, I prefer Inslee to McKenna, and that choice has almost everything to do with just two facts. First, that McKenna is a Republican immediately disqualifies him from consideration. Why? Well, just turn on the television or open up a newspaper to learn what the GOP is about these days. The party’s ideas are crazy, false, or demonstrably wrong in effect (for example, that lower taxes necessarily spur economic growth).

Second, neither candidate can achieve much of anything without legislative support. Since I generally prefer the policies of Democrats to Republicans’ I want an Olympia with Democratic control. If McKenna wins and the opposition party retains majority standing, then he’s not likely to carry out his agenda—which would be a good thing. However, should Inslee prevail under the same circumstances, then those policies I prefer stand a better chance of becoming the law of the land, which is an even better thing.

“Relax”

October 8, 2012 by davald

As a relative newcomer to the urban environment, having been raised and lived for the most part in suburbia, I’m experiencing a learning curve of sorts. Mostly it’s a matter of toleration.

I have to remind myself that I do not live in one of the more civilized cities of the world like Stockholm, Copenhagen, Helsinki, or Amsterdam—at least I imagine them to be such, never having actually set foot in those places. No, I live in downtown Everett, Wash., a half-hour drive north of Seattle.

Older residents of this transitional berg referred to Everett as Old Mill Town, and for obvious reasons. The place was crawling with wood and pulp mills. But the last one, Kimberly Clark, just shuttered its doors. So now it’s just old, although civic leaders are trying to inch it forward into the 20th Century (sic).

I’m a walker, usually putting four-plus miles into a daily route. In so doing I encounter people and vehicles, along with cigarette smoke and dog shit, of which I’ve previously written.

Now I happen to believe that even anarchists obey stop signs. We all need a sense of order, right? You obey the laws. I obey the laws. And we’ll all get along just fine, albeit only barely.

Which brings me to this morning’s challenge to personal toleration.

I am walking along the main street on my return trip. I always pay attention to the traffic signals. I walk only when permitted. This morning was no different. Keep in mind that the weather is clear and warm, and, as you’ve noted, I’m an otherwise boring guy.

I’m waiting for the light to turn green. There is a car facing opposite whose driver is also waiting for the light. The light changes, I get my walk signal. I step into the crosswalk and reach about a third of the way when I notice the car making an abrupt left turn into my path. The motorist did not signal his intention. But there I am confronted with an accelerating multi-ton kluge of metal. My instinct is to stop, of course. But a wrong choice, since I’m dead meat—unless the driver finally notices me. (I can’t see him, because his windshield is tinted.) Mercifully, he stops, which gives me an opportunity to continue.

I could have let it go. But, no, I decide to stop ahead in the crosswalk to confront the motorist who is now moving safely past me. I’m, well, mad. He’s got his window open, so I can finally see him. He’s talking on his cell phone!—a clear violation. As he slips by he looks at me and says, “Relax.”

Relax? I wanted at that precise moment to yank him out of his car and pummel him—to a pulp?

What? Yours truly is a peace-lovin’ man. I dare say a socialist of the democratic kind. And yet I have this eruption of unmitigated rage seared with all thoughts of abject violence of the worst kind.

This toleration stuff is extremely hard.

The significance of party

October 3, 2012 by davald

The Everett Herald recently endorsed Republican Rob McKenna for Washington state governor. Based on the paper’s other endorsements and the views expressed in numerous editorials I can only surmise that the publisher made the call on the gubernatorial race and not the new editorial page editor, the son of the late senator Henry M. Jackson.

The endorsing editorial:

State Attorney General Rob McKenna, one of the most talented lawmakers of his generation, reflects those qualities of leadership. He is a convincing choice for Washington governor in 2012.

First off, McKenna hasn’t been doing much lawmaking, since he is the state’s current attorney general. However, in that position he dissed Governor Gregoire and the Democratically controlled state legislature in formally challenging the Affordable Care Act.

The Herald refers to “hidebound interests” that resist educational reform, a top priority of both the publisher and McKenna. They have in mind the teachers’ union, of course. McKenna, the paper believes, will challenge ” the preserved-in-amber mindset of Olympia’s political class.” That’s the leadership part, I gather.

There are those among us who are quick to assert that they vote for the person rather than, say, a political party. This may be a uniquely American trait, whether or not true, given our “preserved-in-amber” constitutional arrangement, which oddly de-emphasizes political parties while almost guaranteeing that there be only two of them. But we would be wrong to act on this disposition.

The most important quality of a candidate is his or her political affiliation, even if the lines separating Democrats and Republicans have become increasingly blurred over the last couple of decades, as both parties, for example, are beholden to the financial sector. After all, that’s where the money is, and in this Citizens United era, amassing campaign dollars is now an obsession, perhaps a necessary one. Despite this shared dependency, the parties have far different histories and ideologies.

The differences have come into sharp relief during this year’s presidential campaign, with Mitt Romney claiming that he cannot represent nearly half the population, “those people” who lack self-reliance and personal responsibility. He is of the One-percent, for the One-percent, and by the One-percent. Thus, he and his party consistently call for lower taxes, fewer regulations to interfere with the “job creators,” massive cuts in social spending, and even more money for the Pentagon. They detest unions, especially those of the public sector, and would eliminate minimum wage laws. If given the opportunity they would gut environmental laws and lavish even more subsidies on the fossil-fuel industry.

Yet, the opposite party can hardly be mistaken for socialists or social democrats. Though the Democrats have affinity for and draw much financial support from labor unions, they have all but ignored labor’s agenda, which includes removing obstacles to union participation and, I should think, repealing restrictions on secondary or sympathy strikes that are prevalent in Europe.

Nevertheless, Democrats would preserve and strengthen social welfare programs that help the less fortunate, those who were not born into wealth like Mitt Romney or the two Bushes. They would put both Social Security and Medicare on a much stronger financial footing, rather than raise the retirement age or cut benefits as the Republicans advocate.

Democrats support “spreading the wealth around,” increasing taxes on the wealthy to fund the aforementioned programs and reduce deficits. They would at least restore Clinton-era tax rates. They champion government investments in infrastructures, especially public schools and transportation. They are more likely to be Keynesian, advocating a government role in creating jobs to build roads, trains, and classrooms.

Rob McKenna is a Republican. That’s all the Rest of Us need to know as we enter the voting booth. Unless, you’re a One-percenter, then, by all means, vote your selfish, greedy interests.
