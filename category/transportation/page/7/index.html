Taxes and death spirals

September 12, 2012 by davald

Mayor Bloomberg says that Congress should get on with it and just pass the Simpson-Bowles recommendations, even though the committee’s report failed to gain sufficient support from its members. The mayor also believes that lower taxes help businesses.

Well, let’s see. I’ll try a little thought experiment.

Businessman A, who makes Widget X, believes that he could become more competitive if his company’s tax burden were less. To be sure, his costs would decline, all other things being equal, which would free dollars to, say, hire more workers to boost production, even though aggregate demand remains weak.

Businessman B also makes Widget X. If A gets a tax break, so will he. So, both A and B will have no comparative advantage against the other—again, all other things being equal.

Now we could introduce Businessman Chang. He’s Chinese, of course, and he also makes Widget X. Both A and B find it difficult to compete with Chang. So they push for lower taxes in hopes of taking some market share from Chang. A does not better B, nor B, A, but A and B, with lower taxes, better Chang—to limited extent and only temporarily.

Chan has a government that builds all the infrastructure he needs, from high-speed rail shipping, to modern factories, to adequate schools that train his workers. But A and B, because their taxes have been lowered—along with every other business person in America—have lousy infrastructures. The roads are congested or crumbling. Railways are clogged and unable to accommodate high-speed trains. Nor do the would-be workers have the requisite skills to improve production, because the schools are underfunded.

Both A and B press for even lower taxes to compensate for Chang’s advantages. All levels of government comply with their request. But the governments, in turn, have less and less tax revenues to provide those things that Chang takes for granted. Schools, roads, and other transportation options continue to fall apart. Nevertheless, A and B complain about the lousy infrastructure and the failing schools, as they keep lobbying their government representatives to do more with less and, of course, to further lower their taxes.

A and B have created their own death spiral. The more they clamor for and receive tax breaks, the more difficult it is for them to compete against Chang.

But A and B are idiots, of course. They never see beyond the tip of their noses. Indeed, they shout and scream that “they built it” and that they are “job creators” answerable to no one—if only government would reduce their tax burdens and stay out of their way.

For their parts, the different levels of government have no choice. The elected representatives depend on A and B for their campaign donations. Nor do they have any money to get in A and B’s way. They can’t pay for schools and roads and railways. And the governments can no longer invest in research to spur technological innovation.

Mr. Chang’s future looks bright. Not so much Mr. A and B’s.

Russell’s wisdom: why we fight

August 19, 2012 by davald

One essential primer is Bertrand Russell’s The Problems of Philosophy. Therein he provides a concise introduction to issues over which philosophers have long struggled. And if you have any familiarity with philosophy as an academic pursuit, you know that philosophers develop only tentative or provisional answers—such is the nature of the discipline. But if you’re patient, you’ll eventually uncover some kernels of wisdom, and Russell’s writings contain many.

For example, consider these words from the aforementioned book:

Material possessions can be taken by force and enjoyed by the robber. Spiritual possessions cannot be taken in this way. You may kill an artist or a thinker, but you cannot acquire his art or his thought. You may put a man to death because he loves his fellow-men, but you will not by so doing acquire the love which made his happiness. Force is impotent in such matters; it is only as regards material goods that it is effective. For this reason the men who believe in force are the men whose thoughts and desires are preoccupied with material goods.

Thus the conservative mind, since Republicans as a rule are obsessed with “material possessions.” What they have, they fear will be taken. What they lack, they strive to obtain—by almost any means. If they should rule, they will employ the nation’s considerable armaments to plunder the wealth of others. As for defending America’s fortunes, mostly owned by the privileged few, they will spare no expense.

They will not stop at the military or the police state. Their fear-cum-greed affects every fabric of our well-being.

In the hotly contested issue of educational reform, conservatives believe that students should learn those skills necessary to succeed in the acquisition of wealth or to tend to the care and feeding of the oligarchy. Critical thinking, the kind that would ultimately question status quo political arrangements, must be eliminated, since it poses a threat to the powerful. After all, the masses outnumber them.

Investing in the arts and music, except for the products of dead painters and composers, wastes dollars that should be earmarked for exploitation. Public dollars must never go toward self-expression or human development.*

On economic issues, merit determines all, and the rich possess a monopoly, since they made the rules and insist on punishing transgressors. At your workplace, you count for nothing, save for what you contribute to the wealth of your corporate benefactors. Drop any notion of unionism; Labor is evil, because it frustrates Capital’s unrelenting greed. Meanwhile, you are expected to work more hours and extract less from your effort.

 

 

Finally, the Rest of Us must stop worrying and learn to love:

  • decrepit school buildings
  • collapsing roads
  • urban ghettoes
  • ugly strip malls
  • pollution
  • global warming
  • no philosophy, no art, no music, no beauty (these are reserved for your masters, who are happy to pay dearly for them)
  • expensive and ineffective health care
  • mind-numbing TV reality shows
  • perpetual war
  • shit.

But take heart, your taxes are low.

Meritocracy and government

June 7, 2012 by davald

Michels’s grim conclusion was that it was impossible for any party, no matter its belief system, to bring about democracy in practice. Oligarchy was inevitable. For any kind of institution with a democratic base to consolidate the legitimacy it needs to exist, it must have an organization that delegates tasks. The rank and file will not have the time, energy, wherewithal or inclination to participate in the many, often minute decisions necessary to keep the institution functioning. In fact, effectiveness, Michels argues convincingly, requires that these tasks be delegated to a small group of people with enough power to make decisions of consequence for the entire membership. Over time, this bureaucracy becomes a kind of permanent, full-time cadre of leadership. “Without wishing it,” Michels says, there grows up a great “gulf which divides the leaders from the masses.” The leaders now control the tools with which to manipulate the opinion of the masses and subvert the organization’s democratic process. “Thus the leaders, who were at first no more than the executive organs of the collective, will soon emancipate themselves from the mass and become independent of its control.”

— excerpted in The Nation

Hayes is talking about Robert Michels, a German social theorist, who, born of wealth, nevertheless embarked on a political journey, moving further and further left. In each case he found that organization leads ineluctably to oligarchy, rule by a few.

In our own political systems, more properly described as representative government, we ostensibly select those we prefer to govern us. Hayes goes on to say about our current Congress, especially, that we are increasingly represented by elites beholden to monied interests, from which many, if not most, of them spring.

Hayes begins this lengthy excerpt chronicling his own experience as a young boy scoring well enough on a three-hour exam to gain entrance to Manhattan’s most elite educational institution, Hunter College High School, free to those few who attend. The only criterion for admittance is the score on a once-administered three-hour exam. As pure a meritocracy as we have in America. Or, at least it was. Over time, Hayes reports, New York City’s wealthy denizens invested in private tutors and test-taking classes for their young children. And the preparation and investments worked, so that the demographics of the school changed over time, becoming whiter and richer.

Meritocracy, Hayes suggests from his reading of Michels, requires adherence to two principles: difference and mobility. The first assumes that we have vastly different capabilities, which should be not only acknowledged but embraced. The second principle, mobility, assumes that, though we are different, we should all have equal opportunity to compete for the more attractive positions.

I’ll step in here with some comments. The above sounds an awful like conventional conservative thinking: the role of government is to ensure equal opportunity, but also to recognize that the better should prosper and the worse should suffer. After all, that’s what markets are all about. Scarce goods are distributed on the basis of merit rather than, as liberals would argue, to ensure fairness. Hayes has his own take:

But this ideal, appealing as it may be, runs up against the reality of what I’ll call the Iron Law of Meritocracy. The Iron Law of Meritocracy states that eventually the inequality produced by a meritocratic system will grow large enough to subvert the mechanisms of mobility. Unequal outcomes make equal opportunity impossible. The Principle of Difference will come to overwhelm the Principle of Mobility. Those who are able to climb up the ladder will find ways to pull it up after them, or to selectively lower it down to allow their friends, allies and kin to scramble up. In other words: “Who says meritocracy says oligarchy.”

America, for better or worse, and I believe strongly that it’s the latter, is enthrall to “The Iron Law of Meritocracy.” Born rich, you’re likely to stay that way. On the other end of the scale, you should have picked more successful parents.

In such a pseudo-merit system, those who are first when the metaphorical race begins are far more likely to finish first. They will have taken every advantage to ensure that once rich they will leave the world rich. Hayes:

But the Iron Law of Meritocracy makes a different prediction: that societies ordered around the meritocratic ideal will produce inequality without the attendant mobility. Indeed, over time, a society will become more unequal and less mobile as those who ascend its heights create means of preserving and defending their privilege and find ways to pass it on across generations. And this, as it turns out, is a pretty spot-on description of the trajectory of the American economy since the mid-1970s.

Notice the dates. They are too familiar to readers of this blog. They signal the start of the Great Divergence, a term that is both apt and increasingly popular. It coincides most sharply with Ronald Reagan’s occupation of the Oval Office.

While I doubt that he was the architect of the Great Divergence he certainly allowed it to happen, giving it a big nudge, certainly, with the wholesale firing of the air-traffic controllers. He also spoke the language of “meritocracy,” singing the praises of the private sector and its inbred social Darwinism while defaming government as “the problem” rather than the solution.

It has become all too fashionable to denigrate government as did Reagan. Conservatives would go further than crusade against it; they spend their waking hours conjuring up new ways to give effect to their attitude. Government, they say, must be put on a diet. It must be denied revenues, because its expenditures frustrate the “Iron Law of Meritocracy,” nurturing the undeserved at the expense of the privileged.

I hope that you see what’s at stake here, especially in light to what’s been happening in conservative-controlled states like Wisconsin and Ohio, where public employees are under siege. Government, for all its ills, is about the only mechanism available to rein in the extremes, rendering both the rich and poor less so.

Okay, this sounds like socialism! Put down your swords for a moment, wipe off the spittle, and allow yourself to be mentally transported to northern Europe. If you don’t like government doing things on behalf of the Rest of Us, you’ll hate Finland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, et al. Their governments are huge in comparison to the U.S.’s And what do their citizens get in return? Free education. Free health care. Scant poverty. Far less inequality. Higher living standards.

Well, yes, education and health care are not exactly “free.” The citizens of these lands pay higher taxes for these benefits, in most cases willingly if not enthusiastically.

But the Nordic people largely embrace the exchange: higher taxes and larger government for those things we Americans increasingly expect the private sector to provide, in accordance with the “Iron Law of Meritocracy.”

Mark my words. The more we support, either by action or inaction, the shrinking of government, the more the Rest of Us will suffer and the more wealth the already wealthy will siphon off from a protracted, sputtering economy.

Having issued this caveat, I quickly offer another. There are many obvious reasons why we should distrust our government, though not necessarily the concept of government. Congress is the home of “institutional corruption.” And as Hayes points out, regulatory capture is rife in D.C., with federal agencies serving as the source of future employees for the firms they are supposed to regulate.

I’ve got to believe that the Founding Fathers, Abe Lincoln, and certainly Teddy Roosevelt would be aghast at what has happened to the federal government, especially the deluge of billionaire dollars ensuring that the Rest of Us get screwed as the “malefactors of great wealth” extract greater shares of GDP. They would be appalled by Citizens United, which brought unbridled bribery into the open and onto the pages of the U.S. Code.

But in particular for those who cobbled together the Constitution, they would be horrified by how the very concept of democracy as they conceived it has been greatly sullied beyond their recognition. Of course, they wanted no part of monarchy, and none of the associated pomp and circumstance. And they also preferred a limited, less intrusive government than the one they had endured under King George. And they also believed in the entrepreneurial spirit, about fashioning a respectable life through dint of labor and discipline.

However, the virtues of self-reliance and self-actualization cannot be practiced today by the Rest of Us. Those attitudes are reserved for the extremely privileged, for the most part, and unavailable to the masses. We’ve also lost our sense of innovation and imagination, if we take economist Tyler Cowen’s word. With the Great Divergence we may have entered a protracted state of stagnation.

You see, handing everything over to the plutocracy has been bad for business. The plutocrats know no limits. If left to their own appetites and devices, they will cause the whole damn thing to come crashing down on the Rest of Us. After all, they came awfully close to doing that just a few short years ago. They must be constrained for their—and our—own good.

Who regulates, or at least is supposed to? Government, preferably one whose agents are themselves virtuous.

Who can lift the poor? Government.

Who can restrain the malefactors? Government.

Who can provide education, health care, and guaranteed retirement income? Government.

Who can enable scientific research? Government.

But that kind of government, now just a mental figment, can be realized only by a citizenry committed to those achievements. Yet, it’s proved to be far too easy to manufacture the consent of the governed. Actually, we’re pretty damn cheap.

Why do we collectively put up with this? Is the challenge too large? Have we already lost? Did we ever struggle in service to the Rest of Us?

I guess that’s why some people feel the need to occupy something. Doing something—anything—is better than nothing. Lean out your window and scream that you’ve had enough.

We’ve recently witnessed real revolutions. After suffering a long train of autocratic abuse, citizens of the Middle East erupted, literally taking to the streets then occupying them. Something or perhaps several things sparked the collective outrage.

What might be the spark that sets off the Rest of Us? How low do we have to go before we scream “enough”?

Or, horror of horror, perhaps most Americans are quite comfortable with the growing inequality, the decline in public education, the erosion of our public infrastructure. Perhaps they have other things to do than to arrest these developments. Maybe, just maybe, they like not only oligarchy but the plutocrats who run it.

Spark. Where are you?

Right or left?

May 30, 2012 by davald

Add another item to the category “things I didn’t know.” This one on how countries came to drive on the left or right of highways.

As a consumer of many British video offerings, including stuff on the Tudors, I found this bit interesting:

In feudal Europe, people would travel on the left side of the road because most people were right-handed and wanted the arm that they used for their sword (or other weapon) between them and anyone who approached. As a result, traveling on the left became customary.

Napoleon Bonaparte happened to be left-handed. So he ordered his troops and the countries he conquered to travel on the right.

I don’t know about Japan, though. I think they drive on the left, just as the British do. But the Japanese were not colonized by England. Their samurais must have been right-handed, too.

The trouble with democracy

April 26, 2012 by davald

Let’s face it. If we governed ourselves by plebiscites, we’d be in a much worse mess than we’re already in. This is especially true when it comes to raising taxes and we schmucks get a say in the matter.

We like public services, don’t we? We want our kids to attend good schools. We’d like college to be available and affordable. We like having Medicare and Social Security and nice roads to travel on and police and fire departments to protect us.

But here’s our collective problem. We don’t like taxes, and our loathing for taxes apparently exceeds our desires for public services.

A case in point. Governor Jerry Brown has proposed a ballot initiative that would raise taxes on Californians making more than $250,000 a year. Polls show that a slim majority will support the measure. We’ll see. But polls also reveal a pervasive antipathy to increasing taxes on everyone else.

My home state has gone to hell. It was once atop the union, under Brown’s father, with the best schools and transportation systems. Now it ranks with Mississippi on education. Forget about new roads or fixing old ones. The public sector has been squeezed to the bone, as successive plebiscites have cut revenues to Sacramento.

Those who suggest that you get what you pay for are deemed “socialists” of the worst kind. Yet, the same people no doubt rail against their unworkable and underperforming government and criticize cash-strapped schools and their awful teachers. They want better, but will pay only for mediocrity. I guess it gives them something to complain about.

I know, we live in a land of freedom, where the individual is sacrosanct. Yet, somewhere along the way we lost our heads, expecting miracles on the cheap.

For a start, I’d abolish the initiative process, which is even worse in California than in Washington. In the Golden State a successful initiative takes on super status; it can be repealed or modified only by another initiative. At least in Washington, the legislature gets a crack at whacky plebiscites two years after passage.

We’d like to believe that the people are wise, despite overwhelming evidence to the contrary. We don’t know shit, and we’re content to elect representatives who know even less.

But at least legislatures provide the opportunity to argue and debate. Well…maybe not so much these days. We’re a society so polarized that compromise is impossible. Just look, if you can, at our Congress.

No, I am not a happy camper.

Green vehicles

April 5, 2012 by davald

The New York Times reports that owners of more efficient vehicles may not recoup their investment before they replace them. That holds true at even $5/gal. gas prices; they’re currently at $4 or so.

But the article ignores externalities. Burning gasoline emits harmful pollutants, including greenhouse gases. Would incorporating an implicit carbon tax justify the additional expense of buying a hybrid version? As you’ll see, if you can stick with me for a bit, is that the Prius is a better deal than, say, a Camry—even without imposing a carbon tax. But the Civic hybrid may or may not justify its higher costs over the non-hybrid model. The Times says no; my calculations suggest barely. So, let’s take a look at carbon emissions.

Each gallon of combusted gasoline releases about 20 pounds of carbon dioxide, the principal greenhouse gas. If a conventional vehicle averages 20 mpg, a 60-mile round-trip commute would consume three gallons and contribute 60 pounds of carbon dioxide. Let’s assume a five-day workweek for 50 weeks a year. That adds up to 15,000 pounds of carbon dioxide annually, or 7.5 tons.

What is the “value” of a ton of CO2? Estimates vary. The Carbon Tax Center includes this table showing different proposed taxes.

Looking at the 2020 column we see that the tax ranges from a low of $22.31/ton to $115/ton. In our above example, the lowest tax would add $167.33 to the cost of an annual commute. Using the highest figure, that additional cost would be $862.50.

Assuming gas at $4 a gallon, a year’s worth of commuting in a 20-mpg car would cost $3,000 at the pump (3 gallons/day x five days/wk. x 50 wks. x $4/gal.). Adding in the carbon tax yields a range of $3,167.33 to $3,862.50, or between 5.6 percent and 29 percent more than the fuel costs alone.

My wife and I happen to drive a Toyota Prius. I’ll assume the New York Times‘ price of $23,537, which is roughly what we paid for ours. The Times estimates that the car pays for itself in 1.8 years, without factoring in a carbon tax. We’ve been getting about 41 to 45 mpg, depending on which one of us is driving and the nature of the trip, whether it’s mostly freeway or in-town. The Times assumed 49.7 mpg, which may be the case with the latest model.

For comparison’s sake, I’ll assume 40 mpg for the Prius, or twice the rate used in the above example. With the same commute numbers, I’d use half the gasoline, or 375 gallons per year rather than 750. At $4/gal. for gas, I’d save $1,500 a year.

But I’d also cut carbon emissions in half, from 15,000 pounds/yr. to 7,500 pounds (3.75 tons). At the lower tax rate, the Prius’s carbon tax would be $83.66; at the highest, the tax would be $431.25 per year.

The Times assumed that the average vehicle owner keeps his or her car for six years. Over that period the Prius costs $36,804, including a $50/ton carbon tax. A sample vehicle, one costing $28,400, the average price American’s spend on an automobile, and obtaining 20 mpg, would cost $44,464 over the same period. Clearly the Prius saves us money. (I’ve assumed a $5,000 down payment and 5% interest over 48 months.)

Let’s look at another comparison, one done by the Times. In this case we’ll use a Honda Civic and its companion hybrid. The former costs $18,675 at the dealer, according to the Times; the hybrid model costs $23,999. Assuming a $50/ton carbon tax and $4 for a gallon of gasoline, the six-year costs of the Civic would be $32,512; the hybrid, $31,503. The Times calculated a payback for the hybrid 12.1 years, or twice as long as most people hang onto their vehicles before replacing them. When I ran the numbers, even assuming no carbon tax, the Civic Hybrid costs slightly less over the six-year period than the conventional model. (I can’t explain the disagreement, although it’s likely I messed up—somehow.)

It appears, then, that if one wanted to save money and reduce emissions, stick with the Prius. Electric vehicles, says the Times, won’t pay back their premium for two and a half decades. But, once again, I ran the numbers.

The Nissan Leaf, for example, gets about 3 miles per kilowatt-hour. The 60-mile commute used in the above example would require 20 kWh. At a retail rate of 8.3 cents/kWh (the PUD’s retail rate), that works out to $1.66, versus the Prius’s $6 for the back-and-forth trip. The Times compares the Leaf with the Nissan Versa, a gas-powered car. I calculated the six-year costs of the Versa at $25,242; the Leaf, $31,788—again, assuming a $50/ton carbon tax. With no tax on carbon, the six-year difference is about $8,000 in favor of the Versa.

Thus, a purely electric vehicle neither saves money nor captures the externalities of carbon dioxide emissions, even with a $50/ton tax. How high a carbon tax would be needed to equalize the six-year costs between the Versa and the Leaf? Would you believe $294/ton? This is more than twice the highest proposed tax in the above table.

By the way, my wife and I love our Prius. It’s roomy, comfortable, with surprising spunk. Love the hatchback, too.

Back to trains for a moment

March 26, 2012 by davald

But to look for efficiency, in Judt’s view, is to miss the point, or at least the most important point. Too much concern with economic efficiency, at the expense of all else, is precisely what is wrong with intellectuals today. Intellectuals, he claims, stopped asking around the 1970s “if something is right or wrong.” Instead they care far too much “whether or not it improves productivity.” Even, he says, if the kind of privatization demanded by neoliberals since the Reagan-Thatcher era “were the economic success claimed for it (and it most decidedly is not), it remains a moral catastrophe in the making”(my italics).

— Ian Baruma, New York Review of Books

Tony Judt mourned the loss of public-supported transportation systems, but especially the privatization of UK railroads under Thatcher. He believed that trains were the “creator of sociability.”

I wonder. Could it not be the other way round, that we people have to be social or communal or collectivist-thinking in order to establish and maintain good publicly supported transportation?

It seems to me that we have two circles with respect to trains. One is vicious. The other is virtuous. We are the former. Europe, mostly, is the latter. Historical conditions are likely responsible for determining whether or not a country has high or low population density. Europe, certainly, is far more dense than the U.S. Higher density is more conducive to public transport; lower density not so much.

The U.S.’s relatively low population density lends itself to a stronger sense of individualism. This is certainly true in the Midwest. Yet, in our major cities, mostly along the coasts, the clustered populations are more liberal and collectivist. Manhattan has a vast subway system, which is heavily subsidized by taxes. Same with Chicago and its overhead train network. San Francisco has its cable cars and BART terminus, though the Bay Area used to be home to splendid train systems before the tire, oil, and car companies conspired to rip them out. Ditto Los Angeles.

Let’s look at a map showing population densities across the globe (from Wikipedia).

Almost all of Europe has higher densities than the U.S., which is also geographically larger.

The Economist magazine has a nice little iPad app, “World in Figures.” (It’s still a bit buggy, however. Frequent crashes.) I discovered this information about train ridership.

You’ll notice that the U.S. is nowhere to be found on this chart. It’s no surprise that most of the countries on this list are European, although Asian countries, notably Japan, make the list.

Also, with the exception of China, all of these countries above have lower Gini indexes than the U.S.’s. Coincidence? I doubt it.

Americans, by and large, have swallowed the efficiency test, as Judt suggests. Decisions should be made on the basis of maximum bang for the buck. But since we are more individualistic in the U.S. than Europeans, we are unable to appreciate the immense waste that we produce in the aggregate. Thus, we have freeways clogged with cars, while Europeans climb aboard ubiquitous trains, unimpeded by traffic jams. Our “system” of cars-cum-roads is a very expensive way to transport our body from here to there and back again. We don’t see this, because we’re focused exclusively on ourselves. What’s the most efficient way for me to go from A to B?

Yet, what alternatives do we have? We are spread so thin that a vast rail network would be prohibitively expensive. We’d have to focus on major cities first, and there aren’t that many. Connecting them by rail, then perhaps building parking lots near train stations to accommodate Seattle-bound commuters driving their cars from outlying towns.

I get ahead of myself. There is no “we” in the conversation, and that’s my point. It’s all about ‘I” or “me.” Therefore, the vicious circle.

Or perhaps we’re finally moving in the right direction, as this link suggests. I neglected to include the gas-price factor. As gasoline prices rise so do public transportation trips. I paid over $12 for three gallons of gas over the weekend (we drive a Prius). I saw one guy with a big truck paying $80 for 20 gallons. I won’t be taking a train any time soon, since I mostly walk to wherever I need to go. Will that truck driver be found on a bus in the near future as gas prices continue soaring through the summer? I’m guessing no.

Europeans have always paid higher gas prices than Americans. That surely contributes to better and more heavily used public transport.

Therefore, I say, reduce the supply of oil, thus driving up the price of gas, thus spurring more trains and busses. A different virtuous circle that could work.

Stop the drilling! The planet will thank us for it.

More trains requires more brains

March 22, 2012 by davald

There was an article in yesterday’s Everett Herald about the Sounder train that runs between Everett and Seattle. It’s not cheap. A former planner estimates that each rider costs $57, though the fare is only $7.50. Now catch this:

One of the biggest expenses for the system is $258 million the agency paid up front to Burlington Northern Santa Fe Railroad for the permanent right to use the rail lines between Seattle and Everett.

I took the Sounder once. It’s a pleasant enough ride, taking one along Puget Sound and providing spectacular views of the water and the mountains. Yet, the route reflects our collective stupidity.

There aren’t very many people who live next to the railroad track on the shoreline. We taxpayers shelled out $258 million to lease private tracks that mostly serve commercial purposes. The Sounder can run only four times in the morning to Seattle and four times coming back, because the railroad company needs the rails to haul freight.

I’ve never been to Europe. Heck I rarely travel outside my downtown apartment. On occasion, though, I catch Rick Steves’s programs. There are trains all over the continent moving millions from town to village to city and from country to country—along dedicated tracks.

Before he died, Tony Judt railed against the threats to rails.

In continental Europe, despite some closures and reductions in services, a culture of public provision and a slower rate of automobile growth preserved most of the railway infrastructure. In most of the rest of the world, poverty and backwardness helped preserve the train as the only practicable form of mass communication.

America desperately needs public rails, and I’d run them with electricity generated by renewable resources like hydro. It’s dumb to take a plane from Seattle to Portland, and almost as dumb to drive your car. But how nice and convenient it would be to hop a train when you wanted to transport yourself those 300 174 miles.

How crucial timing is. We paved over paradise, ripping out rails in the process. How expensive it is now to run trains where we need them—between population clusters. Yet, it was okay to build a freeway right through the middle of Seattle, displacing thousands and destroying neighborhoods, my wife’s family’s included. But we love cars, right? Such freedom. We can drive anywhere at any time. This worked okay for a while, until too many humans wanting to do the same thing made driving a curse rather than a pleasure.

We’re paying four bucks for a gallon of gas these days. Lets’ say that you average 20 miles per gallon. A trip of 100 miles will consume five gallons and cost you $20. If you commute 30 miles to your job, by the end of the week you will have put 300 miles on your car. That’s $60.

You’re not alone, though. You’re joined by thousands of other commuters who transform the freeway into a barely moving parking lot. Let’s suppose there are 10,000 people driving to Seattle along I-5 each morning and evening and they average 30 miles each way. In total they will travel in a typical work week three million miles (60 miles x 5 days x 10,000). At 20 mpg, they will have spent in the aggregate $600,000. Their combined monthly bill for gasoline alone is $2.4 million. Over 12 months they will have paid $28.8 million just for gas.

Can you imagine what kind of rail system you could buy for $28.8 million a year? If you were to invest that amount each year at five percent interest over three decades you’d have over $2 billion.

Actually, there are more than 250,000 cars that pass each day by milepost 169, which is downtown Seattle. So we could multiply the above numbers by 25, and we’d get $720 million. Now we’re up to $50 billion. That could buy a pretty nice rail system paralleling the I-5 corridor.

But we don’t think this way—collectively, I mean. We are decided individualists, so we take into account only our particular costs of transportation. We buy a car. We purchased gas for it. We pay the maintenance costs, and so on. Few of us bother to consider the aggregate costs of all of us. Fewer still think about what we could collectively do differently with all that money.

Instead, if a politician said that she wanted to build a splendid rail system and it would cost $50 billion, we’d tell her to go to hell. That’s too much money. Period.

Well, Californians voted to spend about $10 billion, or a fifth of the above amount, on a high-speed rail system connecting Los Angeles, San Francisco, and Sacramento. If, and it’s a big if, the project is completed and its total costs are even double the bond issue, the collective value would exceed the sum of all the individual costs of those traveling by car to and from those major cities.

Americans, for all our strutting nationalism, can’t build things any more, even when we could actually reduce our societal costs. Building railroads is just one example. We don’t like planning. We don’t like government. We don’t like big ideas, period. Unless our leaders call for war. Then no expenditure is excessive, even if total spending soars into the trillions of dollars.

I have a hunch that keeping us divided, confined to our own individual, impoverished lives, allows us to be conquered by you know who. As the Rest of Us eat our own, making choices in our own perceived best interest while ignoring the sum of our costs, the One-percenters steal us blind.

To overcome this, in my humble opinion, requires us to get smarter. And we get smarter by thinking collectively, communally, and not as individual agents in a huge scheme designed and operated by just a thin slice of the population, those making millions and billions siphoned from the Rest of Us.

Or, you can continue pursuing the myth of free agency, believing that you are master of your own destiny. The One-pecenters prefer things that way.

A tale of two states

January 11, 2012 by davald

I was fortunate enough to have grown up in California under governor Pat Brown and University of California president Clark Kerr. Education was free, well-funded, and available, even at the tertiary level. Sacramento seemed to have enough money to pay for good schools plus transportation and social services.

But, like Washington state, California allows citizens to write initiatives and place them on ballots. Unlike Washington, however, California’s successful initiatives cannot be repealed or modified by state government. Californians would eventually rue the day that they were given such power, because the halcyon days gave way to a string of crises that now render the state almost ungovernable. It began with the passage of Proposition 13.

With its economy booming (this in the decades of the 50s and 60s), housing prices began their steady climb along with a proliferation of sprawling homes and rising property taxes. Enough, the people said, passing Howard Jarvis’s ballot proposal. Property taxes would be capped at one percent of 1975 assessments, with increases limited to two percent per annum, as long as the owner remained in his house. This had the predictable effect of gradually reducing government revenues. Sacramento was forced to curtail public services.

While the reduction in property taxes would receive the bigger play, the more pernicious feature of Proposition 13 was to require a two-thirds vote of the state legislature to raise taxes. Sound familiar?

Over subsequent years, interest groups, including the teachers’ unions, persuaded the voters to pass a series of initiatives that compelled the legislature to prioritize funding. Fiscal obligations changed, but without new sources of revenue. Sacramento had very little financial discretion. So it robbed Peter to pay Paul.

The Great Recession took a major toll on California, resulting in massive budget shortfalls, government layoffs, and sharply reduced public services. Like Olympia, Sacramento could only cut to balance the budget.

California’s miserable experience is being replicated here in Washington. The one difference, as mentioned above, is that our state legislature can amend or repeal citizens’ initiatives after two years. Washington voters have repeatedly required a two-thirds majority to increase taxes, only to have the matter repealed two sessions later.

Washington is now under the super-majority restriction. Yet, it must balance its budget, which means only cutting is allowed.

However, the state’s constitution makes public education the legislature’s “paramount” duty. After Olympia severely cut funding of public education a group of citizens sued. A few weeks ago the state’s supreme court issued a unanimous opinion. It upheld the constitutional mandate and warned the legislature that it cannot further reduce the education budget. Moreover, the ruling (pdf) states:

  • The word “ample” in article IX, section 1 provides a broad constitutional guideline meaning fully, sufficient, and considerably more than just adequate.
  • Ample funding for basic education must be accomplished by means of dependable and regular tax sources.

The lead plaintiff in the successful lawsuit writes in a Seattle Times op-ed:

Legislators have a sworn duty to uphold that mandate. And they now have a Supreme Court decision that reaffirms their constitutional duty to amply fund K-12 education first. They must therefore take the K-12 budget off the table as they consider cuts this session, and start increasing public-school funding now in order to meet the 2018 deadline of amply funding public schools with “regular and dependable tax sources.”

“The court cannot idly stand by as the Legislature makes unfulfilled promises for reform,” the Supreme Court said in retaining jurisdiction to enforce its ruling.

What can the legislature do, given its inability to raise taxes? Should it stop funding services to the poor? What about transportation funding?*

Governor Gregoire has hinted at two strategies: Ask the voters to increase taxes to fund education (subject to a simple majority vote); petition the supreme court to advise on whether or not the constitutional mandate trumps the two-thirds majority provision.

People’s preferences are as contradictory as they are fickle. There are entire communities that repeatedly turn thumbs down on school levies, Marysville being one of them, even as they complain about the quality of education. My guess is that surveys would show that the general public supports education, but would balk when asked to pay for it. Thus, I don’t have much hope for the public’s agreeing to tax themselves further.

As for the supreme court’s rendering an opinion that would essentially nullify the two-thirds majority requirement when it comes to education funding, I can only guess. Could it carve out an exception for the state’s “paramount” duty? We’ll have to wait and see.

Meanwhile, Olympia faces on ongoing budget challenge: inadequate revenues to offset expenditures. It’s in a short session this year, with few expecting major initiatives to pass, given the need to make further cuts.

If I could play king for a day, I’d abolish the initiative process, establish a unicameral state parliament, the leader of which would serve as governor, and enact proportional representation. I believe that such a government could get things done and fairly quickly. Whether they act for good or ill would depend on whom we elect.

Just dreaming, of course.

* Ironically, she’s also proposed that the legislature pass a 10-year, $3.6 billion transportation package.

To help pay for the package, she asked the Legislature to increase some fees, including adding a $100 fee to electric vehicles and passing a $1.50 fee on every barrel of oil produced in Washington.

Nevertheless, this is hardly adequate to the challenge. As the Times writes:

Gregoire’s proposal is modest compared to a recent report from a transportation task force she convened to examine the issue. The task force determined that state transportation needs in the next 10 years — including road, bridge and ferry projects — would cost up to $21 billion.

Good luck with this, Bay Area

January 3, 2012 by davald

I grew up in the Bay Area, specifically Concord, Calif. (Mention of this typically invokes puzzlement. But when I say that my hometown neighbors Walnut Creek the mental lights return.) The eastern part of this region is quintessential sprawl. Over the last 60 years massive housing tracts have gobbled up agricultural land. They are interconnected by thousands of miles of asphalt, enabling monumental traffic jams each weekday morning and evening as mindless workers take the journey from their garages to San Francisco offices or blue-collar venues along the waterfronts, then return at the end of the day. And the days are long, with commute times easily exceeding one hour each direction.

You can appreciate that this haphazard arrangement between suburban sleep and metropolitan employment wastes time, money, energy, and the environment. There’s got to be a better way.

To the rescue, however too little or too late, is a new initiative called Bay Area One. As this article suggests the challenge is “daunting.” That ostensible challenge is to reduce transportation-related carbon emissions. The trick is to build up rather than out, curbing reliance on private automobiles, a remarkably inefficient means to get from A to B.

Another 2 million residents are expected to live in the Bay Area by 2035, with most of them getting around by automobile, the source of an estimated 41 percent of greenhouse gas emissions in the region.

Planners say they want to steer new development toward public transit corridors and stations and ease reliance on the auto.

Bay Area One is a joint effort of the Metropolitan Transportation Commission and the Association of Bay Area Governments. Neither possesses the authority to direct new patterns of urban development. But they do hold the purse strings. Judicious distribution of transportation dollars, it is hoped, will lead to increased population density situated near and around public transit hubs.

Not everyone is on board. Opposition comes from—who else?—real estate and home-building entities.

“They base their utopian model on high-density housing with shops underneath, no parking, but a lot of cycling and walking,” said Heather Gass, an Alamo real estate saleswoman, in a blog post critical of the growth plan. “What these people don’t seem to understand is that people move to the suburbs to get away from this type of urban lifestyle.”

Invoking the Big Brother specter, Gass adds: “At least the Soviet Union’s big plans were for five years. This plan will transform our area for 25 years.”

These are the same folks who fought Washington state’s Growth Management Act, which attempts to confine population growth to urban centers. If you find yourself on I-5 trying to make your way to Seattle you can reach your own conclusion as to the Act’s success.

We still have suburban tracts, but they tend to be smaller than before. The housing industry has managed to create greater density, but not in the cities so much as in the suburbs—bigger houses on tinier lots. We still lack adequate public transportation; so there is no viable alternative to the automobile—thus, more cars and traffic congestion. Nice try, Olympia.

The Bay Area has B.A.R.T., a rapid transit system that alleviates some highway congestion. But it’s hardly adequate, as the following anecdote underscores.

My brother in law was a San Francisco cop who lived in Antioch, about 50 miles from Baghdad by the Bay. As a police officer he was able to ride B.A.R.T. for free. However, to get a seat he drove his car into Concord, where he caught an outbound train. When it arrived in Antioch, the terminus, he simply remained for the trip into the city.

There is always a chicken-egg syndrome in new urbanism. Attracting existing or would-be suburbanites into downtowns—like Everett, where I currently reside—requires amenities (e.g., entertainment, restaurants, public safety). But the amenities can only be built and sustained with more city dwellers. Who goes first?

Neither cities nor suburbs were deliberately planned; they evolved. Seattle, for example, began as a shipping port, since it was on the water and proximate to raw materials—lumber, for the most part (“skid row“). The growth of suburbia coincided with the Eisenhower-era federal highway act and its thousands of miles of cement corridors. As it became more expensive and probably dirtier and scarier to live in cities, young couples sought a cheaper and cleaner alternative to raise families. Since land cost less as one got farther from the city, suburbia sprouted on dirt once farmed. (At the same time agriculture became more efficient requiring less acreage, which freed up large, easily convertible areas for houses and roads.)

Now many of us recognize that suburbia is unsustainable—too many carbons. And those carbons are becoming ever more costly at the pump and to the environment.

But old habits die hard. Moreover, there are substantial financial interests wedded to the status quo (e.g., oil and car companies) along with new generations pursuing their “American dream.”

My guess, hardly sanguine, is that even if government efforts to change our inherited arrangement succeed they will be too little and much too late. Our whole economy runs on oil, and oil is our doom.
