The right rails against rails

September 4, 2013 by davald

George F. Will doesn’t like trains because they facilitate collectivism, or some such rot. Washington state governor Jay Inslee wanted to replace the Columbia River bridge that connects Oregon and add rails, a move supported by Oregon governor Kitzhaber. But the Republican-dominated Washington senate balked, torpedoing the entire transportation bill. Known as the Columbia River Crossing, the project was opposed by those who don’t like trains.

The Senate majority caucus opposed putting light rail on the bridge…

Ever haughty, nominal Democrat Rodney Tom, who joined forces with the Republicans before the start of this past year’s session, was quoted by the Seattle Times:

“From day one, we told the governor that CRC was a nonstarter in the Senate,” Tom said. “He didn’t quite get that message. I’m glad he’s hearing it now.”

Channeling Mr. Will, Mr. Tom? By the way, transportation in Washington State is an abysmal mess, with too many people driving too many cars on too few lanes—much of which would be relieved with an extensive rail system; and far cheaper in the long run than our persistent addiction to the automobile.

Today’s climate dots

August 20, 2013 by davald

An advance copy of the IPCC’s latest draft document reveals more ominous projections. For example, if we continue to pump carbon dioxide into the atmosphere at current and expected rates, sea levels will rise by as much as three feet by the end of the century. Not a good time to invest in Florida real estate, I suspect. From the New York Times:

The level of carbon dioxide, the main greenhouse gas, is up 41 percent since the Industrial Revolution, and if present trends continue it could double in a matter of decades.

Warming the entire planet by 5 degrees Fahrenheit would add a stupendous amount of energy to the climate system. Scientists say the increase would be greater over land and might exceed 10 degrees at the poles.

They add that such an increase would lead to widespread melting of land ice, extreme heat waves, difficulty growing food and massive changes in plant and animal life, probably including a wave of extinctions.

This report and others on climate change offer a bone: cut emissions now or very soon and disaster may be averted. Easier said than done. The world shows no signs whatsoever of curtailing its energy production and consumption.

The U.S. Energy Information Agency (EIA) publishes an annual report on global energy. You’ll find the most recent here (pdf). Let’s look at some of its charts.

The first shows historical and projected consumption in quadrillion Btu.

Now let’s look at coal consumption, again in quadrillion Btu.

Finally, we have to look at carbon emissions.

It’s the last chart that should bother us, if we care at all about the well-being of the planet and our progeny. The New York Times:

They add that such an increase would lead to widespread melting of land ice, extreme heat waves, difficulty growing food and massive changes in plant and animal life, probably including a wave of extinctions.

So, what are we doing about this?

Despite accumulating evidence that increases the likelihood the planet is warming and that most of that warming results from human activity, we still have our deniers, and they are all too plentiful and feral in their contrariness. Many of them sit in Congress. Imagine that.

Yet, there is too little appreciation for the inertial quality of our everyday habits. We need energy and expect that it will be available now and forever. In looking at the contributions of China to global coal consumption, we should understand that much of that goes toward manufacturing products that we purchase and use, including a myriad electronic gadgets. Indeed, our devices have become routine as they have become ubiquitous. In the aggregate our appetite for technology (and cars and travel and comfortable space) continues to grow. Since that appetite requires energy, the future promises more, not less, carbon emissions.

Here in western Washington, controversy surrounds multiple proposed coal export terminals. The idea is to ship coal extracted in Wyoming to China via boats. That requires trains in the interim and therein lies one aspect of the controversy. After all, who wants dozens of mile-long trains rumbling through our towns and cities?

Well, there are folks who do. They cite the economic benefits of more coal trains.

Seattle mayor Mike McGinn commissioned a coal study to determine the costs and benefits of increased coal-train traffic. According to the Seattle Times, he sat on the final report for months, until the newspaper filed a request. The Times:

The study itself paints a mixed economic picture for the plan to run up to 18 more coal trains a day through Seattle on the way to a proposed new Gateway Pacific Terminal in Cherry Point, Whatcom County.

Once complete, the proposed terminal would export up to 48 million tons of a coal a year to Asia.

The 58-page study, focused solely on Seattle impacts, found that the terminal would trigger some $28 million in permitting, engineering and public-relations work during construction, with an additional $2.4 million in annual, ongoing new jobs created.

The authors of the report were apparently not asked to determine the environmental impacts of burning all that extra coal. And it really doesn’t matter where it is burned, whether here in Washington or in China—the carbon emitted enters the atmosphere adding to the greenhouse effect.

McGinn knows as much, as do many Seattleites, I would assume. Burning 48 million tons of coal does have consequences. Among them, 137 million tons of carbon dioxide.

World, that’s a problem.

Why inequality?

August 16, 2013 by davald

Few doubt that wealth is more heavily concentrated in the top rungs of the population than amongst the Rest of Us. What accounts for the unequal distribution?

Some have suggested that it’s technology or globalization or some combination. If that were true, then we would expect to see heightened inequality in other industrial democracies. But that’s not the case, for the most part.

A recent paper suggests four factors contributing to maldistribution:

The first is tax policy: top tax rates have moved in the opposite direction from top pre-tax income shares. The second factor is a richer view of the labor market, where we have contrasted the standard supply-side model with the alternative possibility that there may have been changes to bargaining power and greater individualization of pay. Tax cuts may have led managerial ener- gies to be diverted to increasing their remuneration at the expense of enterprise growth and employment. The third factor is capital income. In Europe—but less so in the United States—private wealth (relative to national income) has followed a spectacular U-shaped path over time, and inherited wealth may be making a return, implying that inheritance and capital income taxation will become again central policy tools for curbing inequality. The final, little-investigated, element is the correlation between earned income and capital income, which have become more closely associated in the United States.

The authors include a chart that correlates marginal tax rates and percentage of income going to the top one percent.

The second factor needs parsing. I believe the authors are referring to declining union participation rates, which would erode worker’s power vis-a-vis management’s. We might say that employees are mostly on their own these days, unable, if not also unwilling, to rely on collective bargaining.

Inherited wealth is playing a larger role more recently, allowing the rich to get richer. Also, and to the final factor, owning assets (including financial instruments) correlates highly with income, especially in the United States.

Here’s income.

Here’s capital.

In the U.S. we have a fiscal problem. Republicans would have us believe that the government simply spends too much. But the more obvious explanation is declining revenues. As the rich have gotten richer, they’re paying a smaller share of their income in taxes. So we not only increase public debt we can’t afford to pay for public services, including transportation and schools.

Begin with a whine

August 14, 2013 by davald

Architect Mark Hinshaw is writing for Crosscut a series on three Puget Sound cities in the midst of transformation from ugly to livable. They are: Tacoma, Bellevue, and Bremerton.

About Bremerton he writes:

Bremerton might have followed the pattern of countless small towns across the country that have been gutted by big box stores, strip malls and relentless sprawl. Instead, Bremerton reversed its downward spiral.

The remarkable turnaround required some bold actions. Despite previous failed efforts to infuse new life into the old town, a handful of civic leaders took charge and remade Bremerton.

How does a community breed “civic leaders” who take charge of a city’s future? As a resident of Everett, I ask, Where are ours?

A departed colleague believed that Everett has a long history of depending on federal government largesse, most recently the Navy’s homeport. It has never allowed itself the challenge and the opportunity to think things anew with an eye toward creating a sustainable, walkable, and livable downtown—organically produced. By that I mean an urban center built by and for the people who live here.

A generation ago Everett’s then-leaders opted to convert the downtown into a financial district while designating an area on its southern border retail, which gave rise to the ill-fated Everett Mall, whose shuttered storefronts and musical-chair owners amplify the decision’s folly. So, we have banks. Lots of them, with new ones sprouting every year. Meanwhile, major retailers packed up their goods then moved elsewhere, abandoning their space to—what else?—banks.

The problem with our local banks is that money obviously comes through their doors but it never seems to make its way back into the community where it could be productively invested in an attractive, workable sphere of complementary interests. Above all, there is no public space nor any sense of it.

I, for one, like urban parks—even, and especially, downtown—replete with real grass, plants, shade, footpaths, open patches, trees, and lots of seating. Everett has none within its urban core. There are simply streets and buildings. Period.

Where or how to begin the journey from ugly dysfunction to vibrant, comfortable, vital, beautiful, and eminently livable? It will have to begin with a whine, a steadily growing groundswell of complaints. The mediocrity should not stand.

Pass it on.

Boeing busting union

August 9, 2013 by davald

A friend sent me this link about Boeing and labor relations. It seems that management prefers the ability to screw its unionized workforce to making money. Everett’s employees have better skills and are more productive than their non-union counterparts in South Carolina. No matter, as the article suggests, “fuck unions.” Quoting internal company documents:

Boeing determined that choosing the Everett line would result in lower start-up costs, faster ramp-up, higher productivity, and a simplified supply chain, whereas a Charleston line would introduce further production delays, dilute workforce skills, and result in a “negative impact to 787 program profitability.” That is, moving the line would cost $1.5 billion up front, plus reduced earnings on one-third of the 787 backlog, according to Boeing’s own internal numbers. The only consistent advantage attributed to Charleston was the ability to “leverage” the site placement decision toward “rebalancing an unbalanced and uncompetitive labor relationship.”

When Gary Locke was governor of Washington state he marshaled all the forces at his disposal and added cajoling and fear-mongering to extract huge concessions from any person or firm having the slightest connection with Boeing. That was to “save” the 787 project for Everett, which Boeing management came close to torpedoing on its own with its cockamamie scheme to internationalize parts production then ship the components to the Everett plant for final assembly. Disastrously dumb.

I say, “Fuck management.”

The goo

July 27, 2013 by davald

For the past couple of weeks my wife and I have commuted to Seattle and back to babysit our new grandson, which is as delightful a task as we could imagine. However, getting to and from has all the earmarks of society gone mad. Why do so many people join the daily mess on the interstate?

Our distance is about 30 miles. Without traffic, the trip would take less than half an hour. A breeze. Alas, there is always traffic. Last Thursday, upon departing Seattle at four in the afternoon, we didn’t arrive home until 5:30. Yet, the next morning, we managed to go door to door in under 30 minutes. That’s the other annoying aspect to “rush-hour” commutes: the traffic is so unpredictable from day to day and from even second to second, which makes for all those panic stops and rear-end collisions. Skid marks adorn the surface like stripes on a zebra.

Years ago Puget Sound voters had an opportunity to slightly tax themselves to receive matching funds for rapid transit. In a burst of collective insanity, we said no. Atlanta got the dough instead, and we’re now stuck with the same few travel lanes with considerably more vehicles than when the vote was cast. Stupid.

Traveling five miles an hour in densely packed concrete lanes day after day takes a toll. It got to Paul Krugman the other day when circumstances dictated that he drive his car rather than take the train, his normal mode. Not being your typical commuter, the professor conducted calculations in transit on average speeds and whether or not adjacent lanes really do move faster. They don’t. He writes in his blog:

And this creates intense frustration and anger, a sense that it’s grossly unfair that you are in the wrong lane. This sense persists even though (a) you have worked out the analysis above, and realize that in principle the lanes are equally good or bad and (b) you have in fact been playing leapfrog with the same Boltbus the whole way, so that you know that in fact neither lane is better. No matter; you are angry, frazzled, and late for your family event. (Which you make up for by having a good time, and drinking enough wine that it’s past 9 when you realize that you didn’t post Friday Night Music).

Tragic.

Growing separation

July 8, 2013 by davald

I imagine that some of us are either blind to or inured to inequality. I attribute some of this to ghettoization and self-selection of coteries, walling off from our everyday experiences the sharp differences that abound in the main. Thus, we may not see poverty simply because we’re not exposed to it. Wealth, on the other hand, is ostentatious and seeks notice, typically in cultural motifs via standard media.

Such thoughts were triggered by reading this essay in the New York Times about “statusization,” a term coined by the author, James Atlas. He writes about a recent plane trip:

This stark class division should come as no surprise: what’s happening in the clouds mirrors what’s happening on the ground. Statusization — to coin a useful term — is ubiquitous, no matter what your altitude. While you’re in your hospital bed spooning up red Jell-O, a patient in a private suite is enjoying strawberries and cream. On your way to a Chase A.T.M., you notice a silver plaque declaring the existence within of Private Client Services. This man has a box seat at a Yankees game; that man has a skybox. And the skybox isn’t the limit: high overhead, the 1 percent fly first class; the .1 percent fly Netjets; the .01 fly their own planes. Why should it be any different up above from down below?

The U.S. was not always so divided, especially during the post-war decades of the 50s and 60s, when the middle class was large and stable. The trend since the mid to late 70s, what I call the Great Divergence, can be described rather accurately as the rich becoming richer and the poor poorer—and more plentiful. The rising Gini index illustrates the phenomenon.

 

My dystopian vision is that the accumulation of wealth in the hands of the few, while certainly not new or rare, portends severe societal problems now and especially in the future. Aside from the apparent fact that inequality is bad for our individual and collective health, the Great Divergence leads almost ineluctably to separate and, of course, unequal societies within the same country. We will become one nation only in times of war, and that artificial unity will mask the continuing exploitation of what Naomi Klein calls “shocks” by the rich against the Rest of Us. Moreover, even during relatively ordinary times the accompanying consolidation of political power by monied interests guarantees the socioeconomic imbalance, as the needs of the impoverished many succumb to the appetites of the privileged few.

The Times‘ essayist suggests that individuating flying protocols serve as a microcosm of trends writ large:

In its efforts to provide “high-value customers” with a “private, intimate, exclusive” experience, the airline hands out all kinds of cool stuff, from pajamas and a duvet to “branded dermatologic products.” The “in-flight wine tasting” offers five vintage labels. And you’re always plugged in: behind each seat is a wall of electronic outlets to recharge one’s gadgets, in order to ensure “life uninterrupted.” (What a nightmare.)

At the end of my tour, I walked down the aisle past business (less gadgetry, but still a lot), past economy plus (more legroom), and to the vast acreage of economy, row upon row of empty seats that, in a few hours, would be filled with squalling babies, muscled guys in Bubba Gump T-shirts, girls in flamingo-pink tank tops, obese armrest hoggers, divorce lawyers jabbering on their cellphones before takeoff, women in spectacles reading “Wolf Hall,” Hasids, Pakistanis, Japanese — the melting pot of American coach.

At least we got out of steerage. Or did we?

A’coaling we will go

June 14, 2013 by davald

This New York Times article lays out the economics of coal exports. Simply put, there is still lots of coal in the U.S., but domestic consumption has declined, leaving exports as the attractive alternative. The problem: how to get more coal to Asia, the prime target? The solution: build more export terminals in the Pacific Northwest.

The Powder River basin produces more than 500 million tons of coal a year, nearly half the country’s total, but only about 15 million tons are exported every year, mostly because of the lack of export outlets. Some of the basin’s coal can be shipped down the Mississippi River to ports on the Gulf of Mexico, but that mostly goes to the crowded European market.

Coal experts say the basin could export 10 times what it does today if the Pacific Northwest terminals were approved, producing thousands of jobs and bolstering the economies of several states.

Whoa. If you don’t like coal trains going through your neighborhood now, you’ll absolutely hate 10 times as many, should the ports be built.

And what about global warming? What’s that? Oh, right, I seem to remember people talking about it a few years ago. It’s a big hoax, isn’t it?

Timing is everything—again [u]

May 24, 2013 by davald

My wife and I travel I-5 several times a year to head for LaConner or Bellingham or places in between. We call this our ‘El Norte,’ a psychological vacation to still largely unchanged locales and vistas.

If we head for Bellingham or Chuckanut Drive we need to pass through Mt. Vernon via the interstate then over the Skagit River. Yesterday evening that portion of I-5 that traverses the water collapsed. Several cars fell into the river, but there were no fatalities; everyone was safely rescued. Amazing. Here’s a photo from the Seattle Times. You can find other pictures by clicking the link.

We are a nation steeped in denial. Our transportation infrastructure is falling apart, literally, and not even a major highway is immune to our collective neglect. Here’s a link (pdf) to the Washington State Department of Transportation list of “structurally deficient bridges.”

Pathetic.

UPDATE (May 24, 2013):

Here’s more on the pathetic.

How did this happen? States and local governments are the biggest part of the story here. They’ve historically provided the vast majority of spending for roads, highways and bridges, and they’ve been pulling back on spending since 2008 as a result of the economic downturn and requirements to balance their budgets. California’s transportation spending declined by 31 percent from 2007 to 2009, for instance. Texas’s fell by 8 percent.

At the same time, Congress hasn’t filled in the gap. There was a one-time $46 billion infusion of transportation spending in the stimulus bill. But that wasn’t enough to offset the  drop at the state and local level. Meanwhile, the most recent highway bill out of Congress kept federal spending at current levels rather than increasing it.

The big question is whether Congress should be spending more — and if so, how much? We’ve seen various reports arguing that America’s infrastructure is in dire need of an upgrade. The American Society of Civil Engineers gave the nation’s bridges a C+ in its 2013 report card, and said that full repairs would cost $20 billion per year over the next decade, a 60 percent boost in spending. These estimates don’t always take a full account of costs and benefits, but the I-5 collapse will no doubt give these groups more ammo.

Another consideration, meanwhile, is that Congress can borrow money for remarkably low rates right now. And experts say it’s typically cheaper to fix roads and bridges early on rather than wait until they get truly decrepit. That suggests now could be an apt time to invest in repairs, rather than putting them off until later.
