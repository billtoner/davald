Government spending: health care

September 5, 2012 by davald

The Republicans, who strenuously resist the national version of their candidate’s health care law, vow to further privatize medical services. They argue, against all evidence to the contrary, that government does it bad and the private sector does it good.

So, what is the evidence that debunks the privatization scheme? As is my wont, I’ll rely on some charts to visually display data and correlations. (Sources: OECD, Wikipedia, GlobalHealthFacts.org)

Those of us who pay attention know that the U.S. spends more on health care in total and per capita than any other nation. It’s not even close. Now one might infer that we should have better health care metrics than countries that spend less. Well, that depends. If you’re rich like Mitt Romney, then you can buy all the health care you want and probably live longer and feel better. For the Rest of Us, good luck.

Look at that cluster in the middle. Those circles represent the Nordic countries and Germany, all of whom spend less per capita than we do.

What about life expectancy?

 

Well, we must have a lot more doctors, given how much we spend on health care. (With the exception of Canada, the countries clustered in the middle of the following graph are Nordic.)

 

There’s no such thing as a free lunch, we like to say. All those Nordic countries with their “socialist” health care systems must suffer economically.

Ronald Reagan said that government was “the problem.” In America, that’s certainly correct. Our government really is the problem in so many ways, not the least of which being that the very rich have completely captured it. That we have any programs at all that help alleviate poverty (e.g., food stamps) or care for the sick (e.g., Medicaid) or provide some retirement benefits (e.g., Social Security) suggests that the oligarchs didn’t get everything they wanted; or perhaps they realize that eliminating all public services might actually anger the masses to ill effect.

The main reason I engage in these international comparisons is to dispel myths about the role and extent of government. Yes, criticize the U.S. federal government, and rightly so. However, I believe that the Nordic countries in particular offer a different, more positive attitude. Government can and does lessen the extremes of wealth, provides valuable social services, and otherwise protects against life’s misfortunes.

That said, I harbor no illusions about Americans’ capacity for meaningful change. There have been moments in our history when persistent struggle yielded victories we now take for granted, like the eight-hour day, the end of child labor, civil rights, and “entitlements” like Social Security. Yet reactionary forces limited the gains and always threaten to eliminate them entirely. Behold the Republicans.

Tragic.

Debtor nation

August 30, 2012 by davald

While reading Matt Taibbi’s piece on Mitt Romney’s outrageous Bain Capital career I was reminded of a different period, one in which citizens took matters into their own hands to punch capitalism in its big, fat mouth. Our radical history never appears in students’ textbooks, although Howard Zinn’s A People’s History of the United States includes many references to the violent acts of the oppressed against their oppressors.

This Monday, we should remember, is Labor Day. But few of us take the time to recall struggles of another era, one marked by bombings, shootings, strikes, riots, and Pinkerton guards. I’ll mention one incident.

The year before the birth of my grandfather— who would become active in labor’s causes for most of his adult life—a peaceful rally took place in Chicago’s Haymarket Square. Workers and their families were demonstrating for an eight-hour day. Imagine that. Someone threw a bomb. Gunshots were fired. Police and civilians died. (You can read about it here.)

Perhaps we can call upon Zinn to give us a brief background. He’s writing about Albert Parsons and August Spies, described by Zinn as anarchist leaders of the International Workers of the World, to which my grandfather briefly belonged (from pp. 264-265 of the paperback People’s).

On May 3 [1886], a series of events took place which were to put Parsons and Spies in exactly the position that the Chicago Mail had suggested (“Make an example of them if trouble occurs”). That day, in front of the McCormick Harvester Works, where strikers and sympathizers fought scabs, the police fired into a crowd of strikers running from the scene, wounding many of them, and killed four. Spies, enraged, went to the printing shop of the Arbeiter-Zeitung [Workers’ Newspaper] and printed a circular in both English and German:

Revenge!

Workingmen, to Arms!!!

…You have for years endured the most abject humiliations…you have worked yourself to death…your Children you have sacrificed to the factory lord—in short: you have been miserable and obedient slaves all these years. Why? To satisfy the insatiable greed, to fill the coffers of your lazy thieving master? When you ask them now to lessen your burdens, he sends his bloodhounds out to shoot you, kill you!

…To arms we call you, to arms!

Well, one thing led to another, including the aforementioned bomb. But as Wikipedia suggests, “The Haymarket affair is generally considered significant as the origin of international May Day observances for workers.” We could add Labor Day to this. Could, but we won’t.

Now, we would judge Spies’s circular inflammatory and hyperbolic. Not only have we rid our vocabularies of such rhetoric, we generally regard ourselves as more civil these days. We also put up with a lot more pervasive, yet sophisticated shit, dispensed by “the great malefactors of wealth,” as Teddy Roosevelt called his oligarchs. Perhaps we’ve become inured to the abuse. Or, certainly, the stakes are different. We don’t, as a rule, sacrifice our children to “factory lords,” though migrant children toil hours in white farmers’ fields. Nor do Wall Street barons sic their “bloodhounds” on us. There’s no need, since we’re probably sitting in front of the television watching American Idol.

I should offer a little of Taibbi so that you can gain some flavor for what prompted this post. Let’s start at the beginning:

The great criticism of Mitt Romney, from both sides of the aisle, has always been that he doesn’t stand for anything. He’s a flip-flopper, they say, a lightweight, a cardboard opportunist who’ll say anything to get elected.

The critics couldn’t be more wrong. Mitt Romney is no tissue-paper man. He’s closer to being a revolutionary, a backward-world version of Che or Trotsky, with tweezed nostrils instead of a beard, a half-Windsor instead of a leather jerkin. His legendary flip-flops aren’t the lies of a bumbling opportunist – they’re the confident prevarications of a man untroubled by misleading the nonbeliever in pursuit of a single, all-consuming goal. Romney has a vision, and he’s trying for something big: We’ve just been too slow to sort out what it is, just as we’ve been slow to grasp the roots of the radical economic changes that have swept the country in the last generation.

And what is Romney’s grand vision?

Mitt Romney – a man whose own father built cars and nurtured communities, and was one of the old-school industrial anachronisms pushed aside by the new generation’s wealth grab – has emerged now to sell this make-nothing, take-everything, screw-everyone ethos to the world. He’s Gordon Gekko, but a new and improved version, with better PR – and a bigger goal. A takeover artist all his life, Romney is now trying to take over America itself. And if his own history is any guide, we’ll all end up paying for the acquisition.

Taibbi goes on to chronicle how Romney made his fortune, not by weeding out weak businesses or turning them around, all the while putting people to work. Rather, Romney and Bain Capital parlayed a little of their own money along with massive debt to simply extract millions of dollars from companies before they almost inevitable ran aground. He and his minions got out before the axe fell on thousands of workers. Taibbi:

Here’s how Romney would go about “liberating” a company: A private equity firm like Bain typically seeks out floundering businesses with good cash flows. It then puts down a relatively small amount of its own money and runs to a big bank like Goldman Sachs or Citigroup for the rest of the financing. (Most leveraged buyouts are financed with 60 to 90 percent borrowed cash.) The takeover firm then uses that borrowed money to buy a controlling stake in the target company, either with or without its consent…

Romney and Bain avoided the hostile approach, preferring to secure the cooperation of their takeover targets by buying off a company’s management with lucrative bonuses. Once management is on board, the rest is just math. So if the target company is worth $500 million, Bain might put down $20 million of its own cash, then borrow $350 million from an investment bank to take over a controlling stake.

But here’s the catch. When Bain borrows all of that money from the bank, it’s the target company that ends up on the hook for all of the debt.

Taibbi captures the disturbing irony of the man named ‘Mitt.’ Despite the candidate’s rebuke of Obama for creating a “prairie fire of debt,” debt was what made Romney rich. Not his debt, of course. He made it his business to saddle others with so much that they could not possibly get out from under it. Taibbi offers an example of Bain’s modus operandi:

Take a typical Bain transaction involving an Indiana-based company called American Pad and Paper. Bain bought Ampad in 1992 for just $5 million, financing the rest of the deal with borrowed cash. Within three years, Ampad was paying $60 million in annual debt payments, plus an additional $7 million in management fees. A year later, Bain led Ampad to go public, cashed out about $50 million in stock for itself and its investors, charged the firm $2 million for arranging the IPO and pocketed another $5 million in “management” fees. Ampad wound up going bankrupt, and hundreds of workers lost their jobs, but Bain and Romney weren’t crying: They’d made more than $100 million on a $5 million investment.

Taibbi believes that Romney’s end game looks something like this:

Romney is the frontman and apostle of an economic revolution, in which transactions are manufactured instead of products, wealth is generated without accompanying prosperity, and Cayman Islands partnerships are lovingly erected and nurtured while American communities fall apart. The entire purpose of the business model that Romney helped pioneer is to move money into the archipelago from the places outside it, using massive amounts of taxpayer-subsidized debt to enrich a handful of billionaires. It’s a vision of society that’s crazy, vicious and almost unbelievably selfish, yet it’s running for president, and it has a chance of winning.

I think that Taibbi may be on to something in his discussion of debt. It may be no coincidence that the Great Divergence, in addition to increasing the gulf between the Have’s and Have-nots, saddled us with rising debt.

 

I’ve included the following graph in prior posts. It shows income gains by “fractile.”

Since the late 70s the U.S. economy has shifted away from manufacturing toward financialization. We used to build things.

Now we finance—whatever. Financing is all about debt. I want to buy a house, so I borrow money. I’m Mitt Romney and I want to buy a company. I borrow money.

The first chart above shows government debt. What about private, or consumer, debt?

Fifty thousand billion equals $50 trillion. Now that’s a lot of money. Keep in mind that our GDP is about $13.6 trillion. Let’s look at GDP and consumer debt.

Wow! We can then compare the ratio of debt to GDP.

Before 1983, the ratio of consumer debt to GDP was less than one. Today, for every dollar of GDP our economy incurs four dollars of debt.

The financial sector is populated by people like Mitt Romney and, of course, Wall Street barons. The piles of debt, to my simple mind, represent giant Ponzi schemes, with the need for increasing inputs (debt) for any given output (unit of GDP).

Our whole economy, now, is about leveraging, borrowing money to do what? It’s not to put people to work. It’s not to build better roads and schools. It’s not about improving the living standards of the Rest of Us. No, it’s all about making rich people richer.

Do you get this? Well, let’s start printing some circulars.

Tax the things you loathe

August 26, 2012 by davald

America’s love affair with all things fossil comes with inconvenient consequences. I’ll list a few:

  • carbon dioxide emissions
  • dirty air and water
  • cars
  • roads
  • sprawl

One could add to this list obesity and other health assaults, since spending so much time behind the wheel (rather than walking or bicycling) contributes to expanded waistlines and lousy circulatory systems. Add in the mobile Mickey D with fries and you’ve laid out a path to an early grave.

Economists, for the most part, recommend that we tax carbon. But Americans, for the most part, hate taxes, despite our paying fewer than our European counterparts. So, whenever a well-intentioned politician (small in number, certainly) proposes a carbon tax, he or she invites failure; colleagues won’t touch it.

However, we do love our subsidies and expect our own politicians to deliver them in support of local industries. Should we live in coal states like Virginia we appreciate whatever help the federal government can provide, whether in tax relief, subsidies, or fewer environmental regulations.

So, let’s take a look at energy subsidies over time (source).

 

Oil, natural gas, and coal receive the most help from Congress, with oil the runaway winner. It should not surprise us, then, that we use more of these resources than we would without the subsidies. Similarly, if subsidies increased for the green resources, we’d expect greater development of renewables.*

It makes economic and environmental sense to both eliminate all energy subsidies and impose a carbon tax. From the sourced article:

We can either tax carbon or incentivize non-carbon. Debate rages about which is better, but it has always been more effective to tax what you don’t want, rather than to pretend to know what will work in the future.

If Congress imposed an effective carbon tax, we’d see much more innovation in alternative fuels (e.g., geothermal and solar) and greater investments in demand-side resources, including conservation and distributed generation. But I said “effective” tax. It must be high enough to approximate the (negative) value of carbon’s pernicious consequences. (I’ve written about his here.)

I am not hopeful of seeing either a carbon tax or some cap-and-trade scheme in my future, which gets shorter by the year. Fossil-fuel interests are huge and politically well-connected. Moreover, we don’t like taxes. We’re suspicious of government. We’re dangerously ignorant. This unholy combination yields a toxic business-as-usual strategy that promises profits for the few and catastrophe for the many.

Got to go. Time for my walk.

___________

* I am forced to quibble with the “market activity” subsidy for hydro. That’s a euphemism for a controversial, yet longstanding function of federal marketing agencies like BPA. By law, and this goes back to the New Deal, the output from federal systems (in our case, the nearly two-dozen dams along the Columbia and Snake rivers) must go first to public utilities (e.g., Snohomish County PUD) at cost (technically, enough to recover Bonneville’s financial obligations—its revenue requirements). For years, politicians not served by the FMAs have demanded that the FMAs charge market-based rates. Not only would such a policy-shift (requiring a change in the law, by the way) jump up electricity rates in Washington and other states receiving preference power from the FMAs, it would generate more revenues for the federal government—a wealth transfer representing billions of dollars. We should keep in mind that Bonneville’s customers have fully funded the FMA’s statutory obligations over seven decades. To those who might argue that having to pay market-based rates (higher the current preference rates) would spur more conservation, please understand that the Pacific Northwest, along with California, leads the nation in conservation acquisitions. The Snohomish County PUD, for one, has set annual records over the last several years.

Another tool of suppression

August 23, 2012 by davald

A few posts back I developed an alarming hypothesis: The GOP, cognizant of America’s changing demographics, concocted a long-term strategy that would lead to de jure apartheid, cementing existing de facto segregation. Fearing a minority majority, if you will, white conservatives desire to dominate electoral politics despite being outnumbered by the year 2050. My hypothesis relies on connecting a few dots and positing an end game based on self-interest. (Source for chart: U.S. Census)

The de jure part, I suggested, is being accomplished on two fronts: voter ID laws and a racially motivated criminal justice system, one that Khalil Gibran Mohammed believes is used to subjugate Blacks and other minorities. Mohammed:

It [post-bellum Black Codes in the South] tells you something about the invention of the criminal justice system as a repressive tool to keep Black people in their place, from the very moment where 95 percent of the Black population became free. And it’s still with us. It’s still with us, because ultimately, as a social problem, crime has become like it was in the Jim Crow South, a mechanism to control black people’s movement in cities.

But there is a third leg to the strategy: immigration laws. Blacks are already here in abundance, thus the emphasis on crime and punishment and voter suppression. But what about Hispanic and Latino populations that arrive in America, often illegally?

In addition to voter-ID laws, the emphasis will be on keeping out brown-skinned people. Should they get past the border fences and patrols, conservatives would subject their children to all sorts of harassment. Above all, they would prevent them from ever becoming citizens.

So, what are Republicans doing about this in their platform? Julia Preston, writing for the New York Times:

In their debates this week in Tampa, Fla., over the party platform, Republican delegates hammered out an immigration plank calling for tough border enforcement and opposing “any forms of amnesty” for illegal immigrants, instead endorsing “humane procedures to encourage illegal aliens to return home voluntarily,” a policy of self-deportation.

You see, Republicans would like the Hispanic vote, but they don’t like Hispanics. Rather than forcibly evicting those who came here illegally, even though their ancestors once ruled huge swaths of America, the GOP elects to “encourage” self-deportation. What might that look like? It’s not clear. Preston:

A number of immigration amendments were offered by Kris Kobach, a conservative who is secretary of state of Kansas and was an author of laws in Arizona and several other states to crack down on illegal immigration.

Mr. Kobach proposed the plank calling for mandatory use by employers of a federal electronic system known as E-Verify to confirm the legal immigration status of new hires. “If you really want to create a job tomorrow, you can remove an illegal alien today,” Mr. Kobach said.

At his urging, the delegates also adopted a call to complete a double-layer border fence and to end federal lawsuits challenging state enforcement laws like Arizona’s.

I can’t imagine Hispanics rallying behind this platform.

Playing with fire or a coming apartheid?

August 15, 2012 by davald

How long can they keep them down on the farm? Republicans, apparently turning a blind-eye to changing demographics, have launched a coordinated assault on the poor, mostly, and people of color, in particular. Their vehicle, other than America’s draconian criminal justice system, is voter ID. If you don’t have a “proper” one, you cannot vote. As a disproportionately high number of now-minority groups lack the requisite identification, they are instantly disenfranchised in those states passing this latest voter-suppression tool.

Today Judge Robert Simpson upheld a Republican-passed bill in Pennsylvania’s legislature. He declined to grant an injunction sought by the law’s potential victims. If citizens wish to vote this November they must show a government-issued photo identification.

Revealing the party’s true colors, Pennsylvania’s house leader, Michael Turzai, predicted that the law’s passage would guarantee the state for Mitt Romney. The poor and people of color—mostly the same—are not expected to vote Republican in the upcoming or any election. Do you think?

The New York Times editors weighed in on the law and the court decision.

The real reasons for voter ID laws are quite clear. The desire to dampen the Democratic vote after 2006, and particularly in the wake of President Obama’s election, prompted six states to decide, virtually simultaneously, to pass voter ID laws. Their stated reason — combating voter fraud — is easily dismissed, since there are virtually no documented cases of impersonation fraud that could be reduced with an ID card. Mr. Turzai was simply indiscreet; most Republicans know better than to speak the truth out loud.

We live in truly Machiavellian times. The Republicans, composed principally of better-heeled whites, no doubt initiated the surreptitious investigations of ACORN, which had hired mostly poor people to register citizens to vote. It was discovered that many of the signatures were bogus, often explicitly so, with names like “Mickey Mouse,” for example. Conservatives cried “Voter fraud!” Hardly. As the Times editors point out, “there have been no prosecutions or even investigations of in-person voter fraud in Pennsylvania.” Never mind. The message stuck, and large numbers of Americans believe that voter fraud (e.g., voting under an alias or more than once) is rampant.

Anxious to prevent people from voting Democratic, the Republican Party introduced legislation in several state houses with the same requirement: a government-issued identification. The editors tell us:

The requirement will have a disparate impact on minorities, who tend to lack government IDs at a higher rate than the general population and tend to vote Democratic.

History does not stand still, nor do Hispanics. Their percentage of the U.S. population continues to grow.

The U.S. Census Bureau today [May 26, 2011] released a 2010 Census brief on the nation’s Hispanic population, which shows the Hispanic population increased by 15.2 million between 2000 and 2010 and accounted for more than half of the total U.S. population increase of 27.3 million. Between 2000 and 2010, the Hispanic population grew by 43 percent, or four times the nation’s 9.7 percent growth rate.

 

In less than 40 years minorities (i.e., non-whites) are expected to become the majority in the United States. Ten national elections to go. Republicans may certainly recognize the trend, and my guess is that they’re scared to death of so many brown and black people holding office and making laws. I suspect that they’re mounting a pre-emptive assault in the short term in hopes of delegitimizing the future majority; people of color may have the numbers, but, if the Republicans have their way, they won’t have the votes to repel the conservative agenda.

For the Republicans to prevail over the long term they must establish de jure apartheid, leaping beyond the current de facto. A white minority would control a colored majority. There are precedents, of course:

  • U.S. colonists over indigenous Americans
  • White southerners dominating majority black populations—all enslaved
  • White Afrikaners over black African natives
  • Spanish, Portuguese, and Dutch conquering and subjugating populations of black, brown, and red peoples

Should the economy continue to sputter indefinitely I see whiteness becoming as salient as black or brown in how we divide ourselves. That is, even nominal Democrats, increasingly fearful of their economic futures, may switch parties to join the Republicans to more overtly suppress people of color. We’ve begun to see formerly liberal states like Wisconsin and Pennsylvania transform into Republican strongholds, whereupon their legislatures and governors have moved swiftly to de-unionize public employees and restrict voter turnout. Could other states follow?

I’ve written previously about how dismal economies produce political extremism. We’re seeing it in Europe. It certainly happened there before WWII, during the Great Depression. If too many people remain out of work or see their wages stagnate or lose public benefits, will they be more inclined to support an FDR or a Paul Ryan?

Is it possible that the conservative strategists see the writing on the demographic wall and have already set in motion a serious of events and initiatives that, one, ensure that the economy continues to sputter, and, two, further thwart the potential electoral outcomes of a new colored majority?

If you think this is preposterous on its face, I call your attention to the decades-long strategy of the GOP to reverse liberal fortunes, beginning in earnest under Nixon. And it’s all about money, lots of it. Those that have it started investing in conservative “think tanks,” political action committees, electoral races, and lobbying. (Much of this has been chronicled by Dean Baker, Chris Hedges, Corey Robin, Joseph Stiglitz, John Cassidy, and Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson.) That is to say, the rise of the modern-day conservative movement didn’t happen by accident.

The point here is that conservatives have amply demonstrated their commitment to the long haul. They succeeded when so many had written them off.

I would not bet against them. They have the money, the power to manipulate outcomes in their favor, and the resolve to stay the course.

Meanwhile, liberal Democrats are clueless. And this may be the scariest point of all.

Phony squared

August 14, 2012 by davald

Paul Krugman has called Mitt Romney a phony, and I will return to the Nobel Prize-winning economist shortly. If the label applies to the former governor it surely fits his just-announced running mate to a tea. Indeed, Paul Ryan and his truly off-the-wall ideology and policy prescriptions should find their best reception amongst the people of Oz or in the Red Queen’s court. Thus, phony squared.

Ryan, we will recall, is most notable for his budget proposal, which many media pundits have described as “serious,” “bold,” and even “wonkish.” It may be bold in its extremism, but qualifies for none of the other more laudatory adjectives heaped upon the Wisconsin representative, whose introduction to politics began with a professor’s invitation to the peculiar philosophy of Ayn Rand. Ryan ate it up, and, until “objectivism” became too inconvenient, even among the madding crowd otherwise known as the Republican Party, served as his intellectual guidepost for most of his subsequent career.

However, that budget offering deserves opprobrium not approbation. It would further lower taxes on the incomes of Romney and Ryan, along with their very wealthy benefactors, of course. The Rest of Us would either have to figure out a way—and real quick—to become a One-percenter or get used to wallowing in deeper shit. If enacted, Ryan’s proposal would make a mockery of government while denying much-needed public services for a growing percentage of the population.

Now comes Krugman on the question of why. Why did Romney choose Ryan? The conscience of a liberal expresses unvarnished cynicism in his answer. Forget about the merits or demerits of the Romney-Ryan prescriptions for what ails America, because, Krugman avers, their team has nothing to do with advancing serious and respectable ideas. Krugman:

So, let me clarify what I believe is really going on in the choice of Paul Ryan as VP nominee. It is not about satisfying the conservative base, which was motivated anyway by Obama-hatred; it is not about refocusing on the issues, because R&R are both determined to avoid providing any of the crucial specifics about their plans. It is — as Jonathan Chait also seems to understand — about exploiting the gullibility and vanity of the news media, in much the same way that George W. Bush did in 2000.

Could the Fourth Estate be that crucial to our political future? Will reporters and pundits drink the R&R Kool-Aid? Have Romney and his advisors shrewdly discerned the real players in our democratic theater? Do the media determine electoral outcomes? Is the formula really that simple? Do most voters get their marching orders from the local television station or Fox News? When Rush Limbaugh says “jump” do his listeners ask “How high?”

There may have been a time in my life when I believed that politics and its practitioners were above reproach. I think it was when I was 12, although I have prior vague memories of having watched national conventions on TV—in black and white—with outcomes in doubt until the final floor vote. Back then Edward R. Murrow, Chet Huntley, and Walter Cronkite spoke “the truth.” And for all his conservative sensibilities “Ike” was a man of honor and integrity, even though he scored zero votes from our extended family, fiercely dedicated to Stevenson and all Democrats. The adults read the newspapers, books, and magazines to educate themselves. Television news seemed more serious and respectable.

Whether or not my recollections are accurate, one can safely describe a difference between then and now: the two political parties were above sending the country and its citizens into the toilet. Yes, Republicans have always been bastards, but they once were kinder and gentler about it. They did not seek to unravel the New Deal edifice, though they continued to wield small chisels. Social Security survived the 50s, as did the system of financial regulations that helped restore confidence following the Great Depression. The economy grew steadily and robustly during both Republican and Democratic regimes. Marginal tax rates remained high, so government revenues kept up with spending.

I do not recognize today’s Republican. Their rabidity is matched only by their wackiness, and they demand fealty to the ridiculous and the destructive. Predictably enough, the party produces R&R, and the conservative faithful go apeshit.

But November’s outcomes involve a much larger swath of the electorate. Thankfully, the crazies still lack a national majority, though they’ve captured political strongholds in dozens of states and even entire regions. The challenge to R&R is to somehow convince enough non-wackos that their ticket holds true promise for America.

And that’s where the media and punditocracy come in. To survive the general election, R&R must have the blessing of “lamestream media.” They need a bit more than a handful of “respectable” reporters, anchors, and opinion mongers to judge their candidacy and platform more than merely “serious” but also providential. R&R need the crusader’s cloak, and hope that the Fourth Estate will weave it for them.

Only in America.

Inequality’s correlations—blackness

August 13, 2012 by davald

America, for all its collective nationalistic hubris, is a troubled nation. Indeed, it sucks, big time. And the confirming evidence abounds.

One metric that stands out is our country’s wholesale embrace of inequality. The rich are different, and the Rest of Us not only worship them, we coddle, coddle, and coddle some more. In return the wealthy demand that heads they win and tails we lose; no matter the vagaries, they are either rewarded or protected—by the Rest of Us, who are left to suffer in countless ways, from a failed public education system to increasingly impoverished habitats.

But such masochism is as perversely unhealthy as it is preventable. Overcoming the imbalance compels us to abandon our pervasive Stockholm syndrome. Don’t worship the rich; unshackle their chains then beat the shit out of them. (What am I smoking?)

A few evenings ago I watched Bill Moyers’s interview of Khalil Gibran Mohammed, who heads up the Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture. Moyers asked Mohammed for his reaction to New York mayor Bloomber’s policy of “stop and frisk.” As an historian Mohammed provided context, tracing this latest initiative to slavery and lingering identification. By using the salient trait of blackness generations of white political leaders have successfully controlled African American populations. Mohammed:

It tells us that it’s an old and enduring form of surveillance and racial control. So if we think about the moment immediately following the Civil War, there was the invention of something called the Black Codes in every southern state. And those codes were intended to use the criminal justice system to restrict the freedom and mobility of black people.

And if you crossed any line that they prescribed, you could be sold back to your former slave owner, not as a slave, but as a prisoner to work off your fine after an auction where you were resold to the highest bidder. It tells you something about the invention of the criminal justice system as a repressive tool to keep Black people in their place, from the very moment where 95 percent of the Black population became free. And it’s still with us. It’s still with us, because ultimately, as a social problem, crime has become like it was in the Jim Crow South, a mechanism to control black people’s movement in cities.

Note the phrase “the invention of the criminal justice system as a repressive tool to keep Black people in their place.” Mohammed contrasts the fate of white immigrants with their darker counterparts. Italians, Russians, Scandinavians, and so on became “white” on our census rolls. They retained their surnames while melting into the great Caucasian pot. Not so Blacks.

Having been denied equal opportunity, what happens to Blacks, especially young males? They cannot find jobs. They cannot live where they choose. They are perpetually branded by their color. Well, they go to prison after resorting to crime. If one can’t meet one’s needs honestly, desperation dictates larceny or worse.

Well-meaning politicians have suggested that we stop talking about race and focus on poverty. After all, Blacks are more likely to be poor than whites, as this table confirms:

 

I don’t disagree, actually. We should be concerned about poverty in America—and elsewhere. Yet, Mohammed’s historical context for our modern criminal justice system, one that disproportionately imprisons Blacks more than any other race, should at least force us to ask why this is so. Poverty’s effects yield anti-social behavior. But in America especially color affixes socioeconomic status. Blackness equals poverty, and that association stubbornly persists.

So, we put them in jail (source).

 

We are a sorting species. Broadly speaking we divide among “us” versus “them.” What salient features will we employ? Skin color works well, all too well. It serves as a first approximation for identifying in-group and out-group. Further, we are particularly keen on class-based hierarchies. In America the wealth spectrum spreads wide, with most money concentrated in a few hands. Very little, we’ve learned, trickles down to the Rest of Us.

Unfortunately, we seem to enjoy the divisions. We like our rich and detest our poor. We aspire to be the former and live in horror of becoming the latter. Color, again, helps.

Blackness, more so than “brownness,” allows non-wealthy whites to feel relieved knowing that they can never become Black and all that blackness signifies, from poverty to desperation-driven criminality. Meanwhile, they can fantasize about their economic and political captors.

Sick.

Social harmony

July 11, 2012 by davald

Academy Award winner Milos Forman, writing for the New York Times, chastens those Americans who dare brand Obama a “socialist.” As the caption reads: “not even close.”

Forman knows a particular brand of socialism, having lived in Czechoslovakia under Soviet-style socialism. Citizens’ every move was surveilled. Speech itself was controlled. He tells this story from his experience.

My sister-in-law’s father, Jan Kunasek, lived in Czechoslovakia all his life. He was a middle-class man who ran a tiny inn in a tiny village. One winter night in 1972, during a blizzard, a man, soaked to the bone, awakened him at 2 in the morning. The man looked destitute and, while asking for shelter, couldn’t stop cursing the Communists. Taking pity, the elderly Mr. Kunasek put him up for the night.

A couple of hours later, Mr. Kunasek was awakened again, this time by three plainclothes policemen. He was arrested, accused of sheltering a terrorist and sentenced to several years of hard labor in uranium mines. The state seized his property. When he was finally released, ill and penniless, he died within a few weeks. Years later we learned that the night visitor had been working for the police. According to the Communists, Mr. Kunasek was a class enemy and deserved to be punished.

This is the brand of socialism conservatives decry, and rightfully so. However, when those on the American right use the term ‘socialism,’ Forman shudders:
It offends me, and cheapens the experience of millions who lived, and continue to live, under brutal forms of socialism.

There are many shades of socialism, including democratic socialism, viably present in most European countries. Americans, Forman pleads, would do well by realizing the distinction between “Marxist-Leninist totalitarianism” and “Western European-style socialism, and its government provision of social insurance and health care.”

Joseph McCarthy enjoyed a brief career by besmirching Americans for being “communists.” Now that the Soviet Union is gone, the right’s vocabulary has substituted socialist, hoping for the same effect. Among the madding crowd, unfortunately, labels work, however false.

Forman wishes for a different conversation. He ends his post thusly:

What we need is not to strive for a perfect social justice — which never existed and never will — but for social harmony. Harmony in music is, by its nature, exhilarating and soothing. In an orchestra, the different players and instruments perform together, in support of an overall melody.

Today, our democracy, a miraculous gathering of diverse players, desperately needs such unity. If all participants play fair and strive for the common good, we can achieve a harmony that eluded the doctrinaire socialist projects. But if just one section, or even one player, is out of tune, the music will disintegrate into cacophony.

I am not asking Mr. Obama and the Republican leaders to stop playing instruments of their choosing. All I am asking is that every player keep in mind the noble melody of our country. Otherwise the noisy dissonance might become loud enough to wake another Marx, or even worse.

One candidate for worse:

A week after an extremist right-wing party gained an electoral foothold in Greece’s Parliament earlier this summer, 50 of its members riding motorbikes and armed with heavy wooden poles roared through Nikaia, a gritty suburb west of here, to telegraph their new power.

Political polarization marinated in a dismal economy mixed with austerity measures breeds radicalization. We certainly see it in Europe. Our version, fortunately, is neither as strident nor as yet violent. However, the tendency to scapegoat our own is as strong as ever.

Cutting taxes

July 10, 2012 by davald

The New York Times has an interesting, if not also premature, article on two states. Democratically controlled Maryland decided to raise taxes on the rich to sustain funding of public services, including education. Kansas, dominated by Republicans, and very conservative ones at that, elected to lower taxes. The argument: Kansas was losing business to lower-taxed states. Which policy succeeds remains to be seen.

The article points out that Ohio, which cut aid to public schools and social services, announced that it has a $239 million surplus. Will those dollars be used to restore services? No. They will be placed in a reserve account.

But we do have a live experiment on our hands. With the Bush II-era tax cuts, the United States has among the lowest taxes in the industrial world, and both the marginal and corporate tax rate at near historic lows.

So, taxes have decreased. If lower taxes spur economic development, why is the economy in such lousy shape?

If lowering taxes at the national level does not coincide with economic growth, then we should not expect Kansas’s economy to rebound any time soon. Also, Kansas has a lower-than-national-average unemployment rate. With more people working, all other things being equal, tax revenues should be higher than in those states with high unemployment rates.

Maryland’s unemployment rate is a bit higher, but still below the national average.

The recession took a lesser GDP toll on Maryland than on Kansas, but a much larger hit on employment. But it’s difficult to argue that Kansas’s tax rates were adversely impacting its economy, since the economy was growing steadily prior to the Great Recession.

Moreover, despite higher federal tax rates in the 50s and 60s, the U.S. economy was growing. Since tax rates were lowered, the economy has not fared so well. And lower tax receipts have led to annual budget deficits and, therefore, accumulated federal debt.

The post-war period is marked by parallel spending and receipts, with budget surpluses the norm. Beginning in Nixon’s second term, we see a decline in receipts and an increase in spending. That gap grew under Ronald Regan: spending was much higher than revenues. Only during Clinton’s second term do we see the reverse. Then all hell broke loose under Bush II. Spending did not fall (the wars, you know), and receipts fell dramatically. The surplus inherited by G.W. Bush was quickly squandered.

The lesson: Don’t lower taxes to spur economic development. It doesn’t work. Besides, you create increasing government debt. Yet, Mitt Romney, according to Joe Biden, is “Bush on steroids.”
