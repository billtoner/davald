Let philosophy permeate

October 8, 2012 by davald

Jonathan Haidt, writing for the New York Times, acknowledges the importance of philosophical inquiry:

I should also say that philosophers have the best norms for good thinking that I have ever encountered. When my work is critiqued by a philosopher I can be certain that he or she has read me carefully, including the footnotes, and will not turn me into a straw man. More than any other subculture I know, the philosophical community embodies the kinds of normative pressures for reason-giving and responsiveness to reasons that Allan Gibbard describes in “Wise Choices, Apt Feelings.”

The problem with politics is that it is not philosophy. Rather, politics is simple, shallow, and intuitive. Unfortunately, and as Haidt has chronicled in his research, we are far more likely to be governed by instinct or passion than by reason. He writes:

…I’m saying that reason is far less powerful than intuition, so if you’re arguing (or deliberating) with a partner who lives on the other side of the political spectrum from you, and you approach issues such as abortion, gay marriage or income inequality with powerfully different intuitive reactions, you are unlikely to effect any persuasion no matter how good your arguments and no matter how much time you give your opponent to reflect upon your logic.

In his book The Righteous Mind Haidt emphasizes confirmation bias. We “see” the world in a certain way, based on instinct or intuition—for example, that gay marriage is wrong—then choose “evidence” that supports our world view.

The philosopher doesn’t stand a chance.

A shame, I say.

The nature of scientific understanding

September 11, 2012 by davald

Okay, not an original title, but given the present state of willful ignorance about all things scientific, some discussion of knowledge, what it is and how we come by it may be in order. The following was prompted by a comment to my recent post on climate change, which featured a link to the National Academy of Sciences and its brief documentary on the causes and effects of global warming.

Wow, a propaganda video, the NAS produced a propaganda video. Thanks for this, its an eye opener.

The comment, in my judgment, reveals volumes about the writer, none of it flattering. The individual, I should imagine, not only rejects the proposition that the planet is warming and that most of the warming is the result of combusting carbon but also believes that science itself is an untrustworthy discipline, populated by self-aggrandizing charlatans bent on securing research dollars in exchange for promulgating known falsehoods. Besides, most scientists are liberals. Of course, I may be wrong about the writer, and that his comment was facetious, though I doubt it.

Unlike most areas of science, global warming and, we could add, evolutionary theory, give rise to controversy. The latter, because life can be explained without god, though natural selection does not, in itself, refute a deity. Fundamentalist Christians, however, would prefer to adhere to a literal interpretation of the Bible, going so far as to believe that the earth is only several thousand years old and that humans were created by a divine being—just as we are today.

Nevertheless, one does not come easily to an understanding of evolutionary theory, which involves a paradigmatic shift in how life moved from the simple to the complex. In particular, we are to abandon teleology—that, for example, a giraffe has a long neck in order to better reach tall-hanging fruit—in favor of random molecular mutations that render an organism more or less likely to survive under a set of conditions. Since Darwin’s concepts may seem counter-intuitive, we may feel compelled to not only reject evolutionary theory but to question science itself.

Global warming presents similar muddles. First of all, it’s very, very complicated. One must be facile in a number of disciplines to understand the science, which involves chemistry, physics, paleontology, geology, and so on. Lacking the necessary expertise, how should we defer to scientists, even as we have no problem deferring to a cardiologist when it comes to matters of the heart? We may explicitly trust the doctor but suspect the scientist, even though the chemist or physicist has greater formal training and experience.

Moreover, the fundamental cause of global warming—humans burning fossil fuels—strikes many as, well, “political.” Some on the religious right, for example, cling to the belief that god will determine the earth’s future, and that man has nothing to do with it. Others, mostly conservative, reject potential remedies for climate change because the strategies almost necessarily require government actions, from setting a carbon tax to removing fossil-fuel subsidies to favoring renewable energy over traditional coal and petroleum and gas.

Ironically, perhaps, science as a discipline is conservative, though its practitioners may be otherwise. In general, we observe the world around us, form hypotheses about how things work, test them against further observations, then ultimately form theories. The progression from hypothesis to theory or, in rare occasions, “law,” takes time, argument, and repeated confirming evidence. Yet, both the hypotheses (models) and theories are tentative and always subject to revision, if not wholesale replacement.

The ancient Greeks believed that the earth was the center of the universe and that the stars and planets revolved around it in perfect circles. When later observations called into question this explanatory scheme of a geocentric universe, attempts were made to produce ever more complicated mathematics to accommodate the perceived planetary perturbations. Eventually, the model was scrapped in favor of a solar system in which earth was just one of several planets orbiting the sun in ellipses, not circles. Likewise, Newton’s notions of gravity and his three laws of motion, which continue to work for the most part, were superseded by Einstein’s theories of relativity and quantum mechanics.

The philosopher David Hume dove into the process of knowing. He produced a troublesome conclusion, what has come to be known as ‘the problem of induction.’ We can approach this through a standard syllogism:

  • If p, then q.
  • p,
  • Therefore q.

Logicians from Aristotle’s time call this form of argument modus ponens: affirming the antecedent (p) permits inference of the consequent (q). For example, if Socrates is a man then he is mortal. Socrates is a man, therefore he is mortal.

But for those looking for causes of events, problems arise, in particular the one of induction. Hume reasoned that if B follows A we cannot conclude that A necessarily causes B. We can only infer a correlation between A and B, and that correlation may obtain in countless observations. This constant correlation permits only a probabilistic inference: B followed A before, so it’s more or less likely to follow in the future.

Bertrand Russell devoted a chapter of his short book The Problems of Philosophy to induction. Russell, ever the wit, wrote:

Domestic animals expect food when they see the person who usually feeds them. We know that all these crude expectations of uniformity are liable to be misleading. The man who has fed the chicken every day throughout its life at last wrings its neck instead, showing that the more refined view as to the uniformity of nature would have been useful to the chicken.

Science, or its study in various guises, is really about induction. In legal terms, we might say “based on the preponderance of evidence, x is true.” The more confirming observations we gather that support the correlation between the consequent and the antecedent the more likely the correlation is true.

However, it is fallacious to infer p from q, but quite easy, in a logical sense, to negate p under the argument form modus tollens:

  • If p, then q.
  • Not-q,
  • Therefore not-p.

Modus tollens is certain. Negate the consequent and the antecedent is also negated, thus destroying the correlation as fact.

Scientific modeling is an attempt to formalize the correlation between p and q. With respect to climate change, a simple model might assume p as ‘an increase in atmospheric carbon dioxide’ and q as ‘an increase in planetary warming.’

We know that the correlation itself was established a century or more ago. Indeed, the Swedish chemist Svante Arrhenius postulated in 1889 that an increase in carbon dioxide emissions would warm the planet. He even calculated the relationship, and that calculation nearly 125 years on still holds—with remarkable precision.

But, if we observed that the planet was not warming, despite the increase in carbon dioxide, then the simple model above would not obtain. We would have encountered modus tollens.

If one were serious about discrediting the science and apparent fact of global warming, then one could set out to discover instances of cooling temperatures. And that’s exactly what some have been doing on several fronts, though unsuccessfully.

We should appreciate that there are huge quantities of temperature data published in the public arena; they are available to one and all. None of the data sets, whether gleaned from satellites or ocean- and land-based thermometers, has so far negated the antecedent. The crude correlation posited above survives an attack on the temperature record. The chart below is based on NASA data. It reflects anomalies in temperature, using the 1951-1980 period as the base.

Well, So what? the skeptic says. I’ll grant you the rising temperatures, but the cause or causes may have nothing to do with increasing carbon dioxide emissions, notwithstanding the established chemistry. The favored alternative seems to be “natural variability.” Since the sun is the source of earth’s external energy inputs, we would expect there to be a correlation between the sun’s output and our planet’s temperatures. But take a look (source):

The geological record (e.g., ice cores) shows that the planet’s temperature has risen and fallen over millions of years. Here is a chart showing variations over the last 400,000 years.

The curve at the top represents CO2 concentrations; the one below, temperature. There would seem to be a correlation, at least visually. But there could be a confounding variable, say changes in the earth’s rotational axis, that could account for variations in both carbon dioxide and temperature.

How might a scientist go about addressing the presence of such a confounding variable? One strategy is to assume no post-1880 carbon emissions. Another is to determine how well current climate models predict the past.

We should understand straightaway that without any atmospheric carbon dioxide the planet would likely be a frozen ball of ice. Carbon dioxide provides a greenhouse effect, a blanket, if you will, that keeps temperatures stable and habitable for planetary species, including us. Here’s an excerpt from NOAA:

Without a natural greenhouse effect, the temperature of the Earth would be about zero degrees F (-18°C) instead of its present 57°F (14°C).

We can intuit that if atmospheric carbon dioxide keeps the planet from freezing, an increase in carbon dioxide would raise temperatures, ceteris paribus.

As for predicting the past, consider this excerpt from skepticalscience.com:

So all models are first tested in a process called Hindcasting. The models used to predict future global warming can accurately map past climate changes. If they get the past right, there is no reason to think their predictions would be wrong. Testing models against the existing instrumental record suggested CO2 must cause global warming, because the models could not simulate what had already happened unless the extra CO2 was added to the model. All other known forcings are adequate in explaining temperature variations prior to the rise in temperature over the last thirty years, while none of them are capable of explaining the rise in the past thirty years. CO2 does explain that rise, and explains it completely without any need for additional, as yet unknown forcings.

Well, not quite, as the previous discussion about induction warns. Repeated observations of B following A does not prove that B will always follow A. The chicken may have reason to believe that he’s going to be fed the same as every other day, until he loses his head one morning.

On the other hand, the virtue of “hind-casting” is that models are tested against already observed data. If a model could not account for, or explain, the known past, then one would have a very good reason to question its reliability in accurately predicting as-yet-unknown events.

Models happen to be crucial in understanding climate—past, present, and future. So, much of the skepticism of global warming science involves modeling, with even noted physicist Freeman Dyson questioning their validity.

To be sure, building models is a complicated, tortuous process, with lots of trial and error, fine tuning, and the like. The challenge is to develop models that “fit” the evidence over time, taking into account a long array of actual and potential factors, including the role of clouds, water vapor, solar cycles, etc. Basically, climate models attempt to answer the question What if? In the present case, what if carbon dioxide emissions increase?

Now all of this I readily confess to being far over my head. I am not a scientist, though I have considerable experience working with fundamental, Newtonian physics and associated mathematics. (I used to be a forensic consultant, among other occupations.) I do have an appreciation, then, for the application of formulas to collected data. I also have profound respect, if not admiration, for scientists of all stripes, including those like Einstein who just sit and think without spending a minute in the lab.

Science, says my dictionary, is

the intellectual and practical activity encompassing the systematic study of the structure and behavior of the physical and natural world through observation and experiment.

The study of climate, in particular the effects of increasing greenhouse gas emissions, was probably at one time on par with studying species of flora and fauna. Interesting to researchers, but ho-hum to the ordinary layperson.

Then came warming. Thermometers around the globe were recording rising temperatures, at first ever so slight. Were the observed increases “normal” and therefore explainable by conventional theories, including axis tilts and solar output variability? The scientists involved stepped carefully, initially concluding that the temperature variations were within expected bounds.

But the planet kept warming. Now the scientists began speaking and writing about “anomalies,” which appeared to be increasing in frequency and magnitude over time.

In the 1980s NASA’s James Hansen addressed Congress. Having done his doctoral paper on the Venusian climate, which is all about the greenhouse effect, Hansen began to focus on earth’s climate, seeing some modest parallels. If greenhouse gases increased, so did temperatures. This happened on Venus, of course. Now it seemed to be happening on our own planet. Hansen issued a warming, which placed him far out on a political, if not also intellectual, limb: if we humans keep burning fossil fuels, the earth’s climate may become inhabitable for many of its species, including homo sapiens.

Meh, came the reply. Yet, temperatures continued to increase, just as Hansen and others before him had predicted.

Still, the scientists hedged. They began tentatively, talking about modest likelihoods. As research continued and models were refined, the likelihoods became bolder. We can see this progression in the reports of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, or IPCC. Today the world’s climate scientists are repeatedly using the term “very likely” to describe future events, including rising temperatures.

In my discussion above, I mentioned probabilities within the context of induction. Here is the IPCC:

Again, the hedging and “uncertainties”—scientists adamantly avoid making statements of the form: X is true, without a doubt. The history of science, replete with even wholesale revisions in understanding, engenders modesty and formal tentativeness. Over the first four IPCC reports, a period of 17 years, the scientists have escalated from “more likely than not” to “very likely.” The trend suggests that the next report will begin to move to the next level or levels of likelihood.

The challenge to those who would question the scientists and their reports looms ever larger. That’s because the evidence accumulates in favor of the proposition that increasing greenhouse gases raises global temperatures. It is becoming more difficult to find the countervailing fact in a modus tollens argument. Nor do alternative explanations for planetary warming pass muster.

To everyone’s benefit, however, the constant, even hysterical, critiques serve a purpose. The have forced the scientists to double-down on their research and model-building. As a result, their products are even more trustworthy, since they are increasingly rigorous in connecting the proliferating dots in anticipation of further assaults.

Yet, I have no doubt that the skeptics and deniers will neither retreat nor wave the white flag. But their criticisms have less and less to do with the science, which is only getting stronger, and more and more to do with politics and religion.

Unfortunately for the fate of the planet, they blather and purvey on fertile ground.

A bit more on Dewey

August 27, 2012 by davald

In my reading of Finnish Lessons I admit to being taken aback by the reference to John Dewey. To wit, the wholesale reforms of Finland’s education system were based on the philosophy of Mr. Dewey, who died from pneumonia on June 1, 1952, at the age of 92. That was 60 years ago, and yet his teachings evidently ignited or at least informed the so-called ‘Finnish Way.’

My shelves used to contain a lot more books, which became a burden over years’ worth of moving place to place. I’m guessing that my several books by Dewey wound up on local library shelves. But I retained a biography: John Dewey and American Democracy, by Robert B. Westbrook.

That title is important in understanding Dewey’s philosophy, in general, and his views on education, in particular. For Dewey, democracy and education went hand in hand. In his early years he had hoped with many progressives that education could serve as a catalyst for a new and improved democracy, one that valued each individual and allowed for the full development of all. Later, he became increasingly frustrated with political culture and the failure to realize his ideals. Democracy in America, he concluded, had become co-opted by a privileged few at the expense of the many.

My posts on Finland have emphasized the importance of situating educational reform within a larger societal context. The Finns began their reform effort with attitudes and aspirations antithetical to the typical American’s. First and foremost, Finns are dedicated to a strong welfare state, believing that, while success is partly determined by individual initiative and effort, where one lands on the socioeconomic ladder has much to do with fortuity.

That starting point, I suspect, helps explain why and how Finland succeeded in revamping its educational system while we Americans behave cluelessly. Our predispositions, our prejudices, and our biases combine to thwart genuine reform, at least in the Finnish manner.

We can also appreciate why Dewey grew pessimistic over time about education’s principal role in transforming society and government. He realized that the education system, along with nearly everything else, had come under the control of elites who would stop at nothing to prevent a genuine democracy. Here’s a excerpt from Westbrook’s biography:

Students spent much of their time and energy accumulating, memorizing, and largely forgetting a mass of disconnected information and acquiring mechanical skills. Little attempt had been made to change this situation, and as a result “too large a part of our citizens has left our schools without power of critical discrimination, at the mercy of special propaganda, and drifting from one plan and scheme to another according to the loudest clamor of the moment.”

Schools, he believed, were being enlisted to promote special interests, those defined by oligarchs (my term). Westbrook:

Dewey agreed that much of the education in American schools was little more than indoctrination, “especially with reference to narrow nationalism under the name of patriotism, and with reference to the dominant economic regime.”

Finland, as we’ve learned, places the highest priority on selecting and developing the best and the brightest teachers. That was certainly Dewey’s view, and he often railed against the plight of America’s teachers. Westbrook:

School boards as the representatives of those dominant interests “regard themselves after the analogy of private employers of labor and the teaching staff as their hired men and women.”  Teachers had very little control over their work. Administrators made out the course of study, prepared syllabuses for instruction, and established methods of teaching. The teacher simply took orders…Teachers, Dewey argued…, should recognize that they were as much “workers” as farmers and factory laborers, and as such were subject to the control of “the small and powerful class that is economically privileged.”

In the end for Dewey, when it came to democratic reform, it was all hands on deck, including public education. However, America is a very large, perhaps unmanageable country. That its federal government works at all may be judged a miracle. That it works less and less for the Rest of Us and more so for the One-percenters is as predictable as it may be inevitable. Perhaps meaningful reform of any aspect of society, including education, can be accomplished only by carving out the whole into parts. Begin where you’re planted, I guess.

Russell’s wisdom: why we fight

August 19, 2012 by davald

One essential primer is Bertrand Russell’s The Problems of Philosophy. Therein he provides a concise introduction to issues over which philosophers have long struggled. And if you have any familiarity with philosophy as an academic pursuit, you know that philosophers develop only tentative or provisional answers—such is the nature of the discipline. But if you’re patient, you’ll eventually uncover some kernels of wisdom, and Russell’s writings contain many.

For example, consider these words from the aforementioned book:

Material possessions can be taken by force and enjoyed by the robber. Spiritual possessions cannot be taken in this way. You may kill an artist or a thinker, but you cannot acquire his art or his thought. You may put a man to death because he loves his fellow-men, but you will not by so doing acquire the love which made his happiness. Force is impotent in such matters; it is only as regards material goods that it is effective. For this reason the men who believe in force are the men whose thoughts and desires are preoccupied with material goods.

Thus the conservative mind, since Republicans as a rule are obsessed with “material possessions.” What they have, they fear will be taken. What they lack, they strive to obtain—by almost any means. If they should rule, they will employ the nation’s considerable armaments to plunder the wealth of others. As for defending America’s fortunes, mostly owned by the privileged few, they will spare no expense.

They will not stop at the military or the police state. Their fear-cum-greed affects every fabric of our well-being.

In the hotly contested issue of educational reform, conservatives believe that students should learn those skills necessary to succeed in the acquisition of wealth or to tend to the care and feeding of the oligarchy. Critical thinking, the kind that would ultimately question status quo political arrangements, must be eliminated, since it poses a threat to the powerful. After all, the masses outnumber them.

Investing in the arts and music, except for the products of dead painters and composers, wastes dollars that should be earmarked for exploitation. Public dollars must never go toward self-expression or human development.*

On economic issues, merit determines all, and the rich possess a monopoly, since they made the rules and insist on punishing transgressors. At your workplace, you count for nothing, save for what you contribute to the wealth of your corporate benefactors. Drop any notion of unionism; Labor is evil, because it frustrates Capital’s unrelenting greed. Meanwhile, you are expected to work more hours and extract less from your effort.

 

 

Finally, the Rest of Us must stop worrying and learn to love:

  • decrepit school buildings
  • collapsing roads
  • urban ghettoes
  • ugly strip malls
  • pollution
  • global warming
  • no philosophy, no art, no music, no beauty (these are reserved for your masters, who are happy to pay dearly for them)
  • expensive and ineffective health care
  • mind-numbing TV reality shows
  • perpetual war
  • shit.

But take heart, your taxes are low.

Two veils

August 13, 2012 by davald

If you could play Founding Father for a moment, what kind of society, or government, would you design? More important, what set of assumptions would you make about human nature and what processes might you follow to arrive at your intended intellectual destination?

One American philosopher, joining the ranks of noteworthy predecessors (including Hume, Locke, and Rousseau), devoted much of his academic life to this challenge. The result: A Theory of Justice, perhaps the most celebrated 20th century work on political philosophy. The author: John Rawls.

Philosophy, I might add from personal experience, is hard. One reason is that it requires you to think deeply and radically about topics that most of us treat summarily, if at all. Justice? Fairness? Meh. John Rawls expended the energy and time to address these moral issues straight on. And how he came to his conclusions is every bit as fascinating as the conclusions themselves. But appreciating his journey and insights demands extraordinary focus and, I hastily add, an ability to suspend judgment and bias.

In today’s Stone essay, philosopher Benjamin Hale reminds us of Rawls’s Herculean triumph. He contrasts the late philosopher’s “veil of ignorance” with his own neologism: “veil of opulence.” Hale:

The idea behind the veil of ignorance is relatively simple: to force us to think outside of our parochial personal concerns in order that we consider others. What Rawls saw clearly is that it is not easy for us to put ourselves in the position of others. We tend to think about others always from our own personal vantage; we tend to equate another person’s predicament with our own. Imagining what it must be like to be poor, for instance, we import presumptions about available resources, talents and opportunities — encouraging, say, the homeless to pull themselves up by their bootstraps and to just get a job, any job, as if getting a job is as simple as filling out an application. Meanwhile, we give little thought to how challenging this can be for those who suffer from chronic illnesses or disabling conditions. What Rawls also saw clearly was that other classic principles of justice, like the golden rule or mutual benevolence, are subject to distortion precisely because we tend to do this.

Americans, in particular, approach the fundamental challenge in an entirely different fashion, behind a “veil of opulence.” Instead of exploring an “original position” in which we are ignorant of our own circumstances, we imagine ourselves to be the next Bill Gates or a rich hedge-fund manager. We replace ignorance with aspiration. If the goal is to become that wealthy individual, how might we go about achieving it? Moreover, if we assume that we are privileged, how do we answer questions about efficient or equitable taxation? Would I prefer a smaller government, one less intrusive and, therefore, less capable of impeding my personal aggrandizement? What would I think of the poor, the infirm, the “lesser mortals” who demand much but contribute little?

Hale suggests that the two competing veils serve as alternate prisms through which to view political ideologies, in general, and particular policy initiatives, in particular:

Those who don the veil of opulence may imagine themselves to be fantastically wealthy movie stars or extremely successful business entrepreneurs. They vote and set policies according to this fantasy. “If I were such and such a wealthy person,” they ask, “how would I feel about giving X percentage of my income, or Y real dollars per year, to pay for services that I will never see nor use?” We see this repeatedly in our tax policy discussions, and we have just seen the latest instance of it in the Tax Policy Center’s comparison of President Obama’s tax plan versus Mitt Romney’s tax plan. “He’s asking you to pay more so that people like him can pay less,” Obama said last week, “so that people like me pay less.” Last Monday he drove the point even harder, saying that Romney’s plan is like “Robin Hood in reverse.” And certainly, Romney’s selection on Saturday of Paul Ryan as his running mate will keep this issue in the forefront of our political discourse.

I could add yet another veil, the “veil of destitution.” Can we see ourselves as homeless, impoverished, without hope? In our society, with so much insularity and division, we may never encounter a “street person.” Our daily routines may take us from a suburban enclave to the downtown office. We can choose where to go and how to spend our time, completely avoiding contact with those less fortunate. How could we possibly develop sympathy outside experience? And since we don’t have to observe the plight of the indigent, why even have them appear on our intellectual radar screens?

Hale concludes:

The question of fairness has widespread application throughout our political discourse. It affects taxation, health care, education, social safety nets and so on. The veil of opulence would have us screen for fairness by asking what the most fortunate among us are willing to bear. The veil of ignorance would have us screen for fairness by asking what any of us would be willing to bear, if it were the case that we, or the ones we love, might be born into difficult circumstances or, despite our hard work, blindsided by misfortune. Society is in place to correct for the injustices of the universe, to ensure that our lives can run smoothly despite the stuff that is far out of our control: not to hand us what we need, but to give us the opportunity to pursue life, liberty and happiness. The veil of ignorance helps us see that. The veil of opulence keeps us in the dark.

Republicans, by the way, and especially their most recent incarnation, are generally selfish bastards who operate behind the “veil of opulence.” They may not be all One-percenters, but they certainly aspire to be one. About those very special people

July 18, 2012 by davald

Not a day goes by that we don’t read about this or that financial firm engaging in blatantly illegal activities, from submitting false data to produce a bogus Libor to screwing underwater homeowners to defrauding municipal governments on bond issues. The frequency and gravity of the wrongdoing belies the “few rotten apples” trope trotted out to preserve the status quo. The system, we are told, is essentially sound; just purge the rotten apples and we’ll resume “doing god’s work.”

Who are they kidding? Well, surely the self-serving message is intended for the Rest of Us. Sadly it resonates, either because we are too distracted to pay attention, too inured from the innumerable reports of iniquity, or we’re just too damn stupid to appreciate the enormity of the crimes. And I do mean crimes.

Okay. Say that you’re among the few who follow these travesties of justice and you have built up your intellectual self-defense over the years. You will not see these transgressions as anomalies or aberrant behaviors. You will properly understand that the capitalist-cum-political system is putrid to the core. And the source of the putrefaction is plain old opportunistic greed. If someone feels that they can exploit this or that for another buck, they’ll do so with no moral compunction. Indeed, the rules of engagement verily dictate devious acquisitiveness.

Philosopher Peter Singer in his book A Darwinian Left accuses progressives and liberals of ignoring human nature, specifically the tendency of our species to order ourselves into hierarchies and to behave without regard for others’ interests. He suggests that conservatives have a better grasp of human nature; they don’t believe in the inherent goodness of man or his malleability. After all, Adam ate the apple.

So here’s the irony. If Singer is correct, and Berkeley cognitive scientist George Lakoff seems to agree, then conservatives by and large give in to such negative impulses and even go so far as to enshrine them. “Greed is good.” We could add: by any means necessary.

Singer argues that those on the left must first acknowledge moral frailty then do something to limit or remove the circumstances that induce misbehavior and create those that yield better fruits. What the left gets right is understanding the inherent value of cooperation and mutual benefit, creating win-win rather than win-lose situations.

Lakoff’s book Moral Politics parallels Singer’s. He, too, distinguishes between the liberal and conservative mind, describing the differences in metaphors, the nurturant parent and strict father, respectively. But Lakoff is less reserved, less academic, if you will. Lakoff concludes that the nurturant parent is preferable to the other. We’d all be better off if we were raised in families led by adults who support and foster rather than punish and reward, and he thinks that the left should do something about it.

Lakoff chronicles, accurately I think, the rise of political conservatism in America, culminating in electoral victories. The advances resulted from a comprehensive and systematic focus on promoting a conservative worldview, a philosophy. Liberals and progressives, on the other hand, have naturally advocated for society’s more vulnerable members. But, unlike the conservatives, they’ve acted issue-by-issue for the most part and spent an inordinate amount of time, energy, and resources combatting the worst of conservatives’ specific proposals, and, I would add, they’re all bad. Consequently, liberals and progressives conspicuously lack an overarching ideological thrust, a coherent and cohesive set of broad principles that both informs and invigorates appropriate actions. Lakoff:

There is Black Pride and Gay Pride, but no Liberal Pride or Progressive Pride. It’s about time liberals got some.

…

Conservatives have taken the term ‘moral’ for themselves and liberals have let them keep it! It’s time to take it back.

All too often, Lakoff argues, liberals confront conservatives on the latter’s intellectual turf, allowing themselves to be co-opted by the shibboleths of “big government” and “government spending,” for example. As a cognitive scientist, Lakoff talks about framing and context and, yes, metaphors. Narratives are important.

Right now, judging by the recent election results, conservatives have the upper hand. Look at Congress, the state houses, and governors’ mansions. Still-Democratic areas feel threatened. Progressives and liberals find themselves on the defensive, unable or perhaps unwilling to counter the conservative ideology with one of their own, one that Lakaff and Singer believe is vastly preferable to the social Darwinist Republican motif, in which the rich get richer and poor, poorer. The Rest of Us would surely be better off, and we’d get there on our own terms rather than as a byproduct of the elite’s plundering.

Progressive Pride. I like it.

Drones: not so messy

July 15, 2012 by davald

The message here is that if you’re going to kill people, and since we’re Americans, kill we must, do it neatly.

“I had ethical doubts and concerns when I started looking into this,” said Bradley J. Strawser, a former Air Force officer and an assistant professor of philosophy at the Naval Postgraduate School. But after a concentrated study of remotely piloted vehicles, he said, he concluded that using them to go after terrorists not only was ethically permissible but also might be ethically obligatory, because of their advantages in identifying targets and striking with precision.

“You have to start by asking, as for any military action, is the cause just?” Mr. Strawser said. But for extremists who are indeed plotting violence against innocents, he said, “all the evidence we have so far suggests that drones do better at both identifying the terrorist and avoiding collateral damage than anything else we have.”
