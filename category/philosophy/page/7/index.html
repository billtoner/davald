Kill, even if you’re wrong

September 21, 2011 by davald

Troy Davis, having exhausted his options, is scheduled to die today in Georgia. Many prominent people here and around the globe have called for clemency. But, hey, this is the South, and southerners, especially, like to kill people, even if it’s a mistake.

The editors of the New York Times call the execution “a grievous wrong.” Why? Let us count the ways, taken from the editorial:

Seven of nine witnesses against Mr. Davis recanted after trial. Six said the police threatened them if they did not identify Mr. Davis. The man who first told the police that Mr. Davis was the shooter later confessed to the crime. There are other reasons to doubt Mr. Davis’s guilt: There was no physical evidence linking him to the crime introduced at trial, and new ballistics evidence broke the link between him and a previous shooting that provided the motive for his conviction.

Yet, there’s nothing like a good old-fashioned state murder. Why let these silly facts get in the way?

Say, you gonna die?

September 15, 2011 by davald

Well, of course. We’re all going to die—someday.

I try not to think about it, because death has reared its ugly head on several occasions, from grandparents to siblings and in between; and I didn’t care for those experiences very much. Yet, the philosopher in me used to wonder about life’s meaning, as if the ego of one pervaded the ego of all. Hey, we’re “God’s children.” Right?

I eventually abandoned the arrogance of such an enterprise, concluding that life, yours or mine, has no more meaning than a rock’s. I don’t feel lessened by that epiphany. It’s rather liberating, actually.

Roger Ebert is a much different man than he was while working with Gene Siskel. The thumbs still work. But cancer has stolen much of his jaw and below. He can only write, which he’s done well and amply for several decades.

His doctors had all but given up on him during one of his circulation-related episodes. His wife, however, knew he was still “there.” Evidently the physicians were persuaded. Ebert lived…

…to write his memoir, for one. An excerpt appears in Salon magazine. A worthy read, I say. For example:

What I expect to happen is that my body will fail, my mind will cease to function and that will be that. My genes will not live on, because I have had no children. I am comforted by Richard Dawkins’ theory of memes. Those are mental units: thoughts, ideas, gestures, notions, songs, beliefs, rhymes, ideals, teachings, sayings, phrases, clichés that move from mind to mind as genes move from body to body. After a lifetime of writing, teaching, broadcasting and telling too many jokes, I will leave behind more memes than many. They will all also eventually die, but so it goes.

O’Rourke’s had a photograph of Brendan Behan on the wall, and under it this quotation, which I memorized:

I respect kindness in human beings first of all, and kindness to animals. I don’t respect the law; I have a total irreverence for anything connected with society except that which makes the roads safer, the beer stronger, the food cheaper and the old men and old women warmer in the winter and happier in the summer.

You could do worse.

The smell test

August 30, 2011 by davald

Philosophers have long struggled with morality: what is right or wrong and why? It’s the why-question that presents so much difficulty. After all, if I can’t come up with a satisfactory theory of universal application, then I am guilty of being a moral relativist and therefore unconvincing.

Peter Singer argues, from utilitarian principles, that it is wrong to eat meat. Nevertheless, hundreds of millions of people do, evidently feeling no moral pangs. He also sanctions not only abortion but the killing of viable infants, should their quality of life be essentially negative owing to a horribly debilitating defect. Hospital boards now employ ethicists to advise them on life-or-death medical decisions. Thou shall not kill, says the Bible—except…Fill in the conditions. The Catholic Church preaches a just-war doctrine. A strict pacifist believes that all killing, even in self-defense, is wrong.

Identifying acts that are wrong or right usually emanate from moral theories, whether or not religious. But if you tell me that my doing x is wrong because, say, your pastor says as much, then since I do not attend your church, know your pastor, or am even an atheist, I feel free to ignore your admonition.

We can imagine, then, a matrix, with a list of possible acts in one column and rows across denoting moral theories and religions. Then enter ‘R’ for ‘right’ and ‘W’ for ‘wrong.’ A hodgepodge, I’m sure.

Yet, we’re not about to embrace moral nihilism if we can’t produce at least a few moral absolutes. How could we possibly interact in civil fashion if anything goes?

As it happens, there is at least one philosopher who claims to have escaped moralism, after a professional career dedicated to determining right from wrong and the basis for doing so.

Joel Marks offered his opinions on morality in a recent post published by the New York Times. He wrestled with moral relativism.

For essential to morality is that its norms apply with equal legitimacy to everyone; moral relativism, it has always seemed to me, is an oxymoron. Hence I saw no escape from moral nihilism.

But then he had an “anti-epiphany.” He writes:

The dominoes continued to fall. I had thought I was a secularist because I conceived of right and wrong as standing on their own two feet, without prop or crutch from God. We should do the right thing because it is the right thing to do, period. But this was a God too. It was the Godless God of secular morality, which commanded without commander – whose ways were thus even more mysterious than the God I did not believe in, who at least had the intelligible motive of rewarding us for doing what He wanted.

So where did Marks eventually land?

I now acknowledge that I cannot count on either God or morality to back up my personal preferences or clinch the case in any argument. I am simply no longer in the business of trying to derive an ought from an is.

It seems to me that Marks now relies on a personal “smell test” to decide the correctness of actions. He recoils at the thought of animals being slaughtered then butchered. If he sees you eating a steak, he might stop by your table to tell you a lot of facts and figures regarding industrial agriculture and perhaps even show you a picture of a beef rendering plant. Should you resume your chewing, Marks will assume that you have different “preferences,” then move on to the next meat-eating diner.

Well, I find this a bit squishy, even if, as he claims, he’s just as effective reciting cause and effect as he is inflicting moral platitudes. Moreover, how does this process, however pragmatic, not lead in the end to moral nihilism? I may derive perverse pleasure from torturing others, while the rest of us find such behavior abhorrent. If that’s your preference…?

In a previous post I found myself arriving at one moral absolute, which is very pragmatic: obey the laws. Yep, I know that there are problems with this, and I am hardly convinced of this position.

The Führer commands me to gas live Jews, and I happen to think that’s wrong. The Nuremberg Principles later say that I cannot simply follow orders, if I believe the act is wrong. But if I don’t obey, I, too, am killed. David Thoreau argued that if my conscience determined that a law was unjust, then I had a duty to engage in civil disobedience. Indeed, he believed that governments were more harmful than helpful, being essentially corrupt enterprises.

Philosophy makes me tired. It’s hard with no clear end in sight. Maybe just do your own thing, as long as no one else is bothered by it.

The absence of absolutes

August 10, 2011 by davald

Many, many years ago I studied philosophy in college. I have kept many of my books, but have long since forgotten what I may have learned. Or perhaps I forgot nothing because I learned nothing. Ah, the philosopher.

At any rate, we have this post in the New York Times entitled “The Maze of Moral Relativism,” written by a real-life philosopher, Paul Boghossian. He writes:

Denial of moral absolutism leads not to relativism, but to nihilism.

Which may be tantamount to saying “if nothing goes, everything goes.” Or not. Let’s see.

Those many years ago I took an introductory course in the philosophy of religion. I recall the professor trying to probe his semi-alert students for a moral absolute. Since there were no takers, he attempted to light upon one through a process of elimination. He finally got to this: Could we all agree that it was wrong to kill babies? He was not speaking of abortion but rather the murdering of an infant, presumably a healthy, viable one loved by its parents. As I recall, the class collectively nodded its agreement.

I also recall that none of us was able to put forth a rationale for the proscription. I think that we just felt that it would be horribly wrong to toss a baby off a cliff.

But, of course, that’s not philosophy, or at least the philosophy being taught at the university. There must be a reason for our revulsion.

Now, we happen to live in a culture that generally outlaws the murder of babies. Babies, after all, are “innocent”; there is nothing the baby could do to us that justified our taking its life. Even if the young child screams for hours on end to the point that we begin to lose it, should we kill the baby to shut it up, there’s no court in the land that’s going to give us a free pass. We have committed murder. Period.

Yet, I can imagine a culture steeped in a religion that called for periodic sacrifices of infants to, say, placate the gods. If we were the unfortunate parents of the chosen child, we wouldn’t like it very much when the elders came to snatch away our baby. But we would be forced to comply just the same. It’s the law, after all, and, besides, those gods are really angry about something, given the lousy streak of weather we’ve been having.

Should we then conclude that the killing of another’s baby is wrong in our culture but okay in the imagined culture? Is baby-killing a matter of moral relativism and not moral absolutism?

Suppose we take our discussion to less deadly arenas. What about baseball?

I’ll suggest an absolute: the umpire is always right. I’ll add: even when he’s not. Huh?

Those of us who watch ball games know that umpires blow calls or declare a pitch over the plate when the cameras show that it was a foot outside. We can get mad. The manager, say Sweet Lou, could get so angry that he would literally pull the bases out of the ground then toss them. The video replays showed that Lou was “right” and the umpire “wrong.” No matter. Lou also gets tossed and the rest of us fume.

The Supreme Court hands down a decision on a case. Law professors judge the opinion terribly flawed based on x, y, and z. No matter. The decision becomes the law of the land. When acting as a body the “supremes” are always right, just like baseball umpires, even when many disagree.

Back to our moral discussion, we should appreciate that there many different moral philosophies. I’ll name a few: utilitarianism; consequentialism; pragmatism. Since I’m too lazy to tell you the differences, I’ll just say that what might be considered morally good in one system may be rejected in another.

Peter Singer, who now teaches at Princeton, advocates a form of utilitarianism. Put simply, a utilitarian believes that it is right to maximize pleasure but wrong to inflict pain and suffering. Singer, then, could justify the killing of a baby under certain circumstances, provided that doing so improved the utility of others and did not cause the child to suffer. Singer is a devoted vegetarian (actually a “vegan”). He does not eat meat because the cow or chicken is capable of feeling pain, and the way we raise then slaughter animals causes much suffering. He also concludes that eating flesh is inefficient: he can meet his nutritional needs using far less calories on a vegetarian diet.

But suppose that even plants experience pain when uprooted or cut down, as some research suggests. What then, Prof. Singer? You have to eat something. I suppose you can still rely on the efficiency argument and continue to avoid eating meat, even if it’s no longer a matter of inflicting pain and suffering.

Yet, millions of us eat meat without any moral qualms. If I simply refuse to accept utilitarianism, or any other moral philosophy, how can I be bound by its dictates?

Similarly, many of us are people of faith, whether it be Protestant, Catholic, or other. While Christians as a whole profess adherence to adhere to the ten commandments of the old testament, there are differences in moral interpretation between Protestantism and Catholicism. For example, take the old Catholic prohibition against eating meat on fridays. Protestants had no such rule. There are Catholics who are strict pacifists and Catholics who justify almost any war. The commandment “Thou shall not kill” is rarely treated as an absolute by either Protestants or Catholics—it depends. Yet, as with utilitarianism, if I’m not a Catholic, I may find what the pope says interesting, but I’m under no obligation to follow his directives.

Despite this “maze of moral relativism,” could we really function without some absolute rules and proscriptions? If something is wrong for me it should be wrong for you. For example, if I should not kill you, then you should not kill me. We’d be in a heck of a mess if some of us could legally kill without consequence while the rest of us were prohibited from returning fire to defend ourselves.

It would seem, then, that a moral principle is absolute insofar as it can be universally applied. After some reflection, we discover that even the commandment against killing fails to be an absolute principle since there are exceptions, widely deemed valid. These include: self-defense; just war; and in America, the right of the state to kill its citizens following “due process.”

While not itself a moral principle, there appears to be a universal application of adherence to laws, as in the phrase “no one is above the law.” It is assumed that all citizens of the United States are bound by the Constitution, from which all the other laws derive. It really doesn’t matter whether you are Catholic or Utilitarian; you must abide by the laws.

Suppose, however, that you believe a law or its application unjust. Thoreau held that one even has a duty to disobey an unjust law. Yet, he acknowledged that the state had authority to create laws and bind its citizens to them.

Well, it’s time to wrap this up. It occurs to me, at any rate, that a functioning society must have laws that apply equally to all its citizens. That’s not philosophy. That’s not religion. It’s more a matter of commonsense pragmatism. Without this absolute in place, we just might have nihilism.

Individualists, it’s your turn.

Mortal Kombat as free speech

June 27, 2011 by davald

I have mixed feelings about the Supreme Court decision to overturn California’s ban on selling violent video games to children. Here’s the New York Times:

Justice Antonin Scalia., writing for five justices in the majority in the video games decision, Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Association, No. 08-1448, said video games were protected by the First Amendment.

“Like the protected books, plays and movies that preceded them, video games communicate ideas — and even social messages — through many familiar literary devices (such as characters, dialogue, plot and music) and through features distinctive to the medium (such as the player’s interaction with the virtual world),” Justice Scalia wrote. “That suffices to confer First Amendment protection.”

Let’s suppose that viewing violent video games is harmful to children, or adults, for that matter. The state, nevertheless, cannot bar their sales, because images of mayhem and murder have the same constitutional protections as a corporation giving a million dollars to fund a campaign hit piece.

The California law would have imposed $1,000 fines on stores that sold violent video games to people under 18. It defined violent games as those “in which the range of options available to a player includes killing, maiming, dismembering or sexually assaulting an image of a human being” in a way that was “patently offensive,” appeals to minors’ “deviant or morbid interests” and lacked “serious literary, artistic, political or scientific value.”

It’s ironic that the California law incorporates language from prior Supreme Court cases banning sales of pornography to children. Killing, maiming, and dismembering a female image is okay, but not having virtual, explicit sex with the same image.

What might have been the decision had a group of enterprising programmers devised a video game that featured the killing, maiming, and dismembering of current Supreme Court members? I can imagine a psychopathic teenager living in D.C. attempt to transform the virtual into reality. I can also imagine one or more psychiatrists connecting the dots, concluding that but for the deranged adolescent incessantly playing the game he wouldn’t have tried to act on his learned impulses.

Across the pond several European Union states ban the sale of violent vide games—because they are considered harmful to minors. But those nations don’t go to war like the US, so they don’t need to raise young adults to become crazed butchers.

How do we know what we know?

June 17, 2011 by davald

I confess. I studied philosophy in college; quite a bit of it, in fact. But that was 40 years ago, and I’ve forgotten most of it—I think.

Philosophy as an academic discipline is on the wane, with many students justly concerned about how to make a living once they’ve graduated. It’s been a while, but last time I checked the Help Wanted section employers weren’t actively seeking “philosophers.”

Job prospects aside, the study of philosophy could be its own reward. You get to seriously examine fundamental questions that most of us have but dare not ponder. In my studies, the usual opening line to begin a conversation was, “What do you mean by x?” And x could be any word or phrase. I’d like to believe that philosophizing yielded a clearer understanding of, well, everything. Truth be told, however, I am as confused about matters as I ever was.

Nevertheless, I appreciate the New York Times including a section it calls ‘The Stone.’ With some regularity, real, live philosophers—those who teach philosophy at our universities—tackle one “problem” or another. (I inserted scare quotes because what may appear to be a problem for a philosopher is likely judged a waste of time by the rest of us.)

In today’s entry, Professor Gary Gutting of Notre Dame ruminates on the nature of knowledge, or what we philosophers, both real and imagined, call ‘epistemology.’ Prof. Gutting calls into question the prediction of Harold Camping that the world was going to end on May 21 of this year. As the world crumbled, the good people, of course, would be raptured up to heaven. It didn’t happen. Thank God? Prof. Gutting suggests that Mr. Camping’s prophecy was “ill-advised,” which implies that it was not carefully thought out. Indeed.

Prof. Gutting distinguishes between subjective and objective certainty. Forecasting end times falls under the former category. Propositions (another philosophy favorite) of such a form cannot be refuted by appeals to facts. Karl Popper, the Austrian philosopher who eventually called England his home, came up with the “principle of falsification.” That is, you know that you’re dealing with matters of objective certainty when the statements used are subject to falsification. For example, if I utter the statement “The sun is shining,” we know that it can be either true or false depending on whether or not the sun is shining. (By the way, if you don’t believe that the sun is shining despite the bright orb burning brightly in the sky, then that’s your problem.)

So, Prof. Gutting questions Mr. Camping’s epistemology. The forecaster can’t know in advance of an occurrence that x will happen. We just have to wait and see. Here’s Prof. Gutting:

The case against Camping was this: His subjective certainty about the rapture required objectively good reasons to expect its occurrence; he provided no such reasons, so his claim was not worthy of belief. Christians who believe in a temporally unspecified rapture agree with this argument. But the same argument undermines their own belief in the rapture. It’s not just that “no one knows the day and hour” of the rapture. No one knows that it is going to happen at all.

We’ll note that the good professor teaches at Notre Dame, a Catholic college best known for its football teams. Catholic dogma begins, like all major religions, with a belief in God.

And who or what is God? My Catholic catechism featured this definition: The Supreme Being who made all things. An early church theologian, St. Anselm, defined God as “that than which nothing greater can be conceived.” Okay. You squint your eyes, furrow your brows as you try to imagine the greatest thing possible. Anselm then added this interesting trick: Whatever you may have conjured up in your head will still not be the greatest unless it has the property of existence. Neat.

Now let’s explore Prof. Gutting’s argument above. He says that Mr. Camping’s prediction was “not worthy of belief,” because such worthiness requires “objectively good reasons.” Hmmm. What about the Baltimore Catechism’s definition of ‘God’? Does it pass Prof. Gutting’s criteria for worthiness? No, it would seem. There are no “objectively good reasons” for believing that the definition is correct.

However, it would seem that the proposition “God exists” is susceptible to falsification, as is, by extension, the catechism’s definition. They are empirical statements. There just don’t seem to be any “objectively good reasons” for believing them. Philosophically speaking, we must be agnostic.

What has always rankled me is the certainty that so many people attach to matters of belief, which, by definition, require faith in something that cannot be proven by “objectively good reasons.” Thus, global warming is a “hoax” whereas the existence of God cannot be questioned.

Surveys consistently show that there are a whole lot of religious believers in the U.S. Now, that’s a problem.
