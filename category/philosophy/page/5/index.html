You want data on inequality?

March 1, 2012 by davald

You might read through this essay by Michael Yates for Monthly Review, to which I subscribe. (It’s also on my blogroll.) Some excerpts:

  • If we take the total gain in household income between 1979 and 2007, 60 percent of it went to the richest 1 percent of individuals, while just 8.6 percent accrued to the poorest 90 percent. An incredible 36 percent found its way into the pockets of the richest 0.1 percent (one-one thousandth of all individuals).
  • If a person spent $10,000 a day…, it would take 100,000 days to spend a billion dollars, just under 274 years.
  • In 2010, more than 7 million people had incomes less than 50 percent of the official poverty level of income, an amount equal to $11,245, which in hourly terms (2,000 hours of work per year) is $5.62.
  • …in 2009, the top 1 percent of households owned 35.6 percent of net wealth (net worth) and a whopping 42.4 percent of net financial assets (all financial instruments such as stocks, bonds, bank accounts, and all the exotic instruments that helped trigger the Great Recession, minus non-mortgage debt). The bottom 90 percent owned 25 percent of net wealth and 17.3 percent of net financial wealth. The richest 1 percent had 33.1 percent of net worth in 1983 (the chart does not show the numbers for 1980), an increase of 7.5 percent; if we extend our view to the wealthiest 5 percent, we see a rise in share from 58.1 to 63.5 percent, an increase of 9.3 percent. The bottom four-fifths of households suffered a decline in their share of net worth, from 18.7 to 12.8 percent, a loss of nearly 32 percent.

Yates includes a rather lengthy quote from economist Eric Schutz’s book Inequality and Power: The Economics of Class. It bears a careful read, for it provides us with a sense of how wealth accumulates among the already-wealthy and how difficult it is for poor people to elevate their socioeconomic status.

In Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, where I lived for many years, there is an extraordinarily wealthy family, the Hillmans, with a net worth of several billion dollars. One of their homes, along once fashionable Fifth Avenue, is a gorgeous mansion on a magnificent piece of property. About three miles east of this residence is the Homewood section of the city, whose mean streets have been made famous by the writer, John Edgar Wideman. On North Lang Street there is a row of three connected apartments. One of the end apartments has been abandoned to the elements to the rodents and the drug users. This is gang territory, and if you are African American, you do not go there wearing the wrong colors. Poverty, deep and grinding, is rampant on this street and in this neighborhood, which has one of the nation’s highest infant mortality rates.

Consider two children, one born in the Hillman house and another born in the North Lang Street apartment. In the former, there are two rich and influential parents, and in the latter there is a single mother working nights with three small children. Let us ask some basic questions. Which mother will have the best health care, with regular visits to the doctor, medicine if needed, and a healthy diet? Which child is more likely to have a normal birth weight? Which child is more likely to get adequate nutrition and have good health care in early childhood? If the poor child does not have these things, who will return to this child the brain cells lost as a consequence? Which child is more likely to suffer the ill effects of lead poisoning? Which child is more likely to have an older sibling, just 12 years old, be responsible for him when the mother is working at night? Which will be fed cookies for supper and be entertained by an old television set? If the two children get ill in the middle of the night, which one will be more likely to make it to the emergency room in time? Which child will start school speaking standard English, wearing new clothes, and having someone at home to make sure the homework gets done? Which child will travel, and which will barely make it out of the neighborhood?

As the two children grow up, what sort of people will they meet? Which will be more likely to meet persons who will be useful to them when they are seeking admission to college or looking for a job or trying to find funding for a business venture? Which will be more likely to be hit by a stray bullet fired in a war over drug turf? Which will go to the better school? Which will have access to books, magazines, newspapers, and computers in the home? Which one will wear worn-out clothes? Which one will be embarrassed because his clothes smell? Which one will be more likely to have caring teachers who work in well-equipped and safe schools? Which one will be afraid to tell the teacher that he does not have crayons and colored paper at home? Which child will learn the grammar and syntax of the rich? Which child will join a gang? Abuse drugs? Commit a crime? Be harassed by the police because he is black? When these two children face the labor market, which one will be more productive?

To ask these questions is to answer them. And when we consider that this poor child in the United States is better off than two-thirds of the world’s population, we must conclude that most of the world’s people live in a state of deprivation so extreme that they must be considered to have almost no opportunities at all. They are almost as condemned as the person on death row in a Texas prison.

As for the antidote to this great disparity, Mr. Yates suggests embracing a Marxist critique for a more accurate perspective on the market economy and its skewed outcomes. What happens next, I gather, is up to us.

Intergenerational equity

February 22, 2012 by davald

Following several decades of declining average residential consumption, the good citizens of Snohomish County started using more electricity—despite Snohomish County PUD’s record-breaking conservation efforts (see chart immediately below, which shows the average consumption per residential meter in annual kilowatt-hours). In this post I don’t want to focus on why this is happening. Here I wish to use this recent upward trend to explore a philosophical issue.

If population continues to increase and if per-customer electricity consumption also increases, the PUD and other utilities experiencing the same trends will have to increase their power supplies, do even more conservation, or both.

As I mentioned above, the PUD has, since 2005, steadily increased its annual conservation numbers (aMW). Beginning in 2009 the PUD has broken the previous annual record every year. But, even this level of conservation has failed to arrest the rise in electricity consumption. Each of us, on average, is using more kilowatt-hours than before, and there are more of us each year.

Whatever we use today of finite resources will not be available for future generations. Also, with consumption comes waste, which must be disposed of. As we continue to pollute the air and the water and the land we create scarcities of public goods for future generations.

Yet, should we even care about the well-being of future generations? Do we have any moral obligations to make the planet habitable for our progeny?

Let’s try a thought experiment based on John Rawls’s discussion of “the original position.” Imagine that you are in a special room with nine others. Each of you ten represents the interests of a century’s worth of people. However, you don’t know which century you represent. It could be this century or it could have been five centuries ago. The point is, you don’t know. We’ll also assume for this experiment that there are no technological differences between centuries. Thus, what the tenth century could do with machines and gadgets and devices the first century could do, as well. In doing this I want to ignore the positive technological and intellectual capital that one generation bequeaths to the next, so that there is no cost-benefit analysis going on from one generation to another, as in, “I’ll put up with the stinky, rotten air because I’ve got this great Blackberry to play with.” (That’s so 20th century.)

Let’s also assume that there is a grand moderator who tasks the group with making energy resource decisions behind this “veil of ignorance.” How do you decide?

If you knew you represented the earliest of the ten centuries, you might not give a damn about what you leave behind. If you were the second century, you might experience a little less of this and a little more of that; but in total, your well-being is not all that different from the first century’s. However, by the tenth century, the accumulation of I-don’t-give-a-damn decisions leaves you with nine centuries’ worth of garbage, polluted air, global warming, food insecurity, and so on. If you could, you’d ring the necks of the other nine.

But, again, you don’t know which century you represent. If you imagine that your century is at the mid-point or closer to the last, you would very well prefer that the previous centuries had behaved themselves, and didn’t leave the planet worse off than when they found it. Indeed, because you don’t know, you’re likely to make decisions that do no harm, or as little as possible, since you could be on the receiving end of, well, a whole lot of shit. And no one likes shit.

I’ll assume that you generally appreciate the implications of this little thought experiment, and that you understand the do-no-harm principle under the “veil of ignorance.” So, what kind of energy resource decisions do you make?

We might consider those things we would not do, for fear that we could be on the receiving end. Here’s a brief list:

  • mine, pulverize, then burn all the coal under the ground
  • extract oil from tar sands that is later burned
  • buy and drive gas guzzlers
  • extract and burn all the natural gas you can find
  • cut down all the trees and burn them to make room for petroleum-based agriculture
  • toss all your used batteries and gadgets into the garbage
  • have more than two children
  • increase your consumption of electricity

These things that we’d likely not do look awfully familiar, because that’s pretty much what we do today. Suppose we avoid doing them all. Is that enough? Hardly.

You still need electricity to do just about everything, from running your alarm clock to drying your hair to warming up leftovers to powering your computers and gadgets to keeping the lights on. You may also need it to keep warm, especially if you live in a multi-family dwelling.

Can we continue to operate our devices without using up all the resources and leaving the planet in a mess? Maybe, but it will take a different mindset.

First of all, from this thought experiment we can gain a keener understanding of sustainability. We cannot exhaust finite resources as if there were no tomorrow. But are there enough renewable resources to provide our electricity needs over ten centuries and more?

On a positive note, consider that almost all of the electricity consumed by PUD customers is renewable, and most of that is hydro. Only a tiny fraction of the supply comes from fossil-fuel generation plants, and that’s only because the utility is forced at times to buy power from the market. Since that market is largely defined by the regional transmission grid, each utility served by that system can and does use power generated by coal and natural gas plants in Montana, Oregon, Idaho, and any of eleven western states, plus parts of Canada and even northern Mexico.

However, if you lived just about anywhere else in the United States, most of your electricity is generated by combusting carbons. Are there enough renewable resources to replace indigenous coal and gas?

Back to conservation, by using scarce resources more efficiently there would be more electricity to go around, perhaps even enough for future generations. But trust me, that’s going to require a quantum leap in how we live, work, build and transport things, and get from here to there.

To be sure, I really don’t know if the entire population of the planet could meet its electricity needs from only renewable resources plus conservation. If we can’t figure out a way to do so, then we may be consigning future generations to misery and privation.

I’ll offer my quick answer. I don’t think we have the collective will to change course. We’ll continue as before, with a relative few managing to escape the negative externalities imposed by prior generations. The descendants of the Rest of Us will not be so lucky.

Some wise words from a Catholic philosopher

February 16, 2012 by davald

The mistake of the Obama administration — and of almost everyone debating its decision — was to accept the bishops’ claim that their position on birth control expresses an authoritative “teaching of the church.”  (Of course, the administration may be right in thinking that the bishops need placating because they can cause them considerable political trouble.)  The bishops’ claim to authority in this matter has been undermined because Catholics have decisively rejected it. Repetition facilitates memory

February 13, 2012 by davald

I have in mind the TED talk given by philosopher Alain de Botton, the topic of my previous, brief post. He told his audience of a visit to an African-American church in the U.S. South. After nearly every sentence or two by the preacher, the congregation would affirm with an “Amen!” or other such expression. The preacher repeated himself. Indeed, repetition was integral to the sermon, helping those in the pews to get the message.

Readers of this site experience repetition. I am wont to repeat points that, in my humble opinion, bear repeating until we all “get the message.”

The overarching message is that this country is a train wreck waiting to happen. Or, using Bill Moyers’s metaphor, a sinking ship.

Watching what’s happening to our democracy is like watching the cruise ship Costa Concordia founder and slowly sink into the sea off the coast of Italy. The passengers, shorn of life vests, scramble for safety as best they can, while the captain trips and falls conveniently into a waiting life boat.

Here at home we are drowning, with gaping holes torn into the hull of the ship of state from charges detonated by the owners and manipulators of capital. Their wealth has become a demonic force in politics. Nothing can stop them. Not the law, which is written to accommodate them. Not scrutiny they have no shame. Not a decent respect for the welfare of others. The people without means, whose safety net has been shredded, leaving them helpless before events beyond their control, like those passengers on that ship.

Sharp words, Chairman Bill. But sadly true, the evidence all around us.

For the moment, I have concluded that trying to understand the conservative mind is a waste of time and energy. Try as I might, I fail to make sense of the whole proposition, despite the conservatives’ allegedly more accurate view of human nature—that our species is inherently competitive, we form hierarchies, and we’re not all that susceptible to social engineering, what Marx called “the ensemble of social relations.”

For me the two fatal defects in conservatism, at least as it is currently practiced by the Republican Party, are willful ignorance of facts and an almost complete lack of empathy. They just don’t give a damn about others.

And therein, especially, lies our larger problem. Insofar as there are too many conservatives in political office who have the power to thwart a progressive agenda, the only viable solution, it seems to me, is to get rid of them.

No, I’m not advocating violence. There is a polite way to turn them out; it’s called democracy.

Bill Moyers recommended this site to his viewers. Borrowing from the late Saul Alinsky, Newt Gingrich’s favorite whipping boy who can hardly defend himself from the grave, Sara Robinson offers “New Rules for Radicals: 10 Ways to Spark Change in a Post-Occupy World.”

The first rule is to appreciate that “the world is different now.” That’s part of what I try to do here on this site, say and repeat just what this different world is about, in particular how the rich have sucked us dry yet have no desire to stop sucking.

Robinson advises us to drop the cynicism and despair (two of my favorite moods these days) and replace them with anger. And there’s plenty to be angry about. “The answer,” she says, “is to get off our butts and get back to work, because life is short and there’s a whole planet out there that needs to be fixed on our watch.”

“Try everything,” Robinson urges, with exceptions: “Of course, there are boundaries: don’t try anything that violates our principles, or replicates the things we hate the most about the other side. There will be no cheating, no lying, and no crazies with guns taking out people we disagree with. We have to be better than that: if we betray our values, we lose everything.”

I particularly like these two new rules:

  • Focus on our goals, and not on our enemies. (Which is another reason to drop my study of conservatism.)
  • Expect resistance.

Resisting or reacting to liberal and progressive advances (e.g., minimum wage law, environmental protections, civil rights, safety nets) is what defines conservatism, according to Corey Robin. It may be helpful to achieving a better society to summarily dismiss reactionaries as ignorant bastards that need to be purged from the body politic—again, politely so. Don’t try to engage them, since they never make sense and they are all about screwing the Rest of Us.

A Darwinian Left

February 8, 2012 by davald

That’s the title of one of philosopher Peter Singer’s books. It seems apropos of my posts on conservatism and liberalism, for Singer, like Jonathan Haidt and George Lakoff, believes that the right has something to teach the left.

In discussing Haidt’s remarks to Bill Moyers I mentioned that the University of Virginia social psychologist asserts that conservatives have a more accurate view of human nature. Maybe, maybe not. Lakoff disagrees, but Singer falls in with Haidt.

Singer, before making his mark as a prominent ethicist at Princeton, was a student of Marx’s philosophy, though he certainly avoids the label ‘Marxist.’ He seems more comfortable in the world of moral philosophy, specifically his version of utilitarianism, marinated with socialist compassion.

In the book, A Darwinian Left, Singer writes, first quoting Marx from his Theses on Feuerbach (VI):

“…the human essence is no abstraction inherent in each single individual. In its reality it is the ensemble of the social relations.”

It follows from this belief that if you can totally change the “ensemble of social relations,” you can totally change human nature.

Singer suggests that this point forms the core of Marx and most of the traditional left’s political philosophy. But, Singer says, “The Left needs a new paradigm.”

And what might that be?

I may not do justice to Singer here, but, with Haidt, he asserts that human nature is such that individuals will fall into hierarchical arrangements, no matter the circumstances. It is a consequence of Darwinian evolution. Thus, Marx got it wrong, says Singer, for believing that egalitarian societies will emerge from a rearrangement of the “ensemble of social relations.”

The new paradigm for the left, then, begins with a rejection of Marx’s core principal and an acknowledgement that humans eventually form stratified groups, with superiors and subordinates arrayed on one basis or another. In U.S. society, the dominant organizing principle is money. But human societies have always been sorted by color, that most salient of characteristics. We can add to this religion and no doubt a host of other categories that tend to distinguish one group of individuals from another.

In Chapter 5 of his book Singer sets out what a new Left would reject and what it would embrace. I’ll mention a few of each.

  • The Left should reject the notion that humans are essentially good (contra Rousseau) and infinitely malleable (contra Marx).
  • The Left should reject the belief that humans can live in a state of indefinite harmony without war or famine. There will always be conflict between and among us.
  • The Left should not assume that all differences in social or economic status are the result of deliberate discrimination. Some of us, by our inherited abilities (Darwin) will rise to the top (of whatever), some will fall to the bottom, and the rest in the middle.

Meanwhile, the Left should:

  • develop a better understanding of human nature;
  • not infer, as conservatives do, that what is “natural” is therefore morally “right”;
  • appreciate that individuals will compete for fame and fortune in a condition of scarcity, and economics is all about the distribution of scare goods;
  • understand that notwithstanding the innate tendency to compete, individuals will prefer cooperation if they perceive the likelihood of mutually beneficial outcomes; the Left should push for structures and conditions that promote cooperation and “channel competition into socially desirable ends.”

In my previous discussion of Haidt I failed to draw attention to one category that sharply distinguishes the liberal from the conservative. Liberals score highest on the category “care and compassion,” far higher than conservatives, who tend to rank the same in each of the six categories. (You’ll need to go to Moyers website to see the breakdown.)

Singer wants us to embrace our empathetic tendencies, or at least that’s his prescription for a new “Darwinian Left.” But we should always keep in mind that egalitarian utopias are only figments of imagination; our human nature prevents them from forming or certainly enduring.

Nor should we ignore the fact that cooperation is at least as natural as competition. The challenge is to build a society in which the winners compensate the losers through cooperative structures and mechanisms. As Singer puts it, “We need to think about how to set up conditions in which cooperation survives.”

I take from this, though I found no reference to this by Singer, that a more Nordic-like society gives greater expression to the new Left paradigm than our existing winner-take-all system. For whatever historical reasons, and I’m ill-equipped to name them, Sweden, Finland, et al. have more egalitarian societies than the U.S., which is near the top of the inequality scale. Moreover, Nordic citizens also have a stronger regard for government than Americans do, and they evidently view their respective governments as an embodiment and facilitator of cooperation. But these Nordic countries also have market economies, which entails competition between and among private firms. The combination of competition and cooperation is manifested in strong social welfare societies that mitigate the extremes of wealth and poverty.

I think we can—and should—follow Singer’s advice. However, we don’t have to reinvent the wheel. Precedents already exist in northern Europe.

Speaking of conservatives

February 6, 2012 by davald

I’ve got a couple of recent posts on conservatism, one here and the other here. I readily concede my bias; I have strong liberal tendencies. But I confess difficulty trying to understand the conservative mind, so much so, that I believe I’d find it impossible to even have a conversation with someone on the extreme right, although I’ve been able to successfully converse with someone from the polar opposite, who happens to be my doppelgänger. As I’ve also mentioned previously, Berkeley cognitive scientist George Lakoff endeavored to discover the conservative mind, coming up with a set of moral metaphors distinguishing those on the political right from those on the political left.

In last night’s Bill Moyers & Company we’re introduced, at least I was, to Jonathan Haidt (pronounced ‘hite’), a social psychologist with the University of Virginia. He’s just written a book (available for pre-order at the usual outlets) entitled Righteous Mind: Why Good People are Divided by Politics and Religion. His effort resembles Lakoff’s, although from the perspective of a psychologist rather than a linguist/cognitive scientist.

Haidt has discovered a pronounced split between left and right across six categories. These are:

  1. care, or compassion
  2. liberty
  3. fairness
  4. loyalty
  5. authority
  6. sanctity

The professor has devised an online test, which he calls a “morality quiz.” I took it, and here are my results:

The green bars represent my scores. The blue and red depict those of liberals and conservatives, respectively. It seems that I’m certainly not a conservative, and I’m a bit more to the left than the average liberal.

Haidt acknowledges that we are a sharply divided country today, with each side “demonizing” the other, accusing our opposites of being and doing “evil.” Once you get to this point, he suggests, the ends justify the means. We see this playing out in Congress, with the Republicans strongly and stubbornly adhering to what they believe to be their core principles.

To illustrate this rigidity Moyers showed an excerpt from 60 Minutes, wherein Leslie Stahl is interviewing House Speaker John Boehner. It’s almost, but not quite, funny, as Boehner insists that he will “not compromise.” When Stahl insists that the art of governing is all about compromise, Boehner says that the goal of both sides is to find “common ground.” Isn’t that compromise?—Stahl counters. No, we will not compromise, Boehner insists.

America wasn’t always this way. When Bill Moyers was working for Lyndon Johnson in the White House, there were such animals as “moderate Republicans,” whose votes were necessary to secure passage for the Civil Rights and Voting Rights acts. There was still evidence of this species well into Nixon’s administration, whose domestic agenda was more liberal and effective than either Carter’s or Clinton’s.

But Johnson also foresaw the loss of the southern Democrat with his signing of the aforementioned acts. The South will switch to the Republicans, he predicted. And so it was. (Haidt thinks that southerners used to be Democrats because Lincoln was a Republican. Now that the Democrats had finally completed Lincoln’s emancipation agenda, might was well join the GOP.)

Haidt allows that he used to be a solid liberal Democrat. But he’s gradually moved to the center because of his research findings. He’s now agnostic on the liberal-vs-conservative preference. For one, he is now more inclined to accept the lessons of karma: there should be consequences for our actions, both good and bad. Thus, the conservative principle of just deserts appeals to him. Also, he claims, the conservatives have a more accurate picture of human nature. From the interview:

When I began this work, I was very much a liberal. And over time, in doing the research for my book and in reading a lot of conservative writing, I’ve come to believe that conservative intellectuals actually are more in touch with human nature. They have a more accurate view of human nature.

We need structure. We need families. We need groups. It’s okay to have memberships and rivalries. All that stuff is okay, unless it crosses the threshold into Manichaeism [my link]. So I think that it would be very difficult to run a good society without resting much on loyalty, authority and sanctity. I think you need to use those.

Moyers showed Haidt several photographs of Occupy Wall Street and Tea Party rally signs. What do they tell us?—asks Moyers.

JONATHAN HAIDT: So what I’m hoping my book will do is kind of give people almost a decoding manual so they can look at anything from the other side and instead of saying, ‘See, this shows how evil they are,’ you say, ‘Oh, okay, I see why they’re saying that.’ All right, so, let’s take, ‘Stop punishing success, stop rewarding failure.’

BILL MOYERS: I remember seeing that at one of the early Tea Party rallies.

JONATHAN HAIDT: So that’s one version of fairness. Fairness adds proportionality.

BILL MOYERS: What do you mean?

JONATHAN HAIDT: Well, if people work hard, they should succeed. If people don’t work hard, they should fail. And if anyone bails them out, that is evil. You should not bail people out who have failed, especially if it’s because of lack of hard work, something like that. So as the right sees it, government is evil because it keeps punishing success, with redistributive policies, okay, take from the successful and give to the unsuccessful.

And it keeps rewarding failure by giving out welfare and other payments to people who aren’t working. So what I’ve found is that fairness is at the heart of both Occupy Wall Street and the Tea Party. But because the words have different meanings and they relate to additional moral foundations, that’s why they’re really, very, very different moral views.

There was a lot of empathy and caring at Occupy Wall Street. So this sign, “I can’t hurt another without hurting myself.” This is part of the ethos on the left, this is why you get a lot of Buddhists and sort of the Christian left.

It’s a lot of emphasis on care and compassion. When they talk about fairness, it’s in particular, fairness, that will benefit the weak and the poor. So, here’s a sign, “Marching for the meek and weary, hungry and homeless.” “Tax the wealthy, fair and square,” as though because they’re hungry and homeless people, it’s fair to take from them and give to them. Now, I think there are really good arguments for why we need to increase tax rates on the top. But simply saying, ‘Some have and some have not, therefore it’s fair,’ that’s not a moral argument for most Americans.

BILL MOYERS: And what’s the conservative moral position on this?

JONATHAN HAIDT: The conservative moral position is the Protestant work ethic. It’s karma.

So, how do we break the great divide? Haidt:

One is demonization. When you have people saying, you can disagree as much as you want, but when you start saying, “They’re only saying that because they’re, you know, they’re a racist or they’re in bed with this company,” or, and even though sometimes that might be true. But we are so prone to dismiss other people and demonize their motives that we’re usually going to be wrong about that. So if we could begin to see this in each other and even challenge each other and say, “Hey, you’re demonizing.” Like, just, you know, disagree with them but stop attributing bad motives to the other side. So if ten years from now people sort of recognize that and could call each other out on in, that would at least be some progress.

The other one is corruption. Until we develop a massive groundswell of public revulsion at the fact that our Congress is bought and paid for, not entirely of course. Many of them are decent people. I don’t want to demonize. I’m sorry. But the nature of the institution is such that they’ve got to raise tons of money. And then they’re responsive to those interests. So perhaps there’s some norms that we could develop that will put some pressure on Congress to clean up its act.

Haidt avers that the Democrats, the liberals, don’t understand what the Republicans do: that winning the hearts and minds of the voter requires a compelling narrative, one that embraces moral tenets that have broad appeal. And he does mean moral. Politics is religion, he adds. The principles espoused or should be espoused are entwined with what Haidt calls ‘sacralization.’ Haidt:

Politics is about sacredness. Politics is about offering a vision that will bind the nation together to pursue greatness. And Republicans since Ronald Reagan have been really good at that.

Moyers mentions a passage from Haidt’s book that took him aback. It had to do with reason. Here’s Haidt:

But they [liberals and Democrats] sacralize reason itself, as though reason is this noble attribute, reason is our highest nature. And if we could just reason, we will solve our problems. All right, that sounds good on paper. But given all the stuff I just told you about what psychologists have discovered about reason, reasoning is not good at finding the truth.

I, too, did a mental double-take on this. But Haidt might be on to something. We liberals contend that we’ve got reason and the facts on our side. Yet, we’re not winning the electoral arguments. Something’s missing, and it may very well be that strong moral—politics-as-religion—narrative.

Now, having watched the program and pondered its contents, I have no idea what such a liberal narrative would look like. Haidt says that we can’t talk about fairness, as if we’d got that market cornered. Republicans also have a sense of fairness, but it’s expressed in Aesop’s fable about the ant and the grasshopper, suggests Haidt. The ant is industrious, disciplined, and persistent, looking ahead and preparing for the future. The grasshopper sits idly by, mocking the busy ant. When winter comes, the ant is ready for it; not so the grasshopper. Aesop’s lesson: be the ant and take no pity on the grasshopper. That’s only fair.

I should think that appeals to compassion could work, and they’ve worked in the past. Identifying groups as victims resonates (e.g., women’s rights, workers’  rights, civil rights for minorities, gay rights). Are the 99-percenters being victimized by the One-percenters? That’s certainly the message of OWS; yet I doubt that it gains much traction on the right.

The right believes strongly that fertilized human eggs are victims of abortion. Destroying the egg is tantamount to murder. The abortionist, then, is evil and must be stopped by any means necessary.

The left responds with sympathy for women with unwanted pregnancies. Thus, whether or not to abort is the woman’s right to choose. But what about the right to life of the fetus?

Haidt concurs that the Haves have too much and the Have-nots too little. The rich should pay higher taxes, notwithstanding karma. What is a compelling argument for the government as Robin Hood, in which the winners compensate the losers?

Wall Street must be reined in. What’s the argument? I think we’re back to just desserts: the rich should be revered and envied for their success not emasculated. But it’s not fair, you say. That doesn’t resonate, remember?

Lakoff, the Berkeley scientist, concluded that the liberal worldview was preferable to the conservative, since it delivered better outcomes. For instance, the rich wouldn’t be quite as rich and the poor quite as poor. Also, a stronger sense of society would emerge under more compassionate political leadership. Unlike Haidt, Lakoff urged liberals to take up the fight, as if it were a holy one, to defeat, if not figuratively neuter the modern, take-no-prisoners conservative.

Big question: Do we have the votes?

More on the conservative [u]

February 3, 2012 by davald

“We are all liberals,” was the title of a previous post. Professor of philosophy, Gary Gutting, was basically echoing the opinions of Mark Lilla’s New York Review of Books essay on Corey Robin’s newest book, The Reactionary Mind: Conservatism From Edmund Burke to Sarah Palin. Robin is a professor in his own right, teaching political science at City University of New York. Writing for the journal The Chronicle of Higher Education, Robin reprises his view that conservatives are reactionaries. He writes:

Historically, the conservative has sought to forestall the march of democracy in both the public and the private spheres, on the assumption that advances in the one necessarily spur advances in the other. Still, the more profound and prophetic stance on the right has been to cede the field of the public, if he must, but stand fast in the private. Allow men and women to become democratic citizens of the state; make sure they remain feudal subjects in the family, the factory, and the field.

No simple defense of one’s own place and privileges, the conservative position stems from a genuine conviction that a world thus emancipated will be ugly, brutish, and dull. It will lack the excellence of a world where the better man commands the worse. This vision of the connection between excellence and rule is what brings together in postwar America that unlikely alliance of the capitalist, with his vision of the employer’s untrammeled power in the workplace; the traditionalist, with his vision of the father’s rule at home; and the statist, with his vision of a heroic leader pressing his hand upon the face of the earth. Each in his way subscribes to this statement, from the 19th century, of the conservative creed: “To obey a real superior … is one of the most important of all virtues—a virtue absolutely essential to the attainment of anything great and lasting.”

Anyone who has watched Upstairs, Downstairs or Downton Abbey witnessed what Edmund Burke called ‘the natural order of things.’ Superiors commanded their inferiors, as if night follows day.

History, Robin avers, is one long story of ordinary citizens struggling to sever the ties of subordination. If “all men [and women] are created equal,” then why am I owned and oppressed by my master? But history also records the conservative push-back against the people. The conflict between the two forces has erupted in violence, bloodshed, and, often, victory for those who dared to upset the status quo. Robin:

For many, the word “reaction” connotes an unthinking, lowly grab for power. But reaction is not reflex. It begins from a position of principle—that some are fit, and thus ought, to rule others—and then recalibrates that principle in light of a challenge from below. This recalibration is no easy task, for such challenges tend by their very nature to disprove the principle. After all, if a ruling class is truly fit to rule, why and how has it allowed a challenge to its power to emerge? What does the emergence of the one say about the fitness of the other?

Toward the end of his essay, Robin disabuses us of the notion that conservatism implies quiet passivity, a mere clinging to conventions favoring society’s superiors. Quoting numerous conservative writers, including William F. Buckley, Robin distinguishes between the philosophy of conservatism, which, he believes, is very much about retaining the “natural order,” and the means thought necessary to preserve privilege. It was Buckley who wrote that conservatives are “the new radicals.” More recently, Dinesh D’Souza wrote, as quoted by Robin:

Typically, the conservative attempts to conserve, to hold on to the values of the existing society. But … what if the existing society is inherently hostile to conservative beliefs? It is foolish for a conservative to attempt to conserve that culture. Rather, he must seek to undermine it, to thwart it, to destroy it at the root level. This means that the conservative must … be philosophically conservative but temperamentally radical.

The Supreme Court has virtually guaranteed that conservatives will give full effect to their radical temperament, contributing hundreds of millions of dollars to “Super-PACs” in furtherance of their political views this election cycle. The top three SuperPacs in terms of money raised are all conservative. Their names reflect their viewpoints: “Restore Our Future,” “American Crossroads,” and “Make Us Great Again.” The OpenSecrets.org cite:

As of February 03, 2012, 310 groups organized as Super PACs have reported total receipts of $98,650,681 and total independent expenditures of $46,107,191 in the 2012 cycle.

Come November the numbers will boggle the mind. The One-percenters are beginning one long orgy, and the Rest of Us are not invited. Not that I’d care to attend.

We are all liberals

February 2, 2012 by davald

That’s the assertion of Mark Lilla, as quoted by Gary Gutting, who writes regularly for the New York Times‘ “The Stone.” As a philosopher—he teaches at Notre Dame—Gutting is not being glib in agreeing with Lilla; he’s using the term ‘liberal’ in its more literal sense, which he defines:

…But liberals like John Stuart Mill “assume that societies are genuinely constructs of human freedom, that whatever we inherit from them, they can always be unmade or remade through free human action.”

Given this understanding, however, Lilla concludes that, in current America politics, everyone is a liberal: “We take it for granted that we are born free, that we constitute society, it doesn’t constitute us and that together we legitimately govern ourselves.”  This seems right…

Gutting then wonders why all the fuss between “liberals” and “conservatives”? He takes us back to the French Revolution and its promise to distribute power to the people and break the chains of subordination to inherited wealth and monarchical control. Gutting suggests that we, both conservatives and liberals, are still fighting this promise, against and for.

Lilla puts it this way:

The quarrel between liberals and conservatives is essentially a quarrel over the nature of human beings and their relation to society…

Americans’ assumptions about human nature are basically liberal today.

While Gutting falls in with the liberals, he castigates both sides of the promise for hyping their divisions. Liberals tend to think conservatives are trying to “repudiate democracy”; conservatives believe that liberals are well nigh communists and socialists.

He advises us to appreciate our democratic institutions and work together to improve them.

In any case we need to stop fighting the French Revolution and focus on the hard work of governing ourselves with the democratic system we have so successfully built.

Maybe. This campaign season will surely test the admonition.
