Disparate clout

April 3, 2013 by davald

A couple of items in this morning’s newspapers betray America’s misplaced priorities and, I would suggest, convey the increasingly unmistakable message that we’ve succumbed to the loonies. The first is the NRA’s latest whacky initiative, The National School Shield, wherein the gun lobby reveals its answer to proliferating gun violence: more guns, of course. The second is a local matter, with several teachers targeting the home of state senator Steve Hobbs, a nominal Democrat who seemingly relishes the prospect of pissing off educators.

In what kind of universe would a gun lobby wield far more political clout and come closer to realizing its political agenda than associations of teachers? Well, that would be the United States.

Despite the senseless slaughters committed by deranged individuals with legally acquired arsenals of deadly assault weapons, the chances of Congress passing any restrictions on guns diminishes with each NRA lobbying push. Forget about the dead children of Newtown. It’s all about the damn Second Amendment. Oh, and I should mention the money. The NRA long ago ceased being a club of hunters. It’s the frontman for the weapons manufacturers, whose profits soar after each rampaging episode.

So, the gun nuts will get their way, sure enough. What about education and teachers? As I said, this is America.

In America, the politicians believe that teachers are second-class citizens who consistently fail in the classroom. The solution is two-fold: raise the bar and hold educators “accountable.” The connecting glue is standardized testing. Subject school children to yearly performance measurements, then base teacher evaluations on the results.

One need not be a genius to appreciate the consequences of this strategy. Teachers and their principals may be pressured to cheat, and that’s what has happened in several school districts, the ones we know about, with Atlanta being the latest. (We may recall G.W. Bush’s education secretary, Rod Paige, who gained fame for the near-miraculous test-score improvements in Houston. Not.)

In the main, however, educators will focus on the test, jettisoning most of what makes teaching rewarding, from science and literature to arts and music and the interactions between students and teachers that make learning special. For children in Washington state, the Measurement of Student Progress, or MSP, tests for reading and math, mostly. In the weeks leading up to the rigorous exams, teachers try to squeeze as much detail into their students’ heads as possible, hoping, I guess, that some of it will stick by test time.

Imagine that you’re a fourth-grade teacher. Next year your evaluation and, presumably, your pay will be based in part on how well your kids did on the standardized test. The results, we’ll imagine further, aren’t pretty. Few passed. So you get a lousy report card. In your defense you say that you can’t rely on a single year’s worth of scores. Besides, your students’ gaps were too large. Shouldn’t the third and second and first grade teachers bear some of the blame?

Sorry. It doesn’t work that way. Whether or not you are judged mediocre of superb depends only on last year’s results. No excuses.

Teachers and principals know that this system sucks. Yet, they have no clout when it comes to benighted politicians, who always know better than educators, even though they wouldn’t survive ten minutes in a public school classroom. The legislatures continue to pile on the requirements while denying basic funding to the schools. The inevitable decline in public education falls exclusively on the educators; the elected officials, with fingers pointing, escape to do the bidding of the gun lobby and otherwise fail completely at their jobs.

California Browns

March 29, 2013 by davald

California is experiencing a renaissance of sorts. Ironically it’s happening under a Brown, the son of Pat, who, I would argue, ranks among the top governors of the Golden State. The father helped establish the world’s greatest public education system, employed tens of thousands on massive public works projects, and generally presided over the state’s best years, a period marked by rapid economic growth that enriched millions of lives. I know this because I was there, having attended the schools through Berkeley and worked on the California Aqueduct Project that brought water from the Sierra Nevada to the agricultural fields of the Central Valley and millions of Los Angeles-area residents.

It’s 1992. The scene: Madison Square Garden. The event: the Democratic National Convention. On stage was my 16-year-old daughter seconding the nomination of Jerry Brown for president. Our family sat in the seats beaming, of course. During his acceptance speech, Brown grew suddenly misty, trying to control his emotions as he spoke fondly of his dad, Pat, who lay at home ill. Jerry had big ideas, too, including a Youth Conservation Corps that never got off the ground, though my daughter publicly committed to be part of it should it eventuate.

After two terms as governor himself, Jerry Brown then wandered into a political wilderness, returning as mayor of Oakland then as the state’s attorney general. He’s now in his third term as governor giving effect to more big ideas.

The Pacific Northwest’s Timothy Egan, writing for the New York Times:

The Great Recession cost the state 1.3 million jobs — a huge blow. And when Gov. Jerry Brown was sworn in for a long-interrupted third term in 2011, he inherited a budget hole of $25 billion. He cut spending, while convincing people to raise taxes. “Fiscal discipline is not the enemy of our good intentions,” he said, “but the basis for realizing them.”

And the same time, voters elected a Democratic super-majority in the Assembly, assuring that the Party of No would be irrelevant. Brown can crow — for the moment. “Against those who take pleasure singing our demise, California did the impossible.”

Brown is a seasoned visionary, becoming less prickly with age. In a memorable state of the state speech earlier this year, he tied the discovery of gold and the founding of Google to California’s “special destiny.”

The centerpiece of his vision is high-speed rail (also approved by the voters) and re-engineering the water system of the Central Valley to ensure that nature, agriculture and residential growth all have a future, no matter what climate change brings.

I wish the governor well, as well as his father. The state desperately needs Browns at the helm.

Educational apartheid

March 28, 2013 by davald

My wife has taught public school children for over a generation. In that period she has witnessed a steady and dramatic decline in educational quality, the result of unending cutbacks in funding and a backwards slide in the socioeconomic levels of her students. More recently she has expressed a strong sense of dread that public schools will eventually house only the least-advantaged children; those with means will have gone elsewhere.

We know that conservatives, especially, have long championed school vouchers, which, they believe, will accomplish two objectives: increase competition among schools and districts, thereby letting “the market” boost educational performance; and give parents and guardians the “freedom” to choose on behalf of their children. We also know, that despite Democrats receiving a plurality of the votes cast nationwide, the political landscape at the state level is much different, with Republicans controlling many legislatures and governors’ mansions. It is in the states that the conservative ideology manifests itself, from draconian restrictions on abortion to school vouchers.

The New York Times reports on the latter phenomenon.

Proponents say tax-credit and voucher programs offer families a way to escape failing public schools. But critics warn that by drawing money away from public schools, such programs weaken a system left vulnerable after years of crippling state budget cuts — while showing little evidence that students actually benefit.

If we’ve learned anything over these last few years it is that ideology trumps facts. Paradoxically, at least from my perspective, the more inconvenient the fact, the more tightly held the shibboleths. I would add that the conservative viewpoint admits little sympathy for those denied economic well-being. Indeed, it’s their own damn fault.

“This movement is doing more than threaten the core of our traditional public school system,” said Timothy Ogle, executive director of the Arizona School Boards Association. “It’s pushing a national policy agenda embraced by conservatives across states that are receptive to conservative ideas.”

The trend is clear should conservatives hold sway. Shrink state budgets because governments “are the problem” and must be starved. One intended consequence is less money for public education. As teachers and principals struggle mightily to meet conservative-based “standards” with fewer and fewer dollars, the schools spiral downward. Along the way conservatives point out the obvious: schools are failing. Well, duh.

But the conservative solution is not to restore adequate funding. Rather, the goal is to create educational apartheid, with only the poor and troubled students remaining in the money-starved public system.

The rich have always sent their children to private institutions. Those occupying socioeconomic rungs just below will feel increasing pressure to similarly situate their children. Voilà: a growing constituency for vouchers and a furtherance of the conservative agenda.

Quid pro quo

March 20, 2013 by davald

We are in the midst of commentary about the Iraq War’s beginning ten years on. The numbers numb. Over 4,000 U.S. military lost their lives and over seven times as many permanently injured. Now it seems that consensus has developed on how many Iraqi civilians died—over 100,000, a figure that was widely pooh-poohed when first suggested. And the cost: a trillion dollars and counting.

Those responsible for the worst foreign policy disaster in history offer no regrets. Indeed, they either justify the decision, against all evidence to the contrary, or treat it as no big deal—I’m thinking G.W., of course, who acts as if he were never really present at anything throughout his life. Nor have the media, who played a major role in touting the war’s necessity, claimed responsibility. Charles Pierce takes on the New York Times here.

For the moment I want to put the war’s cost into some context. Few raised red flags about the invasion and occupation and how the billions would mount. It’s war, for heaven’s sake, and damn the torpedoes, or whatever. Freedom, after all, is expensive and always worth the price. But look at what a fine financial mess we’re in a decade removed from the first bombs dropped on Baghdad.

If the Republicans had their way and to a lesser extent the hardly liberal Obama, Congress would be systematically screwing the Rest of Us. Oh, wait, they’re already doing that. Well the screwing is evidently not deep enough. Let’s cut every public service, from education to health care to transportation. We just can’t afford to maintain “entitlements” or “promote the general welfare.”

Guns, and killing, and maiming, and torture, and ships, and planes, and tanks, and all manner of devices that go boom day and night deserve  everything we’ve got. And by all means keep coddling the rich.

Trusting yourself spells ruin

March 5, 2013 by davald

I recently skimmed a review by Cass Sunstein of a new book Against Autonomy: Justifying Coercive Paternalism. Although Mr. Sunstein expressed considerable agreement with the author on key points, including the use of cost-benefit analysis, he ultimately criticized her overreach. But I was struck by the underlying assertion of Sarah Conly, the author: We individuals are often not the best judges of our own interests. Here’s Sunstein, who first references John Stuart Mill’s “harm principle“:

In recent decades, some of the most important research in social science, coming from psychologists and behavioral economists, has been trying to answer it. That research is having a significant influence on public officials throughout the world. Many believe that behavioral findings are cutting away at some of the foundations of Mill’s harm principle, because they show that people make a lot of mistakes, and that those mistakes can prove extremely damaging.

Sunstein offers one example, “present bias.” We tend to ignore the future in favor of the present. Thus, we put off saving money for a rainy day or fail to save at all.

For some people, the future is a foreign country, populated by strangers. Many of us procrastinate and fail to take steps that would impose small short-term costs but produce large long-term gains. People may, for example, delay enrolling in a retirement plan, starting to diet or exercise, ceasing to smoke, going to the doctor, or using some valuable, cost-saving technology. Present bias can ensure serious long-term harm, including not merely economic losses but illness and premature death as well.

This morning the Seattle Times revealed the results of a recent poll. It seems that a majority of Washington voters oppose an increase in both gas and car-tab taxes. I’m shocked, I tell you. Shocked.

The people have never supported higher taxes. Indeed, members of one political party count on that attitude at every election. And it’s a winning formula: just oppose taxes, period, and you’ll garner a huge chunk of the vote.

But are lower taxes in the best interests of society? How do we fix our roads, build commuter rail, or fund even an adequate public school system, let alone a very good one? Do you think taxes would help?

Let’s consider Puget Sound’s transportation mess. Geography prevents road networks that could ease congestion. So hundreds of thousands of motorists join the vehicular goo each day on the interstates to make their way to and from Seattle and other locales. This is collective stupidity, of course, since it is the most inefficient means—driving one’s car—to get to where you need to be, namely work. Yet, each morning and evening brings the same miserable experience—and at considerable personal cost.

It would be far better for nearly everyone if all those people could walk a block or two then climb aboard a train. However, building public transportation requires the yes-vote of millions of citizens to make it happen. Yet, when given that opportunity, we refuse to pool our dollars for the good of all. We’d rather pay a small fortune to own and maintain and fuel our very own car! When hundreds of thousands manifest the same preference we get sprawl and goo.

A relevant bit of paternal coercion would be a carbon tax or a congestion fee. The latter almost immediately reduces congestion, as if by Adam Smith’s “invisible hand.” A carbon tax would eventually curtail private transportation and make it feasible for more of us to jump on trains rather than into our cars. The revenues from either tax could be used to fund public rails.

But we don’t like taxes, remember? So each time we reject taxes at the polls or constrain our legislators’ ability to raise them, we allow the eventual costs that must be borne to escalate.

We could have acquired easements to construct commuter rail lines around Puget Sound. But we kicked the can down the road, again and again. Now that every inch is paved over or built upon, real estate acquisitions become increasingly expensive, making us even less willing to pay the necessary taxes.

Each of us believes that we are meeting our own best interests by keeping more of our income in our pockets. We also insist that we are the best judges of how to spend it—not the government.

However, just look around you. Do you see the triumph of individual choice? Do you love your gooey commute, your grossly underfunded schools, and the consequences of planetary warming? If so, you’re not alone.

Hard to believe that we humans are evolution’s ultimate prize.

Have we finally killed a zombie?

February 28, 2013 by davald

Washington state’s flim-flam artist, Tim Eyman, who makes his living pushing initiatives, received a message today from the supreme court in Olympia. The two-thirds requirement for new taxes, which voters have routinely passed, cannot bind the legislature. If the people want a super-majority, they’ll have to get it via a constitutional amendment.

The same supreme court held that the legislature is grossly underfunding education. Fix the problem or else. But Eyman’s initiative hamstrung Olympia; it could not establish new taxes by a simple majority vote. And it’s highly doubtful that the state could fulfill its constitutional mandate without new revenues. Today’s ruling removes the impediment.

But not the politics, unfortunately. A small group of rogue Democrats in the senate switched sides, putting the Republicans in control of that body; the house remains solidly Democratic.

Moreover, I expect Eyman to launch yet another campaign, this time to amend the constitution. After all, there’s money in it—for him.

What’s not to like?

February 26, 2013 by davald

I often forget that there are some progressives in Congress, and they’re not just sitting on their hands. They have plans and initiatives, though it remains to be seen if they have successful strategies to implement their goals and objectives. One plan in particular is “The People’s Budget.” You can view it here.

If you hate federal deficits and debts, you’ll love this: the budget proposes to not only eliminate the debt but to create a surplus over the next 10 years.

If you believe that education is important, then you’ll love this: the People’s Budget proposes to increase funding at all levels.

If you think we’ve wasted a few trillion dollars on senseless wars, then you’ll love this: the budget proposes to bring the troops home and cut billions from Defense.

If you thought Obamacare was incomplete, then you’ll love this: the People’s Budget proposes to establish a public option; also, it would prevent reductions in Medicare payments to physicians over the next decade.

If you’ve always believed that full funding for Social Security and Medicare can be easily accomplished, then you’ll love this: the budget proposes to subject all wages to the payroll tax.

If you’ve wanted a new New Deal, then you’ll love this: the People’s Budget would invest billions in public infrastructure, clean energy, and the information superhighway.

In summary, the People’s Budget includes the following:

  • Deficit reduction of $5.6 trillion
  • Spending cuts of $1.7 trillion
  • Revenue increase of $3.9 trillion
  • Public investment $1.7 trillion

So, what whackos have endorsed or spoken favorably of the People’s Budget?

  • Bill Clinton
  • Paul Krugman
  • Dean Baker
  • The Economist (magazine)
  • Rolling Stone (magazine)
  • Robert Reich
  • The Washington Post
  • The Guardian

 

You can add my name to the list. How about you?

Legislative herding

February 4, 2013 by davald

Legislators sponsor bills that prescribe certain behaviors. For example, when dealing with the environment or global warming, representatives propose legislation designed to induce the acquisition of renewable and efficiency resources over the bad stuff—anything with carbon or soot or toxins. I will assume that such bills result from the best of intentions. However, there is a simpler, more effective approach.

Let’s focus on climate change. If one is serious about preventing or mitigating global warming, then, by all means, dramatically reduce, if not eliminate, the combustion of carbon. A little bit of atmospheric carbon dioxide is a good thing; too much will certainly destroy the planet, one species at a time.

Economists have known for some time now, probably for centuries and before ‘economist’ joined the dictionary, that raising the price of a good or service will reduce demand for the item. If we want less carbon, we need to make it more expensive.

There are many ways to do this. But surely the simplest and most effective is by taxing it. 

But legislators in Olympia and D.C. can hardly even say the word ‘tax,’ let alone establish one. Instead, they attempt to herd us all into metaphorical pastures.

The Snohomish County PUD, of which I am a board member, tracks legislation introduced in both Washington state and Washington, D.C.. Typically, we commissioners receive updates, which are ranked by topic and priority. The latest Olympia update was sent at the end of last week. I’ll mention a few legislative titles:

  • Modifying the renewable energy and cost recovery program
  • Regarding net metering of electricity
  • Regarding the use of an energy storage facility to meet annual targets under RCW…
  • Requiring integrated resource plans developed by electric utilities include an assessment of energy storage systems

Implicit in these bills is an underlying concern for climate change. But they are all prescriptive or proscriptive, another way of “herding” utilities, in this case. So, we at the PUD pay attention on behalf of our customers. If we believe that a bill’s passage will harm ratepayers, we speak against and otherwise try to modify or even “kill” it. On the other hand, some legislation we deem beneficial, in which case we actively support. In most instances the PUD “monitors” or perhaps expresses “concern.” Since legislators are in session for months at a time, the process is long and occasionally tedious. At the end, dozens, if not hundreds, of bills “die,” with only a handful landing on the governor’s desk for signature.

Suppose the legislators imposed a carbon tax. Oregon has one. California established a cap-and-trade regime (a form of taxation). Ireland recently instituted a stiff carbon tax. Wherever taxes or caps have been imposed, consumption of carbon has fallen. What would happen if Olympia did?

Rather quickly, if international examples attest, those subject to a tax would take steps to avoid them. Electric utilities would pursue non-carbon strategies. These would include more conservation and renewable resources. If the tax were high enough, more utilities would have an incentive to pursue such initiatives as energy storage, which has proven costly, given its infancy. (The noble Snohomish County PUD does all this good stuff in the absence of a tax. We board members recognize that carbon will become more expensive; it’s just a matter of time. As always, the PUD is ahead of the curve.)

However, utilities do not hold a monopoly on innovation. A carbon tax would encourage utilities to serve as a conduit between third parties (the entrepreneurs and innovators) and their customers, who, after all, would ultimately pay the tax in the form of higher electricity rates. The higher the tax, the wider the conduit and the greater the opportunity.

Another thing would happen. Legislators could busy themselves with other pressing matters, like education and transportation, confident that the tax would do the herding, like Adam Smith’s “invisible hand.” 

If any legislators are reading this, why not start the conversation among your colleagues? Hey. We’re only trying to save the earth.

Brown’s rebound

January 29, 2013 by davald

Jerry Brown would like to restore his state’s universities to their relative halcyon days of the 60s and 70s, or essentially those decades between the start of his father’s bold education initiatives and Ronald Reagan’s political career. Having benefitted from California’s post-War public schools, from elementary through college, I can both appreciate their former quality and wish the younger Brown good luck.

During my stint at Berkeley (1965 to 1969) I recall seeing a New York Times piece posted on my fraternity’s second-floor landing. (Or, maybe it was in the lobby, but, hey, that was a long time ago, so forgive this old coot.) The article included a table that ranked the nation’s universities according to several criteria that I don’t remember. At the top of the list, above all the Ivy League schools and Stanford, was UC Berkeley. That’s right, according to my recollection, Cal was Number One, no thanks to me, of course.

What may shock younger readers is that there was no tuition back then, only “incidental fees.” When I started in the fall of 1965, the school was on the semester system and those fees were $120 for the whole year! As a sophomore, the university changed to quarters, but kept the fee at $60/each, for a total of $180/year. It remained at that level until I graduated in June 1969, if not longer.

Then came Ronald Reagan and Howard Jarvis. While they may not have conspired, their combined effects on the state proved disastrous, including the unraveling of the once-vaunted university system. Proposition 13, spurred by Jarvis, essentially froze property tax revenues, the major source of public-school funding. Reagan ran for governor as the white crusader determined to rid Berkeley of its hippies and malcontents. As we know, he was not a coddler. So he instituted tuition at the universities.

Over time fees-plus-tuition would increase to their current annual level of $12,834, still a bargain but hardly free. Between 1965 and 2012 inflation rose 628 percent. If those initial fees that I paid back in 1965 were simply adjusted to the C.P.I. they would be about $754 today. Those entering Cal last fall paid 17 times more for their education than I did, not counting books, housing, food, and transportation.

I am not suggesting that Berkeley was great because it was so inexpensive. Nor am I saying that it was absolutely free; universities cost money, and somebody has to pay for it, whether taxpayers, students/parents themselves, or a combination of the two. But then-governor Pat Brown saw value in building a world-class education system from kindergarten to graduate school. Here’s the Times:

During a 1960s renaissance, California’s public university system came to be seen as a model for the rest of the country and an economic engine for the state. Seven new campuses opened, statewide enrollment doubled, and state spending on higher education more than doubled. The man widely credited with the ascendance was Gov. Edmund G. Brown, known as Pat.

I applaud the current governor for trying to revive his father’s legacy. But even his proposals to invest more dollars in the education system will fall far short of what’s needed. Years of neglect, if not venom, have taken a huge toll on nearly everything public in California, especially its schools, which now achieve a ‘C’ from Education Week. 

Not that we’d ever get a vote on the matter, but would you rather we invested more on education than on bombs? You can guess my answer.
