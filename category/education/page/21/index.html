Education in Washington state headed for the toilet

November 20, 2011 by davald

Among the further cuts being discussed or formally proposed is the end of the state’s levy equalization program. If terminated by the legislature the division between the educational Haves and Havenots will widen.

In Marysville, where my wife teaches, the proposed cut would slash over $4 million from the district’s budget. It was forced to cut teachers’ salaries this year by about two percent, because of legislative reductions. With a total budget of just over $100 million, the district will have to slice another three to four percent with levy equalization gone.

Teaching, while always challenging even under the best conditions, has become a nearly unbearable grind. Class sizes have grown and will continue to do so in the near term. Support services have been dramatically reduced; paraprofessionals have become an endangered species in many of the state’s school districts. Outmoded and nonworking technology retards students’ entry into the “21st century” (how quaint). Textbooks are relics from the past, if they exist at all.

So you’re a parent concerned about your child’s education, and good for you. Step inside a public school classroom, particularly those in low-income areas, those that are most hurt by Olympia. For the most part good teachers struggle successfully to rein in distracting behaviors. But they’re fighting uphill all the time. A particular combination of unruly students can send the class into chaos; one is pressed to discern learning there, and not because the teacher isn’t trying.

This damned Race to the Top, as Diane Ravitch told a Seattle crowd recently, pulls the rug out from teachers, blaming them for all of education’s presumed failures. They don’t get no respect, as Mr. Dangerfield would have said. Ironically, the federal NCLB act increasingly diminishes the authority and autonomy of teachers. Race to the Top is being disastrously implemented in the opposite direction: from the top down.

And one other peeve, while I’m at it. I have utter disgust for the majority of voters who ate a bunch of bullshit before refusing to impose an income tax on our state’s One Percenters. Don’t you know we have a revenue problem here? Well the Rest of Us don’t have any money. Yet we collectively blew a splendid opportunity to collect from the few who do. Did you forget that we Washingtonians suffer from the most regressive tax structure in the country?

For shame, I say.

Where are all the brains going? Why Wall Street, my dear.

November 16, 2011 by davald

Like Willie Sutton on banks, our nation’s elite graduates are going where the money is—to Wall Street.

But what if top students didn’t go to Wall Street? What if, rather than creating complex financial products that collapsed the global economy, they were building bridges and creating new technologies instead?

As America struggles to create jobs and get back on its feet after the recession — caused largely by the financial industry’s recklessness — the country is in desperate need of more entrepreneurs, inventors, scientists and other professionals, a complaint regularly made by non-Wall Street business leaders and members of both major political parties.

The Rest of Us would prefer that our brighter bulbs devote their intellectual talent to, say, curing cancer or even death. We could benefit from better technologies, especially of the green variety, or easy conversion of water to wine. But the cream of the crop have chosen in huge numbers to make fortunes designing exotic financial instruments and otherwise getting filthy rich.

And it is filthy, since little, if anything, Wall Street does helps the Rest of Us. Indeed, it is a den of very clever thieves inured to others’ pain and suffering. They caused economic collapse, putting millions out on to the streets. Yet, they’re back again siphoning off boatloads of dollars while squeezing out the Rest of Us.

Imagine that teaching offered the same incentives as Wall Street? The best and the brightest would be in our nation’s classrooms churning out highly accomplished graduates.

But wait a minute. We don’t like our teachers. They already earn too much and do too little. Yep. That’s the ticket. Much better to let the fat cats suck all the money out of the economy, leaving crumbs for the Rest of Us.

Speaking of nominal Democrats…

November 7, 2011 by davald

Washington’s outgoing governor, Christine Gregoire, faced with more dismal revenue numbers, is determined to cut even more from public education budgets. The Herald article this morning focused on the proposal to terminate the levy equalization program, wherein those districts lacking significant commercial and industrial operations—and therefore property-tax revenues—receive some compensation from the state. (Mercer Island is different from all of us in nearly every respect. Its citizens have always supported public education. They can afford to do so.)

Taking the biggest hit in the county would be Marysville, where my wife has taught for 25 years. If the equalization program does indeed end, the state would cut the school district’s subsidy by over $4 million. Its total budget is $110 million.

This year, after the legislature cut funding for teacher salaries by around two percent, the Marysville school board asked teachers and support personnel to take seven half-days off—without pay. The union accepted, perhaps with few options available.

This is madness. For all the ranting about the importance of education and the perennial “crisis” in the classroom, slashing school budgets is hardly the solution. It is counter-productive. Big time.

Yes, I understand that the state’s revenue forecast has been lowered again and that the governor must submit a balanced budget, but Olympia betrays the constitutional mandate to prioritize public education above all else. Shrinking school budgets is also a recipe for long-term disaster.

While I’m at it, we should understand that the Great Recession, occasioned by wacky Wall Streeters, has disproportionately impacted public services in general and the at-risk in particular. Meanwhile, CEO pay has resumed its meteoric rise. As I write the majority of the unemployed have exhausted their benefits. They’re getting nada. Zilch.

We really should be mad as hell. Don’t you think?

By the way, most of us clearly weren’t thinking when we had the opportunity to impose a very modest tax on wealthy citizens’ incomes. Stupid, we are.

On paying teachers for more credentials

November 5, 2011 by davald

I’m aware of no evidence that attaining more degrees boosts teachers’ abilities and effectiveness. Requiring a master’s degree as a condition for either landing an education job or earning a higher salary only ensures that tertiary schools make more money and, more often than not, burdens teachers with much greater debt.

Thus, I very much agree with Yglesias’s recent post. He writes:

The whole system of paying teachers bonuses to acquire largely meaningless extra credentials is a totally wacky back door subsidy to the nation’s master’s degree programs that mostly serves to waste teachers’ time. Even without getting into any more controversial ideas about teacher compensation, it would make much more sense to scrap these bonuses and reallocate the money to higher base pay across the board.

Amen.

Bust the teachers’ union, saith Steve Jobs

November 3, 2011 by davald

We’re all entitled to our opinions. Those with money can actually give effect to their biases, good or ill. Bill Gates is spending billions of dollars to eradicate malaria. But he’s also out to reform public education in dubious ways.

Comes now Steve Jobs through biographer Walter Isaacson to throw his two cents at the problem of schools. Unlike Gates, however, Jobs didn’t start a foundation or lobby Congress. He just voiced an opinion. His wife, Laurene, deserves credit for establishing successful programs for disadvantaged youths.

Jobs believes that teachers’ unions stymie proper education. If he ran your local school district, he’d give authority and autonomy to principals, who, Jobs said, should be able to hire and fire whomever he or she wants.

I have mixed feelings about Jobs’s opinions. While I would like to see principals assume more responsibility for their respective schools, I also recognize that personalities cloud judgments. Teachers’ unions afford protection against whimsical punishment and discharge. Moreover, how can we know that the principal is appropriate to the tasks at hand?

Teaching is an extremely difficult job, especially in schools with troublesome demographics. My wife is a long-time public school teacher who is now in a high-poverty school. She is struggling mightily to achieve academic progress for her students, most of whom score far below grade level. She would love to have more and better resources to help her succeed. But none are forthcoming. Indeed, school budgets are being cut not enhanced. Do more with less is the implicit message.

There are both very good teachers and regrettably poor ones, and they’re easy to identify. Most, however, fit somewhere in the middle of a normal distribution, where the differences are quite small and variable. By mere chance a teacher may get a roomful of respectful, curious students, making teaching relatively easy if not also satisfying. On the other hand, a new school year may bring the proverbial class from hell. These students would rather be any place than school, and their behavior makes that loud and clear. It is left to miracle workers to achieve inroads here, and few, if any, exist.

That most teachers occupy the middle and are not easily distinguishable from the other, unions are important. In addition to advocating for the interests of their members, unions provide job security against capricious administrators who harbor disturbing biases.

The overwhelming majority of those who got into teaching in the first place received good educations, care deeply about the welfare of pupils, and are willing to work beyond the contract hours. As with my wife, most dip into their own small pockets to purchase supplies and books. They are, as a rule, dedicated public servants charged with the noblest of objectives: imparting knowledge in a variety of subjects to students with diverse interests, backgrounds, curiosity, and native intelligence.

Yet it is the teachers and their unions that incur the greatest wrath. It is misplaced, of course. I’d like to believe that criticisms would be tempered if there were a better appreciation of just how difficult teaching is. Few of us would last a single day in the classroom. I know, because I failed miserably at teaching.

Public education, both its structure and challenges, mirrors the greater society. That we happen to live in one of the most unequal societies in the developed world should give us pause before we start pointing fingers at teachers and their professional advocates.

If you want a deserving target, go to Wall Street. It’s a good start, anyway.

Education for what or whom?

October 28, 2011 by davald

We are told that those who graduate college earn more than those who don’t. Okay. But amidst the statistical averages lie sobering details. Thousands of college graduates, strapped with tens of thousands in student loans, can’t find work. Moreover, they’re moving back home, not because they want to but because they must.

I, too, had incurred debt while attending Berkeley. Yet, my amount was very small potatoes—in the low hundreds—compared with today’s students’. After all, Berkeley was still tuition-free when I attended, which gives you some sense of how old I am and how long ago it was that I was studying history and later philosophy.

My own children graduated from the University of Washington. We parents were able to scrape together most of their expenses. Our daughter, however, went on to obtain two Master’s degrees and a law degree from the same institution. Fortunately for her, while our resources were insufficient to pay for graduate school, she has been able to find respectable employment that pays her well and allows her to comfortably retire her debt. She’s also married to an enterprising attorney, who, we’re certain, will soon become a partner in his firm.

Our family’s experience is an exceptions. Far too many children have gone to college and will continue to do so with the expectation that their expensive sojourn in higher education will reap substantial reward. Sorry about that, kids. While a few of you will indeed become successful, both professionally and financially, most will not.

The Northwest’s own Timothy Egan now writes for the New York Times. In his column today he tells us of a parent’s lament.

Her sons jumped from commencement to the real world full of springy confidence. But now, two years after graduation, after hundreds of rejections, after their resumes bounced back like boomerangs to the head, they were living at home, and every day brought another dent to their self-confidence.

Egan regrets the tethering of self-worth to academic performance. He suggests adopting some of Steve Jobs’s wisdom.

What was missing in the life message of child-raising was some of the counter-cultural swagger in that 2005 commencement speech by Steve Jobs, the one that made the viral video rounds after his death. If you listen to the whole speech, it is what he says at the end that seems so apt for these years of diminished expectations. “Stay hungry,” Jobs said, borrowing an admonition from the creators of The Whole Earth Catalogue, an early bible for him, and equally important, “Stay foolish.”

Egan adds that the “hungry” part is easy. Being “foolish,” it seems, requires a different mindset than the prevailing Darwinian philosophy. (I would add that there is and will be only one Steve Jobs, who, ironically, eschewed the culture of consumerism to produce the most lusted after products on the planet. Walter Isaacson’s biography reveals much, and I will have more to say about it in one or more future posts.)

For what does it profit a man or woman to earn the Ivy League credential, itself a product of much discipline, money, and self-denial, only to have to move back with the parents? Unfortunately, in today’s depressing economy there appear to be only two paths: somehow become very wealthy; or struggle to put food on the table. The in-between option seems closed. That first path, by the way, begins very early in life; and it helps immeasurably to be born of rich parentage. Should you not jump on that upward vector, or dare fall off, you may have to find contentment flipping the proverbial hamburger.

Lamentation is thick and widespread. The starving artist is always a possibility.

Tacoma teachers

September 28, 2011 by davald

The teachers fought back, and they won. They refused to accept the demands of management for lower salaries, bigger class sizes, and reduced emphasis on seniority.

For a detailed report of who was involved and what was at stake, read this. You’ll notice several irritants: the Gates Foundation, Teach for America, and Strategies 360.

Strategies 360 was founded by Ron Dotzauer, the husband of a former PUD commissioner. He and his firm have been involved in a number of different issues, from land use matters to political campaigns to lobbying.

It’s added another bullet to its resume: busting unions. From the article:

Strategies 360 is a marketing firm used last year in Seattle by another Gates-funded group, the Alliance for Education, to conduct a push-poll advocating performance-based pay for teachers, the anti-union Teach for America program and an end to seniority protections for teachers. The firm then used the push poll to set up an Astroturf group in Seattle, called the Our Schools Coalition, to push corporate school reform.

Wishing for apples

August 30, 2011 by davald

Some time ago the Marysville School District jettisoned its Apple computers and switched to the dark side of cheap, largely unworkable, and frustratingly complicated Wintel machines. I predicted to a small body of citizens and staff that the district’s IT costs and personnel would mushroom immediately. As it happens, my prediction proved correct. Worse, now that the district is locked in to Redmond it is now decades behind in adopting newer, easier, and more robust technologies—the kind developed by Cupertino.

This past Sunday I accompanied my wife to her classroom in Marysville. Against the far wall were six Acer desktop machines all running Windows XP, a now-ancient operating system. Running from each computer were separate cables to connect the display, the mouse, and the keyboard. Another cord plugged into the electric socket. An Ethernet cable ran from the computer to an Ethernet hub, joined by the five other Ethernet cables. There was also a district-supplied laptop, designed to withstand an IED. It, too, had several cables connecting the laptop to a “doc-cam,” the Ethernet, and an electric socket. Did I say clutter?

Only two of the computers could connect to the Internet, including the laptop. I learned this after an eternity of waiting for the computers to come on. Did I say slow?

I didn’t bother to check the specs of the computers. Suffice it to say, they were vastly underpowered, no doubt possessing stingy quantities of RAM. With all computers on, the heat generated is almost unbearable. Did I say hot?

You may have read Froma Harrop’s recent column on Steve Jobs and Apple devices. She began by complaining about her previous PC experience—all bad; which drove her into the arms of Steve Jobs, figuratively speaking. She writes:

Apple has long worked under the solid assumption that consumers would pay more for sleek design, higher quality and superior service. Nothing it makes looks like it fell from a flying saucer.

Now let’s imagine my wife’s classroom equipped with all Apple products.

First off, there would be few cables. Instead, MacBook Airs and iPads would be connected wirelessly to the Internet, printers, district servers, and each other. You would find a large LCD television connected to an Apple TV. The iPads would be running iOS 5 to take full advantage of Apple’s Airplay feature.*

My wife would have an iPad, of course. Everything she did on her iPad, from slide shows, to math problems, to video would be instantly mirrored to the television via Apple TV.

And everything would “just work,” as Steve says.

Alas, my wife can only wish for such technology. School districts are in a world of financial hurt, with the state cutting education budgets each year. There is simply no money to make the switch. She and her colleagues are stuck with outmoded, misbehaving technology that is cumbersome, at a best, and nonfunctioning, for the most part.

By the way, I know that it would all “just work,” because I have all that Apple technology at home. It’s sweet.
