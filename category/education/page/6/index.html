Stupidity

April 16, 2014 by davald

I’ve checked a lot of categories for this post, which could suggest that I will have a lot to say about a wide variety of topics. Nope. I’ve done so because there is a common cause for our collective failure to remedy the significant and plentiful problems found within each category, from education and transportation to climate change, poverty, and urban renewal.

Notice that I said “collective.” Each of the myriad challenges requires societal solutions, the efforts and resources of we rather than individual I’s.

As I’ve written previously, I grew up in California when government, the necessary we, seemed to work. The education system was second to none. New roads proliferated to accommodate a growing population. Social services were sufficient to meet needs, or at least to a greater extent than at present.

That all began to change with the passage of Proposition 13 and Ronald Reagan’s displacement of Pat Brown. Government became the problem rather than the solution, and Reagan spread that message throughout the land as a two-term president. In England, Margaret Thatcher also took the message to heart when she declared that there is “no society,” only individuals.

So, from the 1980s to the present, support for government at all levels has eroded. To ensure that government can’t work to solve problems, taxes were cut, cut, and cut some more.

Meanwhile, of course, those problems grew in number and significance, so that now the costs to tackle just one seem overwhelming. As a result, when confronted with a ballot initiative to fund schools, parks, roads, and other public services we say no, deeming the request exorbitant. Besides, government is the problem, right?

Here’s one problem—transportation.

My wife and I travel to Seattle each Friday to spend time with our new grandson. We travel along I-5 from Everett, a distance of about 30 miles. If traveled at 3 a.m., the trip takes but a half hour. Yet, despite leaving after the “rush hour,” and despite using the HOV lane, we may spend upwards of an hour and a half to get there in the morning. The return trip takes that long more often than not, regardless of when we depart for home. The great majority of the vehicles have but one occupant, which is the problem, since there are so many of them traveling at the same time.

This seems stupid, that so many people buy so many cars to ride alone each morning and night. It would be far less expensive and a hell of a lot more convenient if all those people spent a fraction of the amount they devote to solitary transportation on a collective solution. And I’m talking about trains. Yet, when given a choice, a rare event to be sure, people eschew the social remedy in favor of buying that new car and joining the freeway goo. As I said, stupid. And my wife and I are caught in it each Friday. (Though the rewards of spending time with our grandson outweigh the inconvenience.)

But given our aversion to government and our unwillingness to convert personal expenditures into taxes, the stupidity steadily increases with absolutely no viable solution in sight. The fix available 30 years ago has long since disappeared under mountains of slow-moving cars.

As for climate change, ditto. We’ll take the oil and the coal any day over a collective solution.

Education. Ditto.

Urban renewal. Ditto.

Ending poverty and inequality. Ditto.

Forget about us.

Money and test scores

March 11, 2014 by davald

I took the SAT way back in 1965. Though classmates studied hard for the big test, I simply showed up. Few, if any of us, had parents who attended college, let alone graduated from one. So, we were in new territory with no parental advice borne of experience. I survived the ordeal and recorded a respectable result. Hardly high enough to be considered genius, but not so low as to disqualify my application to Berkeley. I knew that I was a mediocre student with grades to prove it, and I got about the same grades at Cal. That was a long time ago.

Over time the SAT has changed. It morphed into a three-part test, rather than the two-parter when I took it. I understand that the test is administered by computer; it was pencil and paper in my day. Last week the College Board announced that it would change the test again, dropping the obligatory essay portion and vocabulary nobody uses. The new-and-improved test is supposed to reveal what a student has learned over a dozen years in the classroom and not so much a child’s intellectual inheritance. And, of course, the SAT will align more closely with the Common Core (state) standards.

Regardless, the SAT has never been an accurate predictor of college performance. Two other factors reign supreme: high school grades and parents’ socioeconomic status; and the two closely correlate. Here’s a chart showing family income and average SAT scores for the intervals (data from College Board via the New York Times).

 

If you’ve been paying attention to the education wars of late, you know that testing has taken over. Kindergartners now must answer questions even before they step into their first classroom. They’ve only just begun. There will be tests every year. As seniors in high school, if not before, students may take the SAT but will likely have to take a final comprehensive test to determine whether or not they graduate.

There should be little wonder why so many children come to detest school. Their teachers, who may share students’ distaste for the enterprise, now have another reason to be wary of the system. They will be evaluated, to varying extents, on the basis of student scores on high-stakes tests.

It has been suggested that we should choose our parents well. If you’re an educator, you may want to consider the household incomes of students before deciding where to teach.

Endless struggle

February 20, 2014 by davald

I admit it. Sometimes I just don’t feel like tossing a few more words into the rhetorical jungle we call ‘the blogosphere.’ For me, it’s a matter of repetition: write something about the sorry state of the world only to witness further decay—repeat.

For example, It bothers me that some people have ridiculously high financial fortunes that are placed in service to simply awful initiatives that erode the wellbeing of the Rest of Us. You name it, from educational “reform” and government austerity to blaming the poor for their own poverty.

One piece of news on the education front is the lawsuit underway in Los Angeles: Vergara v. California. The lawsuit seeks to abolish teachers’ employment rights on the grounds that students’ educations are being thwarted or undermined by allegedly lousy teachers who are impossible to fire—or so the argument runs.

Writing for Hullabaloo, David Atkins comments.

The main backers of the case against teachers are the ultra-wealthy magnates Eli Broad, Charles Schwab and Fischer family (owners of the Gap, among other things.) Billionaires have been aggressively funding education “reform” efforts for years under the theory that there’s nothing wrong with education that destroying teachers’ unions and privatizing education can’t fix. It’s important to remember that these are the same people who spent millions in 2012 trying to defeat California’s Proposition 30 to fund schools, and to pass Proposition 32 preventing unions from spending on elections while allowing corporations free rein.

He continues:

And that’s really the whole point here: destroy teachers’ unions, eliminate the possibility of the government being able to attract decent teachers, then use the resulting chaos to privatize the entire education system. The billionaires want to do this not only because private education is a big, booming business, but also because they want to change the way children are taught to make them more docile, pliable units fit for the brave new corporate workforce of the 21st century. That’s what the anti-Proposition 30 efforts were all about, and that’s what the Vergara case is all about.

It’s certainly not designed to actually help children. We already know what produces good results in education, based on examples around the world: 1) incentivizing adults to pursue a career in teaching by paying a decent salary, 2) teaching real critical thinking skills instead of rote memorization; and 3) properly funding education.

But the billionaires aren’t interested in any of that. If they get their way, the country will be much the worse it.

See what I mean about crawling into a cave from time to time?

Gun culture

January 16, 2014 by davald

The New York Times reports on schools adopting a new culture in response to recent tragedies. There will be more.

For students across the country, lockdowns have become a fixture of the school day, the duck-and-cover drills for a generation growing up in the shadow of Columbine High School in Colorado and Sandy Hook Elementary School in Connecticut. Kindergartners learn to hide quietly behind bookshelves. Teachers warn high-school students that the glow of their cellphones could make them targets. And parents get regular text messages from school officials alerting them to lockdowns.

Since this is my blog, and blogs are all about opinions, I’ll offer mine. I detest guns, find them absolutely unnecessary for “the security of a free state,” and cannot begin to fathom their justification, especially after yet another massacre. Even the slaughter of dozens of young children fails to rein them in. Indeed, in a perverse way, each travesty triggers an increase in gun purchases on the pretext that the government will react to the bloodshed with controls, however minimal and ineffective.

Among the gun nuts is the counterargument that the Swiss have lots of guns, yet they don’t go around shooting one another. So keeping and bearing arms, per se, does not necessarily produce violence and mayhem.

I would suggest that we have an unwell society, unlike the Swiss. Certainly we tolerate pervasive poverty and inequality—absent among the Swiss, whose Gini index (level of income maldistribution) is much lower than ours, 29 to about 47, respectively.

Inequality is important, I believe, because it’s virtually impossible that extreme wealth can peaceably coexist with vast numbers of poor and near-poor. Moreover, the U.S. is a giant country compared to, say, Switzerland, and its huge expanse contributed to America’s well-deserved reputation as a “gunfighter nation.”

Within the U.S. both gun-murder rates and Gini coefficients vary, though not as much as between the U.S. as a whole and western European nations. Here’s a graph comparing the two variables among the states:

 

Inequality explains almost half the variance. We note that the District of Columbia exhibits the highest inequality and murder rate. It’s also under the jurisdiction of Congress, which has been loath to pass meaningful restrictions on guns.

That said, given the current complexion of the Supreme Court and the considerable influence of the gun lobby I do not see much hope of restrictive legislation in the near future. The violence will continue—along with inequality, about which Congress is also loath to address

Being apolitical

January 13, 2014 by davald

Bad men need nothing more to compass their ends, than that good men should look on and do nothing.

— John Stuart Mill

Is it in my genes? My grandfather was heavily involved in politics from the left, being a strong union man when that meant something. My father followed in his footsteps, belonging to the Oil, Chemical, and Atomic Workers union for most of his adult life. (I recall the dark days of the extended strike at the Richmond, Calif., refinery when belts were severely tightened and food judiciously distributed at the table.) We were, as a breed, Democrats to the bone. The only mention of Republicans was in cursed language. The favored adjectives were “damn” and “bastard.”

With such background I inherited a history of workingman politics, in which the rich were scorned, the poor pitied and assisted, and the Rest of Us struggled to make a better life. It helped that I was raised in California, then under the governorship of the first Brown, where the education system was the envy of the world and good-paying jobs plentiful. I matriculated at Berkeley—tuition free.

To be sure, I did not quickly take to the political life, largely skating through high school and even college, generally oblivious to events around me, save for the Kennedy assassination, which struck me deeply. I pursued baseball, eschewing protests of any kind, though there was surely much to oppose, from overt racial discrimination to the Vietnam War, which I managed to avoid by lottery luck.

I married early, while still a junior at Cal, became a father before I graduated, then set out to earn a living at whatever. I had no plans and nothing, really, to suggest a career, let alone a successful one. Unlike my father and his father before him, the jobs that I landed required a coat and tie rather than overalls and gloves. I did not sweat. Nor did I get dirty, save for whatever food and drink may have dribbled onto my clothing during lunchtime.

But I eventually became political. When? I would say that it happened the moment that I decided to form a union at Pemco Insurance.

That was a fool’s errand back in 1977-78. The CEO at the time, Stanley O. McNaughton, would often tell the employees that capitalism was the “greatest human invention.” He detested unions. But I naively soldiered on, gradually gaining reluctant adherents to “the cause.” In the end, after months of frustrating efforts to win the hearts and minds of a majority, we canceled the vote, realizing that we potential unionists would lose big time. Within the year I was gone. Yet now I was hooked. Damn.

The title of this post is “being apolitical.” Let’s say that I’m imagining what it might be like to fit the description.

Now I imagine that one can be apolitical (as in not paying all that much attention to current events and governments at any level) because one is otherwise engaged in something really interesting that consumes all one’s waking hours. Being a musical director of a major symphony orchestra comes to mind.

Then, I suppose, there are millions of us who just don’t give a darn about who wins elections or what the legislature or city council is up to. You may believe that you can go about your everyday activities seemingly untouched by political acts. Your pursuits may be trivial or profound leaving scarce minutes to do anything else, including casting your ballot.

Many of us are likely cynical of politics in general. This article in the New York Times triggered my post. It’s about buying elections. Shocked? Well, we shouldn’t be. Yet, the amount of money and the degree of sophistication involved in skirting campaign finance laws, however toothless, could make cynics of us all.

Exploiting a loophole in the state law and a network of political action committees in Alabama and Washington, Mr. [Mike] Hubbard [head of the Alabama Republican party] shuffled hundreds of thousands of out-of-state dollars into the Republican organization in Alabama, vastly outraising the state Democratic Party. On Election Day, Republicans won majorities in both the State Senate and House of Representatives for the first time since Reconstruction — and Alabama joined the rapidly growing fraternity of states where government is controlled by a single political party, now the largest it has been in more than half a century.

Alabama’s transformation was the product, in part, of a sophisticated political apparatus designed to channel political money from around the country into states where conditions were ripe for Republican takeover. In 2010, the effort achieved striking success, moving a dozen states to sole Republican control, including presidential swing states like Ohio and Pennsylvania.

Oh, and you contributed $25 to your favorite candidate expecting to make a difference? Good luck with that.

So, why not simply turn off the political faucet, the one that gushes out at me every day? I could play computer games or watch reality TV or read the classics, start a hobby—do anything but let the cesspool of politics disturb my life, liberty, and pursuit of happiness.

Well, there are a couple of nagging considerations that prevent me from doing so. First, I am, by definition, a politician. I hold elective office, and it’s a responsibility I take seriously. Besides, in my tenure I believe that I have made a positive difference for the community.

Second, politics is really inescapable. We may choose to ignore what government officials do, but their impacts can be huge, from how much we pay in taxes and to what ends, to whether or not we kill thousands of civilians in foreign lands, to the degree of suffering visited on citizens existing at the margins.

Politics, then, is ultimately a moral endeavor. Those we elect (with or without our vote) can do good or evil. That’s why it matters.

Rich vs. poor education

January 7, 2014 by davald

Reading the promotional material of exclusive private schools in the U.S. one quickly surmises that the rich have different priorities for their own children than they do for the offspring of the Rest of Us. For example, the schools for the elite are typically called ‘academies.’ But let’s consider a short list of what private schools intend for their pupils. I’ll focus on Lakeside School in Seattle, which was home to Bill Gates and Paul Allen. First the mission statement:

The mission of Lakeside School is to develop in intellectually capable young people the creative minds, healthy bodies, and ethical spirits needed to contribute wisdom, compassion, and leadership to a global society. We provide a rigorous and dynamic academic program through which effective educators lead students to take responsibility for learning. 

We are committed to sustaining a school in which individuals representing diverse cultures and experiences instruct one another in the meaning and value of community and in the joy and importance of lifelong learning.

I was particularly struck by this statement on academics:

Each student’s curiosities and capabilities lead them to unique academic challenges that are sustained through a culture of support and encouragement. All students will find opportunities to discover and develop a passion; to hone the skills of writing, thinking, and speaking; and to interact with the world both on and off campus. Lakeside trusts that each student has effective ideas about how to maximize his or her own education, and that they will positively contribute to our vibrant learning community.

The Lakeside School boasts that 100 percent of its graduates enter college, with a high percentage of those achieving leadership positions in their chosen fields. One alumnus, Gates, went on to become the richest person in the world. Not bad.

Now this same Gates, the bright one carefully nurtured by the Lakeside faculty, likes to meddle in education for the Rest of Us, and because he has so much money to throw around, Gates wields far more influence on such matters than any other single individual. 

For the children of the Rest of Us, Gates demands data derived from endless tests. Here is Diane Ravitch on the subject, from her book Reign of Error:

To obtain waivers [from No Child Left Behind requirements], states had to agree…to adopt “college and career-readiness standards,” which most states understood as the Common Core State Standards that were funded mainly by the Gates Foundation and promoted by the Obama administration. They had to agree to test students to measure progress toward meeting the goals of college and career readiness; these were the tests funded by the Obama administration to assess the Common Core standards. They had to agree to submit their standards and assessments to the U.S. Department of Education for review. They had to agree to evaluate teachers and principals using student test scores as a significant part of their evaluation. They had to agree to establish a system of recognizing schools as “reward,” “focus,” and “priority,” with a plan to intervene aggressively in the “priority” schools, which were the lowest performing. They had to develop a plan to establish measurable objectives for all their schools.*

Now, one thing you learn about elite private schools is that their pupils are busy exploring their “curiosities,” developing their “capabilities,” and pursuing their “passions.” They do not spend time preparing for or taking tests. That’s for the Rest of Us.

At Lakeside the student-faculty ratio is 15:1. My wife, a recently retired teacher, volunteers at her former public school. The fifth-grade classroom she visits contains 26 students. Other classrooms in the same school district approach or even exceed 30 pupils, or twice the average of Lakeside classrooms. Having fewer students allows teachers to nurture their students in the Lakeside way, ably assisted by extensive resources denied to the children of the Rest of Us.

It occurred to me a while ago that Gates and other wealthy people who meddle in the affairs of the ordinary have different priorities and expectations for others’ children than they do for their own. It further occurred to me that they may very well intend that the public school system serve as a giant sieve, dividing children of the Rest of Us into two basic categories: those who have the skill sets demanded of the plutocrats and those who don’t. After all, I thought to myself, the progeny of wealth cannot be expected to write software code or design more efficient automobiles or engineer more profitable industrial processes. Rich children, those who graduate from academies, will go on to lead, never to follow.

How shall they be divided? Test scores, of course. Tests of what? As it happens, a very narrow range of academic subjects: math, reading, and writing. The children of the Rest of Us are not expected to pursue their own passions. Nor will they have time for art, music, humanities, or anything that detracts from the winnowing protocols. 

As for those discarded by the sieve, good luck. Then again, that’s why we have prisons and fast-food restaurants and hotels. If the plutocrats need workers, why, they’re all over the world at a very cheap price.

Down in the West

December 10, 2013 by davald

The OECD released the results of last year’s PISA tests, which are administered to randomly selected 15-year-olds in OECD countries, plus a few more, including China. There are a couple of notable observations regarding the mathematics portion.

First, the Asian countries outperformed western nations by a considerable margin. Shanghai, China, scored highest. But Hong Kong, Taipei, and Korea were close behind.

Second, several western countries showed significant declines from prior years, in particular Sweden and Finland, the latter’s educational achievement mentioned frequently on this blog. The mean math scores fell 3.3 and 2.8 percent, respectively, from the previous test year. The U.S., as usual, continued to fare poorly.

 

OECD representatives issued a “warning” to western leaders. The New York Times:

Mr. [Andreas] Schleicher, the head of education at the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development, which administers the tests every three years to about half a million 15-year-olds in 65 countries around the world, also noted significant improvement in Vietnam. He described it as a poor country whose students outperformed peers from many wealthier nations — and did even better once differences in income were taken into account.

“On a level playing field, the British look even worse,” he said at a press conference here.

Western countries, Mr. Schleicher warned, should not to comfort themselves with the myth that Asian high performance is the result of education systems that favor memorization over creativity.

“The big success in East Asia is not a success in drilling,” he said, adding that the mathematics test required creativity and problem solving skills based on a deep understanding of mathematical concepts.

Deep understanding.

If you’re familiar with what’s going on in our public school classrooms these days, the talk amongst faculty and administrators is all about “the tests.” Not the PISA, mind you, but the endless battery of tests administered to students beginning in—kindergarten!. There must be data, big data, since that drives nearly everything, leading, of course, to now-annual high-stakes examinations states must administer or else lose federal funding. So, there is simply no time to gain a cursory knowledge of subjects, let alone develop a deep understanding.

The U.S. used to be the envy of the world in educational matters. While we still boast the world’s best universities, there’s little doubt that we’ve slipped terribly at the elementary and secondary levels.

The continuing slippage gives rise to a constant demand for “reform,” which generally translates into “higher standards” and, more recently, subsidizing charter schools, reducing the influence of teachers’ unions, and jettisoning educators deemed inadequate to train today’s youth.

Yet, the PISA results provide only a gross accounting of academic performance. The U.S. is a large country with huge swaths of poverty and clusters of wealth. So it should not surprise us that kids in and around Boston, for example, score much higher than their counterparts in Mississippi and other decidedly “red” states whose poverty levels vastly exceed the national average. Indeed, many states are much larger than entire OECD nations, and the differences between them are as big as those between, say, Germany and Italy.

What differentiates states, aside from geography, is socioeconomic status. The so-called “blue” states are wealthier, better educated, and more literate than the people inhabiting the southern region. In these statistics we see sharp variation in education spending.

However, the PISA results also reveal some interesting exceptions to the above. Vietnam, for one, yielded higher scores than the U.S., Sweden, Norway and the European southern periphery. It is not a wealthy nation. Nor, for that matter, are Estonia and Latvia, whose students bested America’s. For some perspective, the wealth per capita of the U.S. is over 25 times higher than Vietnam’s. (Note: Data on wealth and inequality from Wikipedia.)

The diamond at the lower right represents Shanghai, China.

Nor is there much of a correlation on the country-by-country scores between PISA performance and the Gini coefficient.

The diamond at the far upper right represents Shanghai, China.

The linked New York Times article:

Though most of the press coverage in Britain focused on its performance, John Jerrim, a professor at the Institute for Education, said he thought that the big news in the results was neither the continuing rise of Asia nor the relatively flat results from the United States and Europe over all but a sharp drop in the scores for Swedish schools.

“Sweden has the biggest decline of any country in the world,” Dr. Jerrim said in an interview. “They’re down 3.3 percent in mathematics, 3.1 percent in science and 2.8 percent in reading, and that continues a trend from 2009.”

Yet the American and British governments “have followed the Swedish model by opening more and more ‘free schools’ or charter schools,” he said.

His skepticism was echoed by Mr. Schleicher, the O.E.C.D. official, who said, “The data shows no relation between competition between schools and the overall performance level.”

Asked afterward what the highest-performing systems had in common, Mr. Schleicher said: “High performers pay teachers more. They are also systems with a commitment to universal achievement.”

In Shanghai, more than half the students tested finished in the top two categories in mathematics, he noted, and the figure in Singapore was 40 percent.

Only about 13 percent of French students made it into the top two categories in mathematics while in the United States only 8.8 percent did.

I’m not sure that we can reach obvious conclusions as to why Asian countries do better than western nations. China is now, as a whole, a more unequal country than the U.S. Nor is it nearly as wealthy as the United States. The OECD attempts to make sense of the performance differences. It’s report included some graphs as well.

This first graph compares math scores and GDP.

Another compares the same scores to educational spending.

The researchers found a lower correlation between the math scores and parents’ education.

Finland may have had lessons to teach us and perhaps still does. The author of the book Finnish Lessons commented in the Washington Post. He writes:

Finland should not do what many other countries have done when they have looked for a cure to their ill-performing school systems. Common solutions have included market-based reforms, such as increasing competition between schools, standardization of teaching and learning, tougher test-based accountability and privatization of public schools. Instead, Finns must protect their schools from the Global Educational Reform Movement (GERM) that has failed to help schools to get better in other countries.  The better way for Finland is to ensure that schools are able to cope with increasing inequality, that teachers have tools to help students with individual needs, and that all schools get support to succeed.

PISA results are too often presented as a simple league table of education systems. But there is much more that the data reveal. The Finnish school system continues to be one of the most equitable among the OECD countries. This means that in Finland, students’ learning in school is less affected by their family backgrounds than in most other countries. Schools in Finland remain fairly equal in learning outcomes despite the rapid growth of non-Finnish speaking children in schools.

Finland should also continue to let national education and youth policies — and not PISA — drive what is happening in schools. Reading, science, and mathematics are important in Finnish education system but so are social studies, arts, music, physical education, and various practical skills. Play and joy of learning characterize Finland’s pre-schools and elementary classrooms. Many teachers and parents in Finland believe that the best way to learn mathematics and science is to combine conceptual, abstract learning with singing, drama, and sports. This balance between academic and non-academic learning is critical to children’s well-being and happiness in school. PISA tells only a little about these important aspects of school education.

But I’m afraid that the usual politics will prevent the U.S. from gaining a “deep understanding” of its public schools, teacher preparation, and the needs of children. Lacking such commitment by government and educational leaders, our schools will continue their long journey toward mediocrity. That’s a scandal.

America sucks—a qualification

November 21, 2013 by davald

In a previous post I suggested that America sucks. I should explain.

First off, America is a country or nation or geographical location. The term ‘America’ is often used to describe the sum total of what and who exists within 50 states and includes our economic and political systems along with considerable real estate. Moreover, whether or not the expression ‘America sucks’ fits depends in large part on one’s point of view.

Truth be told, I’m doing okay, as is my family, including two grown children. We all have sufficient incomes on which to live comfortable lives with some left over to secure decent retirements. Well, then, if I’m okay then you’re okay. Right?

Not really, as I’ve written about on these pages on countless occasions. Millions of Americans struggle mightily to put food on the table and a roof over their heads. It bothers me that rampant economic insecurity exists at all in a country that is so rich in the aggregate. Take this chart, for instance. It shows the Gini index along with the share of income enjoyed by the top ten percent of Americans (data from Emmanuel Saez and FRED).

As the country has grown more unequal (the Gini ratio) those at the top increase their wealth, taking an ever greater share of the economy’s total income. Now think a moment about the red line and where it ends on the right. Just one in ten of us now capture half of all income. One of out of every two dollars made in America goes to that top 10th of households.  Again, these numbers bother me.

Yet, many of us don’t seem to care about such statistics. Perhaps we believe that we are brought into this world as individuals dependent exclusively on our own abilities and fortunes for survival. Few prosper; most do not. Such is life. This ideology promotes suspicion of government, which exists only to ensure property rights and common defense. Though, if I should care about someone else, I am free to help. If I want to join a group or exclude others from the ones to which I belong, I’m free to do so, and no government should say otherwise. Nor, of course, should the government confiscate my wealth to give to Those People. Besides, giving money or services to the destitute only encourages laziness and dependency, moral failings far worse than avarice.

I am describing, loosely, a collage of right-wing attitudes and beliefs that includes the Tea Party, libertarians, devotees of Ayn Rand, and the more conservative strains that still call themselves Republicans. I should probably add those who are apolitical, who avoid the ballot box and choose to ignore those outside their in-group—these are the solipsists.

I concede that one can get through life from birth to death without regard for the well-being of others. It’s also possible that this lifespan may be relatively free of misfortune. Lucky or God’s will. Take your pick.

In that same post linked above I shared my conflicted feelings about watching Bill Moyers’s television program. Each week he interviews people—some famous, most not—who cite appalling numbers quantifying the problems faced by many in America (and the world). The pictures they rhetorically paint make me angry or sad. Almost all of Moyers’s guests devote the majority of their daily lives in service to others, either directly or through advocacy. I am simultaneously disturbed and overwhelmed. What can I do? What should I do? What happens if I do nothing?

I may delude myself in believing that there was a time in America when the notion of commonweal mattered, a stronger sense of society among relative equals. Regardless, it’s dramatically clear to me, at any rate, that we are now a nation deeply divided by religion, race, socioeconomic status, and surely politics. In brief, we cannot agree. Instead, we argue and bicker and get altogether too angry with each other to solve problems collectively, with many of us condemning the very concept of public.

If I’m only slightly correct in this surmise, the future bodes ill.  The problems faced by the Rest of Us are many and acute requiring urgent, concerted action. They will not be resolved by Bill Gates or the Koch brothers or any of the plutocrats who must rely on exponential numbers to record their wealth. Indeed, their intrusions into education, energy, and the political arena as a whole only exacerbate the challenges.

But how depressing this all is. That is, if you care.

Checking the boxes

November 18, 2013 by davald

I confess to a hiatus in viewing Bill Moyers; I recently resumed watching. I really can’t say why I stopped or why I returned. But after seeing the last two episodes I suspect a possible explanation for both: The societal needs are as large and numerous as my abilities and energy are inadequate.

You may have noticed that I checked multiple categories for this post. I could have checked more. While it would be comforting to believe that all’s well, any sentient being with an ounce of compassion cannot escape the sobering conclusion: America, at least, sucks.

Surely not for the One-Percent, of course. They’re doing just fine and actually increasing their wealth. The Rest of Us, however, appear to be stuck, along with the economy, which may have entered a new normal of “secular stagnation.” Let’s look around to consider the state of affairs.

Our politicians refused to pass a simple piece of legislation that would have provided “Medicare for all.” Instead, they succumbed to the moneyed interests. As a result, we have a labyrinthian Affordable Care Act that actually transfers more dollars from the Rest of Us to the health care industry. Worse, whatever the benefits of ACA, and there are some, Obama was too busy elsewhere to ensure and insist that implementation be effectively executed. His signature legislation was held hostage to an easily distracted president who always finds time for other things, like spying and killing and golf.

Millions of Americans can’t find a job, even those who did what they were told by securing college degrees. Unemployment remains staggeringly high, and this Congress couldn’t care less.

And speaking of Congress, our government structure is obviously broken. A tiny minority known as the Tea Party can and does control what bills get passed, which don’t see the floor for a vote, and who assumes an office or a position on the president’s nomination. I think they’re crazy, but just enough citizens, aided and abetted by diabolical gerrymandering, elect and retain them to stymie major, and sorely needed, efforts to put people back to work, repair and improve public infrastructures, and provide sufficient income for all, including retirees.

While scientists accumulate evidence that the planet faces catastrophe without urgent and immediate action, the aforementioned Tea Party and almost all Republicans either deny the science of climate change or steadfastly believe that we shall somehow overcome through God’s will or exotic geoenginnering. Or we’ll simply adapt, though the details of how escape description.

On his last program, Moyers interviewed two physicians who are “fighting the good fight.” Both have been arrested multiple times for acts of civil disobedience. Here’s an excerpt from the exchange:

BILL MOYERS: So what do you do when you write a statement that the press ignores, or you organize rallies and events where maybe, five or six or ten people turn out and the press, again, totally ignores you? What, how do you feel about that?

MARGARET FLOWERS: Well, the mass media press is not going to cover this movement. They’re not covering it. I mean, there’s a very vibrant movement going on in this country right now. And that’s why we created PopularResistance.org. It’s a daily movement news site where we cover those protests.

BILL MOYERS: And what do I find if I go there?

MARGARET FLOWERS: You’ll find news stories about protests that are going on in the United States and around the world, as well as informational articles about current events that you’re not going to hear in kind of, the mass media. And you can go there and find articles about what’s going on. If you want to start learning about strategy or how to organize in your community, there are tools for that.

If you want to connect with resistance groups, and we try to uplift the groups that are in the frontlines of struggle, that are actually taking on these challenges very effectively. You can plug into those or the alternative is create, creating alternative systems. We have information about that too.

What gets me is that Bill Moyers has been interviewing political activists for decades, and almost invariably he asks how they keep going against insurmountable odds. Without exception his guests mention this or that protest group or organization that is working valiantly to make a difference.

What difference has been made? As I said, Moyers has been at this almost since he left the White House as LBJ’s press secretary. He’s got to admit that things have only gotten worse across a wide array of checked boxes, from poverty and war to civil liberties and health care. Yet, each week he sits across the table from very concerned citizens who throw out facts and figures that paint another dire picture of what ails us. What to do? Well, organize, protest, and otherwise engage in this or that struggle. After all, they are all struggles and they’re all against heavily entrenched interests.

Depressing as this comes across, Moyers interviewed John Nichols and Robert McChesney the week before. Their recent book, Dollarocracy, describes a political system ruled by the dollar rather than the people. Each page presents evidence to support their subtitle’s message: How the Money-and-Media Election Complex Is Destroying America.

Destroying America. Hmmm.

I don’t doubt the conclusion. I just wonder if America was ever privileged. Though I’ve often referenced “the relative halcyon days” of the 50s and 60s, the country at the time was rife with conspicuous racism, codified in draconian Jim Crow laws. White northerners exhibited hostility to people of color; they were just a bit more subtle about it. Women were generally treated as second-class citizens, forced to submit to dominant males, many, if not most, of whom were living their own lives of quiet desperation and anxiety. Oh, and it was not a good time to be a red, if ever there is.

Meanwhile public education is being attacked. It’s once again in crisis, a repeated offense. This time we’re all about testing the hell out of kids, even before they enter kindergarten. After all, we need data. For what purpose? The cynical goal is to trash public schools themselves, teacher by teacher. Then what? Since this is America, everything is about making money. Billions of dollars are spent each year educating children. Much, if not all, of that could be siphoned off by private entrepreneurs proclaiming the virtues of markets and competition and choice and all those presumed virtues that have delivered the world’s greatest economy.

Which brings us back to the beginning. How could it be great when it serves so few?

Okay. I feel a bit better. On with the struggles.

Plutocrats and poverty

November 3, 2013 by davald

Chrystia Freeland, author of Plutocrats, has an op-ed in today’s New York Times. She believes that, despite the accumulating wealth and its commensurate political power, the populists are winning. I doubt it. After all, she subtitled her book: “THE RISE of the NEW GLOBAL SUPER-RICH and the FALL OF EVERYONE ELSE”

But, having spent time amongst the elite in researching her book, she possesses a unique insight into their habits and attitudes. One quote from her article:

Plutocrats inhabit a different world from everyone else, with different schools, different means of travel, different food, even different life expectancies. The technocratic solutions to public-policy problems they deliver from those Olympian heights arrive in a wrapper of remote benevolence. Plutocrats are no more likely to send their own children to the charter schools they champion than they are to need the malaria cures they support.
