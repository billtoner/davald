Education fads

June 10, 2013 by davald

The title may be redundant, as anyone who has been a teacher can attest. One thing remains constant, however. There is always a crisis in education. There’s also money, which creates cottage industries preaching this or that “program,” with gullible school boards and principals consuming with insatiable appetites.

When I taught, and I use the term reluctantly, my principal desperately tried to find something that would work in my classrooms, since I was a novice and not having much success with 40+ students at a time. She discovered a hybrid pedagogical approach, we might say. It involved dividing up the classes into teams. Each team would compete with the others. But within the teams cooperation would rule. Moreover, each team would be comprised of a share of the brightest, the dimmest, and the in between. Students would cooperate so as to compete, assuming that two disparate motivations would magically combine to achieve the desired effect, namely academic learning. Let’s just say that in my incapable hands, another program collapsed into oblivion.

In today’s New York Times we learn that ability grouping has made a quiet comeback, after being quashed some decades ago, allegedly for damaging children’s self-esteem. After all, who wants the world to know that you’re dumb? So, students were lumped together in elementary classrooms, receiving the same instructions all the time. Of course, problems arose. The quick kids got it, then looked for things to do, much of it mischievous. The slow students never could, so they also acted out. Those in the middle struggled along mostly learning, and it was to those in the middle the teacher directed her lessons. This wasn’t working, which explains the resurgence of grouping children by ability. The Times quotes one educator on the difficulties of whole-class teaching:

“My instruction aimed at the middle of my class, and was leaving out approximately two-thirds of my learners,” said Ms. Sears, a fourth-grade teacher at Woodman Park Elementary in Dover, N.H. “I didn’t like those odds.”

The Times article also talks about the not-so-subtle influence of high-stakes testing. Teachers size up their students’ capabilities rather quickly. They know which students are bright and which are hopeless. They also identify those children who are on the cusp of getting it. With some added attention these struggling students could improve enough to pass the test, and it’s the damn tests that count, after all.

Now I would not call ability grouping a fad or a program. It just makes sense, though it’s hardly easier for the classroom teacher, who must plan separate strategies for lessons to accommodate the different abilities among her students.

There are real fads and programs, however, and a visit to today’s classrooms would reveal an alphabet-soup array of them. I’ll mention three that are prominent in my wife’s school: RTI, AVID, and CIA. In turn, each of these programs comes by way of a separate cottage industry, which charges money for both materials and teacher-instruction, usually in the form of extended conferences housed in resort-like destinations.

In reading about Finland’s approach to education I was struck by the distinction between “renting” and “owning.” The programs mentioned above are “rented” by school districts for a substantial fee, usually. No one in the schools “owns” the programs, since they are not the product of teachers working with their students and with each other. Finnish teachers are trained every bit as much as doctors and lawyers before they enter the profession. That training concentrates on research. So that when teachers assume responsibility for educating pupils, they have a solid grasp of what works and what doesn’t. Also, and most important, the teachers themselves, either individually or collaboratively, design their own curricula. They “own” the programs they create.

This past Sunday the New York Times featured an article by Andrew Hacker and Claudia Dreifus on Common Core Standards. This is the mother of all programs, and it’s being rented by just about every state in the union.

By the 2014-15 academic year, public schools in 45 states and the District of Columbia will administer Common Core tests to students of all ages.

When helping my wife clean out her classroom over the weekend at the end of her last school year, she showed me just a couple of binders filled with Common Core Standards, these on reading and literature. Hacker and Dreifus have identified over 1,300 such standards across the disciplines, from math to reading. Many an educator can abandon sleeping pills for one of the binders. A few pages will do.

WHAT became the Common Core began quite modestly. Several years ago, many organizations, including the National Governors Association and the Council of Chief State School Officers, whose members are top-ranking state education officials, independently noticed that the content and scoring of high school “exit” tests varied widely between states. In 2006, for instance, 91 percent of students in Mississippi passed a mathematics exit exam on the first attempt, while only 65 percent did so in Arizona. At the same time, students’ performance on the National Assessment of Educational Progress often differed from the state results.

This was not just embarrassing: it looked unprofessional. The governors and the school chiefs decided to work together to create a single set of standards and a common grading criteria. Private funding, led by some $35 million in grants from the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, allowed the coalition to spread its wings. Aligning tests became an opportunity to specify what every American child should know.

You can guess who also embraces Common Core. Yep. Arne Duncan, who wants all children to “race to the top.” All they need to do is pass the tests aligned with the 1,300 skills and standards.

I predict that this mother of all programs will be another colossal failure, but not before billions of dollars have been spent and countless teachers have been drummed out of the profession, many of their own volition. They, like my wife, will refuse to participate in creating a nation of “imbeciles,” as Noam Chomsky suggests.

Finland’s policy makers ultimately concluded that improving their public schools had to begin and end with teaching. So, they set out to develop the best and the brightest teachers on the planet, then gave them the resources needed, both within and outside the schools, to produce a nation of academic achievers.

The Finns understand that impoverished children make for poor learners. We Americans, on the other hand, believe that we can rent our way out of poverty. Not going to happen.

I’ll give Hacker and Dreifus the last words:

For our part, we’re tired of seeing teachers cast as scapegoats, of all the carping over unions and tenure. It is time teachers are as revered in society as doctors or scientists, and allowed to work professionally without being bound by reams of rules.

Still, there’s an upside to the Common Core’s arrival. As the public better appreciates its sweep, there is likely to be much discussion about schools and what we want them to do. Ideally, this will involve a reconsideration of the contours of knowledge and the question of how we can become a better-educated nation.

Guns, guns, guns

June 6, 2013 by davald

Writing for the New York Review of Books, David Cole alerts us to Joe Nocera’s blog, The Gun Report. Cole says it’s a must read, however depressing. I think you’ll agree.

Cole cites some grim statistics:

Meanwhile, gun violence in the United States continues to far outpace that in other developed nations. Since 1960, more than 1.3 million Americans have died from firearms, either in suicides, homicides, or accidents. By this grim metric, we are unquestionably a world leader. The US firearms homicide rate is twenty times higher than the combined rate of the next twenty-two high-income developed nations. Between 2000 and 2008, there were more than 30,000 gun deaths a year in the US, for an average of more than eighty every single day. And in 2010 alone, emergency rooms treated more than 73,000 people for nonfatal gunshot injuries.

We read with horror of terrorist attacks around the world, mostly in far-flung places that regularly endure suicide bombings, improvised explosive devices, and the like. We breathe a sigh of relief that we don’t have to live with such violence, while we spend billions of dollars annually to prevent such attacks occurring here. But every year, about twice as many people are killed in the United States by guns than die of terrorist attacks worldwide. Americans face a one in 3.5 million chance of being killed in a terrorist attack, but a one in 22,000 chance of being murdered.

Despite polls showing overwhelming support for background checks, for example, the Senate was unwilling to pass even modest legislation in response to the terrible killings at Sandy Hook Elementary. So much for outrage, with Bastard Baucus continuing to do his utmost best to ensure that everyone has a gun, any time and everywhere—the slaughter be damned.

Yet, Cole tries to persuade us to change our focus. While the headlines concentrate on assault weapons and multi-round clips, ubiquitous handguns account for most of the killings. Also, a disproportionate share of the deaths occur in poverty-ridden urban ghettoes. What to do? Here’s Cole:

…Gun violence is concentrated in economically devastated neighborhoods in cities like Chicago, Baltimore, and Los Angeles. And precisely because the problem is focused in these locales, the majority can and does ignore it. Closing background check loopholes and mandating gun safety are sensible and desirable reforms. But if we really want to do something about gun violence, we must go beyond such measures and invest real resources where the problem is most acute.

Time magazine reports that Chicago has the highest murder rate of America’s three largest cities—18.6 murders for every 100,000 residents, and most of the killings are “black-on-black” and 86 percent involve handguns. Its June 10, 2013, issue includes a map of the city showing the location of homicides. Few of the dots are in the affluent sections of Hyde Park (Obama) and Lincoln Park. Hey, they’re parks after all. The magazine notes that Mayor Rahm Emanuel’s school closures happen to be in those neighborhoods with the most shootings.

Getting a job [u]

May 29, 2013 by davald

If you search economics journals you will eventually cross paths with those who assert that all unemployment is voluntary. That you may out of a job is simply a matter of excessive wage demands. The obvious corollary: be willing to work for peanuts at anything.

Or, and you can find plenty of people making this point, you lack the skills employers need. Acquire those skills and, voilà, you’re employed. Thus spake Thomas Friedman is his column today. He writes:

Underneath the huge drop in demand that drove unemployment up to 9 percent during the recession, there’s been an important shift in the education-to-work model in America. Anyone who’s been looking for a job knows what I mean. It is best summed up by the mantra from the Harvard education expert Tony Wagner that the world doesn’t care anymore what you know; all it cares “is what you can do with what you know.” And since jobs are evolving so quickly, with so many new tools, a bachelor’s degree is no longer considered an adequate proxy by employers for your ability to do a particular job — and, therefore, be hired. So, more employers are designing their own tests to measure applicants’ skills. And they increasingly don’t care how those skills were acquired: home schooling, an online university, a massive open online course, or Yale. They just want to know one thing: Can you add value?

Friedman is being Friedman here. Fashioning a few anecdotes into a grand theory, this time on the pressing topic for millions of unemployed, “How to get a job,” which is the title of his essay.

But I have an alternative scenario, one that doesn’t involve education-based skillsets, regardless of how you develop them. If the economy ever recovers to pre-recession norms there will be plenty of new jobs, and most of those jobs will not require a college degree.

As I’ve posted before, I believe that a good case can be made that such a recovery will be led by a resumption of home-buying and construction. In turn, a healthier housing industry will beget a host of other jobs, including retail, entertainment, home-improvement, and the like. Again, none of these will require college.

UPDATE (May 30, 2013):

The Bureau of Labor Statistics published a report on the nation’s fastest growing jobs. Most do not require a college degree. As you’ll note, there’s an increasing demand for health-care workers. They will be necessary to care for old farts like me.

Get an education, if you can pay for it

May 24, 2013 by davald

I count myself lucky for having attended California schools in the 50s and 60s. They were the best in the nation, from K through graduate school—and education was cheap, as in $60 a quarter for Berkeley. Yep. You read that right.

Today I probably couldn’t afford to go to school, if I assume the same income as my parents had way back then, even adjusted for inflation. Paying for college imposes severe burdens on the children of the Rest of Us.

But everyone tells you go earn a degree, if you expect to make any money in your life. If you lack sufficient income to fund tuition, your only recourse, short of some rare, generous scholarship, is to take out a loan.

However, even should you graduate, you have no guarantee of landing that coveted job. Something about a lousy economy. So, you’re stuck with this huge student debt with little means to pay it back.

Now, if you were a bank, you could grab all the money you want, and it would be dirt cheap, with record-low interest rates now. Of course, you’re not a bank. You’re a poor graduate struggling to survive, and the interest that you pay on that giant debt (on average, about $25,000 after four years) is very high, and it could get higher.

Moreover, as if to really put the screws to young college grads, you can’t file for bankruptcy after you’ve fallen way behind on your payments. You can thank the bastards in Congress for that, in another genuflection to the financial sector, the folks who run everything.

It would seem that the powers that be are sending a clear message. College is only for the well-to-do. Otherwise, you’re screwed.

Only in America.

One reports that UW Medicine has signed an agreement with PeaceHealth, a Catholic health care system. The article quotes a lawyer with the American Civil Liberties Union:

“We are troubled and concerned because we have yet to see the final details,” said Sarah Dunne, legal director for the ACLU of Washington. “What we don’t know is whether this is a Trojan horse. … They’re saying they’re independent organizations, but we’ve seen this before, and lo and behold, once they’re connected, one takes over the other.”

There is much evidence to support her suspicion, including a previous agreement between Swedish Medical Center and Providence Health and Services. Spokespersons at the time gave assurances that the two entities would remain separate and independent, just as the statement on the PeaceHealth-UW Medicine arrangement asserted. Which brings me to the second article, this one an op-ed by the New York Times‘s Frank Bruni. He writes about the fate of one high school physical education teacher who had the misfortune of teaching at a Catholic institution and suffering the sudden loss of her mother. Rather suddenly, her mother died, and an hour afterward, she and her brother numbly went through the paces of a standard obituary, listing survivors. Her brother included his wife. So Carla included her partner, Julie, whom her mother had known well and loved. Leaving Julie out would have been unthinkable, though Carla didn’t really think it through at the time. Her grief was still raw.

One of those goody-two-shoes parishioners caught the reference while reading the obituaries then wrote an anonymous letter (such fortitude) to the school and diocese. You can guess what happened.

“A lot of people want me to be bitter and go after the Catholic Church,” [Hale] said, adding that others want to cast her as a lesbian heroine. She just wants her job back, a recognition, she said, “that I’m a moral individual who happens to be gay.”

Determining b.s.

May 13, 2013 by davald

On occasion I cross breaths with climate change skeptics, although I strongly suspect that in my absence they descend into group-thinking denial, following the lead of that noted eminent scientist Jim Inhofe, who has repeatedly called global warming “a hoax” and likely something stronger when he’s surrounded by fellow imbeciles. A preponderance of facts should never get in the way of a good story, as Ronald Reagan opined—he of “trees pollute” and “ketchup is a vegetable” fame. The stories range from carbon dioxide is as natural as flower petals to god will decide humanity’s fate, so might was well not worry about it. Oh, and it seems that those who hold one conspiracy embrace dozens, all whackier than mimes on meth.

The combination of social media and the Internet breeds incoherent screeds that instantly infect idiots. Have you heard the latest on the Clintons’ murders? Did you know that the moon landing was fake? Vaccinations will kill you, of course.

Suppose that you have the good fortune of having landed a position in a university, and it really doesn’t matter which, save for those founded and run by aforementioned idiots. Let’s further assume that you teach political science or sociology, fields that don’t easily lend themselves to formulas and laboratory observations as physics or chemistry do. Now you pass out your first assignment, asking students to explore a topic, reach a conclusion, and base findings on research. What should you expect from your students?

A midwestern professor of political science, who goes by “Ed,” has a blog that I’ve referenced several times, ginandtacos.com. He prefers to remain somewhat anonymous, and who can blame him? His prose is colorful. His tone irreverent. Today he mourns the state of student stupidity.

He mentions an actual paper submitted by an undergrad, replete with footnotes!

Many years ago a student submitted a research paper that I continue to use (without personal information, obviously) in classes as an example today. Unfortunately it’s not a positive example. The paper is a ten-page treatise on how the American presidents who died in office were all secretly murdered by the Freemasons.

The student was quite unhappy with the (failing) grade this mess of internet conspiracy theories received. “I did research and cited all of the sources”, s/he stated. It was true; the paper was exhaustively cited and clearly represented a good deal of research. The problem – and we/I did cover this in class repeatedly throughout the semester – is that not all “sources” are made equal. Some are legitimate, some are questionable, and some are flat-out nonsense. And back when I was still learning how to teach, it surprised the hell out of me to find that a non-trivial minority of students cannot tell the difference.

“Ed” goes on to discuss how we can distinguish between obvious bullshit and trustworthy sources. As he puts it:

My best advice, I think, is to err on the side of caution when the objective is to cite supporting research. Sure, government or major media outlet sources will not always be correct and may be flawed. But if the choice is between the Benghazi story on CNN or a post on something called WheresTheBirthCertificate.com, it is in students’ interest to play it safe.

Indeed.

The “sensible center”

May 2, 2013 by davald

Alliterative, to  be sure. That’s Peter Jackson, writing this morning in the Everett Herald. He came to his keyboard not to bury Steve Hobbs but to praise him. Hobbs, a nominal Democratic senator in Olympia and member of the Road Kill Caucus, presumably a group of sensible centrists, annoys the hell out of liberals and progressives for his stubbornly conservative positions on key issues affecting the Rest of Us. Here’s Jackson, the son of Henry M., if you don’t know:

Two recent examples involving Hobbs merit flagging. Leaders of Washington CAN! gave the senator an ultimatum to renounce his leadership role in a bipartisan national group called “Fix the Debt” or they’ll begin to leaflet and campaign against him. (Hobbs wants to start a conversation about our colossal national debt. Oh, the shame.) Note to pressure groups: Ultimatums only irritate harried lawmakers who are trying to do their best. Hobbs also has been the target of WEA activists for his support of education reform (a teacher doorbelled his family home in Lake Stevens, Hobbs said.)

I’ve discussed “our colossal national debt” on these pages (e.g., here). It’s a bogus problem, as several prominent economists have painstakingly explained (one example here). It’s especially so in the midst of the anemic jobless recovery from the worst economic downturn since the Great Depression. The “sensible centrists” love to exploit our ordinary sensibilities about debt (sinful) to whittle away at public services, including education and so-called entitlements, the programs that most of us will rely on as we enter our golden years.

As for the “conversation,” it’s occurring amidst the numbers, which are dismal. European leaders trumpeted austerity (or fiscal consolidation, if you prefer) as an antidote to “colossal national debt.” It’s not working, Messrs. Jackson and Hobbs. Indeed, cutting federal spending during liquidity-trap conditions actually increases deficits. Why? Because governments continue to suffer from inadequate revenues occasioned by depressed aggregate demand, and consumers buying goods and services accounts for the majority of GDP.

Besides, those like Jackson and Hobbs ignore a more important consideration about debt: its affordability. With interest rates at or near zero, debt service payments impose minimal challenges to the fiscal balance sheets.

 

Before departing, I have to comment on this quote from Jackson’s editorial:

This Editorial Page has disagreed with Hobbs on a few issues, including the wisdom of a coal-export facility at Cherry Point near Bellingham. Nevertheless, Hobbs embraces positions that align with his core principles.

To offer an extreme example, Hitler embraced positions consistent with “his core principles.” He did not like Jews, so he killed as many as he could get his hands on. Any fair reading of Mein Kampf or the history of Der Führer’s rise in Weimar Germany reveals that fundamental hatred of all things Hebrew. That he acted in accordance with this core principle is hardly commendable. It was and continues to be unreservedly condemned.

The problem with Steve Hobbs and others of his ilk is precisely a matter of “core principles.” In my judgment, they’re decidedly wrong for the people of Snohomish County, Washington state, and the nation.

The race is to the rich

April 29, 2013 by davald

Americans appear to be preternaturally ignorant. We just don’t do very well with facts and figures. Much of this syndrome can be blamed on our equally strong tendency to adhere fast to bullshit theories about human nature and religion, which render us impervious to reason and reality.

Unfortunately, the ignorance is widespread, though it’s most evident among our politicians, who will do or say anything to obtain your stupid vote. And, yes, I’m suggesting that we look in the mirror.

This episode of my periodic rant concerns a spate of research confirming two obvious features of American society, in particular. First, the rich continue to get richer. Second, the children of the wealthy do much better academically than the offspring of the Rest of Us.

I’ve posted extensively on the wealth gap, so no need to restate my position here. Just perform a search of my site with the words “wealth gap.” I’ve also commented on the second point, about the growing educational achievement divide between children of the rich versus everyone else.

Recently I wrote about “educational apartheid.” Before that I commented on the  rigidity of achievement gaps—once rich or once poor, always so. Research suggests that money spent on schools to close those gaps may be wasted.

Today I came across this article written by a Stanford professor for the New York Times. Sean Reardon tells us:

Here’s a fact that may not surprise you: the children of the rich perform better in school, on average, than children from middle-class or poor families. Students growing up in richer families have better grades and higher standardized test scores, on average, than poorer students; they also have higher rates of participation in extracurricular activities and school leadership positions, higher graduation rates and higher rates of college enrollment and completion.

Professor Reardon chides all of us for blaming the schools and the teachers therein for academic failures. The achievement gap increases by only ten percent from kindergarten to high school between the children of the rich and the children of the Rest of Us . The disparity, which is huge, already exists before five-year-olds start school.

Moreover, test scores of poor and middle class students have actually risen over time. What is so remarkable is the rapid improvement of rich kids, and it has everything to do with enrichment, which begins at birth and continues throughout a wealthy child’s educational development.

Liberals like Barack Obama deceive themselves by insisting that the focus of reform should be on creating better schools. Wrong. Here’s Reardon:

The more we do to ensure that all children have similar cognitively stimulating early childhood experiences, the less we will have to worry about failing schools. This in turn will enable us to let our schools focus on teaching the skills — how to solve complex problems, how to think critically and how to collaborate — essential to a growing economy and a lively democracy.

What gets me angry is how far away we are as a society from recognizing this important truth. We don’t build a more egalitarian society through education. Rather, we increase educational achievement by fashioning a more egalitarian society. Indeed, children of impoverished homes actually have difficulty learning in the first place.

Yet, as the New York Times editors exclaimed, we’re “heading the wrong way” by slashing funding for public services. To our point, and as Professor Reardon urges, government dollars should be aimed at reducing inequality and providing enrichment for all children, not just those of the wealthy.

Writing for the New York Times, Floyd Norris opines that we are “starving the beast” as conservatives have long desired. But in so doing, we’re depriving our children of both educational and economic opportunities over their lifetimes. Norris:

In the 1950s, government spending fell quickly after the Korean War ended. In the second quarter of 1956, the total government gross domestic product was $91.4 billion, at an annual rate. That was 0.4 percent lower than the $91.8 billion rate three years earlier, in the third quarter of 1953.

Notions on high and low finance.

It would be almost 60 years before another such decline was recorded.

The G.D.P. report released Friday states the total government part of G.D.P. – federal, state and local – came to $3.0306 trillion in the first quarter of this year. That is 0.01 percent below the $3.0309 trillion recorded four years earlier.

Those are nominal figures, not adjusted for inflation (as are the figures in the chart below). On a real basis, the decline was 6.5 percent.

His chart:

 

When are we going to wise up, folks? We can—and must—do better.

Once poor, (almost) always poor

April 3, 2013 by davald

We don’t like to read that message. But the research is strongly suggesting that in America, especially, upward mobility is a fiction, now more than ever.

Yet we learn that it’s nearly impossible to break the trend by investing more in education, the way we do it in the U.S. Here, we put far more of our education dollars in college than at other levels. But if we do so with the expectation that more children will graduate with degrees then go on to earn decent livings, we would be mistaken. Indeed, we’re mostly wasting money.

These depressing thoughts come by way of an article in the New York Times. All this Race to the Top and No Child Left Behind hoopla, however well-intentioned, distracts us from focusing on the underlying problem: poor kids perform much worse in school than their richer peers, and the gap either stays constant or, more likely, grows over time.

So, where should we be spending our dollars? The research cited tells us to invest in early childhood programs. We’d get a far bigger bang for the buck.

American students from prosperous backgrounds scored on average 110 points higher on reading tests than disadvantaged students, about the same disparity that exists between the average scores in the United States and Tunisia. It is perhaps the main reason income inequality in the United States is passed down the generations at a much higher rate than in most advanced nations.

That’s a scandal, considering how much the government spends on education: about 5.5 percent of the nation’s economic output in total, from preschool through college.

And it suggests that the angry, worried debate over how to improve the nation’s mediocre education — pitting the teachers’ unions and the advocates of more money for public schools against the champions of school vouchers and standardized tests — is missing the most important part: infants and toddlers.

Research by Mr. [James] Heckman and others confirms that investment in the early education of disadvantaged children pays extremely high returns down the road. It improves not only their cognitive abilities but also crucial behavioral traits like sociability, motivation and self-esteem.

We can, and should, do better.
