Why inequality?

August 16, 2013 by davald

Few doubt that wealth is more heavily concentrated in the top rungs of the population than amongst the Rest of Us. What accounts for the unequal distribution?

Some have suggested that it’s technology or globalization or some combination. If that were true, then we would expect to see heightened inequality in other industrial democracies. But that’s not the case, for the most part.

A recent paper suggests four factors contributing to maldistribution:

The first is tax policy: top tax rates have moved in the opposite direction from top pre-tax income shares. The second factor is a richer view of the labor market, where we have contrasted the standard supply-side model with the alternative possibility that there may have been changes to bargaining power and greater individualization of pay. Tax cuts may have led managerial ener- gies to be diverted to increasing their remuneration at the expense of enterprise growth and employment. The third factor is capital income. In Europe—but less so in the United States—private wealth (relative to national income) has followed a spectacular U-shaped path over time, and inherited wealth may be making a return, implying that inheritance and capital income taxation will become again central policy tools for curbing inequality. The final, little-investigated, element is the correlation between earned income and capital income, which have become more closely associated in the United States.

The authors include a chart that correlates marginal tax rates and percentage of income going to the top one percent.

The second factor needs parsing. I believe the authors are referring to declining union participation rates, which would erode worker’s power vis-a-vis management’s. We might say that employees are mostly on their own these days, unable, if not also unwilling, to rely on collective bargaining.

Inherited wealth is playing a larger role more recently, allowing the rich to get richer. Also, and to the final factor, owning assets (including financial instruments) correlates highly with income, especially in the United States.

Here’s income.

Here’s capital.

In the U.S. we have a fiscal problem. Republicans would have us believe that the government simply spends too much. But the more obvious explanation is declining revenues. As the rich have gotten richer, they’re paying a smaller share of their income in taxes. So we not only increase public debt we can’t afford to pay for public services, including transportation and schools.

How we learn

August 15, 2013 by davald

Consider two hypotheses about learning. In one, students’ brains are assumed to be more or less like blank slates. It is the educator’s function to deposit, fill, transfer, or whatever metaphor one prefers to convey that knowledge is imparted outside in. In the second hypothesis, it is assumed that we humans learn when we are invested in subject matter. That is, we are interested and, therefore, intrinsically motivated to acquire knowledge from multiple sources. Inquiry dominates and is open ended.

If you subscribe to the first hypothesis chances are you favor the type of educational reform that establishes a curriculum that all students master. Or, at least, that would be the ideal. Let’s call the curriculum Common Core Standards. Its elements would be fashioned by “experts.” Children would be evaluated on the basis of how much of the curriculum they’ve ingested. Likewise, teachers would be held “accountable” for their students’ learning, defined as the extent of mastering the standards. The process and structure are an engineer’s delight, with focus on inputs and outputs.

But is this the way we learn? If not, then widespread adoption of the Common Core Standards could be a disaster. At a minimum, it would only serve to accentuate the disparities in educational achievement, which correlate heavily with parents’ socioeconomic status.

Suppose that we learn in a way briefly described in the second hypothesis. The learning process is natural, stemming from innate curiosity about the world. (I think of the rapid development of my five-month-old grandson, who delights in discovering objects around him, including my fingers and nose.) Parents, the initial and most important educators, would read to their young children, providing a language-rich environment in which acquiring and comprehending text just happens with increasing exposure.

If learning is interest-driven, then schools would build on the child’s early years at home. Classrooms would be places to explore. Teachers, we should imagine, would be experts in a wide variety of subjects, including the psychology of learning. Rather than imposing knowledge, as in the first hypothesis, they would elicit understanding from the child, who is, as I said above, intrinsically motivated. Since the classroom teacher is in the best position to assess the strengths and weaknesses of her individual pupils, she would have the authority and responsibility to develop curricula tailored to her unique assortment of children.

What I am describing here is a very Dewey-like approach. As it happens, the work of John Dewey lives on and flourishes in Finland, whose education system ranks at or near the top of all countries. In Finland, children are not bombarded with standardized tests or one-size-fits-all curricula. Teachers spend almost as much time planning, developing, and evaluating as they do actually “teaching.” Almost all students receive some form of assistance, depending on their individual circumstances.

But, this is America, and we prefer to do things our way, however wrong. So, a coalition of faux liberals and deep pockets is foisting Common Core Standards on the nation’s public schools. The good news is that resistance is brewing, though among strange bedfellows: both the Tea Party and leftists oppose the imposition.

Motoko Rich, writing for the New York Times:

The Common Core, a set of standards for kindergarten through high school that has been ardently supported by the Obama administration and many business leaders and state legislatures, is facing growing opposition from both the right and the left even before it has been properly introduced into classrooms.

Unfortunately, whether or not Common Core fails to gain a foothold in our schools, genuine reform—in the manner of Finland’s—will never be adopted. That would take intelligent leadership devoid of monied interests.

The Boehner rule [u]

July 24, 2013 by davald

House speaker John Boehner has a “hearing problem,” according to David Firestone. Writing for the New York Times, Firestone tells us that Ben Bernanke’s clear message to Congress, that its fiscal austerity policies prevent economic recovery, became garbled on the way to Boehner’s brain.

At a news conference this morning, Mr. Boehner was asked about Mr. Bernanke’s comments, and here’s what he said:

“Well, I think what the chairman’s referring to is the fact that we’ve got spending that continues to be out of control, a red tape that’s out of control.”

Another budget deadline looms. If Congress refuses to raise the debt ceiling by October 1, the government faces another partial shutdown.

“We’re not going to raise the debt ceiling without real cuts in spending,” he said. “It’s as simple as that.” He added: “I believe that the so-called Boehner rule is the right formula for getting that done.”

Oh, and the Republicans continue to insist that Obamcare be repealed and social services gutted. The Times:

On Tuesday, a House Appropriations subcommittee formally drafted legislation that would cut the Environmental Protection Agency’s budget by 34 percent and eliminate his newly announced greenhouse gas regulations. The bill cuts financing for the national endowments for the arts and the humanities in half and the Fish and Wildlife Service by 27 percent.

For the fiscal year that begins Oct. 1, Mr. Obama requested nearly $3 billion for renewable energy and energy efficiency programs — a mainstay of his economic agenda since he was first elected. The House approved $826 million. Senate Democrats want to give $380 million to ARPA-E, an advanced research program for energy. The House allocated $70 million.

A House bill to finance labor and health programs, expected to be unveiled Wednesday, makes good on Republican threats to eliminate the Corporation for Public Broadcasting. The labor and health measure — for years the most contentious spending bill — will protect some of the White House’s priorities, like Head Start, special education and the National Institutes of Health, but to do so education grants for poor students will be cut by 16 percent and the Labor Department by 13 percent, according to House Republican aides.

It would seem that the only way to change things for the better in this country is to vote the GOP out of office. Then comes step number two, which is to weed out “moderate” Democrats, that strange breed of politicians exemplified by Max Baucus at the federal level and the so-called ‘Road Kill Caucus’ in Washington state.

UPDATE (July 24, 2013):

All of this clearly upsets Paul Krugman. He writes in his blog this morning:

Still, how can the GOP be acting this crazy again? This time around, it’s hard to see Obama caving in. So why the brinksmanship?

Well, my guess is that despite being drenched in reality-repellent, Republicans are beginning to suspect an inconvenient truth: Obamacare is not going to be a self-destroying train wreck. Instead, it’s going to work — not perfectly, not as well as it should, but well enough to help far more people than it hurts. And if that’s how it turns out, it will be irreversible. So here comes a last-ditch effort to stop it, at all costs.

But think about that for a moment: the cause for which the GOP is willing to go to the brink, breaking all political norms, threatening the US and world economies with incalculable damage, is the cause of preventing people with preexisting conditions and/or low incomes from getting health insurance. Apparently, the prospect that their fellow citizens might receive this help is so horrifying that nothing else matters.

Spatial

July 16, 2013 by davald

We sometimes hear the expression, “She’s spatial.” This can be viewed as a good thing, but, on occasion, the adjective can disparage, since we place so much emphasis on “quantitative skills,” the kind measured in achievement tests like the SAT.

I recall receiving the same label as an elementary student. I did well on those tests that required the ability to virtually manipulate objects or to see how different forms found expression in alternative dimensions. Whatever. While I did okay on the standardized tests, my scores did not catapult me into full-ride scholarships at prestigious universities, though throwing a baseball past most hitters did.

As it happens, students that show an aptitude for spatial reasoning tend to me more creative as adults. I missed the memo. Then again, there are exceptions, and I must be one of them. The New York Times:

A gift for spatial reasoning — the kind that may inspire an imaginative child to dismantle a clock or the family refrigerator — may be a greater predictor of future creativity or innovation than math or verbal skills, particularly in math, science and related fields, according to a study published Monday in the journal Psychological Science.

Buy your kid some Legos. You’ll be doing him or her a favor.

Meddling Gates

June 19, 2013 by davald

These days whenever you read or hear about education reform you’ll likely notice that the Gates Foundation is busy behind the scenes. So I was not surprised to learn that the report card on teacher-education programs, pretty dismal in itself, received funding from the foundation, as the Seattle Times reports.

Readers of this blog are familiar with my opinions on the state of teacher training in America. It sucks, as compared with Finland’s, for example. The differences are stark, beginning with selectivity. Finland’s political and educational leaders determined a couple of decades ago that they would reverse the country’s then-mediocre schools by choosing only the best and the brightest to enter what became a very rigorous training curriculum.

The report by the National Council on Teacher Quality (NCTQ) reached an obvious conclusion, that most of our teacher-ed schools set a low bar for acceptance, then poorly prepare would-be teachers for the challenges they will face in the classroom. Well, duh.

Yet critics of the report were quick to pounce. From the Times:

Washington State University’s program was one of only 104 in the nation that made NCTQ’s honor roll for quality — earning three stars out of four.

While he was pleased by the ranking, WSU education dean Mike Trevisan said the evidence is thin that the programs cited as best or worst belong in those categories.

“This is a group that has a political agenda,” he cautioned, describing NCTQ as wanting “market forces to weed out weaker programs … and to increase the strength of programs that meet their expectations.”

In America money infects all. Let’s consider the recent high school graduate who must choose an academic program. Even tuition at public universities has quickly outpaced inflation, so that the typical college student departs with a huge debt, on average $25,000. Since teaching draws smaller salaries than other professions, it can take decades to retire the loan. Becoming a lawyer, doctor, or engineer promises higher income and a shorter repayment schedule. Moreover, teaching is hardly the noble calling it may have been, since, as the Everett Herald points out in its coverage of the report, most teacher-ed programs are:

…”churning out first-year teachers with classroom management skills and content knowledge inadequate to thrive in classrooms” with an ever-increasing diversity of ethnic and socioeconomic students, the report’s authors wrote.

In Finland, education is free to students, up to and including graduate school. Though the Nordic country spends less money on its public system, it graduates highly capable teachers who become part of a profession that enjoys as much, if not more, status than those of doctors and lawyers.

Of greater importance, perhaps, is that Finland eschews high-stakes testing like Washington state’s Measurement of Student Performance, formerly called ‘the WASL.’ Nor does it set schools up in a grand competition. The Gates Foundation, on the other hand, is about Arne Duncan’s Race to the Top and the Common Core Standards. All students will be expected to learn the same things, deemed important by others, including the Gates Foundation. U.S. teacher-education programs, then, receive higher marks if they focus on the Standards in training teachers. 

To conclude, I agree with the report’s obvious assessment on teacher-ed mediocrity. Where I differ is that Gates et al. aren’t really all that serious about creating genuine professionals and what that entails, from high reputation and generous financial support for education to teacher-based curricula and student evaluations—conspicuously lacking in the one-size-fits-all approach of Common Core and Race to the Top.

By the way, save for his commendable efforts to eradicate diseases, any initiative that receives financial support from the Gates Foundation should be viewed with suspicion. Somebody there is up to no good.

Get an education

June 14, 2013 by davald

Or, perhaps not.

A couple of op-ed columns in this morning’s New York Times, one by David Brooks the other by Paul Krugman, address the plight of workers, albeit from completely different perspectives. Brooks bemoans the changing mores of the wealthy, suggesting that “seemliness” has given way to more conspicuous, in-your-face consumption. Krugman invokes the Luddites, suggesting that most of us are similarly situated in the modern economy.

Brooks begins with a story of Walter Judd, who would serve 20 years in Congress after becoming a doctor. Denied financial assistance by his father, Judd was able to work his way through college cleaning dishes for the local Y.M.C.A. That, of course, would be impossible to pull off today, since tuition costs greatly exceed the wages of washing. (Note: I was able to pay for a university education by cleaning the slopes of the football stadium and washing pots and pans at a fraternity. But that was a long time ago.) After acknowledging the remarkableness of Judd’s story, Brooks then writes, curiously:

More important, that people then were more likely to assume that jobs at the bottom of the status ladder were ennobling and that jobs at the top were morally perilous. That is to say, the moral status system was likely to be the inverse of the worldly status system. The working classes were self-controlled, while the rich and the professionals could get away with things.

I think that Brooks is too quick to generalize from an inference about Judd and washing dishes. First off, it seems that Judd had little choice but to find work if he was to complete college. Second, it’s clear that Judd was not content to stay in the kitchen; he aspired to become something much more, including a national political figure. That Judd parlayed his physician’s degree into missionary work suggests the importance of religion in his life, which is one of Brooks’s principal themes. The presumed moral precepts that may have governed or guided Judd’s career, from college to Congress, had their roots in the Bible, Brooks avers. Maybe. From this, Brooks laments:

Over the years, religion has played a less dominant role in public culture.

It’s quite possible that the rich don’t bother going to church any more, if they ever did, but it’s quite a stretch, I think, to say that “religion has played a less dominant role in public culture.” Has he forgotten about the rabid Republicans of the Christian right, who appear to have gained a foothold in the party, essentially determining the outcomes of primary elections?

One thing is clear, however. There can be no more Walter Judds, or yours truly, for that matter. Religion or no, part-time menial labor cannot purchase a college degree. Which brings me to Krugman.

The Princeton professor wonders if we have been forced into neo-Luddite situations. He quotes from the originals:

“How are those men, thus thrown out of employ to provide for their families?” asked the petitioners. “And what are they to put their children apprentice to?”

Today’s youth are urged to go to college to gain new or different abilities. But at what cost? As Brooks points out, the path of Walter Judd is no longer available. Instead, as Krugman reminds us:

…we go deep into debt to acquire the skills we’re told we need, only to learn that the economy no longer wants those skills…

Krugman’s prescription will surely be denounced by the religion-soaked conservatives.

Education, then, is no longer the answer to rising inequality, if it ever was (which I doubt).

So what is the answer? If the picture I’ve drawn is at all right, the only way we could have anything resembling a middle-class society — a society in which ordinary citizens have a reasonable assurance of maintaining a decent life as long as they work hard and play by the rules — would be by having a strong social safety net, one that guarantees not just health care but a minimum income, too. And with an ever-rising share of income going to capital rather than labor, that safety net would have to be paid for to an important extent via taxes on profits and/or investment income.

Not going to happen.
