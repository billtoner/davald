A bit more on Dewey

August 27, 2012 by davald

In my reading of Finnish Lessons I admit to being taken aback by the reference to John Dewey. To wit, the wholesale reforms of Finland’s education system were based on the philosophy of Mr. Dewey, who died from pneumonia on June 1, 1952, at the age of 92. That was 60 years ago, and yet his teachings evidently ignited or at least informed the so-called ‘Finnish Way.’

My shelves used to contain a lot more books, which became a burden over years’ worth of moving place to place. I’m guessing that my several books by Dewey wound up on local library shelves. But I retained a biography: John Dewey and American Democracy, by Robert B. Westbrook.

That title is important in understanding Dewey’s philosophy, in general, and his views on education, in particular. For Dewey, democracy and education went hand in hand. In his early years he had hoped with many progressives that education could serve as a catalyst for a new and improved democracy, one that valued each individual and allowed for the full development of all. Later, he became increasingly frustrated with political culture and the failure to realize his ideals. Democracy in America, he concluded, had become co-opted by a privileged few at the expense of the many.

My posts on Finland have emphasized the importance of situating educational reform within a larger societal context. The Finns began their reform effort with attitudes and aspirations antithetical to the typical American’s. First and foremost, Finns are dedicated to a strong welfare state, believing that, while success is partly determined by individual initiative and effort, where one lands on the socioeconomic ladder has much to do with fortuity.

That starting point, I suspect, helps explain why and how Finland succeeded in revamping its educational system while we Americans behave cluelessly. Our predispositions, our prejudices, and our biases combine to thwart genuine reform, at least in the Finnish manner.

We can also appreciate why Dewey grew pessimistic over time about education’s principal role in transforming society and government. He realized that the education system, along with nearly everything else, had come under the control of elites who would stop at nothing to prevent a genuine democracy. Here’s a excerpt from Westbrook’s biography:

Students spent much of their time and energy accumulating, memorizing, and largely forgetting a mass of disconnected information and acquiring mechanical skills. Little attempt had been made to change this situation, and as a result “too large a part of our citizens has left our schools without power of critical discrimination, at the mercy of special propaganda, and drifting from one plan and scheme to another according to the loudest clamor of the moment.”

Schools, he believed, were being enlisted to promote special interests, those defined by oligarchs (my term). Westbrook:

Dewey agreed that much of the education in American schools was little more than indoctrination, “especially with reference to narrow nationalism under the name of patriotism, and with reference to the dominant economic regime.”

Finland, as we’ve learned, places the highest priority on selecting and developing the best and the brightest teachers. That was certainly Dewey’s view, and he often railed against the plight of America’s teachers. Westbrook:

School boards as the representatives of those dominant interests “regard themselves after the analogy of private employers of labor and the teaching staff as their hired men and women.”  Teachers had very little control over their work. Administrators made out the course of study, prepared syllabuses for instruction, and established methods of teaching. The teacher simply took orders…Teachers, Dewey argued…, should recognize that they were as much “workers” as farmers and factory laborers, and as such were subject to the control of “the small and powerful class that is economically privileged.”

In the end for Dewey, when it came to democratic reform, it was all hands on deck, including public education. However, America is a very large, perhaps unmanageable country. That its federal government works at all may be judged a miracle. That it works less and less for the Rest of Us and more so for the One-percenters is as predictable as it may be inevitable. Perhaps meaningful reform of any aspect of society, including education, can be accomplished only by carving out the whole into parts. Begin where you’re planted, I guess.

The dark side

August 27, 2012 by davald

I use technology as a tool to accomplish things like writing this blog. I do a fair amount of spreadsheets and some graphics. Of course, I use the Internet constantly in search of information and opinion. Since I’m old enough to appreciate a slide rule and an Olivetti typewriter, the emergence of personal technology impresses the hell out of me. I have more computing power on my iPhone than the IBM 360 that I used back in the 60s.

As regular readers of this blog know, I’m also in league with Apple, Inc., having acquired my first Macintosh in the mid-80s. What struck me about that product and its successors were its ease of use, simplicity of design, and functionality—Cupertino’s products “just work.”

As readers also know, my wife teaches public school. Her particular district switched from Apple to Windows about 20 years ago. I predicted at the time that the school district would incur mounting support costs, integration nightmares, and frustrated users. I was right, of course. Cash-strapped administrators are enticed by the promise of cheap, then forever forced to suffer the consequences of their shortsightedness.

Meanwhile, Apple continues to grow, both technologically and financially. For some time now it has reigned as the world’s most valuable company. Its products sell like no others, reaping inestimable returns for the company and its millions of customers.

But for those school districts that switched to the dark side not only have they become blind to Apple’s innovation, they have acquired technological albatrosses that consume precious time and dollars best spent on education. All of this came into immediate relief on a recent visit to my wife’s school.

She had been told on several occasions that iPads would be incorporated into the classroom. As an avid iPad user, I can attest to the power and creative potential of the device. For teachers, an iPad linked to an Apple TV connected to a widescreen monitor spells presentation success. Everything appearing on the iPad can be simultaneously mirrored on the television. The teacher can be anywhere in the room casting images while free to assist her pupils individually.

During that visit I looked for iPads. Nada. What I discovered, to my chagrin, were dozens of Windows machines, tiny Thinkpads. Even to a casual observer these devices represent industrial design from hell. There are so many buttons and keys and ports that serve to overwhelm rather than invite. Then there is the red felt ball. Ghastly, I say. Kids are supposed to use these things?

When I returned home I looked up the Thinkpad. It’s made now by Lenova. It comes in a gazillion different varieties, recalling the tyranny of choice.

Both Steve Jobs and Tim Cook attribute much of Apple’s success to just saying no. There are so many brilliant ideas awaiting tangible expression. But Apple’s CEOs understand something their competitors completely fail to grasp: endless features and myriad design options introduce complexity that subtracts from those qualities ingrained in Apple’s products but so lacking elsewhere.

That said, I get geek. I appreciate the lure of granularity, of digging down into the infinite variables of the operating system and software applications. Why, you can do this and that and don’t forget about x, y, and z. Using a Wintel machine presents the user with endless customization.

However, not everyone is a geek. Indeed, most of us have no need whatsoever for variation. We prefer usability above all. And usability demands simplicity in those tasks we do all the time, every day.

Should you visit an Apple store, go to the section displaying iPads on short-legged tables. There you will find several small children poking and swiping different applications, producing oohs and aahs from kids and parents alike. Should they be fortunate enough to have one in their homes, children will graduate to ever more sophisticated programs and higher levels of learning. If their schools should have them in abundance, knowledge limits cease to exist. And if their teachers have them and employ them extensively in the manner suggested above, the education experience improves dramatically for all.

But that experience is being denied teachers and pupils at my wife’s school. They are stuck with complicated devices, too few in number, with an antiquated projector system attached to a dark-side machine. A pity.

Human capital

August 25, 2012 by davald

Capital, as we know, belongs to a privileged few, the Haves and Have-mores—otherwise known as the One-percenters. The Rest of Us scramble and struggle to make ends meet, while being forced to constantly look up to rich men’s butts. But, please, we don’t have to like the emanating shit.

Charles Blow, writing for the New York Times, tells us that “America is in trouble.” He’s thinking about the future, and at present course, that future belongs to China and India. Why? Because those nations (and others, especially central and northern European) are investing heavily in their children’s education and well-being, while America is content to let most of our young people do without. The privileged few made it, then pulled up the ladder of success, relegating the Have-nots to lives of misery and hopelessness. That we long ago surrendered to this perverse order and now embrace it evinces a Stockholm Syndrome second to none.

So I return to education. Blow cites the results of a recent study (pdf) that compares and contrasts investments made by different countries in their children’s present and future. America, as I said above and elsewhere, dropped the ball, which it had only loosely carried for generations. We shortchange our children and thus their and, collectively, our future, whereas China, India, and the Nordic countries, in particular, understand that enhancing human capital makes all the difference—across numerous fronts, including international competitiveness.

Here’s an excerpt from that study that strikes a familiar note with readers:

A consequence of unequal education funding is that the least experienced and prepared teachers receive the lowest pay and teach the most disadvantaged students. And the teaching corps itself is weak. America’s teachers have strong academic credentials, yet with no improvement on national or international student assessment in decades it’s clear that many teachers struggle to help their students learn.

This may be due, in part to the fact that high school students who choose to enter undergraduate programs for education have SAT scores on average in the bottom third of all students tested. That stands in sharp contrast to nations with impressive student results, such as Finland, which successfully recruit teachers from their top high school graduates.

The report reaffirms upfront the importance of teacher quality in properly reforming America’s education system. Despite the emphasis of No Child Left Behind on mandating that only “highly qualified” teachers teach, there has been no significant increase in students’ academic performance. We might conclude, correctly, I believe, that we still don’t get it when it comes to selecting then preparing our nation’s best and brightest educators. I submit, based on the Finnish experience, that focusing on this crucial ingredient and acting accordingly will enhance human capital.

Sadly, not only do we fail at delivering the best teachers to our public school classrooms, we deprive our children of those social and economic benefits enjoyed by their counterparts in Finland, Sweden, et al. Worse, America is trending toward increased privatization of education, marked by charter schools and class-based segregation—all in the name of competition and choice. The report:

Economists studying the root causes of economic growth increasingly stress that investments in early childhood development and learning are especially critical for the human capital development needed in a strong, competitive economy. That’s because prenatal care and experiences from birth through early childhood affect children’s physical and brain development—and therefore the cognitive, social, and emotional development that will carry them through their lives.

Though we bang our “We’re Number One” drum loudly and repeatedly, with no evident reason, we ignore a glaring societal defect: too many of us are poor. Nor do we seem at all concerned about diminishing poverty. Once poor in America you’ll likely stay that way; and the same holds if you’re born rich. How do we stack up against other countries on the inconvenient poverty metric (OECD, 2005 data)?

All of the above countries, including the U.S., of course, have market economies, whose outcomes relegate substantial portions of their populations to poverty. That’s before taxes and transfers. The Nordic countries, especially, re-distribute national wealth to their “losers,” in the form of economic security, education, and social welfare benefits. We, on the other hand, take less from the rich, the “job creators” (not), therefore depriving everyone else of a decent life.

John Lennon imagined a better world. In America, that’s all we can do—evidently.

What you won’t see in Finland

August 23, 2012 by davald

Here is the cover of the Summer 2012 issue of NEA Today, a publication of the National Education Association:

For those who just stepped off the bus after a generations-long journey, teachers and their unions are under constant attack, which is nothing new, actually. Today, however, the barrage is truly bipartisan, with both Obama and Republicans preaching radical reform and especially teacher-accountability.

It is simply assumed that the present public education system sucks. Too many students flunk standardized tests designed to either leave no child left behind or fuel a race to the top. Whom shall we blame? The teachers, of course.

Now everyone who can read this went to school. No doubt the reader spent many years in classrooms ostensibly learning to read, write, and gain some proficiency in math and science. You may have also ingested a thing or two about history or how to incorporate perspective in drawing. All that seat time confers on the reader—and nearly every politician—the expertise to rant and rail about what goes on in our schools. In particular, everyone thinks they know not just what to teach but how.

It’s no wonder that teachers, like Rodney Dangerfield, get no respect. Even the apparently best and brightest educators go about their careers as if their principals “couldn’t care less,” in the words of one disaffected teacher. Parents are even worse.

As the title of this post suggests, the NEA headline wouldn’t appear in Finland, if Finnish Lessons is credible. Finland’s teachers need not be concerned with protection, since they are not under attack. Indeed, as Diane Ravitch has observed, those who teach in Finland enjoy as much prestige and respect, if not more, than that country’s doctors, lawyers, and other professionals. I should reprise her remarks:

First, Finland has one of the highest-performing school systems in the world, as measured by the Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA), which assesses reading, mathematical literacy, and scientific literacy of fifteen-year-old students in all thirty-four nations of the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), including the United States. Unlike our domestic tests, there are no consequences attached to the tests administered by the PISA. No individual or school learns its score. No one is rewarded or punished because of these tests. No one can prepare for them, nor is there any incentive to cheat.

Second, from an American perspective, Finland is an alternative universe. It rejects all of the “reforms” currently popular in the United States, such as testing, charter schools, vouchers, merit pay, competition, and evaluating teachers in relation to the test scores of their students.

Third, among the OECD nations, Finnish schools have the least variation in quality, meaning that they come closest to achieving equality of educational opportunity—an American ideal.

Fourth, Finland borrowed many of its most valued ideas from the United States, such as equality of educational opportunity, individualized instruction, portfolio assessment, and cooperative learning. Most of its borrowing derives from the work of the philosopher John Dewey.

The logic driving repetitive commercials is that sooner or later the message will register with a few viewers, enough, I guess, to compel them to buy a vehicle or, better yet, a particular one. Here, I preach the Finnish Way.

Repeat.

Finnish lessons

August 22, 2012 by davald

My post “After vs. before” generated numerous comments. I should reply to a few.

One commenter suggested that Finland is a “monoracial” society, more homogeneous than America’s. Along this line another commenter assumed that Finland’s largely white population possessed a higher IQ than people of color, both here and in Finland. (I won’t touch this, by the way.)

In his book Finnish Lessons, Pasi Sahlberg addresses Finland’s homogeneity. It’s changing. Ever since Finland joined the Eurozone the number of foreign-born residents has skyrocketed, as the chart below illustrates (data from Sahlberg):

 

In 2010, 248,135 of Finland’s residents were foreign-born, accounting for 4.7 percent of the country’s total population. Sahlberg tells us that only a small percentage of this cohort obtain formal citizenship given the government’s requirement that a candidate must be “proficient” in one of Finland’s three domestic languages: Finnish, Swedish, and Sami. As he says, very few people outside of Scandinavia speak them.

Nevertheless, despite the increase in immigrants, Sahlberg emphasizes that Finland maintains its commitment to educate all. Here’s Sahlberg:

The Finnish education system follows the principle of inclusiveness regarding the treatment of students with differing characteristics and needs. Students are placed in regular schools unless there is a specific reason to do otherwise. Therefore, in a typical Finnish classroom, one finds teachers teaching different abilities, interests, and ethnicities, often with the help of assistant teachers. The increased diversity in Finnish schools also suggests that variance in student performance within schools may increase. Cultural heterogeneity in Finnish society would suggest that variance in student learning among schools may become wider. However…a very high overall student performance in science, mathematics, and reading is evenly distributed throughout schools across Finland [pg. 69].

To be sure, Finland’s test scores declined from 2006 to 2009, though only slightly, and they were still far above the OECD average. Could this be the result of increasing immigration? I can’t say. However, Sahlberg writes, “Immigrant students in Finland scored on average 50 points higher than their peers in other countries.”

We should return to Sahlberg’s essential point. Finnish schools were not always superior. Indeed, a principal motivator for reform was mediocre performance on international tests. Finland’s leaders deigned to change things. What they accomplished, in my judgment, is truly remarkable. That even foreign-born students improve academic performance after being taught in Finnish schools suggests that the country’s educational system deserves study by U.S. educational and political leaders.

Now there is one factor that adversely impacts both teaching and learning: poverty. Fortunately for Finns, there is very little of it in their country. That’s hardly the case in the U.S. Here’s a chart comparing poverty rates and scores on the 2009 math portion of PISA, the international test administered under the auspices of the OECD.

 

We’re number one in poverty, not so much in academics.

One commenter averred that the U.S. education system deserves more respect. After all, we set about educating an immigrant nation (though our predecessors relegated the native population to a life of misery and poverty). Children from dozens of disparate countries wound up in our public school classrooms. They spoke different languages and enjoyed different cultural traditions. Yet, many, if not most, received a more-than-adequate education, as manifest in the socio-economic attainment of individuals possessed of a broad variety of surnames.

The commenter focused on high school graduation rates, which have risen dramatically over time. He writes:

However, there are 193 countries represented in the UN, putting the United States High School graduation rate in the top 5.69% of all the nations that are members of the UN. That means 94.31% of the Earth’s countries have lower high school graduation rates.

I’ll assume his numbers are correct. Should we take comfort in the knowledge that American secondary students graduate at a higher rate than, say, Ethiopia’s or Haiti’s? Or would we prefer to compare ourselves to Canada or Denmark or Finland? We can even shuck the comparisons and ask, Do we not want out schools to be better than before?

Let me return to the homogeneity argument before closing. Do less diverse societies perform better on the international tests?

The data offer little to support an affirmative answer. Certainly most nations are both poor and homogeneous. I have in mind the countries of Africa and Central and South America. But let’s take a look at other Scandinavian countries, whose populations, unsurprisingly, are almost exclusively Nordic. If homogeneity explained academic success, then we would expect to find a correlation.

What we find is that, if we exclude Finland, there does not seem to be a significant relationship between the percentage of foreign-born and test scores.

That Finland is such a strong outlier suggests—are you ready?—a superior educational system.

Russell’s wisdom: why we fight

August 19, 2012 by davald

One essential primer is Bertrand Russell’s The Problems of Philosophy. Therein he provides a concise introduction to issues over which philosophers have long struggled. And if you have any familiarity with philosophy as an academic pursuit, you know that philosophers develop only tentative or provisional answers—such is the nature of the discipline. But if you’re patient, you’ll eventually uncover some kernels of wisdom, and Russell’s writings contain many.

For example, consider these words from the aforementioned book:

Material possessions can be taken by force and enjoyed by the robber. Spiritual possessions cannot be taken in this way. You may kill an artist or a thinker, but you cannot acquire his art or his thought. You may put a man to death because he loves his fellow-men, but you will not by so doing acquire the love which made his happiness. Force is impotent in such matters; it is only as regards material goods that it is effective. For this reason the men who believe in force are the men whose thoughts and desires are preoccupied with material goods.

Thus the conservative mind, since Republicans as a rule are obsessed with “material possessions.” What they have, they fear will be taken. What they lack, they strive to obtain—by almost any means. If they should rule, they will employ the nation’s considerable armaments to plunder the wealth of others. As for defending America’s fortunes, mostly owned by the privileged few, they will spare no expense.

They will not stop at the military or the police state. Their fear-cum-greed affects every fabric of our well-being.

In the hotly contested issue of educational reform, conservatives believe that students should learn those skills necessary to succeed in the acquisition of wealth or to tend to the care and feeding of the oligarchy. Critical thinking, the kind that would ultimately question status quo political arrangements, must be eliminated, since it poses a threat to the powerful. After all, the masses outnumber them.

Investing in the arts and music, except for the products of dead painters and composers, wastes dollars that should be earmarked for exploitation. Public dollars must never go toward self-expression or human development.*

On economic issues, merit determines all, and the rich possess a monopoly, since they made the rules and insist on punishing transgressors. At your workplace, you count for nothing, save for what you contribute to the wealth of your corporate benefactors. Drop any notion of unionism; Labor is evil, because it frustrates Capital’s unrelenting greed. Meanwhile, you are expected to work more hours and extract less from your effort.

 

 

Finally, the Rest of Us must stop worrying and learn to love:

  • decrepit school buildings
  • collapsing roads
  • urban ghettoes
  • ugly strip malls
  • pollution
  • global warming
  • no philosophy, no art, no music, no beauty (these are reserved for your masters, who are happy to pay dearly for them)
  • expensive and ineffective health care
  • mind-numbing TV reality shows
  • perpetual war
  • shit.

But take heart, your taxes are low.

Educational reform

August 19, 2012 by davald

The topic will not go away, nor should it. Educational reform, that is.

We value the education of children, whether for their own sake or in service to a greater good, which is often translated as “to compete in the global economy.” But whatever the reason, we would prefer all offspring to be “above average,” in a Lake Wobegon sense.

Today the New York Times‘ Frank Bruni places a soon-to-be-released movie in the broader context of what’s wrong with our schools and what can and should be done to improve them. The movie, which stars Maggie Gyllenhaal, pits an anxiously concerned mother against the tyrannical forces of entrenched mediocrity, aided and abetted by the recalcitrant teachers union. Don’t Back Down will surely contribute to the ever-widening chasm between what is and what needs to be, no doubt inflaming the passionate advocates for anything-but-public in education and elsewhere. Private charter schools. Yeah, that’s the ticket, despite no evidence that such institutions do any better than their public counterparts; they do worse, more often than not. But both political parties have thrown down the gauntlet: reform or else. And that bipartisan pressure undermines, if not altogether prevents, a reasonable conversation about the problems and their solution. Unions, I dare say, must be part of that conversation, should they escape persistent vilification.

Despite unswerving support from the NEA and the AFT, Barack Obama is no friend to either. Bruni:

Perhaps most striking are the rifts that have opened between teachers’ unions and Democrats, who had long been their allies. President Obama’s appointment of Arne Duncan as education secretary and the administration’s subsequent Race to the Top initiative weren’t exactly music to the unions’ ears.

Lost in the heated rhetorical skirmishes is the glaring fact that teachers unions in other countries have played key roles in reforming entire countries’ educational reform. As readers of this blog know, Finland is surely the most sterling example, almost all of whose teachers are represented by labor organizations.

But American unions have been forced into defensive positions, trying desperately to preserve teachers’ jobs and benefits against the constant, increasingly hysterical attacks from both Republicans and Democrats. Bruni:

“When did Norma Rae get to be the bad guy?” asks a union leader (Holly Hunter) in the movie. I don’t know, but that’s indeed the state of play when it comes to teachers’ unions, and it’s a dangerous one.

Finland, as I’ve noted previously, began that important conversation several decades ago. Its country’s leaders—drawn from the public, private, and political sectors—quickly agreed on a fundamental and decidedly obvious point: education is paramount, just as Washington state’s constitution declares. Okay, now what?

Here is where Finland took a road less taken, one that hardly exists in America. The primary focus of educational reform will be on teachers, ensuring that the country’s most gifted academic citizens enter the classroom fully prepared by the nation’s principal universities. Finnish leaders did not set about bashing educators or condemning unions or introducing choice and competition into the public school system. Instead, Finland selected the most talented, paid fully for their academic training, then, and only then, deployed them in the schools, where they were entrusted to autonomously develop curriculum, pedagogy, and evaluation methods.

What we have to understand from the Finnish experience, other than its wholesale departure from American attitudes and dispositions, is that it worked. I should also add that the Finns are a patient lot.

The reforms did not produce success overnight. They have been the product of stable and persistent strategies all based on a fundamental concept: education is paramount, and teachers will be the vehicle for achievement. Each decade of such constancy of purpose yielded better results, so that Finland now boasts the best education system in the world. And it’s all tuition-free.

If I were in charge of the NEA or the AFT I would turn the nation’s eyes across the pond and just over Sweden’s boarder. I would ask what passes for American leadership to suspend jingoism for a moment and open up to the possibility that another country may have the answer to what vexes us. Once I’d gotten leaders’ attention, I would ask my constituency, politely at first but forcefully and dogmatically if necessary, to embrace the notion that teachers must be the foundation for any successful reform initiative.

This is when things could get dicey. The uncomfortable truth about America’s teachers is that they are not drawn from our best-and-brightest pool. Yes, there are certainly exceptions, my wife for one. But for those with academic prowess, pursuing other professions becomes a no-brainer. Why spend four or more years in school, racking up huge financial debt, to become a poorly paid educator, and a reviled one at that? Far better for me to earn a medical or law degree, then recoup that educational investment and much more.

So, how would we go about enticing the intellectually gifted to embark on an educator’s career? Let me list a few possibilities:

  • states would fully fund the education of those preparing to be teachers, including graduate school
  • the selection process would be stiff, at least as stiff as for would-be doctors, lawyers, and engineers
  • teacher salaries would be higher than they are now and rise with longevity
  • once hired by schools, teachers would be fully entrusted with the education of children
  • there will be no high-stakes tests, save, perhaps, for one determining whether students advance to a college path or pursue an apprenticeship-cum-skills-based training
  • in the words of Parsi Sahlberg, the author of Finnish Lessons, the purpose of education, utterly foreign to American leaders, will be to: “Create a community of learners that provides the conditions that allow all young people to discover their talent.”
  • governments, fully committed to that purpose, would direct funds to facilitate its achievement; that means, among other things: ensuring that children receive basic support in terms of health and well-being; providing sufficient resources to help students struggling academically and socially; and staying the hell out of schools
  • abandon the entire teacher-evaluation edifice; after all, if the requisite strategies are in place to guarantee the highest caliber of teachers in the first place, trust them to do their jobs

Now, lest you think that education costs would mushroom under this scheme, consider this: Finland spends far less per pupil than does the U.S.

 

Finland has lessons to teach us. Don’t you think?

After vs. before

August 18, 2012 by davald

Finland, as I’ve written previously, completely revamped its education system. It did so for both obvious and more subtle reasons. First, its international test scores sucked. Second, Finnish leaders were anxious to create a more vibrant, competitive private sector, one it hoped would be spurred by increased educational performance. They succeeded, spectacularly so (source: Wikipedia).

But you won’t hear much about the Finnish reform movement. It is so un-American in both concept and execution. Moreover, even the author of Finnish Lessons, who himself played a prominent role in the revamping, realizes that educational reform of the Finnish kind cannot occur in a vacuum. He quotes from an external audit conducted by the Organization for Economic and Cooperative Development:

…it is hard to imagine how Finland’s educational success could be achieved or maintained without reference to the nation’s broader and commonly accepted system of distinctive social values that more individualistic and inequitable societies may find difficult to accept.*

The United States, as these pages have documented on numerous occasions, is an intensely “individualistic and inequitable” society. We have given new meaning to unfairness and inequality. So, it does not surprise me in the least that we cannot comprehend, let alone even acknowledge, that there is a different and far better way to reform our public schools.

In America, we do things insanely backwards, always focusing on after-the-fact approaches while expecting different outcomes with each wave of educational reform. No Child Left Behind and Race to the Top are two egregious examples, both of which attempt to change an entrenched system via “accountability.” It works in broad outlines like this:

Mary wants to be a teacher. She attends college, with an emphasis on education, of course. She graduates, receiving her teaching certificate. A school district hires her. Go forth and teach, she’s told, probably ill-equipped to do so without months if not years of constant struggle with disturbing child behavior and frustrating institutional pressures. Many of her colleagues will give up the grueling process. And no matter how well Mary believes she’s doing in the classroom, increasingly she will be judged by how well her students perform on standardized tests.

All teachers in America are under threat by those who presume to know best how to improve academic learning, though they may be furthest removed from the educational enterprise, what I call the crucial interface between teacher and pupil. New York City’s Mayor Bloomberg offers us the latest initiative, one designed to weed out under-performing teachers. Here’s the headline from this morning’s New York Times:

Many New York City Teachers Denied Tenure in Policy Shift

The accompanying article begins:

Nearly half of New York City teachers reaching the end of their probations were denied tenure this year, the Education Department said on Friday, marking the culmination of years of efforts toward Mayor Michael R. Bloomberg’s goal to end “tenure as we know it.”

To many of us, such measures, however draconian, seem reasonable. After all, very few of us are guaranteed employment security. We may think ourselves fortunate to land a job and even more so if we retain it over time. Why should teachers enjoy the same privilege as Supreme Court judges?

Yet, this is certainly not the Finnish way. Nor will Bloomberg’s harsh strategy improve academic performance. And the reason is as simple as it may appear impossible: successful reform is all about preparing teachers before they enter the classroom and not afterwards. But that’s not all.

What is the purpose of education? That should be first and foremost in any effort to change the system. Here is Sahlberg’s suggested answer:

Create a community of learners that provides the conditions that allow all young people to discover their talent.

Huh? No accountability? No high stakes testing? No rewards and punishment? No consequences for underperformance? Nope. Finland has none of the above.

To understand why requires a completely different mindset. The Finns have established a selection and training process that yields that only the best and the brightest teach their children. Once successful candidates enter the classroom they are judged professional, with all the prestige and respect accorded doctors and lawyers and architects, if not more so.

But as I mentioned above, the Finnish educational system exists within a broader context, one that is clearly and intensely social democratic. The Finns believe in equality and willingly tax themselves for generous public services. Again, so un-American. Oh, and I should add that nearly one hundred percent of Finnish teachers belong to a labor union, one that is part of the solution and not the problem.

Sahlberg:

The Finnish Way of educational change should be encouraging to those who have found the path of competition, choice, test-based accountability, and performance-based pay to be a dead end. The future of Finnish education described above can moreover offer an alternative means to customized learning. For the Finns, personalization is not about having students work independently at computer terminals. The Finnish Way is to tailor the needs of each child with flexible arrangements and different learning paths. Technology is not a substitute but merely a tool to complement interaction with teachers and fellow students.

As a countervailing force against the global educational-reform movement driving school systems around the world, the Finnish Way reveals that creative curricula, autonomous teachers, courageous leadership and high performance go together. The Finnish Way furthermore makes plain that collaboration, not conflict, with teacher unions leads to better results. The evidence is clear and so should be the road ahead. [pg. 144 of Finnish Lessons]

Ironically, the modern Finnish education system draws its intellectual foundations from an American philosopher, John Dewey. Fancy that. Sahlberg invited a U.S. professor, Seymour Sarason to visit Finnish schools and universities. This was a few years ago. At the end of his of his observations and interviews, Sarason told Sahlberg:

Why did you bring me here? Your school system to me looks very close to what John Dewey had in mind and what I’ve been writing about teaching and schools for the last three decades.

Arne Duncan, Mayor Bloomberg, G.W. Bush, Barack Obama, and a host of governors and other politicians offer nothing of value when it comes to reforming our schools. The solution, it seems, is on our library shelves under Dewey.

* Finnish Lessons: What can the world learn from educational change in Finland, by Pasi Sahlberg (p. 132)

New York’s Gini and human capital

August 1, 2012 by davald

New York, according to Wikipedia information, has the highest level of inequality among the 50 states. Its Gini index is 0.499; and the higher the number the greater the inequality. The nation as a whole has an index of 0.469. Utah has the lowest, at 0.419.

At first glance, New York’s high index makes intuitive sense. Not because its poor may be poorer than in the other states, but that its rich are likely much richer. Manhattan is home to Wall Street and the headquarters of major corporations whose CEOs count their incomes in millions of dollars. (Source for wage data is Bureau of Labor Statistics.)

The primary y-axis measures the Gini index; the secondary shows mean annual wages (orange line). New Yorkers, according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, enjoy a mean annual wage of $52,810, ranking just below citizens of Connecticut and Massachusetts.

As it happens, there is a mild positive correlation between inequality and mean wages (0.233). However, the graph also shows that a few states with higher-than-average incomes also have lower Gini indexes than the three richest states, although we could label Alaska an outlier, given its physical isolation from the contiguous states.

Yet, despite New York’s higher income (along with Connecticut’s and Massachusetts’s), the standard deviation across all states is only $5,160. And that’s what the orange line suggests, since there is not all that much variation across the continent. There are exceptions.

Mississippi, the poorest state, with a mean annual wage of $34,770, about 34 percent less than New York’s, also has a higher level of inequality, with a Gini index of 0.468. Alabama, Tennessee, and Louisiana are among the poorest states, and they all have higher Gini coefficients.

Let’s look at some New York occupations. First the big shots.

If we take the mean wages and multiply by the number in these occupations we get this chart:

The business and financial sector, mostly in Manhattan, represents $36.9 billion a year in salaries. That’s about 12 percent of Washington state’s total real GDP.

 

Teachers are my favorite workers, since I’m married to one. How do they fare in New York? The chart  below shows annual mean salaries.

 

There are almost 200,000 public school teachers in the state of New York, less than half the number of those employed in business and financial operations. I suspect that New York educators live quite modestly, by force rather than desire, though they make more than their Washington state counterparts, whose average salaries range from around $42,000 to $45,000 a year.

In a previous post I discussed briefly Finland’s approach to education and how that country emphasizes the development of human capital via their public schools. A kinder, gentler nation would follow Finland’s advice. We’d invest much more in training teachers before they enter the classroom and relegate the financial sector to performing boring stuff like extending credit and managing deposits. We know that good teachers make a difference while vainglorious Wall Streeters extract huge rents from the Rest of Us while jeopardizing the global economy through government-insured risk-taking.

Prioritize, America.
