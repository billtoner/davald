Educating China

February 5, 2012 by davald

My two children graduated from the University of Washington, with my daughter then acquiring three graduate degrees, including law, also from U.W. They both received good educations at an affordable cost, the virtues of a top-ranked public institution.

But times are changing. Faced with diminishing revenues, the legislature has been busy cutting the budget in order to balance it. The university has taken big hits from Olympia, compelling the regents to raise tuition. While still a bargain compared with Harvard or Yale, the higher tuition forces more students into debt, which can be staggering after four years or more. The state legislature has not kept pace with population growth, failing to expand existing or building new facilities. Thus, competition for admission has skyrocketed. With too few slots and too few dollars from the legislature, the university has recently opened its doors to Chinese students, who evidently have the means to pay treble the in-state tuition rate.

In 2006, according to the New York Times, just two percent of the student population were from foreign countries. This year the percentage is 18, and most of this cohort comes from China—who comprise 11 percent of the 5,800 freshmen.

Nationwide, higher education financing has undergone a profound shift in recent years, with many public institutions that used to get most of their financing from state governments now relying on tuition for more than half their budgets. But legislators and taxpayers still feel deep ownership of the state institutions created to serve homegrown students — and worry that something is awry when local high achievers, even valedictorians, are rejected by the campuses they have grown up aspiring to.

One does not have to be xenophobic to appreciate that Washington’s future economic health, not to mention the well-being of its citizens, is at risk if the state does not provide sufficient educational opportunities for its high school graduates. Already businesses complain that many U.S. applicants lack requisite skills.

Here’s Boeing’s CEO in a Northwestern University address:

So it is absolutely critical that we address the tightly related issue of education and the readiness of our workforce. I’m sure it’s no surprise to you that technology-based companies face an impending skills shortage. This is a global circumstance, by the way; no single nation can produce enough creativity, talent, or knowledge to meet today’s marketplace challenges alone. But the problem is growing acute in the United States, where many seasoned and skilled workers are close to retiring, and insufficient numbers of capable workers are being prepared to replace them. I emphasize “capable,” because in the United States today we face a skills shortage, not necessarily a labor shortage.

Ironic, it would be, if Boeing and other businesses began hiring China-born graduates of U.S. public universities.

A mole hill becomes a mountain

January 20, 2012 by davald

There was quite a bit of chatter following Apple’s big announcement yesterday. It was all about the company’s EULA (end-user license agreement) attached to its new application iBooks Author.

The application allows developers or really anyone to create content that can be read on an iPad using iBooks. But there’s a catch—perhaps. If you submit your creation to Apple for approval and sale through its online digital bookstore you cannot market your product elsewhere. Boo!—say early critics.

Apple makes iBooks Author available for free. Yet, it’s a very robust, professional application. (Click here to see a video of Apple’s event, and I think that you’ll appreciate my judgment.) If you submit to Apple, the company takes a cut—thirty percent. The highest price allowed is $14.99; you can price it lower, however. What you cannot do is to then try to sell the same content created with the iBooks Author to other retail outlets. Apple is exercising proprietary control. In exchange the developer gets a free content-creating tool and the opportunity to make some big bucks.

What critics overlook is that you can make your product available to anyone—provided they have an iPad and the free reader, iBooks. You just can’t charge for it. How does that work? You simply hit the “Export” button.

iBooks Author, which I’ve played with, will make it easy for classroom teachers, and their students, to produce curriculum materials, including text, animation, presentations, and video. (Again, watch the keynote to see what I mean.)

What will inhibit widespread adoption of this new ecosystem is the cost of an iPad, the cheapest of which is $500 retail. But I would not be surprised if Apple removes or at least reduces this obstacle.

Note: I found it interesting that the Seattle Times made no mention of yesterday’s Apple event. Is there a bias in favor of Microsoft at the paper? After all, they covered Redmonds’s earnings report. The Everett Herald printed coverage, including this damper:

Albert Greco, a professor of marketing at Fordham University in New York and a former high-school principal, said schools would need to buy iPads for its students if it were to replace printed books.

It wouldn’t work to let students who can afford to buy their own iPads use them in class with textbooks they buy themselves, alongside poorer students with printed books.

“The digital divide issue could be very embarrassing. Because if you don’t have the iPad, you can’t do the quiz, you don’t get instant feedback … that is an invitation for a lawsuit,” Greco said. “I would be shocked if any principal or superintendent would let that system go forward.”

Greco said hardback high-school textbooks cost an average of about $105, and a freshman might need five of them. However, they last for five years.

That means that even if an iPad were to last for five years in the hands of students, the e-books plus the iPad would cost more than the hardback textbooks.

We can do some back-of-the-envelope calculations. If the high school student needs five textbooks per year at $105 each, then over four years the school district is spending $2,100 on books. However, as Prof. Greco says, the books will last about five years. If true, then we should divide the $2,100 by five to get $420 per student for his or her high school career.

The drawback is that printed textbooks are heavy and unwieldy, deterring students from carrying them around and into the classroom. But the biggest problem has to do with currency, and I’m not talking about dollars here. I’m talking about the chronological relevance of the textbooks’ content. The world and our knowledge of it are changing very rapidly. A textbook, once issued, is already outmoded, whether it be about history or science.

What about the iPad option? First of all, the content can and will be upgraded frequently; updates will be pushed out to users via the Internet. As for price? If the device costs $500 and each textbook costs $15, then five textbooks would cost $75. Over four years the district would spend $300 per student on the digital books. The total over four years is $800, including the cost of the iPad.

So it would cost more to go the iPad route, by about $380 per student. If the high school has 1,000 students, that works out to be $380,000 a year more than the current system.

Suppose that Apple drops the price of its iPad 2 with the release of its new iPad 3, slated for presentation next month. To make the Apple option cost-competitive, Cupertino would have to charge school districts about $120 for the legacy device. Would Apple do this?

The company released its first iPhone at a price of around $300, with a two-year carrier contract. (There are three prices, depending on the size of RAM. The lowest is $200; the highest is $400. I’m using the middle tier for comparison purposes.) The next version was at the same price; and the next. This past year Apple introduced the 4S at $300. But it continues to manufacture and sell the two previous versions, the 4 and the 3S, at $99 and $0, respectively—again, with a two-year commitment.

Apple’s not in the business of losing money. It earns two-thirds of all smartphone profits with only 30% market share. I presume that Apple continues to earn profits on the 4 and 3S models. The component costs of the iPhone 4 are somewhat less than $190, according to this source. The carriers subsidize the costs via the contracts.

The iPad 2’s hardware costs are about $300, Wi-Fi only. It would lose money on each unit if it priced it at $120; it needs another $180 to break even, since there are no carrier subsidies involved.

But $300 is less than $500, and given the added benefits of portability, usability, and content timeliness, more districts would be able to afford the iPad option.

We’ll find out soon enough.

Not all that progressive

January 19, 2012 by davald

American Progress has done some arithmetic using publicly available sources. They produced this chart:

In 2010, according to the U.S. Census, the distribution of total income looked like this:

While this is hardly scientific, why not set the rate according to each quintile’s share of wealth? Or, we might take the average rate (in 2009 that was 17%) as a base, then set the marginal rate for all income above the average of adjusted gross income, which was $49,000 in 2009, for each quintile. Thus, if you were in the top quintile, your marginal rate on all income above $49,000 would be 50.2%.*

Several of the GOP presidential candidates have proposed tax reform strategies, including a flat tax on all income. The flat tax would certainly simplify matters, which is what Jerry Brown argued when he ran for the White House in 1992; he suggested 17%, as I recall, which happens to be the average tax in 2009 (see footnote). However, a flat tax yields hardship for the lower quintiles and a boon for higher-income individuals.

Suppose we assume a flat tax of 17% on all income. For someone making only $10,000, the tax obligation would be $1,700. If the income were $1,000,000, the tax would be $170,000. Under the existing tax structure, the rate is 10% on incomes below $17,000 (married with joint returns). For the millionaire, it’s a bit more complicated. I come up with a shade less than $320,000 that goes to the IRS, slightly different from the figure shown in the footnote, but nearly 90% more than under a flat tax.

After taxes, the person earning $10,000 has $9,000 left over. The millionaire still has $680,000 on which to live. Chances are the poorer individual spends every single penny of the $9,000; but the millionaire can pay all her expenses and still have money to save, invest, and play.

One may ask by what rationale should tax rates be progressive? (Or, in Ron Paul’s case, why should there be any federal taxes at all?) Good question. Here’s one answer:

The real justification for progressive taxation has nothing to do with compassion or fairness or funding the welfare roles. The rationale for progressive is the preservation of competitive markets in which people are not prevented from entering into a business and/or employment because of monopoly or oligarchical control by a few large “owners” of the productive means.  Nowhere is this more evident than in the “financial” sector.  It should be obvious that if corporate income in the billions is taxed much more heavily than corporate income in the millions then earnings per share will be better in smaller corporate entities. And if that is the case then we have a very elegant control on the size of corporations.

I would add to this that the situation that we’re in now, with increasing accumulation of wealth by one small sliver of society, leads ineluctably to mischief. The two great economic upheavals of the past 100 years followed huge increases in wealth by the One-percenters. They have so much money they literally don’t know what to do with it; so they start gambling. Yes, ladies and gentlemen, Mr. Keynes was right: Wall Street is nothing more than a casino. With so much money involved, their mistakes imperil the Rest of Us. We dodged a monumental bullet a couple of years ago. As bad as things are, they could have been much, much worse.

And one more thing, echoing Justice Brandeis: We can either have a democracy or concentrated wealth, but we can’t have both. We pretend to favor democracy. Yet we have allowed a tiny few to amass great fortunes. They operate by different rules than the Rest of Us, and those rules further enrich the powerful while eroding our ability to effect changes that benefit the many. Plutocracy wasn’t what the Founding Fathers had in mind. But that’s what we’ve got.

One way of limiting the power of the few is to tax the hell out of them, then put that money in service to the Rest of Us. Our schools are dilapidated and ill-supplied. The roads are in disrepair. We have no alternative transportation to speak of. Education is too costly. The planet is warming. (Though I write this in the midst of unusual snow showers.)

Time to resurrect genuine democracy. Except there’s all these damn Republicans in the way.

A tale of two states

January 11, 2012 by davald

I was fortunate enough to have grown up in California under governor Pat Brown and University of California president Clark Kerr. Education was free, well-funded, and available, even at the tertiary level. Sacramento seemed to have enough money to pay for good schools plus transportation and social services.

But, like Washington state, California allows citizens to write initiatives and place them on ballots. Unlike Washington, however, California’s successful initiatives cannot be repealed or modified by state government. Californians would eventually rue the day that they were given such power, because the halcyon days gave way to a string of crises that now render the state almost ungovernable. It began with the passage of Proposition 13.

With its economy booming (this in the decades of the 50s and 60s), housing prices began their steady climb along with a proliferation of sprawling homes and rising property taxes. Enough, the people said, passing Howard Jarvis’s ballot proposal. Property taxes would be capped at one percent of 1975 assessments, with increases limited to two percent per annum, as long as the owner remained in his house. This had the predictable effect of gradually reducing government revenues. Sacramento was forced to curtail public services.

While the reduction in property taxes would receive the bigger play, the more pernicious feature of Proposition 13 was to require a two-thirds vote of the state legislature to raise taxes. Sound familiar?

Over subsequent years, interest groups, including the teachers’ unions, persuaded the voters to pass a series of initiatives that compelled the legislature to prioritize funding. Fiscal obligations changed, but without new sources of revenue. Sacramento had very little financial discretion. So it robbed Peter to pay Paul.

The Great Recession took a major toll on California, resulting in massive budget shortfalls, government layoffs, and sharply reduced public services. Like Olympia, Sacramento could only cut to balance the budget.

California’s miserable experience is being replicated here in Washington. The one difference, as mentioned above, is that our state legislature can amend or repeal citizens’ initiatives after two years. Washington voters have repeatedly required a two-thirds majority to increase taxes, only to have the matter repealed two sessions later.

Washington is now under the super-majority restriction. Yet, it must balance its budget, which means only cutting is allowed.

However, the state’s constitution makes public education the legislature’s “paramount” duty. After Olympia severely cut funding of public education a group of citizens sued. A few weeks ago the state’s supreme court issued a unanimous opinion. It upheld the constitutional mandate and warned the legislature that it cannot further reduce the education budget. Moreover, the ruling (pdf) states:

  • The word “ample” in article IX, section 1 provides a broad constitutional guideline meaning fully, sufficient, and considerably more than just adequate.
  • Ample funding for basic education must be accomplished by means of dependable and regular tax sources.

The lead plaintiff in the successful lawsuit writes in a Seattle Times op-ed:

Legislators have a sworn duty to uphold that mandate. And they now have a Supreme Court decision that reaffirms their constitutional duty to amply fund K-12 education first. They must therefore take the K-12 budget off the table as they consider cuts this session, and start increasing public-school funding now in order to meet the 2018 deadline of amply funding public schools with “regular and dependable tax sources.”

“The court cannot idly stand by as the Legislature makes unfulfilled promises for reform,” the Supreme Court said in retaining jurisdiction to enforce its ruling.

What can the legislature do, given its inability to raise taxes? Should it stop funding services to the poor? What about transportation funding?*

Governor Gregoire has hinted at two strategies: Ask the voters to increase taxes to fund education (subject to a simple majority vote); petition the supreme court to advise on whether or not the constitutional mandate trumps the two-thirds majority provision.

People’s preferences are as contradictory as they are fickle. There are entire communities that repeatedly turn thumbs down on school levies, Marysville being one of them, even as they complain about the quality of education. My guess is that surveys would show that the general public supports education, but would balk when asked to pay for it. Thus, I don’t have much hope for the public’s agreeing to tax themselves further.

As for the supreme court’s rendering an opinion that would essentially nullify the two-thirds majority requirement when it comes to education funding, I can only guess. Could it carve out an exception for the state’s “paramount” duty? We’ll have to wait and see.

Meanwhile, Olympia faces on ongoing budget challenge: inadequate revenues to offset expenditures. It’s in a short session this year, with few expecting major initiatives to pass, given the need to make further cuts.

If I could play king for a day, I’d abolish the initiative process, establish a unicameral state parliament, the leader of which would serve as governor, and enact proportional representation. I believe that such a government could get things done and fairly quickly. Whether they act for good or ill would depend on whom we elect.

Just dreaming, of course.

Nevertheless, this is hardly adequate to the challenge. As the Times writes:

Gregoire’s proposal is modest compared to a recent report from a transportation task force she convened to examine the issue. The task force determined that state transportation needs in the next 10 years — including road, bridge and ferry projects — would cost up to $21 billion.

Good teachers

January 6, 2012 by davald

Like every reader of this post I attended school, though I skipped kindergarten, but not because I was brilliant; our family’s move occurred after the registration date. From grades 1 through 6 I had six different teachers. Since this was a very long time ago, I have only vague memories of them. All were female, with several newcomers joining veterans.

The one teacher I recall most fondly was Mrs. McKelvy, who taught sixth grade. She had a certain élan, an elegance that suggested to me that she belonged elsewhere, perhaps in fashion design or in the sitting rooms of high society. But she stood out from the rest as an educator, too. I attribute this to her ability to facilitate self-discovery among her students. She seemed to figure us out then exploit our varied interests and talents.

One rather strange classmate had long straight hair and loved horses, so much so that she imitated them in the classroom by galloping to and fro and neighing from time to time. Annoying. But she also loved to draw. Mrs. McKelvy encouraged her idiosyncrasies by first creating floor space, then providing butcher paper and crayons. And draw she did, rather elaborate yet realistic images of countless horses. I have no idea what happened to the horse-girl. However, I suspect that she found self-esteem and self-expression in at least one year of her schooling.

In my case, I must have shown an interest in music. So Mrs. McKelvy encouraged me to join the orchestra, as a string bass player. A couple of points here: I had no formal training in music, including note-reading; and, yes, we had an orchestra in elementary school! This was California in the golden days of education.

There was one other teacher, I think it was in third grade, on whom I had a crush. She was young and energetic, which helped. When she changed her last name via marriage about half way through, my little heart broke. Sad.

In high school there was Mr. Goltra, a graduate of Rutgers University in New Jersey. His head devoid of hair, he’d busily write formulas on the board, demonstrating trigonometry and elementary functions. My table mate and I would pound our fists and shout out, “Rutgers is proud!” Mr. Goltra beamed. He made math interesting, and I did reasonably well at it.

Now to my point: after spending so many years in the classroom I can remember fewer than three “good” teachers, from first grade through twelfth. And something else: there were few tests, and none induced fear and anxiety. How did I and hundreds of classmates learn without them?

I put scare quotes around good, because I have no idea if these three teachers added value to their students. And this seems to be the rage these days, “value-added teaching.” Several states have established criteria to grade teachers. The most controversial is test scores.  What makes a good teacher? Improved test scores. How does one improve test scores? With good teachers. Note the circularity.

Now comes a couple of researchers who believe they can demonstrate that good teachers have long lasting effects on their students. To wit, their graduates earn more money as adults. The New York Times:

Elementary- and middle-school teachers who help raise their students’ standardized-test scores seem to have a wide-ranging, lasting positive effect on those students’ lives beyond academics, including lower teenage-pregnancy rates and greater college matriculation and adult earnings, according to a new study that tracked 2.5 million students over 20 years.

Again, we’re back to a logical circle. If we define “good” teachers by their students’ test scores, then, it seems, to boost scores you’ll need good teachers. And how will you know if you have good teachers in the classroom? By their students’s test scores.

Here’s the tricky part. Aside from this definition, what are the ingredients of good teaching? What do good teachers do differently from their middling or poor colleagues? If we walked into a good teacher’s classroom, what might be the observable indicators of quality teaching?

On to an even stickier widget: Whatever qualities we might discern in good teachers, can they be taught to others? Could we create good teachers out of bad ones? Or, as one researcher suggests:

“The message is to fire people sooner rather than later,” Professor [John] Friedman said.

Ouch. Skip the training and go straight to the pink slip.

The New York Times previously asked the question: What makes a good teacher?

But what makes a good teacher? There have been many quests for the one essential trait, and they have all come up empty-handed. Among the factors that do not predict whether a teacher will succeed: a graduate-school degree, a high score on the SAT, an extroverted personality, politeness, confidence, warmth, enthusiasm and having passed the teacher-certification exam on the first try. When Bill Gates announced recently that his foundation was investing millions in a project to improve teaching quality in the United States, he added a rueful caveat. “Unfortunately, it seems the field doesn’t have a clear view of what characterizes good teaching,” Gates said. “I’m personally very curious.”

Aren’t we all?

Lacking an answer to the question are we left with the great sort? That is, school systems could track teachers over a number of years, collect and analyze data, then produce results similar to the aforementioned study. My guess is that there would be a normal distribution, with one tail of superior teachers and the other tail lousy teachers, with everyone else clumped around a mean—all determined by students’ test scores over time.

So, please tell me. How did I learn anything without all those tests? Or, maybe I’m dumber than a brick.

American “exceptionalism” debunked

January 5, 2012 by davald

Americans derive a perverse sense of pride in their self-delusion. They refuse to believe obvious truths (e.g., nearly anything scientific) while embracing falsehoods like angels.

Among the latter is the myth of social mobility. Against the evidence, too many of our citizens rationalize rank inequality of outcome because each of us has equal opportunity.

Along the way to our misguided notions we lazily accept the shibboleth that “socialist” Europe—with all of its taxes, generous public services, and history of feudalism—squelches the entrepreneurial spirit and denies inhabitants the chance to get ahead.

Well, it just ain’t so. As the New York Times headline tells us, “[It’s] Harder for Americans to Rise From the Lower Rungs.” Harder than whom?—you may ask. From the linked study (pdf):

Mobility is lower in the U.S. than in the U.K., where it is lower again compared to the Nordic countries. Persistence is greatest in the tails of the distributions and tends to be particularly high in the upper tails: though in the U.S. this is reversed with a particularly high likelihood that sons of the poorest fathers will remain in the lowest earnings quintile. This is a challenge to the popular notion of ’American exceptionalism’. The U.S. also differs from the Nordic countries in its very low likelihood that sons of the highest earners will show downward ’long- distance’ mobility into the lowest earnings quintile.

Why is this? The New York Times:

One reason for the mobility gap may be the depth of American poverty, which leaves poor children starting especially far behind. Another may be the unusually large premiums that American employers pay for college degrees. Since children generally follow their parents’ educational trajectory, that premium increases the importance of family background and stymies people with less schooling.

We may think of the latter in terms of a steadily rising conveyor belt. The offspring of the privileged already occupy a spot. Many will not have to lift a finger to remain on board; they have earned their money the old-fashioned way—via inheritance. Others, say those with supportive, educated parents, are provided with the necessary conditions for the upward rise. Many more will have to struggle mightily to stay with it.

There’s another conveyor belt, this one headed downward. It’s really easy to remain on the belt. You can’t really fall off this one. The occupants are already poor from birth and are likely to remain so, as the study reports. Their parents lack education and probably a steady job paying adequate wages. The schools they attend are old, dilapidated, and ill-supplied. Teachers there try their best to engage unmotivated pupils; many give up. The children live in squalid conditions and suffer food insecurity.

Some on the upper belt fall off. They may drop into a kind of purgatory, occasionally getting a break to jump back on the rising conveyor. But the longer they stay in this in-between realm, the more difficult it is to rejoin their former peers; the gap grows larger over time. Others who fall land on the lower belt, from which they may strive to reach purgatory. It is nearly impossible to get from the lower to the upper belt.

The American middle class used to be larger and more confident. They did not need college to land family-wage jobs. With their incomes they could afford a home, a decent car, a vacation, and an occasional trip to a restaurant. They could also set some money aside and perhaps even help pay their children’s college expenses. That was my family.

Today’s middle class has shrunk; its occupants worried about the future. They are in this purgatory of unrealized dreams and diminished opportunity. If they are laid off, chances are they will be out of work for many months if not years, and if they do find another job it will be at a much lower wage. They risk falling to the lower belt, forever doomed.

It really doesn’t have to be this way, as the Nordic countries attest. We are supposedly a free citizenry living in a democracy. We could decide our futures. But it seems that too many of us have become fatalists, as if the savage inequalities are just part of “the system,” just the way things are.

A pervasive sense of profound futility looms large. That should bother us all.

Ask your iPhone

January 4, 2012 by davald

I have a well-deserved reputation as a technophile. My in-home office has been jokingly called ‘NORAD,’ as if I could launch the next world war from my chair. In addition to my desktop computer, HD display, scanner, printers, wireless routers, and a myriad of external hard drives (along with four internal hard drives), I have an iPhone and an iPad and an Apple TV. Yep, I’m all Cupertino. And there is no doubt that my personal setup vastly exceeds most office workers’ technology.

Indeed, there is a new trend in town. It’s called ‘the consumerization of IT.’ Employees’ personal devices are better and more user-friendly than the employer’s standard issue. Increasingly, companies have decided to accommodate rather than resist the invasion.

As some of you know I sit on the board of the Snohomish County Public Utility District, PUD for short. I’ve been openly touting the praises of non-Windows gadgets and software, Apple in particular, of course. I’d like to think that my affection for Jobs’s creations had nothing to do with it, but I am happy nonetheless that PUD management is dipping its collective toe in the non-Redmond pool. To wit, the utility is adopting iPhones (for “security,” don’t you know). This month the board will begin using iPads rather than bulky binders to access meeting packets. Information will be immediately sent and updated to our tablets, so that we commissioners are assured of receiving the latest information and data.

I’ve already been using my own iPad for packet materials. All of a meeting’s documents are available online. I use the iPad during the board meetings. I don’t have to carry around the binder, which would be a nuisance given that I walk to and from the PUD. My colleagues are understandably wary of abandoning the binders in favor of the new technology. But with a few hiccups, I’m certain that they will get the hang of it in short order. Will the PUD adopt other Apple products? Not my call, though I wouldn’t be surprised if it happened.

It is widely rumored that Apple will announce in New York an agreement, and perhaps some new technology, regarding educational textbooks, the iBook application, and who knows what else? Steve Jobs had long believed that students and teachers need not tote a bushel full of books and papers. Why, he thought, we could put all this stuff and more on an iPad, which weighs just a bit over a pound. Everything, including the Internet, would be instantly available by simply swiping the tablet face then tapping on an icon.

I should also mention that Jobs loathed teachers’ union. Not so much because he was necessarily anti-labor, but because he thought that stubborn unions were stuck in the past and resistant to change; and technology is all about change.

Well it seems that some Apple people have been busy in Idaho, of all places. They, and other technology companies, successfully lobbied/persuaded that state’s governor, superintendent of schools, and the legislature (all or mostly quite conservative Republicans, as is the entire state population) to adopt a far-reaching technology initiative, replete with laptops and tablets. Moreover, high school students will be required to take some online courses in lieu of a traditional classroom-cum-teacher.

Not all teachers are happy with this, as this article reports. The New York Times:

To help pay for these programs, the state may have to shift tens of millions of dollars away from salaries for teachers and administrators. And the plan envisions a fundamental change in the role of teachers, making them less a lecturer at the front of the room and more of a guide helping students through lessons delivered on computers.

This change is part of a broader shift that is creating tension — a tension that is especially visible in Idaho but is playing out across the country. Some teachers, even though they may embrace classroom technology, feel policy makers are thrusting computers into classrooms without their input or proper training. And some say they are opposed to shifting money to online classes and other teaching methods whose benefits remain unproved.

As much as I like technology, especially the Apple variety, I detest top-down decision-making on educational issues. I especially loathe far-removed politicians dictating what happens in a classroom. This is so un-Finland-like.

Yet, research supports the proposition that technology boosts academic performance. Here’s an article (pdf). You can find more on, of all places, the Internet! Heck, my brain would be simple mush without the worldwide web. Just about anything and everything is available through your favorite browser.

But does technology facilitate thinking? Here’s what one teacher told the Times:

 “I’m teaching them to think deeply, to think. A computer can’t do that.”

That makes sense, doesn’t it? We don’t lack for information. The challenge is figuring out what to do with it, and that involves thinking about something not just retrieving data.

Neither Idaho’s governor nor legislators can know students, discern their strengths and weaknesses, then fashion appropriate curricula and pedagogy to develop successful learners. It is the classroom teacher who is in the best position to make appropriate judgments about the use of technology, whether it helps or hinders student progress.

I’ve always wondered why there are no people in government or education who engage in the most obvious behavior: asking the classroom teachers what they need to do their job. Instead we get presumptuous dictators.

Rising inequality [u]

December 27, 2011 by davald

New York Times columnist David Brooks tends to write in broad generalities (a penchant he shares with fellow columnist Thomas Friedman) coated with a thick layer of moralism. Today’s column is more of the same, which is titled “Midlife Crisis Economics.”

He correctly notes that the United States and other industrial democracies are much different societies than they were a half-century ago. Brooks cites rising inequality as one factor marking the difference.

Inequality is growing in nearly every developed country. According to a report from the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development, over the past 30 years, inequality in Sweden, Germany, Israel, Finland and New Zealand has grown as fast or faster than inequality in the United States, even though these countries have very different welfare systems.

As we see Canada, Finland, and the Netherlands have experienced only a moderate rise in inequality since the mid-1970s (data from OECD). It may help to consider more inequality statistics. The higher the index the greater the inequality.

The United States is the most unequal society in the group. Indeed, very few countries (e.g., Mexico and Brazil) have higher Gini indices. Even before the Great Divergence in the mid-70s, the U.S. was much less egalitarian than the Nordic countries and Canada.

So, what accounts for the growing gap between the Haves and the Have-nots? The OECD suggests (pdf) several plausible explanations:

  • globalization
  • technological innovation
  • policy choices, including regulations

OECD dismisses the first.

This report finds that neither rising trade integration nor financial openness had a significant impact on either wage inequality or employment trends within the OECD countries.

As for technology and capital flows, the OECD writes:

The study also shows, however, that increased financial flows and technological change had an impact on inequality. Growing outward FDI [foreign direct investment] was associated with increases in wage dispersion, albeit only in the upper half of the wage distribution, while technological progress contributed to the increase in overall wage dispersion, chiefly in the upper half of the distribution.

I am not surprised that the OECD concluded that education-derived skills explain most of the wage gap.

The growth in average educational attainment thus appears to have been the single most important factor contributing not only to reduced wage dispersion among workers but also to higher employment rates. On the basis of these results, the evolution of earnings inequality across OECD countries over the past few decades could be viewed mainly as the difference between the demand for and supply of skills or, as neatly summarised by Tinbergen (1975), the outcome of a “race between education and technology”.

It also occurred to me that there may have been a decline in tax revenues, which, in turn, may have reduced wealth transfers from the Haves to the Have-nots. Let’s look at tax rates (percentage of total worker compensation) of selected countries. They’ve declined for the countries selected, especially in Sweden.

The OECD publishes two sets of data on the Gini index of multiple countries. One pertains to inequality before taxes and government transfers; the other after taxes are collected and spent on public services benefiting the less wealthy. I’ve created a chart that shows the difference in the two as a percentage change and over time. The higher the percentage change the greater the wealth transfer.

There was not much change over time for Canada and the US. However, after rising from the mid-70s to the mid-90s, the Nordic countries’ saw a decrease in the difference between the before and after (taxes and transfers).

Yet, whatever the causes of income inequality the OECD authors conclude that governments must enact policies to reduce the gap between the Haves and the Have-nots.

Rising income inequality creates economic, social and political challenges. It can stifle upward social mobility, making it harder for talented and hard-working people to get the rewards they deserve. Intergenerational earnings mobility is low in countries with high inequality such as Italy, the United Kingdom, and the United States, and much higher in the Nordic countries, where income is distributed more evenly (OECD, 2008). The resulting inequality of opportunity will inevitably impact economic performance as a whole, even if the relationship is not straightforward. Inequality also raises political challenges because it breeds social resentment and generates political instability. It can also fuel populist, protectionist, and anti-globalisation sentiments. People will no longer support open trade and free markets if they feel that they are losing out while a small group of winners is getting richer and richer.

Those countries with high inequality, including the United States, experience low “intergenerational earnings mobility” and reduced economic performance. Moreover, public support for public institutions wanes most in unequal societies. We’ll note that David Brooks wrote: “According to a Gallup survey, 64 percent of Americans polled said they believed that big government is the biggest threat to the country.”

What should be done?

The OECD:

…policies that invest in the human capital of the workforce are key. Over the past two decades, the trend to higher educational attainment has been one of the most important elements in counteracting the underlying increase in earnings inequality in the long run. Policies that promote the up-skilling of the workforce are therefore key factors for reversing the trend towards further growth in inequality.

Human capital policies comprise two main strands. First, better job-related training and education for the low-skilled (on-the-job training) would help to boost their productivity potential and future earnings. This requires measures to ensure that training markets perform better, as well as ensuring sufficient incentives for both workers and firms to invest more in on-the-job training (OECD, 2006). To compensate for mobility (staff turnover), corporate tax policies that encourage employers to make additional investments in the human capital of their employees are warranted (e.g. deduction of training expenses as business costs).

The second strand is equal access to formal education over working life. Access to tertiary education is important for improving the prospects and living standards of lower-skilled people and giving individuals the opportunity to acquire the skills needed in the labour market. Educational or learning accounts can be a means to help achieve this objective (OECD, 2005), but tax incentives need to be designed in such a way that they do not disproportionally benefit higher-wage earners in high marginal tax rates.

Alas, a huge segment of our country, including almost all Republicans, are loath to adopt such policies. Indeed, the GOP in both houses is determined to increase inequality. As for accessing tertiary education (college), good luck with that. It’s expensive, with less and less government aid. Thus, the average college graduate faces over $25,000 in accumulated debt.

I wonder if I could have afforded a college education today. When I attended Berkeley in the 60s there was no tuition—just small “incidental fees.” When I entered as a freshman an academic semester cost me $60 in fees plus books and living expenses. When I graduated four years later the fees had risen to $100 a quarter. (Berkeley switched from the semester to the quarter system during my tenure.) Today it will cost $13,200 per year for tuition and fees (still a bargain, I suppose). The university estimates that total costs will exceed $30,000. That’s $120,000 for four years.

But if college is evidently so important to avoid poverty, why does a student have to become a pauper in the process? Shouldn’t the state invest in human capital, thereby facilitating an inexpensive tertiary education?

I have an idea. Take billions from the Pentagon and transfer the dollars to universities to make education free.

Pass it on.
