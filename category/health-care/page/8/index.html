Washington state governor

October 16, 2012 by davald

Rumor has it that Democratic gubernatorial candidate Jay Inslee pales in side-by-side comparisons with his opponent, Republican Rob McKenna. Whether he’s being interviewed by editorial boards or various interest groups Inslee appears weak, ill-prepared, and unimaginative. McKenna, on the other hand, comes across as bright, energetic, and quick.

Now, as I said, I’m relying only on vague rumors here. While I have met Inslee and seen McKenna only on television, I have no personal experience for rendering the above judgments. I’m in the same position as most voters: dumb and dumber.

Yet, we have a problem in this either-or proposition based on personality only. When it comes to governor, for example, the position for which Inslee and McKenna are vying, there would seem to be two important questions: (1) What would each candidate want to do if elected? (2) What could each candidate do?

We should remember candidate Obama, with his rather bold set of promises, versus President Obama. While the latter has his share of accomplishments, they are but mere shadows of the hopes and dreams he encouraged and facilitated before assuming office. He disappointed many of his supporters, in large part because his opposition desired to achieve that effect, blocking legislative initiatives designed to advance his goals and objectives.

As a Republican, McKenna would surely wish to adopt the austerity regimen championed by his party’s faithful. That is, he would endeavor to cut government spending and lower taxes, all the while proclaiming that these two strategies—and perhaps these alone—would make us all prosper.

In the Voters’ Pamphlet McKenna rails against the state’s “burdensome tax system and redundant regulations,” which, he implies, impede economic growth. He “knows the private sector creates jobs, not government.”

Certainly the recession took a major toll on public-sector employment. State and local governments have continued to shed jobs, including teachers.

Moreover, the percentage of the total workforce employed by Washington state governments has also declined.

It seems difficult to argue that “bloated” governments are the problem, the reason that the economy is lagging. What about taxes?

People and businesses buying things and services represents nearly half of all tax revenue. However, when people lose their jobs, their wages are cut, and firms contract or shut down retail sales fall, which means that the state receives fewer dollars to fund schools (about 40 percent of expenditures) and other social services. Taxes on businesses (B&O) deliver much less revenue to the state, suggesting that they have less effect than McKenna suggests.

What McKenna doesn’t or can’t grasp is that the economy—whether national, state, or local—depends on those retail purchases. And it doesn’t matter whether people are working in the public or private sector. It matters that they have a job with sufficient income to buy food, shelter, clothing, and so on.

Moreover, governments can create jobs, provided they have adequate revenues. They can employ teachers, health care and transportation workers, and technicians to monitor and protect the environment. In turn, those public employees will spend their money, generating taxes.

Inslee, according to the Voters’ Pamphlet, has a “mission”: “to create a stronger and growing economy for Washington.” He would do so by emphasizing “innovations” and “revolutions” in technology and aerospace (read Boing). Inslee has long advocated renewable resources and would surely direct public resources to facilitate their development.

I’m going to reveal a bias here. I am not very excited about Inslee. He lacks administrative experience, most of all. Nor do I care for his government-based approach to renewable resource development; I would prefer a carbon tax, then let the innovators innovate.

Yet, I prefer Inslee to McKenna, and that choice has almost everything to do with just two facts. First, that McKenna is a Republican immediately disqualifies him from consideration. Why? Well, just turn on the television or open up a newspaper to learn what the GOP is about these days. The party’s ideas are crazy, false, or demonstrably wrong in effect (for example, that lower taxes necessarily spur economic growth).

Second, neither candidate can achieve much of anything without legislative support. Since I generally prefer the policies of Democrats to Republicans’ I want an Olympia with Democratic control. If McKenna wins and the opposition party retains majority standing, then he’s not likely to carry out his agenda—which would be a good thing. However, should Inslee prevail under the same circumstances, then those policies I prefer stand a better chance of becoming the law of the land, which is an even better thing.

Unintended, but predictable consequences

October 10, 2012 by davald

First, we have the parent company of the Olive Garden and Red Lobster restaurants. In order to avoid the increased costs associated with Obamacare, which the president now “owns,” management has moved more of its workers into part-time positions.

The health care law mandates that employers with more than 50 employees provide coverage. Darden Restaurants, Inc., has over 180,000 at 2,000 different locations. But there’s a catch. The law applies only to full-time workers. Despite having three-quarters of its workforce already in part-time status, the company is adding to the total.

Unless they are monopolies, companies cannot simply pass along their costs to retail customers. Obamacare imposes more costs, which Darden Restaurants, Inc., can ill-afford, given that the recession has already taken a toll on sales.

The irony, of course, is that in an attempt to cover more workers with health care insurance, the flawed legislation perversely incentivizes companies to further screw its employees, cutting not only their wages by converting them to part-time but also their access to medical coverage.

Another example of unintended yet predictable outcomes is America’s deeply dysfunctional presidential election system. The antiquated and hopelessly inane electoral college guarantees that campaigns will spend the bulk of their dollars on just a few key states, those that too close to call. The other states have long been decided either blue or red, so why waste precious resources trying to alter foregone conclusions? Better to focus time and money and energy on Ohio and Florida and the handful of other states where the results are still in the balance.

Many, including this writer, count the latter a blessing in disguise. The fewer campaign ads I’m forced to watch the better.

On a less cynical note, there are simple fixes to both problems: a single-payer, universal health care system that Canadians and most industrial democracies enjoy; abolish the electoral college and winner-take-all contests. One would think that employers would embrace the former, since the costs of health care would be borne by all and not just companies.

However, I can see a predictable consequence of the second presumed fix. There would be even more money in politics, as each state would be in play. In turn, we’d have to overturn Citizens United and impose restrictions on campaign donations and spending.

Back to cynicism, it seems.

The debates

October 4, 2012 by davald

I grudgingly watched last night’s presidential debate, only to turn it off less than half way through. What a charade. Between Romney’s effrontery and Obama’s fecklessness (“sorry”), I learned nothing, except that our form of government is a shambles, fundamentally so.

Let’s begin with the implicit premise of the debates, that presidents matter. One would think that all that was bad in America, and the list is virtually endless, is the fault of Obama. What little good that has emerged from the sordid mess is also his doing. The problem for the incumbent, of course, is that there is much to ridicule and little to defend.

The worst shame belongs to voters who base their ultimate decision in November on debate performances. Romney was “bold.” Obama was “calm.” Or whatever.

Before I leave, I’ll offer one comment about taxes, a subject that occupied the initial part of the debate that I watched. Romney made two statements that are either contradictory or wishful thinking. He said that he would lower rates and reduce the deficit. How is this possible?

It’s possible only if you believe against overwhelming evidence to the contrary that trickle-down economics works. You know, a rising tide lifts all boats. Giving more money to the super rich like Romney in the form of further tax reductions will spur job creation, and when more people work the taxes they pay will boost federal receipts. Voilà.

There’s a huge problem with this. It hasn’t worked in the past and there is absolutely no reason to believe that it would work now. The only sure outcome from lower tax rates is fewer revenues and, therefore, larger deficits. The Bush-era tax cuts did not ignite an economic boom and rapid hiring. But they did squander a huge surplus left over by the Clinton administration.

Gosh, this gets old.

The significance of party

October 3, 2012 by davald

The Everett Herald recently endorsed Republican Rob McKenna for Washington state governor. Based on the paper’s other endorsements and the views expressed in numerous editorials I can only surmise that the publisher made the call on the gubernatorial race and not the new editorial page editor, the son of the late senator Henry M. Jackson.

The endorsing editorial:

State Attorney General Rob McKenna, one of the most talented lawmakers of his generation, reflects those qualities of leadership. He is a convincing choice for Washington governor in 2012.

First off, McKenna hasn’t been doing much lawmaking, since he is the state’s current attorney general. However, in that position he dissed Governor Gregoire and the Democratically controlled state legislature in formally challenging the Affordable Care Act.

The Herald refers to “hidebound interests” that resist educational reform, a top priority of both the publisher and McKenna. They have in mind the teachers’ union, of course. McKenna, the paper believes, will challenge ” the preserved-in-amber mindset of Olympia’s political class.” That’s the leadership part, I gather.

There are those among us who are quick to assert that they vote for the person rather than, say, a political party. This may be a uniquely American trait, whether or not true, given our “preserved-in-amber” constitutional arrangement, which oddly de-emphasizes political parties while almost guaranteeing that there be only two of them. But we would be wrong to act on this disposition.

The most important quality of a candidate is his or her political affiliation, even if the lines separating Democrats and Republicans have become increasingly blurred over the last couple of decades, as both parties, for example, are beholden to the financial sector. After all, that’s where the money is, and in this Citizens United era, amassing campaign dollars is now an obsession, perhaps a necessary one. Despite this shared dependency, the parties have far different histories and ideologies.

The differences have come into sharp relief during this year’s presidential campaign, with Mitt Romney claiming that he cannot represent nearly half the population, “those people” who lack self-reliance and personal responsibility. He is of the One-percent, for the One-percent, and by the One-percent. Thus, he and his party consistently call for lower taxes, fewer regulations to interfere with the “job creators,” massive cuts in social spending, and even more money for the Pentagon. They detest unions, especially those of the public sector, and would eliminate minimum wage laws. If given the opportunity they would gut environmental laws and lavish even more subsidies on the fossil-fuel industry.

Yet, the opposite party can hardly be mistaken for socialists or social democrats. Though the Democrats have affinity for and draw much financial support from labor unions, they have all but ignored labor’s agenda, which includes removing obstacles to union participation and, I should think, repealing restrictions on secondary or sympathy strikes that are prevalent in Europe.

Nevertheless, Democrats would preserve and strengthen social welfare programs that help the less fortunate, those who were not born into wealth like Mitt Romney or the two Bushes. They would put both Social Security and Medicare on a much stronger financial footing, rather than raise the retirement age or cut benefits as the Republicans advocate.

Democrats support “spreading the wealth around,” increasing taxes on the wealthy to fund the aforementioned programs and reduce deficits. They would at least restore Clinton-era tax rates. They champion government investments in infrastructures, especially public schools and transportation. They are more likely to be Keynesian, advocating a government role in creating jobs to build roads, trains, and classrooms.

Rob McKenna is a Republican. That’s all the Rest of Us need to know as we enter the voting booth. Unless, you’re a One-percenter, then, by all means, vote your selfish, greedy interests.

Taxes and death spirals

September 12, 2012 by davald

Mayor Bloomberg says that Congress should get on with it and just pass the Simpson-Bowles recommendations, even though the committee’s report failed to gain sufficient support from its members. The mayor also believes that lower taxes help businesses.

Well, let’s see. I’ll try a little thought experiment.

Businessman A, who makes Widget X, believes that he could become more competitive if his company’s tax burden were less. To be sure, his costs would decline, all other things being equal, which would free dollars to, say, hire more workers to boost production, even though aggregate demand remains weak.

Businessman B also makes Widget X. If A gets a tax break, so will he. So, both A and B will have no comparative advantage against the other—again, all other things being equal.

Now we could introduce Businessman Chang. He’s Chinese, of course, and he also makes Widget X. Both A and B find it difficult to compete with Chang. So they push for lower taxes in hopes of taking some market share from Chang. A does not better B, nor B, A, but A and B, with lower taxes, better Chang—to limited extent and only temporarily.

Chan has a government that builds all the infrastructure he needs, from high-speed rail shipping, to modern factories, to adequate schools that train his workers. But A and B, because their taxes have been lowered—along with every other business person in America—have lousy infrastructures. The roads are congested or crumbling. Railways are clogged and unable to accommodate high-speed trains. Nor do the would-be workers have the requisite skills to improve production, because the schools are underfunded.

Both A and B press for even lower taxes to compensate for Chang’s advantages. All levels of government comply with their request. But the governments, in turn, have less and less tax revenues to provide those things that Chang takes for granted. Schools, roads, and other transportation options continue to fall apart. Nevertheless, A and B complain about the lousy infrastructure and the failing schools, as they keep lobbying their government representatives to do more with less and, of course, to further lower their taxes.

A and B have created their own death spiral. The more they clamor for and receive tax breaks, the more difficult it is for them to compete against Chang.

But A and B are idiots, of course. They never see beyond the tip of their noses. Indeed, they shout and scream that “they built it” and that they are “job creators” answerable to no one—if only government would reduce their tax burdens and stay out of their way.

For their parts, the different levels of government have no choice. The elected representatives depend on A and B for their campaign donations. Nor do they have any money to get in A and B’s way. They can’t pay for schools and roads and railways. And the governments can no longer invest in research to spur technological innovation.

Mr. Chang’s future looks bright. Not so much Mr. A and B’s.

Government spending: health care

September 5, 2012 by davald

The Republicans, who strenuously resist the national version of their candidate’s health care law, vow to further privatize medical services. They argue, against all evidence to the contrary, that government does it bad and the private sector does it good.

So, what is the evidence that debunks the privatization scheme? As is my wont, I’ll rely on some charts to visually display data and correlations. (Sources: OECD, Wikipedia, GlobalHealthFacts.org)

Those of us who pay attention know that the U.S. spends more on health care in total and per capita than any other nation. It’s not even close. Now one might infer that we should have better health care metrics than countries that spend less. Well, that depends. If you’re rich like Mitt Romney, then you can buy all the health care you want and probably live longer and feel better. For the Rest of Us, good luck.

Look at that cluster in the middle. Those circles represent the Nordic countries and Germany, all of whom spend less per capita than we do.

What about life expectancy?

 

Well, we must have a lot more doctors, given how much we spend on health care. (With the exception of Canada, the countries clustered in the middle of the following graph are Nordic.)

 

There’s no such thing as a free lunch, we like to say. All those Nordic countries with their “socialist” health care systems must suffer economically.

Ronald Reagan said that government was “the problem.” In America, that’s certainly correct. Our government really is the problem in so many ways, not the least of which being that the very rich have completely captured it. That we have any programs at all that help alleviate poverty (e.g., food stamps) or care for the sick (e.g., Medicaid) or provide some retirement benefits (e.g., Social Security) suggests that the oligarchs didn’t get everything they wanted; or perhaps they realize that eliminating all public services might actually anger the masses to ill effect.

The main reason I engage in these international comparisons is to dispel myths about the role and extent of government. Yes, criticize the U.S. federal government, and rightly so. However, I believe that the Nordic countries in particular offer a different, more positive attitude. Government can and does lessen the extremes of wealth, provides valuable social services, and otherwise protects against life’s misfortunes.

That said, I harbor no illusions about Americans’ capacity for meaningful change. There have been moments in our history when persistent struggle yielded victories we now take for granted, like the eight-hour day, the end of child labor, civil rights, and “entitlements” like Social Security. Yet reactionary forces limited the gains and always threaten to eliminate them entirely. Behold the Republicans.

Tragic.

Converging on teachers

September 5, 2012 by davald

Having spent some time trying to teach adolescents I can attest to the challenges confronting those who would presume to educate our nation’s youth. Each classroom presents its own unique problems, among them the broad disparity in skills, curiosity, and self-discipline. Nevertheless, teaching has become a thankless endeavor, as reformers—always the reformers—seek to blame teachers for poor student performance and everything else.

Writing for Salon.org, Paul Tough argues that such focus is not only wrong but counterproductive, given persistently high poverty in America. He offers a dubious syllogism to explain the reformers’ logic:

First, scores on achievement tests in school correlate strongly with life outcomes, no matter what a student’s background. Second, children in low-income homes did much worse on achievement tests than children in middle-income and high-income homes. And third, certain schools, using a very different model than traditional public schools, were able to substantially raise the achievement-test scores of low-income children. The conclusion: if we could replicate on a big, national scale the accomplishments of those schools, we could make a huge dent in poverty’s impact on children’s success.

Against this argument Tough cites research linking poverty with arrested intellectual and, I would add, emotional development. Simply put, poor kids struggle with learning to a much higher degree than privileged pupils. Thus, placing the onus on teachers for reducing or eliminating poverty is doomed to failure.

Tough asks why no one talks about poverty anymore. It used to be front and center in the national dialogue, especially during the Kennedy and Johnson administrations. The Republicans mention the poor only to blame them for their own misfortunes. Tough:

Where the typical conservative argument on poverty falls short is that it often stops right there: Character matters … and that’s it. There’s not much society can do until poor people shape up and somehow develop better character. In the meantime, the rest of us are off the hook. We can lecture poor people, and we can punish them if they don’t behave the way we tell them to, but that’s where our responsibility ends.

Democrats have joined their conservative counterparts in pushing reform by accountability, embracing “value-added” performance criteria, as if developing better teachers were only a matter of data. Collect it. Analyze it. Act on it. Today it’s “race to the top,” which exacerbates the flawed approaches in No Child Left Behind.

But, again, both political parties and a host of educational theorists and pundits completely ignore the elephant in the room: poverty. The percentage of Americans who are poor reached 15.1 in 2010 and is expected to be almost 16 percent this year. These are the highest levels since the early 60s.

 

Several years ago I painstakingly combed through data from the Washington State Office of the Superintendent of Public Instruction, or OSPI for short. I correlated the results of standardized test scores (it was called the WASL back then) with the percentage of students receiving free or reduced lunch, a proxy for poverty. The conclusion was obvious: the higher the poverty rate the lower the test scores.

I compared two schools: Shoultes Elementary in Marysville, Wash., and Mercer Island Elementary. Here is a chart showing fourth grade math scores on the WASL:

 

Now let’s look at poverty rates, again using the free-or-reduced lunch as proxy:

 

What a glaring contrast. Mercer Island’s poverty rate is so low as to barely register on the graph, whereas the Marysville school’s is above fifty percent as of 2010.

It would seem that the answer to improving academic performance is to make kids wealthier. But reformers mistakenly put it backwards: wealth follows higher test scores. Author Tough:

So when we’re looking for root causes of poverty-related underachievement, why do we tend to focus on the wrong culprits and ignore the ones that science tells us do the most damage?

Tough suggests that the science is difficult to understand and not widely publicized. He also believes that we’re uncomfortable talking about parenting, and that good parenting skills tend to be more prevalent in wealthier households and neighborhoods. Finally, Tough agrees that character matters. However, he adds, those traits that lend themselves to higher academic performance (e.g., perseverance and optimism) are teachable themselves and not the product of genetic code.

As I’ve written about Finland’s education system, Finnish students with special needs—and nearly all students have them—receive special attention, including free meals, free health care, and targeted intervention. It helps, of course, that the Finns are dedicated to social justice and generous welfare for all. Less than four percent of the population lives in poverty, although, again, being poor does not deny Finns access to health care or a quality education.

Diane Ravitch, who was a big proponent of the NCLB law, did an about-face. She now condemns it and Obama’s Race to the Top. She writes:

Here’s an interesting contrast: We claim to be preparing students for global competitiveness, and we reward mastery of basic skills. Our guiding principles: Competition, accountability, and choice. Finland has this singular goal: to develop the humanity of each child. Isn’t that a shocking goal? Their guiding principles: equity, creativity, and prosperity.

Finland rightly deserves attention today as a nation that treats its children as a precious resource and that honors the adults who make education their passion and their career. Someday, I hope, we will recognize the failure of the behaviorist approach now in vogue; someday we will see that our current “reforms” are appropriate for the industrial era of the early 20th century, not for the needs of the 21st century. When that day arrives, we will understand the deep wisdom of Finland, with its love for children and its respect for educators, and we will be grateful that there is a successful alternative to our own failed model.

Hmm. What a novel idea: “develop the humanity of each child.” Education as if children mattered.

Russell’s wisdom: why we fight

August 19, 2012 by davald

One essential primer is Bertrand Russell’s The Problems of Philosophy. Therein he provides a concise introduction to issues over which philosophers have long struggled. And if you have any familiarity with philosophy as an academic pursuit, you know that philosophers develop only tentative or provisional answers—such is the nature of the discipline. But if you’re patient, you’ll eventually uncover some kernels of wisdom, and Russell’s writings contain many.

For example, consider these words from the aforementioned book:

Material possessions can be taken by force and enjoyed by the robber. Spiritual possessions cannot be taken in this way. You may kill an artist or a thinker, but you cannot acquire his art or his thought. You may put a man to death because he loves his fellow-men, but you will not by so doing acquire the love which made his happiness. Force is impotent in such matters; it is only as regards material goods that it is effective. For this reason the men who believe in force are the men whose thoughts and desires are preoccupied with material goods.

Thus the conservative mind, since Republicans as a rule are obsessed with “material possessions.” What they have, they fear will be taken. What they lack, they strive to obtain—by almost any means. If they should rule, they will employ the nation’s considerable armaments to plunder the wealth of others. As for defending America’s fortunes, mostly owned by the privileged few, they will spare no expense.

They will not stop at the military or the police state. Their fear-cum-greed affects every fabric of our well-being.

In the hotly contested issue of educational reform, conservatives believe that students should learn those skills necessary to succeed in the acquisition of wealth or to tend to the care and feeding of the oligarchy. Critical thinking, the kind that would ultimately question status quo political arrangements, must be eliminated, since it poses a threat to the powerful. After all, the masses outnumber them.

Investing in the arts and music, except for the products of dead painters and composers, wastes dollars that should be earmarked for exploitation. Public dollars must never go toward self-expression or human development.*

On economic issues, merit determines all, and the rich possess a monopoly, since they made the rules and insist on punishing transgressors. At your workplace, you count for nothing, save for what you contribute to the wealth of your corporate benefactors. Drop any notion of unionism; Labor is evil, because it frustrates Capital’s unrelenting greed. Meanwhile, you are expected to work more hours and extract less from your effort.

 

 

Finally, the Rest of Us must stop worrying and learn to love:

  • decrepit school buildings
  • collapsing roads
  • urban ghettoes
  • ugly strip malls
  • pollution
  • global warming
  • no philosophy, no art, no music, no beauty (these are reserved for your masters, who are happy to pay dearly for them)
  • expensive and ineffective health care
  • mind-numbing TV reality shows
  • perpetual war
  • shit.

But take heart, your taxes are low.

The tyranny of choice

August 14, 2012 by davald

A friend and his family trekked off to Amsterdam a few years ago; both he and his wife having secured a transfer to the Netherlands. As is their wont, they made the best of living abroad, though they did not quite assimilate; neither one of them mastering Dutch—that achievement reserved for their daughter, now nine years old. Their wanderlust was not sated, however. Soon they depart for Australia for yet another adventure, which I’m certain they will make magnificent.

Before squeezing in a few holidays in advance of their next move, the friend paid a brief visit. He is self-confident and ebullient, anticipating wonders where I would surely foresee dread. But we are different, completely so, though we share a love for all things Apple.

Our friend shared a contrasting experience of a more trivial nature. Grocery shopping. It had been a long while since he had set foot inside a Safeway or a QFC or a Fred Meyer. He was truly astounded by the “canyons of choice,” extended, well-stocked shelves of seemingly infinite variety. He mentioned cereal. My local QFC devotes an entire side of one aisle to oats and wheats and granolas, in small to behemoth packaging. The choices overwhelmed my friend.

In Amsterdam, he reports, one fetches groceries from a tiny storefront, likely a converted apartment building. There is cereal, but only three or four types. The Dutch readily substitute for their “favorites,” since they may be unavailable when they shop. Where my wife and I secure weekly provisions, necessitating multiple (cloth) bags and two crates on wheels, a denizen of Amsterdam purchases enough for a day’s meals—and whatever box of cereal still remains. Since the Dutch store is just a brief walk away, groceries fill only a single convenient sack. Simple. Easy.

Americans, on the other hand, spend an inordinate amount of time fretting over which product and in which quantity to buy. Many consult with significant others via cellphone before making their choice. They may spend as long deciding on one item as my friend devotes to an entire shopping trip.

I suspect that our tyranny of choice has everything to do with money. After all, this is America. The more choices available to us the greater the confusion, whether we’re in the market for cars or bread. In turn, the confusion provides opportunity to extract what economists call ‘rents.’

Okay, so we don’t hire a professional to advise us on whether to buy Shredded Wheat or bran flakes. But, if we have the means, we are typically driven into the arms of a consultant when it comes to that increasingly elusive period of our lives known as retirement.

Think about this for a moment within contrasting contexts of grocery shopping: Amsterdam vs. Everett (where I live). If I need food in the Netherlands, chances are I will take a quick stroll to a small shop with only a few basic items. I need not dwell on my choices, since there aren’t many, if any. Here’s the cereal. Here’s the bread. Here’s the milk. The typical American grocery store is huge, with multiple aisles and hundreds of different products in scores of different sizes and shapes. What to do?

My wife and I are planning to retire soon. I use ‘planning’ most advisably. I know, the rich will have had their parents plan for them, perhaps before they were born. But the Rest of Us, should we be lucky enough to foresee a meaningful retirement ahead, are inundated with choices, just like in a grocery store.

The one ingredient in our future that does not confuse is Social Security. That will be fixed for each of us. Check. What about Medicare? When we reach the age of 65 we are automatically eligible for the first part, which costs us nothing. However, it’s very, very basic, as in limited in the extreme. If you think you’ll need prescriptions or doctor visits or one or more medical devices you’ll need to enroll in other parts, all of which have layers of coverage at different price schemes.

My wife will receive a “defined contribution” upon her retirement. That’s a sum of money accumulated over time that will supplement her “defined benefit” portion. What to do with that pile of cash? (I should add that the volume does not warrant consideration of off-shore accounts.) Do we keep it where it is? Do we convert it into an annuity? What if we should need some “liquidity” down the road?

We are confronted with a tyranny of choice that compels us to seek the advice of a consultant, who will charge us for his presumed expertise. He collects rent on our confusion.

What I find frustrating about “the market,” aside from its ubiquitousness, is that our lives could be much simpler and happier without all the opportunities for rent seekers. Here are two alternative approaches to partially addressing the tyranny of choice:

  1. Social Security could be one, big public pension system covering all. (We could add a means test if you like.) No need for an employer-provided plan or an IRA or a 401(k).
  2. Medicare for all, but without all the choices. Everyone, and I mean everyone, gets covered as in Canada or England or Germany or any other western industrial democracy. No need for private insurance companies. No need for profit-extracting firms. Health care is a right not a privilege. Simple. Easy.

A final point: we don’t have to dream about this stuff; it already exists elsewhere. America, ladies and gentlemen, does not have all the answers. It has only choices, and choices cost money, lots of it.

Trickle down

July 18, 2012 by davald

A recurring theme amongst the G.O.P. is that there are very special people, as in superior, whose success should be praised. Not because they necessarily serve as exemplars for the Rest of Us; we’re to know out station in life and accept the fact that we’re just not good enough. Rather, our obvious betters are principally responsible for anything that comes our way, from our jobs to the food on our table. As such, we should be grateful, and envy is clearly the wrong attitude, since we stand no chance of joining the elites.

Mitt Romney said as much in a speech before the N.A.A.C.P., suggesting that we live in a merit-based society that rewards those with superlative talents, including natural intelligence, of course, who work hard, take risks, and at all times pursue the almighty dollar. From this faith, really, it follows that government must favor the the best in its various policies. That means lower taxes and fewer regulations (too constraining, don’t you know).

What about the Rest of Us? Well, you see, extending any benefits and “entitlements” to the 99-percenters produces a lazy, discontented rabble who neither appreciate their lot in life nor exert sufficient effort to make the capitalist engine work. Besides, the government subsidies to the vast majority of citizens subtracts from the resources that should and must find their way to our superiors. If they don’t succeed, evidently spectacularly so, the Rest of Us are shit out of luck.

This theme figures prominently in the behavior of several Republican governors, notably Texas’s Rick Perry, who have pugnaciously declined to participate in the federal government’s Medicaid program. They may cloak their actions in the rhetoric of opposing Washington encroachment, somehow interfering with “states’ rights,” but in so doing they are unashamedly consigning our most vulnerable brothers and sisters to lives of further desperation and misery.

The New York Times editors call this a “rush to abandon the poor.” Apt words. The editors:

Mr. Perry, too, opposes the existing Medicaid program, and, in a recent letter to Washington, said that expanding it represented “brazen intrusions into the sovereignty of our state” and would “threaten even Texas with financial ruin.” The truth is that Washington will pay all the cost of the expansion for three years (then scale back to 90 percent). Mr. Perry’s bold resistance may play well politically in his state, but it’s nothing more than a stomach punch to the millions of uninsured in Texas who will have to stay that way.

The editors remind us of some egregious statistics about Texas meanness. If any state could use federal assistance for health care it is Mr. Perry’s.

The state with the country’s worst health care record just happens to have a governor who has been the loudest voice against national efforts to improve it.

A quarter of the residents of Texas, 6.3 million people, are uninsured, by far the highest percentage in the country. (That number includes more than a million children.) Texas ranks last in prenatal care and finished last on a new federal assessment of overall health quality that examined factors like disease prevention, deaths from illnesses, and cancer treatment.

It’s always been true that Republicans don’t give a damn about the poor and infirm. Hell, care and compassion are utterly foreign concepts. But to be so “brazen” about it.

Those who fail in our winner-take-all society are not only responsible for their own fate. Any diversion of funds from the coddled rich to help them threatens the aforementioned capitalist engine. Insofar as we adopt liberal policies, god forbid, we’re only killing the golden goose. That means even more misery for the Rest of Us.

Only in America.
