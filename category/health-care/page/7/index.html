Maybe not

February 22, 2013 by davald

The New York Times‘ editors tell us “why taxes have to go up.” For the time being, they suggest that the rich pay the higher taxes; the Rest of Us will join them after the economy recovers. There’s another option.

The editors cite the Bush-era tax cuts as playing the largest role in the budget deficits, followed by the two wars (about $1.5 trillion so far) and the recession (the collapse of the housing bubble represented a loss of $8 trillion, by economist Dean Baker’s estimates). Indeed, and contrary to Speaker John Boehner’s assertion, we have faced a revenue problem for a very long time, at least since the days of Ronald Reagan.

While I think the editors are correct, we tend to overlook a solution that doesn’t involve raising taxes. That solution is all about health care in America.

We spend more on health care than other industrial democracies, yet we realize lower benefits (OECD data).

U.S. GDP is roughly $16 trillion. At 17.6 percent of the economy, U.S. health care spending is about $2.8 trillion a year. Suppose we spent the same percentage of our GDP on health care as, say, Sweden, whose health care costs represent about 9.6 percent of GDP. We would be spending, then, $1.5 trillion, or $1.3 trillion less than we currently do on health care.

Hmmm. Our deficit is around $1.4 trillion. We would solve our fiscal woes if we had a health care system like Sweden’s. 

Just saying.

Growing old

January 31, 2013 by davald

Last year I joined the ranks of “the elderly,” which entitled me to receive Medicare. Later this year I’ll begin collecting Social Security. We old people, as you might surmise from watching Fox News or listening to Mitt Romney, are “moochers.” Most of us cannot afford to be self-reliant; we will depend on the federal government to supplement our meagerly pensions and pay for our rising health care costs. Hey. When you get old you get sicker, as much as I’d like to avoid both.

The U.S. Census Bureau keeps track of demographics, which include age along with sex, race, and bunch of other metrics. We are growing older.

Matthew Yglesias suggests that “we” will have a problem, and it’s all about us old farts. He writes:

So what are the real problems? Simple. One thing the government does is tax the labor and investment activities of non-seniors to cut checks to elderly people (Social Security). Another thing it does is tax the labor and investment activities of non-seniors to cut checks to elderly people’s doctors (Medicare). And a third thing it does is tax the labor and investment activities of non-seniors to cut checks to elderly people’s long-term care providers (one of Medicaid’s functions). In the future, the ratio of elderly people to non-elderly people is supposed to rise, and the ratio of doctors’ fees to average incomes is also supposed to rise. Which is to say that [even while] holding program functions constant the burden of placed on the non-elderly is expected to rise. But this increased burden exists whether the payment comes in the form of taxes (people don’t like paying taxes!) or in the form of borrowing (which will eventually lead to taxes people don’t like to pay!) so the issue is all about spending money on old people and not about debt or deficits.

Yep, we’re an expensive lot, we elderly, and expected to become even more so. One response to our changing demographics is to cut benefits or decrease the life-time payouts by raising the eligibility age. That may sound like music to your ears if you’re decades away from joining us. You don’t want to pay more taxes. I get that. Who does? But would you dig a little deeper to help out your aging parents, or would you just let them rot in their bedrooms? I’m guessing that you’d feel compelled to do something, however minimal.

I, for one, was grateful that my parents had pensions, Medicare, and Social Security to ease their financial burdens–and mine—as they grew sick and eventually died. That happens, you know. We all die sometime. I’m hoping that I won’t have to depend on my children for assistance, and that I can rely on what my wife and I have set aside and, yes, federal programs.

A more civil and humane culture would want to care for the elderly through insurance at levels far grander than America’s public programs. But paying for a more European system would cost us more in higher taxes.

However, they wouldn’t have to be so high were we to get serious about reining in the private health care sector, including Big Pharma. In effect, we Americans have allowed the Rest of Us to transfer our wealth to insurance companies and the makers of drugs, the latter insisting that we be denied much cheaper generic versions. (The New York Times writes about the latter here.) Yglesias talks about the artificial supply constraints on doctors and mentions the Canadians:

We either need more doctors or more adequate substitutes for doctors. We need more long-term care providers. Taxpayers don’t need to foot the bill for the whole thing, but the whole medical infrastructure is very bound up with public policy and something has to be done. Canada reduces the burden that health care places on the taxpayer by paying providers less for the services they render, which could be a promising start.

So let’s get started.

Collectivism

January 22, 2013 by davald

David Brooks, as is his wont, criticizes Obama’s second inaugural address for ignoring “decentralizing genius,” which he chooses to symbolize by ‘Wall Street,’ ‘State Street,’ ‘Menlo Park,’ and ‘Silicon Valley.’ He contrasts the U.S. and Europe, the latter having “invested in national welfare states, American localities [having] invested in human capital.” As is my wont, I continue to wonder about Brooks’s grasp of reality.

There is perhaps no place on earth that both clings to “democracy” and “rugged individualism” to the measure of America. We have manifestly preferred a more Hobbesian dystopia of nasty, brutish, short-lived lives to the utopian dreams of social and economic security in which individuals maximize their potential within a state that values personal dignity.

After all, we love our guns, the more the merrier, and have little compunction about putting people to death, even if their ignominious fates were determined erroneously. Instead of demonstrating sympathy and compassion for the poor and near-poor we dismiss them as “moochers,” condemning their lack of appropriate attitudes, the alleged modus operandi of the privileged and the decentralized genius.

Americans lack basic necessities, guaranteed health care, and the ability and conditions to improve their existence. Once the model of social mobility, the United States has ossified into rigid economic strata, as it blocks the poor from becoming rich, and vice versa. College now means debt rather than enlightenment and self-fulfilment.

Meanwhile, besotted with manufactured myths, we have permitted the rise and aggrandizement of the super-wealthy, whose fortunes belittle others and deprive the Rest of Us of our due. Indeed, we believe that we’re undeserving of anything beyond our morning porridge. They are geniuses, one and all. Don’t disparage them. Embrace them. Worship them. If you possibly can, emulate them.

As for Europe, having established social welfare states, its citizens are free to realize their potential. Several of its countries enjoy higher economic growth and greater opportunity for personal advancement than do Americans. They do not brazenly kill one another, and they deny their civil authorities the license to execute comrades for their crimes, however heinous. Their societies are more egalitarian; education is typically free.

Europe’s nations are not utopias on earth. But their denizens seem to have understood that an obsession with ducats demeans rather than ennobles. They understand and appreciate that life is full of vagaries that are best addressed by a collective response than Randian self-reliance. So Europeans pool risks, making it difficult to become either insanely rich or desperately poor.

Obama hinted in his speech that we have more than our fair share of decentralized geniuses, many of whom threw us under the bus last time round. Now its finally time for America to build a better society, one that would make Dr. King proud.

We’ll see.

You know, we are number one in some things

January 16, 2013 by davald

A recent study (pdf) attempts to explain why Americans are less healthy than their counterparts in other industrial democracies. As the authors say, we spend more on health care than anyone, yet we live shorter and more difficult lives. Why?

The report speculates, offering some plausible reasons for the disadvantage. Here’s one:

…the U.S. health system is highly fragmented, with limited public health and primary care resources and a large uninsured population. Compared with people in other countries, Americans are more likely to find care inaccessible or unaffordable and to report lapses in the quality and safety of care outside of hospitals.

Well, we knew this, which is one reason that Obama sought to broaden coverage, though Obamacare should be considered only a first step on the road to universal health care.

Another finding that shouldn’t surprise, we Americans consume more calories per capita than our counterparts. Yep. We’re fatter. Yet why are so many of us in surplus? The study authors suggest that we’re far more automobile dependent, which means that we drive rather than walk to routine destinations. We don’t burn off too many calories turning the steering wheel and applying pressure to the brake pedal.

Also, the poorer one is the more likely he or she is to buy and eat high-caloric foods, such as those found at McDonald’s. Fresh fruits and vegetables are expensive, and many of us lack the requisite equipment to prepare healthy meals using them.

British health researcher Richard Wilkinson has studied “Unhealthy Societies,” the title of one of his books. (He also wrote Spirit Level, mentioned on this blog.) His data suggest that inequality can explain a great deal of the variance between nations. Also, and the aforementioned study’s authors confirm this suspicion, even rich Americans suffer from society’s inequality: “The problem is not confined to socially or economically disadvantaged Americans; as noted above, several recent studies have suggested that even Americans with high socioeconomic status may experience poorer health than their counterparts in peer countries.”

Our winner-take-all society is not good for us, ladies and gentlemen.

When economists gather

January 9, 2013 by davald

Paul Krugman and three other economists formed a panel over the weekend in San Diego. Ostensibly the goal was to extract answers to four or five questions. As it happened, each panelist proffered his or her views on the role of government in the economy, whether in good or bad times. The substitute moderator, Brad DeLong, who teaches macroeconomics at Berkeley, provides a “rough” transcript of the event, which is well worth your time.

Yet, I must add that I am not any more confident in the economics profession now than I was before, a small increment above zero. Krugman and DeLong have argued and argue here that the government should be stimulating the economy by spending on useful projects. Other panelists, most notably Valerie Ramey, counter that the multiplier effect of fiscal expansion is less than zero. She is joined in this view by Harald Uhlig.

A cursory reading of the statements reveals a fundamental divide between Krugman and the others. The difference is compassion, for lack of a better word at the moment. Krugman believes that our collective failure to address the immediate problems of high un-and under-employment means not only long-term disaster for the overall economy but also tragedy for those directly affected. At the end of the Q&A one senses strong exasperation on his part for what he surely holds to be wrongheaded arguments by his colleagues, who exhibit the sympathy of a prune.

If people attended the forum in hopes of finding wisdom via consensus they were sorely disappointed. The state of macro is very muddy indeed.

Sad.

That said, one obvious point made by Prof. Ramey. If we could significantly reform the health care system in the U.S. we would get close to solving the fiscal mess. As I’ve written on these pages, the U.S. spends far more per capita on health care but achieves less aggregate benefit.

Where Ramey gets into trouble, in my opinion, is by drawing a simplistic conclusion from headline data. To wit, she concludes that U.S. government spending on health care must be inefficient, since Canada spends the same amount as a percentage of GDP as we do (roughly eight percent) but 100 percent of Canadians are covered, whereas only 25 percent of Americans are. If our federal government were to cover all U.S. citizens, then health care spending would rise dramatically, she opines.

I see two problems with this extrapolation. First, Ramey ignores the whole idea of insurance, something that Ben Franklin understood over two hundred years ago. All Canadians belong to one giant pool of risks. The healthy therefore subsidize the relatively unhealthy. In America, at least prior to the Affordable Care Act, government programs covered those who lack private health insurance because they have none through their employer or have pre-existing conditions the carriers refuse to cover. As a result, publicly funded health care is more expensive here than in Canada. Second, Ramey ignores the inherent inefficiencies of the private sector, with its multiple providers and high administrative costs. Also, no need for advertising budgets in universal health care systems.

Before leaving, I refer to a comment made by Prof. Krugman. He tells the audience:

The basic story—at least as many of us see it—is that we had this really, really dramatic shock to private spending. This is the private-sector financial deficit: gross private domestic investment minus gross private domestic saving as a share of potential GDP as estimated by the CBO.

GOP debt fallacies

January 3, 2013 by davald

Republicans, as we surely know, insist that the essential problem in the U.S. is high government debt. Their proffered solution is to cut spending. They consistently and steadfastly reject the notion that we’ve got a revenue problem, so they refuse to consider higher taxes, though they have for the moment agreed to a slight retreat from that position to avoid the “fiscal cliff.” (Yet, just a modest increase in corporate tax rates, for example, would come close to wiping out the deficit entirely and thus contract accumulated debt.)

What has always disturbed me about the Republican stance is that our federal government is significantly smaller as a percentage of GDP than our northern European counterparts, for example, whose relatively generous social welfare programs require higher taxes than paid by Americans.

Interestingly enough, those governments have lower central government debt than does the U.S.

We will note the sharp rise in the U.S. debt, which was actually the lowest among these selected countries in 2001. What happened then? Well, G.W. Bush proclaimed that “it’s our money,” and that he would, and did, return the Clinton-era surplus “to the American people.” He accomplished this by dramatically lowering tax rates on the rich. And why not launch and prosecute a couple of costly wars without paying for them. Yep. That’s the ticket—to rising deficits.

The country with the lowest government debt is Sweden, though its debt was among the largest in 2001, at almost 60 percent of that nation’s GDP. In 2010 the level had dropped below 40 percent.

Why doesn’t it occur to Republicans that high government spending need not be accompanied by high debt? Why would they disdain rather than investigate Sweden, for example?

Moreover, Sweden collects a lot of taxes from its citizens, evidently because Swedes expect and receive from their government public goods and services they desire. In the chart below (Eurostat data) Sweden is “SE.” The numbers reflect tax receipts as a percentage of GDP.

I hasten to add that all of the above countries, save for the U.S., have a universal health care system. And they spend less on medical treatment and medicines than do Americans—far less.

The Republicans’ arguments suffer from ignorance of facts and correlations. They believe, against the evidence, that bigger government (as a percentage of GDP) is the cause of the following conditions:

  • debt
  • retarded economic growth
  • a nation of “moochers”

Again, there is no correlation between government debt and government spending. Indeed, the larger governments have lower debt-to-GDP ratios.

The Swedish economy fared better than the U.S.’s, despite the Swedes enjoying universal health care and far greater social spending.

Are all Swedes “moochers”? If they were, how to explain their comparatively robust economy?

But Republican misguidance persists in Congress. They surely expect to extract major concessions on “entitlement reform” (i.e., screwing the Rest of Us) in the negotiations on raising the federal debt limit two months from now. I suspect that the House GOP caucus eventually conceded on the Obama-Senate bill only after the more feral members were promised blood in February.

Predictable reaction [u]

December 17, 2012 by davald

I admit to being outraged by the events in Connecticut. So I posted an expletive-filled heading on the Second Amendment, with a link to to the New York Times article on the massacre at Sandy Hook Elementary. How could my choice of words, however inappropriate, trump the tragedy itself? Twenty kindergarteners were butchered by a deranged youth who had used his own mother’s firearms to kill her before he executed the innocents and six adult educators. Is that not inappropriate?

But, of course, we live in a crazy country, and the reaction to my post exhibited more of the same. My heading was judged “puerile” and a reflection of a”total lack of understanding regarding the subject.” What is there to understand beyond what happened and the culture that not only enabled but perhaps encouraged loonies with guns?

I read that there are nine firearms for every 10 people in America. That’s enough to kill deer and varmint with surely enough ammo left over to wipe out a wide swath of the republic.

Other countries “keep and bear arms,” to be sure. But their citizens don’t seem to be as whacko as ours, and that combination of mental pathology and guns leads to senseless slaughters.

So, shouldn’t we curb access to weapons, at least until we’ve figured out how to address the pathologies? If you abide by the law, condemn the wanton violence, and believe that guns nevertheless have a special place in your lives, wouldn’t you agree to some reasonable restrictions on how they are acquired and used? Also, do you really need a semi-automatic pistol to shoot game?

For those who suggest that an armed nation would be a more peaceful one, consider that the Connecticut crazy man used his mother’s guns to kill her—repeatedly. And it’s reported that she loved her guns. For self-protection? Against her own son?

Yet, I suspect that it’s futile to argue against Gun Nation. Better that we stop arguing and just act—legislatively. That’s the American way, too.

The beatings will continue

December 12, 2012 by davald

I am as baffled as Paul Krugman by all the blather regarding the “fiscal cliff” and, especially, “reforming” entitlements to effect a “grand bargain.” One element of “reform” is raising the eligibility age for both Social Security and Medicare. Doing so, it is alleged, will save the government money. But as Krugman and others have pointed out repeatedly and with much frustration, this is simply wishful thinking. Indeed, with respect to the presumed Medicare “fix,” the change would actually increase health care costs by transferring millions of older Americans from the less expensive government program to the much more expensive private sector, with its administrative bloat.

Krugman briefly addresses the proffered rationales for upping the eligibility age. For example, the right, he believes, pushes this “reform” because liberals hate it. Petty, certainly, but likely true. Others, both conservatives and centrists, always prefer smaller government, even if that means millions must suffer. The message made explicit during the presidential campaign is that if you find yourself needing public assistance, including Social Security and Medicare, it’s your own damn fault. You’re an undeserving moocher.

But one intriguing explanation that warrants consideration is political: raise the eligibility age and the constituency for those programs shrinks. Krugman quotes from the reason.com website, Peter Suderman in particular.

Raising the Medicare eligibility age would make additional reforms easier. The most important likely effect is political. Reforming Medicare is difficult in part because of resistance by beneficiaries, who hold a lot of political influence; indeed, the fact that some beneficiaries might have to pay more for their insurance (CBO estimates that nearly all would still end up insured) is the primary argument cited by opponents of raising the eligibility age object to the change. That people who benefit from a program like it and/or get financial rewards from it, however, is not much of an argument for refusing to accept reforms, especially with an obviously unsustainable entitlement like Medicare. Diminishing the size of the beneficiary class is likely to diminish resistance to further change, and while it’s not enough, it might ultimately make reform easier.

Medicare is “obviously unsustainable” if you deny the program sufficient funds. Also, it follows that the private sector is even more unsustainable.

Only in America.

Return on investment [u]

November 8, 2012 by davald

I happened to catch an episode of Rick Steves’s travel program. He was in Denmark, showing us the sights and sounds and people. One comment stood out, at least for me. The Danes, Steves suggested, willingly pay high taxes, but, and this is the interesting part, they demand a return on their investment. So far, that calculus seems to work, and Danish people enjoy splendid public services, including universal health care and transportation.

This got me thinking. We Americans generally loathe taxes, and we believe that ours are too high. Never mind that they are among the lowest of the industrial democracies. With few exceptions, we record our antipathy via the ballot box, as Washingtonians did again, passing the latest Tim Eyman super-majority tax requirement for Olympia. Otherwise, we vote Republican.

Could contempt for taxes be a consequence of poor return? When we look about us we see bridges and highways in disrepair. Our school buildings are falling apart. We have no well-functioning passenger trains to speak of. Our cities are generally dirty, because we don’t hire people to clean them. And everyone knows that our public education system doesn’t work; provide your own check list of dissatisfaction, whether it be the teachers’ fault or crowded classrooms or misguided curricula.

In our heads we may judge tax revenues adequate, since they’re already too high. So when considering the lousy state of public services, we instinctively resist giving the bastards any more money, which will surely be wasted or otherwise spent foolishly.

Consider this possibility. Imagine that there is a critical threshold of taxing, below which we get only mediocrity combined with persistent loathing of taxes, and above which we begin to realize societal value, the kind of value expected by the Scandinavians but rarely experienced here in America. How high could we go? (source of data below)

Emanuel Saez, French-born but now a professor of economics at Berkeley, wrote a provocative paper with his longtime collaborator, Thomas Piketty, arguing that marginal tax rates could be raised significantly (up to 80 percent) without adversely affecting economic growth.

The authors believe that extreme inequality is problematic in itself, thus warranting greater wealth distribution via a more progressive tax code. But for our purposes here, the extra revenues derived from higher tax rates would go to schools, transportation, health care, and other public services that the private sector is unable or unwilling to provide.

Of course, there is no way that Congress would even attempt to increase tax rates, and they’d surely run for the hills at the first mention of Scandinavia. Thus, we are consigned to mediocrity.

But at least we know that it’s possible to have a much better public sector and that citizens are evidently willing to pay for it. Just not in America—yet. Wait a minute, we used to pay much higher taxes and had good schools and roads. Ah, those were the days.

The broad brush

November 8, 2012 by davald

Some Republican candidates lost their elections, it is surmised, because of their over-the-top comments on rape. The remarks—though I’m sure they were as earnest as they were off-putting—likely enraged enough women to ensure the candidates’ defeat. I want to focus a bit on this excerpt from the New York Times:

“What was really frustrating is that there was this myth manufactured by Democrats in Washington that the Republican Party as a whole is against women,” said Senator Susan Collins, Republican of Maine, who said she watched with disappointment as her friend Senator Scott P. Brown of Massachusetts was tarred by the broader fight and lost his bid for re-election, to a woman. “There is no doubt we need to do a better job as a party in reaching out to women, recruiting strong women candidates and sending a more positive message,” Ms. Collins said.

What Ms. Collins may not appreciate is that for those who care about women’s issues, in particular the right to choose and to protection of their own bodies and persons, defeating Republicans was paramount. While it’s true that not all Republicans share the views of Todd Akin or John Koster, it is true that their party is dominated by social conservatives. Just look at the party’s platform and the pronouncements of the GOP’s current standard bearers. Thus, preventing reactionary voices from pursuing their agenda required wholesale defeat of Republicans, regardless of their individual views.

Also, simply “sending a more positive message” to and about women is no substitute for having a lot more real, live Republicans embracing and acting upon such a message. Ms. Collins decided to retire from Congress knowing full well that her party had been commandeered by the loony right whose views and policies antagonized much of the electorate. Moreover, consider the primary defeats of long-time Republicans judged to be too moderate for the Tea Party and Christian evangelicals.

Ms. Collins, no doubt, got out in the nick of time. Her species of social liberalism combined with fiscal conservatism is no longer welcome in the GOP.
