Retirement planning

December 28, 2011 by davald

I suppose that I should have put scare quotes around ‘planning,’ since few of us have the acumen or the resources to do it properly. But we all get old and at some point too old to work, even if we need the income or actually like our jobs.

I’m approaching the magic age of official retirement; next year my wife and I turn 65. We’ll also celebrate our 44th wedding anniversary and, if the stars are in alignment, her retirement from public school teaching. My term of office has three years remaining, and, if my health doesn’t deteriorate, I’ll likely run for re-election.

So, how does one go about retiring? Nearly every other life phase is relatively simple; more often than not it’s encountered by accident. Retirement is different and vastly more cumbersome. Besides, the big question looms large: Will we have enough income to support ourselves at all, let alone in the style with which we’re accustomed?

During the past few weeks we’ve been studying online materials, acquiring the necessary forms, and consulting with school and union staffers—in my wife’s case. We’ve toted up the projected incomes from her pension, Social Security, my salary, and, later, my rather modest public pension. We think we’re going to be okay. In this we are fortunate.

The Pew Research Center has issued a report on the widening gap in net worth between 65-and-older households and those headed by individuals under the age of 35. It’s always been lopsided in favor of the elderly, about 10-1 in 1984. It’s gotten much worse of late. The ratio is now 46. What accounts for this disparity? It’s got almost everything to do with home ownership, despite the housing bubble collapse.

However, subtract the value of the home and at least half of the elderly are left with only their Social Security benefits. Pew Research:

If it had not been for home equity, the median net worth of older American households in 2009 would have been 33% lower than that of older households in 1984, instead of 42% higher.

We read much these days about the extravagant wealth and lifestyles of the rich and occasionally famous. But the Rest of Us exist in modest fashion. Take a look at this graphic from the Pew report.

Lumping everyone together yields a median net worth of $71,635. Those under 35, many of whom face years of college debt, have only $3,662—assets minus debts. Of course, most people in this age group do not own their homes, while nearly 80 percent of those 65 and older do, and their mortgages probably retired.

Economist Dean Baker:

In short, most of the country is looking at a situation where they are desperate for work or fearful about losing their job. Older workers are looking at a retirement where they are not far above the poverty level, even after spending a life working in middle class jobs.

Earlier in this post I remarked that my wife and I will likely be all right as we enter our “golden years.” I must say, though, that this wouldn’t be the case were it not for Social Security.

Unfortunately, conservatives and worse obsess over “reforming entitlements.” That’s thinly veiled code for reducing the benefits of Medicare and Social Security, despite their vital importance to most of the elderly. The Republicans shower contempt on anything public, including, of course, “government” in all its forms.

Against this right-wing tide Matthew Yglesias counters that Social Security is much too small. He argues that we should not have to “invest” in a myriad of private schemes or pay financial consultants through the nose. He writes:

But the best way to “save” for retirement is to live in a country with a properly functioning Social Security system. The way a system like this works is that a certain share of national income is set aside to support the living standards of elderly people. That share is collected as taxes from working people and paid out as benefits to retired people. The benefits received are proportional to the taxes you paid when working, so incentives to maximize earnings are preserved. This way everyone “saves” (i.e., reduces consumption below income) while working in exchange for a payoff that’s properly calibrated to overall long-term economic growth without needing to act as an “investor.”

Imagine that. Dispense with all the various pensions, public and private, eliminate individual retirement accounts and 401(k)s. Transfer all the money involved in these various instruments, including marketing, to one big Social Security system. No need to fear retirement, as the elderly would receive sufficient defined benefits to live comfortably.

The One-percenters can still “invest” all they want and live lavishly—or lose it all. They would contribute to Social Security as they do now, and the contributions would be capped, just as they are currently. I’d even let them take their SS benefits upon retirement just like the Rest of Us.

As for Medicare, imagine this.

No more private insurance companies. No more marketing. No more administration. No more caps. No more deductibles. No more co-pays. Instead, we’ll have a national health system in which every single person receives a card entitling them to proper health care, including prescription drugs. Need treatment? Show your card. Need a prescription filled? Show your card. Need long-term care? Show your card. The provider need not know if you’re rich or poor. Besides, the provider has the confidence that his or her bills will be paid.

Once again, if you’re rich and insist on lavish health care consistent with your lavish lifestyle, go ahead and buy supplemental coverage from your favorite broker. The Rest of Us won’t mind, since we’re covered.

Losing weight

December 28, 2011 by davald

A couple of years ago I experienced chest pains. When my wife arrived home I calmly asked her to take me to the hospital. Given my symptoms, the hospital staff admitted me. My blood pressure was dangerously high, and it wouldn’t decline. After staying overnight, I was subjected to a stress test the next day. Hours later a physician entered my room to report “good” news and “bad” news. The good: I have a healthy heart with no arterial blockage. The bad: I had Type II diabetes. I was then finally weighed. The scale revealed 276 pounds! Take a look. I’m standing next to my daughter, Kelly. The shirt is 3X.

 

I followed up with a visit to my regular doctor, who warned me of the dangers of diabetes. I had to lose weight by reducing calories and burning more through exercise. He referred me to a dietician, who, ironically, was as big around as she was tall. She gave me some reading materials, including a chart showing the carbohydrates of various foods. She also filled in a form that broke down what I was to eat and how much each day.

I learned what I had long suspected: there are many factors, including heredity, that play a role in both gaining and losing weight. My family, most of whom are dead and gone, carried a surplus of fat on their bodies. As a child, my weight fluctuated, though I was usually declared “husky,” wearing appropriately sized clothes. Here’s an early shot of me in the year of my birth.

As for wearing “husky,” my waist required pants befitting a much taller child. You can see for yourself in this photo.

I’m the one with the jeans rolled way up. Standing to my left is sister Nancy, who, though a year younger than I, was a bit taller for a few months, which caused me considerable grief. She, too, was prone to pudgy. Our father is standing between Nancy and me. My sister Liz is on the far left, with our brother Jim between the two of us. I should add the obvious: my shirt amplified my girth—bad choice.

I carried the “husky” label through the first couple of years of high school. Then I started growing up rather than out. Here I am on the far left at the age of 16. As you can see, I was now taller than sister Nancy to my left.

My college years at Cal yielded several ups and downs in my weight—from 165 lbs. as a high school senior to about 185 playing baseball on Evans Field in Berkeley. Once I left college, my weight tended to increase far more often than the opposite.

On two occasions, however, I managed to quickly shed bushels of pounds, at one point getting below 170. Otherwise, the waist expanded. I used to delude myself into believing that my body had a certain set point; I would simply stop gaining weight. Didn’t happen.

So let’s get back to the post-diabetes diagnosis. Frightened about the prospects of that insidious disease, I began walking three miles every day, then upped it to 4.5 miles, which I now traverse each morning at a 15-min/mile pace. Following a rigid diet, which I recorded dutifully in a spreadsheet along with my weight and blood pressure, I lopped off 70 pounds at one point, bringing me to just a bit over 200. I’ve since added back about 15, though I no longer keep track.

While I’m still considered overweight I’m a far cry from “morbidly obese,” which is what I was at 276. My vitals are within normal, as is my blood-sugar reading—my A1C dropped from over 7 to under 6, actually 5.5. That, too, is a normal level.

Readers who have struggled with their weight can attest to how difficult it is to lose pounds and keep them lost. It seems that we chubby people have bodies that don’t especially care to lose mass. After shedding a few pounds, our chemistry changes, making it difficult to stay slim or slimmer.

Such thoughts were triggered upon reading “The Fat Trap” in the New York Times. The author, who also struggles to keep off the pounds, offers us a bit more sympathy.

If anything, the emerging science of weight loss teaches us that perhaps we should rethink our biases about people who are overweight. It is true that people who are overweight, including myself, get that way because they eat too many calories relative to what their bodies need. But a number of biological and genetic factors can play a role in determining exactly how much food is too much for any given individual. Clearly, weight loss is an intense struggle, one in which we are not fighting simply hunger or cravings for sweets, but our own bodies.

I know that each pound on my body represents 3,500 calories. If I subtract 500 calories from my daily intake, I’ll lose one pound after a week. But the article suggests that this simple calculus may not work so well for chubby bodies. We may have to reduce the caloric intake a bit more and we won’t burn off the calories as efficiently as a “normal” person at the same weight.

Well, time for my walk.

We’ll save lives, but will it stand?

December 22, 2011 by davald

E.P.A. head Lisa Jackson announced yesterday that electric-generating plants will have to substantially reduce mercury emissions and other toxins. The Times:

The new rule, unless blocked by Congress or the courts, will be the first time the federal government has enforced limits on mercury, arsenic, acid gases and other poisonous and carcinogenic chemicals emitted by the burning of fossil fuels.

Mercury is bad stuff. It kills thousands of people a year, although you won’t find ‘mercury’ on the toe tag. That it took 20 years to finally get the regulation in place attests to the strength of financial interests vis-a-vis clear scientific evidence that mercury is not good for your health.

The biggest source of mercury, however, is coal combustion. About half of the electricity produced in the U.S. comes from coal-burning plants. Although the new regulation will not force closure of the plants it will require utilities and independent power producers to install scrubbers and other devices to prevent the mercury from entering the atmosphere. Here’s Wikipedia:

Mercury is a highly toxic element commonly found in coal and municipal waste. Wet scrubbers are only effective for removal of soluble mercury species, such as oxidized mercury, Hg2+. Mercury vapor in its elemental form, Hg0, is insoluble in the scrubber slurry and not removed. Therefore, additional process of Hg0conversion is required to complete mercury capture. Usually addition of the halogens to the flue gas are used for this purpose. The type of coal burned as well as the presence of a selective catalytic reduction unit both affect the ratio of elemental to oxidized mercury in the flue gas and thus the degree to which the mercury is removed.

Burning coal, a relatively cheap way to generate electricity, will now get more expensive, though not so much as to render the plants economically inviable. Nevertheless, there are more than 600 coal plants in the United States and both their owners and endusers wield bipartisan political influence. So, it remains to be seen whether or not E.P.A.’s new regulation will survive.

Of course, for the good of the planet and its numerous species all coal plants should be terminated. It’s going to get too damn hot if they’re not.

Sticking with the good news

December 8, 2011 by davald

From the Huffington Post:

After a year of unprecedented destruction attributed to weather extremes, federal officials and environmental advocates are focusing increased attention on the potential health impacts of global warming, which most scientists expect to spur not just more frequent instances of extreme heat, but also increases in rainfall, drought, snow, floods and violent storms.

“Climate change poses a serious threat to public health,” Dr. George Luber, the associate director for climate change at the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, said Thursday. Luber was speaking to reporters as part of the launch of an online, extreme-weather mapping tool developed by the Natural Resources Defense Council.

I wish it were otherwise, but I don’t expect anything significant to come out of the most recent talks on climate change. There’s just too damn much money to be made by relatively impatient people, who have apparently concluded that, since they won’t be around when all the fun starts, they might as well get a big piece of the financial action right now.

What a legacy we’re leaving our children and grandchildren.

Could politics trump science?

December 7, 2011 by davald

Silly question, of course. Politics in America almost always trump science. Why just today we get another example via the New York Times.

Health and Human Services Secretary Kathleen Sebelius on Wednesday overruled the Food and Drug Administration’s decision that emergency contraceptives be sold freely over the counter, including to teenagers 16 years old and younger.

The article goes to some length to detail the FDA’s studies, analyses, and conclusion—that the so-called morning after pill is safe and effective. Other medical professionals have confirmed that conclusion, which the Times reports.

Sibelius issued a statement, an excerpt of which appears in the article:

After careful consideration of the F.D.A. summary review, I have concluded that the data submitted by Teva do not conclusively establish that Plan B One-Step should be made available over the counter for all girls of reproductive age.

Now you might be wondering about Ms. Sebelius’s qualifications as a medical or scientific expert. Briefly put, she has none. She’s a lawyer by profession and training. Oh, and she’s a Roman Catholic.

The Church along with the Right Wing believe that conception confers personhood. Since the Plan B pill works to either prevent conception or, worse in the minds of conservatives, hinder a fertilized egg’s attaching to the uterus, as in a normal pregnancy, the emergency contraceptive is anathema, a favorite expression of popes.

We can be certain that Sebelius, notwithstanding her religious views, had the blessing of Obama, her boss. The risk of alienating independent voters in Iowa or Kansas outweighs the risks of unwanted pregnancies.

The president has his priorities, and poor teenage girls are way, way down the list. Let’s see how NOW reacts to this.

Rise in federal spending

November 27, 2011 by davald

Paul Krugman is frustrated. No matter how frequently and thoroughly he debunks would-be critics, he’s repeatedly barraged with the same pseudo arguments. This morning is no exception.

The topic in this case is the increase in federal spending, and how Obama is behaving like a conventional liberal—expanding the size of government. Krugman has taken this on again and again. So he does today.

Indeed, we should agree that federal spending as a percent of GDP has risen since Obama assumed office. But what are the numbers?

Well, part of the answer is that the ratio is up because the denominator is down. According to CBO estimates, in fiscal 2010 the economy operated about 7 percent below potential. This means that even if what the government was doing hadn’t changed, the federal spending share of GDP would have risen by 1.4 percentage points.

Krugman then breaks down the 1.4 figure spending increase. As sentient beings should understand, there is a recession going on for a huge slice of the population; and it’s not the One-percenters. Over several decades “those liberals” have established public safety nets to help those who need it, mostly the poor, the aged, and veterans. We should not be surprised, then, that spending has increased for unemployment benefits, Medicare  and Medicaid, and, since we’ve got wars going on that tend to injure military men and women, veterans’ health care and benefits.

Here’s a chart I prepared based on Krugman’s numbers. (Mine, I think, is prettier.)

Under “Other,” Krugman identifies stimulus money and an increase in the use of Medicare and Social Security by the Baby Boomers.

Krugman concludes:

What isn’t there, no way, nohow, is a massive expansion of government, which is a figment of the right wing’s imagination.

I’ll segue here to the PUD’s experience with its rate-discount programs. The utility offers lower rates to qualified low-income customers, including seniors. The tiers are 20, 40, and 60 percent, depending on income. We’ve discovered the rather obvious relationship between the unemployment rate and the number of customers receiving benefits under the programs. (We also provide similar discounts to water customers, whose numbers are a fraction of electric customers.)

The graph below shows program participation over the years. After climbing steadily during the last decade, the numbers tapered off after 2004 and actually fell in 2005. But we can see a huge jump over the last couple of years, reaching an all-time high of 15,346 as of October 2011. The corresponding budget for these programs has also risen, as we would expect.

Meanwhile, Washington’s legislature convenes tomorrow for a special session to wrestle with the newest revenue forecast, which is not pretty. As I mentioned in a previous post, the overwhelming majority of the state’s budget goes to education and just slightly less to human services, the programs that assist our at-risk citizens, whose ranks have swelled with the recession.

Unfortunately, albeit predictably, the Republicans assert that the revenue shortfall must be covered by cuts alone—no revenue increases. Olympia Democrats, as in D.C., will push for a combination of cuts and new revenues. The Herald warns that things could get “ugly” next week. The Seattle Times, as is its wont, pushes the legislators to show “courage.” WTF.

A bit more hypocrisy

November 22, 2011 by davald

Yesterday Governor Gregoire proposed a half-percent increase in the state’s sales tax. Faced with an even steeper decline in forecasted revenues than the last projection revealed, Gregoire, who must submit a balanced budget to the legislature, identified further spending cuts. But, she added, “I have seen the ramifications of the cuts. I can’t live with it.” So, she wants the voters to decide on whether or not to bump up the sales tax to restore some program funding. (Thanks to one of Tim Eyman’s initiatives, and the voters’ acquiescence, two-thirds of both Olympia houses must approve tax increases. As the Times points out, that’s a “political impossibility.” However, a simple majority of voters can raise taxes.)

In typical knee-jerk fashion, Republicans expressed outrage. The Times:

Senate Minority Leader Mike Hewitt, R-Walla Walla, said Gregoire is trying to frighten voters into supporting a tax increase.

“I think the governor sent Chicken Little out to scare people that the sky was falling, and she probably accomplished it,” Hewitt said. “I don’t agree that we should rush out and impose a tax on taxpayers. This is not the proper time to be doing these things.”

He should have added that for Republicans there is no time to raise taxes. Here comes the hypocrisy part.

The programs facing haircuts or decapitation are education and human services. The Everett Herald included a list:

* If voters approve a half-cent increase in the sales tax, Gregoire would use the revenue to restore these cuts.

Republicans when talking about taxes would have us believe that they are truly concerned about the burden on, well, taxpayers. It’s as if the GOP has a heart after all. You would be wrong to think this.

A substantial majority of the state’s budget goes to education and assisting at-risk citizens, the people who are down on their luck, sick, or otherwise struggling to survive. Those taxes we pay, however burdensome, educate our children and provide safety nets for the downtrodden, an increasing portion of our population.

When you hear a Republican grieve for the taxpayer know that he doesn’t give a rat’s rectum for the Rest of Us.

The “near poor”

November 19, 2011 by davald

The New York Times asked the US Census Bureau to analyze its considerable data to determine how many households are near poor. They do not live in poverty; they survive just above it, precariously so. The resulting numbers surprised Census analysts.

When the Census Bureau this month released a new measure of poverty, meant to better count disposable income, it began altering the portrait of national need. Perhaps the most startling differences between the old measure and the new involves data the government has not yet published, showing 51 million people with incomes less than 50 percent above the poverty line. That number of Americans is 76 percent higher than the official account, published in September. All told, that places 100 million people — one in three Americans — either in poverty or in the fretful zone just above it.

Critics from the right were quick to condemn the analysis. Here’s one example from the article:

“I don’t have any objection to this measure if you use the term ‘low-income,’ ” [Robert Rector of the Heritage Foundation] said. “But the emotionally charged terms ‘poor’ or ‘near poor’ clearly suggest to most people a level of material hardship that doesn’t exist. It is deliberately used to mislead people.”

I suspect that conservatives as a rule believe the poor are not all that poor and the rich are not all that rich. In practice, they strive to impose further hardship on low-income households and coddle the wealthy.

The article includes a graphic that visually displays the analysis. It really shouldn’t be used as a Rorschach test.

This should scare you

September 27, 2011 by davald

Health insurance premiums are soaring. If you’re lucky enough to have a job and your employer provides medical insurance, his or her annual cost has doubled in the last 10 years, from around $7,000 per annum to roughly $15,000 today. But hold on. That annual premium is expected to double again by the end of the next decade.

These frightening stats come by way of the Kaiser Family Foundation’s annual survey. (You can download the full report here.) The survey also found that employees are having to foot more of the bill. Couple that inconvenience with stagnant or falling wages, and we can appreciate that a worker’s take-home pay has declined, which also means that the worker is spending less on other things, which helps explain why the economy continues to sputter.

What I’ve never understood is why the business community and far too many of us oppose universal health care systems. They really do save money. And they relieve businesses of the complex and dreary task of managing employees’ insurance. For that matter, purveyors of medical care should also welcome universal health care; they wouldn’t have to fill out those endless forms for an unwieldy array of different insurers.

So, if a universal health care system is better than our private, market-based system, why don’t we have one? Simple. It’s called bribery, and it’s legal.

The job of members of Congress is to stay in office. To do so, they must raise and spend a boatload of dollars each election cycle. Who’s got the money? Corporations and rich individuals, many of whom are in the health insurance and delivery businesses. They buy Congress.

And they also fund huge propaganda campaigns to convince us that we have the best health care system in the world; anything else is just “socialism.” We believe this crap, which is why we re-elect Senator X.
