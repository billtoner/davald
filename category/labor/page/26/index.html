MLK was dangerous

January 16, 2012 by davald

Meek men don’t get assassinated. That fate is reserved for those who dare to pester the rich and the powerful. Martin Luther King annoyed the hell out of our nation’s elite. It was not for his stand against racism, though he played a significant role in the eventual passage of the Civil Rights Act. Rather, MLK condemned the fortress of capitalism, calling for a massive redistribution of wealth.

In a 1968 Memphis speech he called it “criminal” that a nation so rich would force men and women to work for “starvation wages.” He advocated a guaranteed income, and it was Richard M. Nixon who pushed Congress to pass relevant legislation; the idea of a basic income has been championed by those on the left (Karl Marx) and the right (e.g., Frederich Hayek and Milton Friedman).

Here are some quotes of King that appeared in a Socialist Party USA pamphlet:

The dispossessed of this country — the poor, the white and Negro — live in a cruelly unjust society. They must organize a revolution against that injustice, not against the lives of the persons who are their fellow citizens, but against the structures through which society is refusing to take means which have been called for, and which are at hand, to lift the load of poverty.

…

We must rapidly begin the shift from a ‘thing’-oriented society to a “person”-oriented society. When machines and computers, profit motives and property rights, are considered more important than people, the giant triplets of racism, materialism and militarism are incapable of being conquered.

…

I am now convinced that the simplest approach will prove the most effective — the solution to poverty is to abolish it directly by a now widely discussed measure: the guaranteed income.

In 1965 Martin Luther King wrote a letter for Pageant magazine. The essay was titled “The Bravest Man I Ever Knew.” That man was Norman Thomas, the leader of the Socialist Party. In a preface he apologized for being unable to attend the 80th birthday of Thomas; King was on his way to Oslo to accept the Nobel Prize for Peace. Here are some excerpts from the essay:

I can think of no man who has done more than you to inspire the vision of a society free of injustice and exploitation. While some would adjust to the status quo, you urged struggle. While some would corrupt struggle with violence or undemocratic perversions, you have stood firmly for the integrity of ends and means. Your example has ennobled and dignified the fight for freedom, and all that we hear of the Great Society seems only an echo of your prophetic eloquence. Your pursuit of racial and economic democracy at home, and of sanity and peace in the world, has been awesome in scope. It is with deep admiration and indebtedness that I carry the inspiration of your life to Oslo.

…

During our historic March on Washington in the summer of 1963, when 250,000 Negro and white Americans joined together in an outpouring of fellowship and brotherly cooperation for a world of freedom and equality, a little Negro boy listened near the Washington Monument to an eloquent orator.

Turning to his father, he asked: “Who is that man?”

Came the inevitable answer: “That’s Norman Thomas. He was for us before any other white folks were.”

…

Yet America has never fully utilized Thomas’s great abilities. He has been a marvelous unofficial ambassador-at-large to our friends in Europe, Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Would it not make sense to make him our official representative to the United Nations?

In his column today Paul Krugman acknowledges Dr. King’s contribution to ending legal sanctions against blacks and other people of color, though racism still casts a pernicious pall over our society. As an economist, also a Nobel Prize winner, Krugman concludes that de jure racism has given way to economic classism. Krugman:

Economic inequality isn’t inherently a racial issue, and rising inequality would be disturbing even if there weren’t a racial dimension. But American society being what it is, there are racial implications to the way our incomes have been pulling apart. And in any case, King — who was campaigning for higher wages when he was assassinated — would surely have considered soaring inequality an evil to be opposed.

King spoke of the growing chasm between the Haves and the Have-nots. In one of his speeches (you can view it yourself by clicking on the last link) he said that America spends over $320,000 for “each enemy we kill in Vietnam,” whereas we spend only $53 for each poor person in the War on Poverty. Krugman:

Mitt Romney says that we should discuss income inequality, if at all, only in “quiet rooms.” There was a time when people said the same thing about racial inequality. Luckily, however, there were people like Martin Luther King who refused to stay quiet. And we should follow their example today. For the fact is that rising inequality threatens to make America a different and worse place — and we need to reverse that trend to preserve both our values and our dreams.

Dr. King “refused to stay quiet,” for which he paid the ultimate sacrifice. MLK, rest in peace.

I feel a little bit better

January 13, 2012 by davald

Several weeks ago I started writing a post that was pure speculation, and it was about Apple, the company. It had occurred to me, without much evidence at all, that Tim Cook, who replaced Steve Jobs, would move differently on three fronts: employee relations, supplier labor, and the environment.

Having attended Michael Daisey’s one-man show, The Agony and Ecstasy of Steve Jobs, I was appalled by the working conditions at Foxconn, Apple’s principal supplier of iPhones and iPads. Nor was Apple faring well within the environmental community. Greenpeace, in particular, gave the company black marks for its production processes.  I believed then that Jobs could easily afford a kinder, gentler Apple, one that respected both labor and the environment.

Well, my speculation about Tim Cook may have been correct. He just sent an email to employees announcing significant changes. Among the highlights:

  • the release of a supplier responsibility report, which provided details on over 200 audits of supplier processes and policies, in particular the use of child labor (Daisey’s revealed that young teenagers were employed by Foxconn to assemble by hand hundreds of iPhones per day; after they developed disabling injuries from repetitive tasks, they were discarded then replaced with another batch.);
  • Apple has just joined the Fair Labor Association, the first technology company to do so;
  • Tim Cook is pushing for a “greener” Apple.

However, Tim Cook is no Steve Jobs, and here I mean that as a compliment—with respect to how he relates to employees. He comes across as modest, unassuming, and gracious; traits absent in Jobs. Almost immediately after assuming the reins, Cook told Apple employees that the company would match their charitable giving. From Walter Isaacson’s biography, we learned that Jobs was prickly at best on the topic of philanthropy, shunning invitations by Bill Gates and Warren Buffet to join their endeavors.

It’s about time, Apple. Now keep it up.

Political incest

December 19, 2011 by davald

For Obama and Democratic leaders who are trying to set an election agenda focused on income inequality, wage stagnation, and downward mobility for the middle and lower class, the prominence of Democratic lobbyists has become problematic.

Writing for the New York Times, professor Thomas Edsall treats us to some more disturbing news. Former Democratic members of Congress, who may have championed the needs and aspirations of the Rest of Us, let greed do their walking—straight to K Street. They now peddle on behalf of corporate interests as they put the screws to the narrowing middle class.

First on the list of malevolent turnarounds is Dick Gephardt, who used to defend labor and unions. Edsall:

Upon leaving Congress in 2005, Gephardt joined the Washington offices of DLA Piper as senior counsel. Two years later, he founded his own lobbying firm, the Gephardt Government Affairs Group.

By 2010, annual firm billings had shot up from $625,000 in 2007 to $6.59 million. Gephardt’s client list was blue chip, Goldman Sachs (paid Gephardt $200,000 in 2010); Boeing Co. ($440,000); Visa Inc. ($200,000); Ameren Corp, the energy holding company ($200,000); and Waste Management Inc., the leading provider of trash and garbage removal ($320,000).

A normally voluble political strategist and committed liberal who has worked with Gephardt in past elections said about the former Congressman’s lobbying clientele, “I don’t want to talk on the record, it sucks. It’s a sad situation for us.”

Union leaders like Richard Trumka (president of A.F.L-C.I.O.) spoke to this issue in a recent speech, quoted in the article:

Our role is not to build the power of a political party or a candidate. It is to improve the lives of working families and strengthen our country. It doesn’t matter if candidates and parties are controlling the wrecking ball or simply standing aside — the outcome is the same either way.

Money corrupts, and never more so than in Washington, D.C. The Supreme Court, in a series of rulings, has catapulted the role of money into the stratosphere. Obama is expected to raise and spend a billion dollars in his campaign for reelection.

And speaking of campaigns, they are obviously far too long, which makes them even more expensive. Parliamentary democracies limit the blabbering season as they also limit campaign spending.

More insidious, it seems, is that the corrupting power of money has left the Rest of Us devoid of effective advocates. Why represent workers for a pittance when you can siphon off millions in defense of the strong and mighty?

I’ve never been to Washington, D.C., despite many opportunities to do so on the public’s dime. I suspect that were I to travel there, the stench would overwhelm from miles away, through the plane’s ventilation system.

I like it

December 14, 2011 by davald

The Everett Herald‘s editors have broached an interesting alternative for the soon-to-be-shuttered Kimberly-Clark plant. They suggest converting it to produce hemp-based paper products. The hemp could be grown in Washington state.

Why do we cling to the notion that paper products must come from trees? At least two sustainable alternatives exist: Hemp and bamboo.

Industrial hemp, the blue-collar cousin to marijuana that has no psychoactive properties, has been grown for at least 12,000 years for fiber (textiles and paper) and food. (Thomas Jefferson drafted the Declaration of Independence on hemp paper, according to the North American Industrial Hemp Council.) It was grown commercially in the U.S. until the 1950s.

If it’s good enough for Tommy Jefferson, it’s good enough for the Rest of Us. Better to keep the plant running then to shut it down and cast several hundred workers to the street.

Not my father’s economy

December 12, 2011 by davald

I was born in 1947. I’ve been on the planet long enough to experience what now must be considered America’s glory days, years filled with rising prosperity, the realization of the American Dream, and financial wellbeing, as most workers were employed by large corporations promising job security and adequate pensions. This period began after WWII and lasted until the mid- to late-70s. In the 80s, conditions for the Rest of Us changed dramatically, and not for the better.

Consider this graph (I found savings data back to 1959, so that’s where the series begin.):

Not only were households spending more money they were also saving more. (Savings rate is percentage of disposable income.) About the time Ronald Reagan assumed office the trends began a downward trajectory. If the Rest of Us started putting fewer dollars in the bank and also reducing our spending, that can mean only one thing: our incomes were falling. Well, not for everyone (data from Emmanuel Saez).

 

Paralleling the Great Divergence was a major transformation of the economy. While we used to making things, increasingly the money began flowing out of manufacturing and into the financial sector, which really doesn’t make anything.

America’s middle class was strongly associated with blue-collar employment, which required skills but not necessarily college. A young man could land a job with General Motors, for example, and expect to stay there until retirement. He’d receive a continuously rising wage, medical and dental benefits, then a defined-benefit retirement sufficient to support a modest lifestyle for his remaining years. Moreover, he made enough money to buy a house, a car, appliances, and raise a family. His wife didn’t have to work. (I have essentially described my family’s existence, although my father worked at Standard Oil rather than General Motors.)

Granted, this Norman-Rockwell-America was not without its deep fissures. Women often felt unfulfilled if not also oppressed, their talents underutilized in the home; people of color were largely excluded; and suburban life was gradually eroding the environment. But for the predominantly white population things were mostly good and always looking better.

I used to think that the assassination of JFK signaled America’s loss of innocence, shattering Rockwell’s mythology. In retrospect, change was already afoot before that fateful Dallas afternoon, as these charts suggest.

The Sixties were an inchoate reaction to something going terribly wrong in our society. We were fighting a nasty, deadly war in the jungles of Southeast Asia, still engaging the communists around the globe, watching the murders of MLK and RJK on television, and witnessing the emergence of Black political consciousness—what had been suppressed for generations finally erupted in marches and massive demonstrations.

Meanwhile, the rich were sucking more and more wealth out of the economy as they purchased a compliant Congress to eliminate regulations and put the screws to the Rest of Us. We weren’t paying all that much attention.

The Great Divergence cannot long endure. Too much concentration of wealth combined with unchecked greed threatens everyone, including the One-percenters. Prof. Krugman opines that we’re in a depression, with the entire global financial sector behaving like a house of cards.

There must be an alternative, maybe an economy “as if people mattered.” Our current one is not working for the Rest of Us.

Still looking for a job? Good luck with that.

November 4, 2011 by davald

Whoopee. The economy added 80,000 jobs, nudging the unemployment rate down a tick. But don’t get your hopes up.

It appears that Marx was right about a few things, including this point:

The greater the social wealth, the functioning capital, the extent and energy of growth, and therefore also the greater absolute mass of the proletariat and the productivity of its labour, the greater is the industrial reserve army… [Capital, Vol. 1, 798; my italics].

Consider this sobering comment, too:

“We are very gradually moving to a labor-less society,” said Bernard Baumohl, chief global economist at The Economic Outlook Group. Firms, he said, “are making sure that the number of workers they have now are able to produce more if necessary. The problem is that there are going to be a lot of Americans who are going to remain unemployed for a very long time.”

Those lucky enough to be working are producing more with less.

About those one percent, they’re doing quite well, thank you very much. While the bottom 99 percent have seen their incomes stagnant or falling, the very rich are gobbling up more and more of the nation’s total wealth.

Notice that incomes were rising steadily, albeit modestly, following the end of WWII—until the mid-70s. (Chart based on data from Emmanuel Saez, UC Berkeley) The Congressional Budget Office has data to 2007. It’s most recent report includes this graph:

 

By the way, to be eligible for the top one percent you’ll need an minimum income of about $350,000. The first quintile’s income starts at zero dollars and ends at around $20,000. Entrance into the top 10 percent requires an income of almost $75,000. So, 90 percent of Americans make less than $75,000. The average income of those in the top .01 percent is above $4 million.

Yes, the rich are different, extremely so.

Higher wages and higher employment

September 28, 2011 by davald

This seems counterintuitive: raising wages yields higher output. Intuition suggests that by lowering wages, all other things being equal, more people would be working, since employers prefer low unit labor costs. And with more people working at lower wages, firms would produce more output, thereby increasing GDP. However, this situation does not obtain under certain conditions. What are those conditions? Zero interest rates and either deflation or very low inflation, both operative in the current economy.

I argued previously that higher wages were necessary to spur recovery. That’s because we’re suffering from insufficient aggregate demand. In other words, we don’t have enough money to buy goods and services, which means that companies will produce less, if at all. Declining production leads to layoffs and reduced aggregate demand—and so on. Indeed, the latest report on wages shows that we’re making less money and therefore buying fewer products and services.

Increased productivity can, and I think should, lead to increased wages. But compensation hasn’t kept pace with productivity. Somebody has benefited from the higher output per worker. Here’s a hint: it’s not the worker.

There’s an interesting paper linked by the Bureau of Labor Statistics: “The compensation-productivity gap: a visual essay.” As the title suggests, there are lots of graphs and charts. Here’s one:

Since the end of WWII, we have been working more efficiently, increasing output with fewer labor-hours. But beginning in the early 70s employers, rather than workers, pocketed the difference. (By the way, this growing gap fits in nicely with my Great Divergence observation. America, in very many significant respects, began to look and feel different after Nixon assumed office.)

We hear a lot these days about “inflexible” wages. Those who use such terms believe that workers make too much. That if wages fell, employment would rise. Krugman and others disagree. Here’s Krugman:

Under depression-type conditions, with short-term interest rates near zero, there’s no reason to think that lower wages for all workers — as opposed to lower wages for a particular group of workers — would lead to higher employment.

Krugman refers to a paper by Gauti Eggertsson of the New York Federal Reserve. It’s worth quoting from the abstract:

Can government policies that increase the monopoly power of firms and the militancy of unions increase output? This paper studies this question in a dynamic general equilibrium model with nominal frictions and shows that these policies are expansionary when certain “emergency” conditions apply. These emergency conditions–zero interest rates and deflation–were satisfied during the Great Depression in the United States. Therefore, the New Deal, which facilitated monopolies and union militancy, was expansionary, according to the model.

The Great Divergence coincided with a decline in union participating rates and a sharp fall in the marginal income tax rate. That’s also when the really, really rich began extracting huge—and I mean huge—dollars from the economy, at the expense of the Rest of Us.

By the way, the vertical axis represents annual income.

Union workers began disappearing during the late 50s (source). How low will the red line go?

FDR evidently encouraged union militancy or, at least, looked the other way. But irascible Labor, demanding more money and better working conditions, helped pull the nation out of the Depression.

No such parallels today, however. With union participation rates falling and falling, Labor has but a small voice to push back against the rising power of Capital.

As I’ve suggested elsewhere, this lopsided arrangement bodes ill for the Rest of Us, but also for Capital itself. We ain’t got no money to buy Capital’s products.

You’re not making enough money

September 16, 2011 by davald

You may hear “experts” talk about structural unemployment, which is kind of code for inflexible wages, which, in turn, means that we’re making too much money. Gosh, we could employ everyone if we’d work for nothing.

But I have a different idea. The problem in America and elsewhere is that wages are too low. The steady gains made by workers after WWII suddenly stopped in the 70s, leading to the Great Divergence. Yes, the rich got richer and the poor got poorer. That’s a problem, and here’s why.

The Rest of Us spend most of the money we make. The rich, on the other hand, are too busy enhancing their portfolios to invest in productive enterprises. And they can only spend so much to meet their needs and satisfy their wants; the rest is surplus, and it’s mostly surplus. Contrary to Speaker Boehner’s b.s., the rich are not “job creators.” They’re money hoarders who don’t give a damn about the Rest of Us.

If the Rest of Us made more money we’d actually get the economy humming again. Also, we’d be paying more taxes, helping governments fund education, build transportation systems, clean the air and water, and so on.

Okay. How do we get more money?

Well, the rich have cornered the market on stealing it. We’ll have to rely on legitimate means.

We could, of course, simply shut down the economy with a massive withdrawal of labor. Not going to happen? I hear you.

The wonkheads, including Ben Bernanke and Paul Krugman, have written that the central bank can and should target a higher inflation rate. Today that target is around two percent. But because we’re in a liquidity trap, a higher inflation rate would lead to higher wages—and prices, too.

We could form and join unions, which should deliver higher wages. Moreover, higher union participation rates correlate with greater wealth equality. (Note: The higher the Gini index the greater the inequality.)

We could demand that Congress increase taxes on capital gains and otherwise deter non-productive uses of money. While they’re at it, the senators and representatives should restore marginal tax rates to the levels associated with the era of economic growth, namely the 50s and 60s. Huh? Higher tax rates correlate with a rising GDP? It seems so.

By the way, a growing economy yields higher wages. So whatever strategies designed to stimulate the economy should be employed. And, no, it’s not lower taxes and fewer regulations, pace Boehner. Indeed, it’s just the opposite.

Do you see a pattern here? The richer the rich get the worse things are for the Rest of Us.
