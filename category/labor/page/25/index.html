Agglomeration

March 1, 2012 by davald

Not long before he died Steve Jobs and other Silicon Valley entrepreneurs had dinner with Pres. Obama. Obama wondered aloud how the U.S. might resurrect its manufacturing sector. In particular, why couldn’t Apple’s products be made in America.

Jobs replied that Apple’s Chinese suppliers employed 700,000 workers. This huge workforce required 30,000 engineers for support. There is no way, Jobs reportedly said, to cobble together that many technical people in the U.S. “If you could educate these engineers,” Jobs told Obama, “we could move more manufacturing plants here.” (Walter Isaacson’s biography of Steve Jobs, page 546)

One other selling point for China’s “free enterprise zones” is what economists call “agglomeration.” In the case of the iPhone, for example, all of the parts that must eventually be assembled are made in plants located in the same geographical area. It helps, of course, that China has a surfeit of qualified assemblers and technicians whose wages are much lower than those of would-be American counterparts. The Times:

Apple executives say that going overseas, at this point, is their only option. One former executive described how the company relied upon a Chinese factory to revamp iPhone manufacturing just weeks before the device was due on shelves. Apple had redesigned the iPhone’s screen at the last minute, forcing an assembly line overhaul. New screens began arriving at the plant near midnight [Jobs didn’t like the plastic screens because they too easily scratched. He persuaded Corning’s CEO to begin massive production of “gorilla glass” for the new iPhone. Corning built a factory next door to Foxconn.]

A foreman immediately roused 8,000 workers inside the company’s dormitories, according to the executive. Each employee was given a biscuit and a cup of tea, guided to a workstation and within half an hour started a 12-hour shift fitting glass screens into beveled frames. Within 96 hours, the plant was producing over 10,000 iPhones a day.

“The speed and flexibility is breathtaking,” the executive said. “There’s no American plant that can match that.”

In his blog this morning Paul Krugman talks about the “Motor City” and why it made economic sense to bail out Detroit. The automobile companies that received federal largesse indirectly employed many thousands of additional workers who supplied Chrysler, General Motors, et al. with the parts for their vehicles. Krugman:

Agglomeration economies exist because the whole is more than the sum of the parts — because the network of suppliers, the skills, the interchange of knowledge supported by a geographical industry concentration in turn gives firms in that industry concentration an advantage over firms elsewhere.

Boeing obviously didn’t get the memo when its leaders decided to globalize the manufacturing and assembling of its new 787 Dreamliner. To accommodate this vast network, Boeing persuaded Washington state’s legislature to subsidize the construction of a new port on Puget Sound to receive parts manufactured elsewhere. In other words, Washington taxpayers contributed to Boeing’s flawed experiment.

The Apple way, in contrast, takes advantage of “agglomeration economies.” While its devices are designed in Cupertino, they are manufactured and assembled in SE China. With the possible exception of Motor City, the U.S. lacks the necessary geographical nexus of skills and equipment to churn out thousands of electronic devices a day.

With respect to Boeing, I thought it made more sense to agglomerate all of the parts-making and assembly within a geographical network. The Everett site, as far as I know, was ideal.

If agglomeration works for Detroit and Apple, why not for Boeing?

Sure, it’s the workers’ fault

February 29, 2012 by davald

Stockton, California, faces imminent bankruptcy. Should that likely event occur, the city’s default will be the nation’s largest. Located 80 miles east of San Francisco, it is home to 300,000 residents.

As the housing bubble expanded people flocked to this Central Valley city to snatch up homes far cheaper than those in the Bay Area. Property tax revenue flowed into the city’s coffers then spent as if housing prices would rise forever.

But when the bubble burst, property values plummeted, leaving the city with increasing liabilities and fewer means to pay for them.

City Manager Bob Deis blamed previous administrations for the city’s troubles, saying that in his 32 years of municipal management he had “never seen such poor fiscal management practices.”

So, who gets the blame? That’s right, the city workers and their “generous retiree health benefits.”

Give me a break.

Before, rather than after, the fact

February 26, 2012 by davald

The current education reform movement, one of dozens since the establishment of compulsory public schooling more than a century ago, emphasizes teacher evaluations using standardized test scores. Teachers’ unions, understandably, resist such efforts, arguing that test scores are inherently unreliable predictors of future academic performance and adulthood success. Moreover, the most determining factor is a student’s socioeconomic standing; poverty and low test scores strongly correlate.

This past week the New York Times acquired the ratings of New York City teachers. They were graded on a typical bell curve, based on their students’ test results. That’s only the second time that a school district’s individual teacher evaluations were made public, Los Angeles being the first.

In the Washington state legislature the matter of teacher evaluations is generating considerable heat, producing fractures in the Democratic Party, with the Washington Education Association pitted against well-heeled party donors. There are two main issues at stake: the use of test scores in evaluating educators and charter schools. (On several occasions voters have turned thumbs down on the latter.)

All of this strikes me as wrongheaded, because the focus is after the fact rather than before. What do I mean?

I have written several posts on Finland’s educational reform, mostly to offer a contrast to our reform movements (e.g., here and here). Finnish students didn’t always do well on international tests. Indeed, political and educational leaders judged their system deeply flawed. What to do?

Here is where the Finnish experiment sharply diverges from America’s, with one exception. In both the U.S. and Finland the powers that be acknowledged that the classroom teacher made all the difference, after factoring for external influences (e.g., poverty, ethic background, parents’ education). In America, however, the emphasis is on rewarding or punishing existing teachers, depending on their test-based evaluations.

The Finns decided to do things differently. They would strive to put only the best and the brightest teachers in the classrooms, then leave them alone to educate children. They would not subject students to standardized tests until they had reached 16 years of age, at which point they would take a comprehensive examination to help determine their future path: academic or vocational. The split, by the way, is about half and half.

To ensure that only the best become teachers, the Finns adopted a rigorous selection process. It is now more difficult to become a teacher in Finland than an engineer or a biologist. Those students who choose elementary education acquire competency in all of the subjects they will eventually teach, including math, science, history, literature, art, and so on. Those electing to teach in academic high schools leave their programs with a thorough understanding of their chosen discipline, whether it be math or biology or philosophy or drama.

It’s no secret that those entering America’s teacher-training programs have lower GPAs and SAT scores than their counterparts in other fields. I would not doubt that the relatively low salaries of teachers (as compared to the incomes of lawyers and doctors and other professions requiring a similar amount of college) deter a large chunk of the best and the brightest from wanting to teach, especially given the high costs of a college education. (One can more easily retire student debts as a lawyer than as a teacher.) Another factor, I suspect, is the relatively low prestige associated with teaching. Finnish teachers, on the other hand, are almost revered, and after several years of teaching their salaries reflect their societal standing.

It seems to me, then, that we are wrong to focus on after-the-fact teacher evaluations. Better to concentrate on developing superior teacher-training programs, select only the best and the brightest applicants, then pay these highly qualified teachers more money.

Before closing, I should mention two other large differences between the U.S. and Finland. The Finns have a much more egalitarian society than we have in America, and their poverty rate is much lower than ours (eight percent for Finland; 17.3 percent for U.S.—50% of current median income). Also, the Finns invest heavily in education, constructing state-of-the-art learning facilities and making schooling free, up to and including college and university.*

Despite the Finnish success, however, there have been no major efforts to adopt Finland’s model. Instead, it’s pretty much union-badgering in America, conveniently ignoring the fact that almost all workers in Finland, including teachers, belong to a union.

Yes, it would take time to transition from the status quo to a more Finnish experience. And it would likely take more money.

However, America doesn’t lack for money, but its priorities are definitely perverse. We say that we value education. We say that we want nothing but the best for our children. We say that we want the best teachers. We say that we want high-quality learning facilities.

Well, what are we waiting for?

* Nevertheless, the U.S. spends more per capita on education than do the Finns. See this info-graphic for more details. While there, note the test-score comparisons. I don’t have an explanation at the moment as to how Finland provides for a free university education while spending less than the U.S. on a per-student basis. Off the top, I’d suggest that we’re not spending our dollars wisely vis-à-vis the Finns.

An ironic visit

February 25, 2012 by davald

As my wife and I walked to dinner last night along Colby Ave. we couldn’t help notice a couple of young people holding signs on the street corners. Drawing closer we saw the text: “Newt 2012.” The young man sported a T-shirt, despite the shivering temperatures. The young woman, perhaps only a girl, was enthusiastic, certainly, though I wonder if either of these sign-holding supporters could tell me why a Newt Gingrich in the White House would improve their lives.

So, Newt did arrive, giving a campaign pitch in the historic Everett Theater. Such irony.

Just a block away, around the corner at Hewitt and Wetmore, the Wobblies began a series of demonstrations in 1916, culminating in the “bloodiest battle in Pacific Northwest labor history.” Several hundred “armed anarchists” boarded the vessel Verona in Seattle with an Everett port destination. Word of their impeding arrival galvanized law enforcement and volunteers to greet the boat. A shot rang out, triggering a violent exchange, which left a dozen Wobblies dead.

Almost a hundred years later the very conservative Newt arrives in Everett with a much different message than One Big Union. He told his audience that if elected he would bring the price of gasoline down to $2.50 a gallon. He’d initiate strategies that would make the U.S. the world’s largest producer of oil. America would become energy independent. The Herald:

Gingrich said that, if elected, he would approve a proposed oil pipeline to carry Canadian oil produced from tar sands to refineries in Texas. He also would reopen offshore leases in the Gulf of Mexico for development and allow oil exploration in Alaska.

Added up, those opportunities could increase the U.S. supply by more than 2 million gallons a day. [This must be a misquote; it should probably read “2 million barrels a day.”]

“If we set our minds to it, by the end of this decade we’d be the largest oil producer in the world,” he said.

Recall that the U.S. currently produces about six million barrels a day. But we consume three times that amount.

Newt also said that if elected he would abolish the capital gains tax, reduce the corporate tax rate to 15 percent, and impose a flat 15-percent tax on everyone’s income. If you like government debt, you’ll love Gingrich’s proposals. So would Romney, since almost all his income is in the form of capital gains.

Sad to learn that Everett, a former union stronghold, could play host to one of the GOP loonies. And Newt has got to be the looniest of the bunch.

Whither democratic markets?

February 20, 2012 by davald

Thomas B. Edsall, writing for the New York Times, asks a provocative question: “Is this the end of market democracy?” While he stops short of providing an answer, he surveys the political and economic landscape to reveal sharp divisions in both the cause of and the cure for what ails us. He quotes Lawrence Summers, now back at Harvard:

Serious questions about the fairness of capitalism are being raised. These are driven by sharp increases in unemployment beyond the business cycle – one in six of American men between 25 and 54 is likely to be out of work even after the economy recovers – combined with dramatic rises in the share of income going to the top 1 percent (and even the top 0.01 percent) of the population and declining social mobility. The problem is real and profound and seems very unlikely to correct itself untended.

Focus on that last line. The problem “seems very unlikely to correct itself untended.” This suggests more of the same, rising wealth amidst a shrinking middle class marked by high unemployment—unless someone tends.

How shall we tend?

Edsall cites Jeffrey Sachs, who advocates: “a social democracy — capitalism plus a hefty dose of state support for families, education, early childhood development, higher education, and active labor market policies — can still do the job. The performance of northern Europe, around 120 million people including Germany, Austria, the Netherlands, Denmark, Sweden and Norway, provides a good illustration of this success.”

Readers of this blog will surely recognize that theme. But, as Edsall quickly points out, the Republicans, especially this current crop of presidential hopefuls, rebuke such a perceived turn toward “socialism,” exposing their ignorance, their cynicism, or both. If they are aware of the Nordic exception, they refuse to admit its lessons for fear of denigrating the U.S.A., which at all times and under all circumstances must be forever praised as the “shining beacon on a hill.”

Is the Great Divergence a sign of market failure? Not so, according to a couple of economists cited in Edsall’s piece.

In a paper published by the Council on Foreign Relations, Spence and co-author Sandile Hlatshwayo argue that the employment problems of the United States do not result from market failure. Just the opposite: the problems arise from an exceptionally efficient global marketplace. Instead of benefiting from the market, many in the United States, particularly those holding mid-level skill jobs that can be performed at lower cost overseas, are paying the costs of efficiency — the victims, in effect, of creative destruction.

All of which begs the question: What about the Nordic experience? Why doesn’t “an exceptionally efficient global marketplace” adversely impact Finland, or Sweden, or Norway as it allegedly does here? Why is “creative destruction” operating in the U.S. and not Germany and Denmark?

Edsall concludes his article thusly:

The debate over the workings of democracy, the market, technology and globalization remains unresolved. The political system instinctively avoids this debate, despite its salience and centrality, because the political costs of engagement are likely to substantially outweigh any potential gains. At an undetermined point in the not too distant future, however, as the “gale of creative destruction” blows through the heartland, the debate will become inescapable.

For better or worse, and I think it’s the latter, our political mechanisms, however defined or originated, thwart genuine tending. Edsall suggests that there are high “political costs of engagement.” I believe he’s correct. Neither side of the debate dares to address the fundamental problems of our economy, which is clearly not working for the Rest of Us. Instead the politicians appeal narrowly to their wealthy beneficiaries as they also stoke the fires of ideological hate.

In a previous post I mentioned an op-ed that appeared in the New York Times. Eric X. Li, a self-described “venture capitalist,” offered this gloomy assessment:

History does not bode well for the American way. Indeed, faith-based ideological hubris may soon drive democracy over the cliff.

I suspect that the very wealthy shrug off such premonitions. Regardless of what happens in America they will surely benefit, because their wealth allows them to exploit opportunities on a truly global scale. They are not, strictly speaking, Americans. Nor are they democratic in any meaningful sense. Rather, they are nation-neutral, just like the international economy; and they don’t give a damn about the Rest of Us, because they don’t have to. Indeed, we are a mere nuisance, at best, invisible, at least.

What I find truly pathetic is that the Rest of Us don’t seem to give a damn either. We’re as likely to chalk up our struggles to the natural order of things or the efficient operation of markets as to believe that all of this is deliberate, the continuing result of unopposed machinations funded and orchestrated by the One-percenters.

We are therefore right to condemn Congress and its corrupt politicians.* Yet, our political system was devised to give the people voice—”we the people” established a written constitution. As it turns out we are a representative democracy. Thus, we are supposed to rely on the people we elect to carry out our preferences. Therein lies the fatal flaw.

What we would prefer gains no traction, because our voice can hardly be heard over the din of unbridled capital. The folks we send to Washington elevate remaining in place to the highest priority. While they will need our votes to stay in office, how we vote is largely determined by campaign rhetoric, bought and paid for by monied interests. To assume that none of the propaganda makes a difference is to ignore what has happened in the Republican primaries. Candidates’s fortunes have risen and fallen in the minds of the voters by one slew of attack ads or another. This expensive negative shit matters.

Insofar as our “representatives” cotton to the well-heeled with impunity, there seems little prospect of crafting an alternative economy that benefits the Rest of Us—unless we dare think outside the political box. If the status quo isn’t working, we might consider something else.

All options are on the table. The debate is “inescapable.”

*  Corrupt, def.: “having or showing a willingness to act dishonestly in return for money or personal gain.”

Inequality a persistent curse

February 16, 2012 by davald

I just finished a refreshing essay on income inequality and what political leaders did a hundred years ago to mitigate its effects. The very wealthy back then were fearful of revolution, so they took some minor steps to keep the rabble in line.

Following a series of violent strikes, including murder-by-bombing, President William Taft formed the Commission on Industrial Relations. Many were called to testify, including William D. “Big Bill” Haywood, general secretary of the IWW. (The “Wobblies,” as IWW members were called, may have for a time claimed my grandfather as a member.) Beverly Gage writes:

Imagine the federal government inviting Noam Chomsky to weigh in on American class relations at an official hearing, throw in a murder charge and a series of mass revolts, and you have some idea of how just how ambitious the commission’s experiment in democracy really was.

After hearing testimony, the commission recommended a number of changes. Congress eventually adopted an inheritance tax and support for union organizing. The year was 1912.

One hundred years later, the wealthy have no designs to address income inequality. Absent violent protests, they would sooner larger gaps between the rich and the Rest of Us, otherwise known as timid sheep.

Making cars—again

February 13, 2012 by davald

You may have seen the Chrysler Super Bowl ad, the one featuring Clint Eastwood’s voice telling us that it was “half-time in America.” It seems that Detroit is making a comeback, thanks mostly to federal bailouts orchestrated by Pres. Obama. Cue the GOP boos.

I had an uncomfortable feeling while watching this slick video. Is that the only thing America can make these days—cars and trucks and SUVs? Whether or not true, the planet asks for fewer automobiles, regardless of who manufactures them. Surely there are better things to do.

We’ve got huge problems, and they’re getting worse. Millions still out of work, with scant hope of reclaiming their previous jobs. We lack what every other industrial democracy has—universal health care. People are losing their homes, often through no fault of their own. We face mounting public and private debt. We’ve got precious few surplus dollars to stick in the bank. Comfortable retirement may be the new American Dream, with so many of us baby boomers reaching 65 with collapsed 401(k)s. Rising Medicare costs show no signs of abating. The gap between the very rich and the Rest of Us is greater than at any time since the Great Depression. Our schools need urgent repair, as do our roads and bridges. We’ve done nothing to cure our addiction to oil and coal, so greenhouse gases continue to accumulate, promising wide-scale devastation by the end of the century. Most of us will be long gone by then, but our grandchildren will not thank us for the mess we’re now brewing.

I’ve begun reading Ronald Susskind’s Confidence Men: Wall Street, Washington, and the Education of a President. The year is 2007. Obama is still a senator from Illinois, but already in the midst of the presidential campaign. Polls show that he trails Hillary Clinton by 21 points. He’s set aside a couple of hours in a tight schedule to confer with his economic advisory team, led by Austan Goolsbee. That morning Obama had delivered a speech on Iran, quoting JFK’s line, “Let us never negotiate out of fear, but let us never fear to negotiate.” It did not go well. So Obama was hoping for a domestic issue that could rally the faithful and overcome his deficit with Clinton.

Already there were signs of a fragile housing market; yet Wall Street had bet heavily on the mortgage-based collateralized debt obligations. One of his advisors suggested that things could begin unraveling, although Ben Bernanke had estimated that only $100 billion was at stake should the housing bubble collapse. Not one of your finer moments, Ben.

Obama took a swig from his water bottle and sat up, ramrod straight. “Okay, in year two of my administration, when the housing bubble finally bursts, I come to you guys as my economic advisers and say, ‘What do we do!’ Well, what do we do?”*

Prescient, certainly. As for the advisors’ plan?

Everyone in the room was concerned about the ongoing decline in work traditionally done by men, and that would only exacerbate when the bubble collapsed, kicking hundreds of thousands of construction workers to the curb. A few talked about renewable energy. Obama could address global warming while putting people into green-collar jobs. How many? Two million tops, came the reply. What else?

The health care sector was expanding, adding more jobs each day. But Obama said that was mostly “women’s work,” a phrase that took everyone aback. Obama:

“Look, these are guys,” he said. “A lot of them see health care, being nurse’s aides, as women’s work. They need to do something that fits with how they define themselves as men.”

Then Alan Kreuger, a Princeton economist, blurted out: “Infrastructure. Rebuilding infrastructure.”

Obama liked it. Susskind:

And there it was: the mind of a man who hoped to be president, showing how it bent toward integration; coolly fitting disparate, competing analyses into a coherent whole and then seasoning this with a dollop of trenchant human insight. And just like that, a policy to repair the nation’s infrastructure was born. The federal government, in partnership with the private sector, would call upon the underemployed men of America to rebuild the country, and in doing so restore their pride.

Ah, but we know how this story ends. The Republicans dropped a giant turd into Obama’s punchbowl.

So, we build cars, while the nation’s infrastructure further rots. The bastards.

Social output gaps

February 10, 2012 by davald

Some economists have rightly focused on the gap in GDP from the pre-recession trend to the present. I calculated that the economy lost about $16 trillion in gross domestic product, or about $1.3 trillion a year since the collapses of the housing bubble and Lehman Brothers.

There is another important gap, which has drawn much attention since the release of Charles Murray‘s new book, Coming Apart: The State of White America, 1960-2010. Therein Murray described the income gap between the rich and the poor as “more a symptom than a cause.” New York Times columnist David Brooks quickly glommed onto Murray’s central thesis, that a breakdown in moral virtues prevents the lower classes from landing and maintaining adequate-wage jobs, the foundation for a stable family life.

Which is it, virtue before money, or the other way around? Consensus is lacking, as this New York Times article reveals.

One reason for the growing gap in achievement, researchers say, could be that wealthy parents invest more time and money than ever before in their children (in weekend sports, ballet, music lessons, math tutors, and in overall involvement in their children’s schools), while lower-income families, which are now more likely than ever to be headed by a single parent, are increasingly stretched for time and resources. This has been particularly true as more parents try to position their children for college, which has become ever more essential for success in today’s economy.

A study by Sabino Kornrich, a researcher at the Center for Advanced Studies at the Juan March Institute in Madrid, and Frank F. Furstenberg, scheduled to appear in the journal Demography this year, found that in 1972, Americans at the upper end of the income spectrum were spending five times as much per child as low-income families. By 2007 that gap had grown to nine to one; spending by upper-income families more than doubled, while spending by low-income families grew by 20 percent.

“The pattern of privileged families today is intensive cultivation,” said Dr. Furstenberg, a professor of sociology at the University of Pennsylvania.

…

There are no easy answers, in part because the problem is so complex, said Douglas J. Besharov, a fellow at the Atlantic Council. Blaming the problem on the richest of the rich ignores an equally important driver, he said: two-earner household wealth, which has lifted the upper middle class ever further from less educated Americans, who tend to be single parents.

The problem is a puzzle, he said. “No one has the slightest idea what will work. The cupboard is bare.”

Paul Krugman falls clearly on the money before virtue side of the argument. If morals were so important to family cohesion, Krugman asks, then why the “plunge in teenage pregnancies among all racial groups since 1990 or the 60 percent decline in violent crime since the mid-90s. Could it be that traditional families aren’t as crucial to social cohesion as advertised?”

The evidence, Krugman asserts, suggests that job opportunities for poor and under-educated males have fallen because women work and their wages are closing in on men’s. Krugman:

Most of the numbers you see about income trends in America focus on households rather than individuals, which makes sense for some purposes. But when you see a modest rise in incomes for the lower tiers of the income distribution, you have to realize that all — yes, all — of this rise comes from the women, both because more women are in the paid labor force and because women’s wages aren’t as much below male wages as they used to be.

For lower-education working men, however, it has been all negative. Adjusted for inflation, entry-level wages of male high school graduates have fallen 23 percent since 1973. Meanwhile, employment benefits have collapsed. In 1980, 65 percent of recent high-school graduates working in the private sector had health benefits, but, by 2009, that was down to 29 percent.

Men without family-wage jobs are less likely to get married and raise families. Moreover, working women rely less and less on men for income, since they have their own. Thus, marriage rates have fallen for all classes, but especially among those without a college education.

Krugman concludes:

So we should reject the attempt to divert the national conversation away from soaring inequality toward the alleged moral failings of those Americans being left behind. Traditional values aren’t as crucial as social conservatives would have you believe — and, in any case, the social changes taking place in America’s working class are overwhelmingly the consequence of sharply rising inequality, not its cause.

While I tend to concur with Krugman, that money precedes virtue in social and economic achievement, it does not follow that becoming a more egalitarian society will evaporate individual differences. We discussed this in a previous post. Humans are not “infinitely malleable,” so changing conditions or social relations—as in putting all of us on a more level playing field—will not eliminate either competition for scarce resources or what seems to be the natural tendency to stratify and form hierarchies.

But I beg to differ with Mr. Besharov that “no one has the slightest idea what will work.” As followers of this blog can attest, we do have answers, just not here. If it is our goal as a nation to reduce poverty, educate the masses, and otherwise provide the requisite foundations for a well-functioning society—then we can certainly learn a thing or two from the Nordic experience, if not also from France and Germany.

These countries have lower unemployment rates, lower poverty rates, and better-educated populations. Health care is deemed a right rather than a privilege, as it is in the U.S. People in Sweden, Norway, et al. don’t have to worry as much about their retirements or the vagaries of life. Ample social safety nets are in place, paid for by higher taxes than we Americans pay.

We say that we don’t know what will or will not work because of our national myopia. There is only the U.S. and nothing else matters. Tragic.

Unions and jobs

February 1, 2012 by davald

Indiana has just today become the 23rd state to become “right-to-work.” Quoted in this New York Times article is Governor Mitch Daniels (who gave the anti-state-of-the-union address):

“Seven years of evidence and experience ultimately demonstrated that Indiana did need a right to work law to capture jobs for which, despite our highly rated business climate, we are not currently being considered.”

So, does a high union participation rate—more likely found in closed or open shops states—retard employment? (data from BLS)

We’ll note that the unemployment rate was lower in the U.S. during periods of higher union participation rates. So do unions cost jobs? Certainly not at first glance.

What about elsewhere? (2010 data from OECD)

The U.S. has the lowest union density rate among the selected countries, yet has the highest Gini index, indicating greater inequality. Its unemployment rate is nevertheless higher than Germany’s, Finland’s, Norway’s, Denmark’s, and the OECD average. At a second glance, it does not seem that unions interfere with job creation, as Mitch Daniels contends.

John Kenneth Galbraith, writing many years ago, suggested that unions, or Labor in general, served as a counterforce to Capital, keeping a lid on capitalism’s excesses. He made these remarks when union participation rates were much higher than they are today, at just over six percent of the private sector work force. The decline of unionization in the U.S. coincided with the rise in wealth of the One-percenters, which paralleled the rise in inequality.

We’ve also learned that the more egalitarian countries stand a greater chance of recovering from the Great Recession than the U.S. Also, countries with lower Gini coefficients have greater social mobility; members of the lower class have a better shot at moving up than those in the U.S.

In fine, I would describe Mitch Daniels’s comments as bull shit.

Point of view

January 18, 2012 by davald

It’s trivial to make this point, but I’ll make it anyway. What we see (or think we see) is largely dependent on our point of view, the perspective from which we observe the world; and it applies not just to ordinary experience (it’s cold or wet outside) but also to the significant issues of the day.

Take this example from the most recent Harper’s Index:

Factor by which an American is more likely to cite unemployment than the deficit as the country’s “most important problem”: 3

Factor by which a wealthy American is more likely to cite the deficit than unemployment: 3

Now consider the media emphasis placed on the deficit vs. unemployment, a drumbeat that even got the White House on board. The fact that 23 million Americans are either out of a job or are underemployed took a back seat to deficit reduction.
