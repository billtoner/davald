Disparate clout

April 3, 2013 by davald

A couple of items in this morning’s newspapers betray America’s misplaced priorities and, I would suggest, convey the increasingly unmistakable message that we’ve succumbed to the loonies. The first is the NRA’s latest whacky initiative, The National School Shield, wherein the gun lobby reveals its answer to proliferating gun violence: more guns, of course. The second is a local matter, with several teachers targeting the home of state senator Steve Hobbs, a nominal Democrat who seemingly relishes the prospect of pissing off educators.

In what kind of universe would a gun lobby wield far more political clout and come closer to realizing its political agenda than associations of teachers? Well, that would be the United States.

Despite the senseless slaughters committed by deranged individuals with legally acquired arsenals of deadly assault weapons, the chances of Congress passing any restrictions on guns diminishes with each NRA lobbying push. Forget about the dead children of Newtown. It’s all about the damn Second Amendment. Oh, and I should mention the money. The NRA long ago ceased being a club of hunters. It’s the frontman for the weapons manufacturers, whose profits soar after each rampaging episode.

So, the gun nuts will get their way, sure enough. What about education and teachers? As I said, this is America.

In America, the politicians believe that teachers are second-class citizens who consistently fail in the classroom. The solution is two-fold: raise the bar and hold educators “accountable.” The connecting glue is standardized testing. Subject school children to yearly performance measurements, then base teacher evaluations on the results.

One need not be a genius to appreciate the consequences of this strategy. Teachers and their principals may be pressured to cheat, and that’s what has happened in several school districts, the ones we know about, with Atlanta being the latest. (We may recall G.W. Bush’s education secretary, Rod Paige, who gained fame for the near-miraculous test-score improvements in Houston. Not.)

In the main, however, educators will focus on the test, jettisoning most of what makes teaching rewarding, from science and literature to arts and music and the interactions between students and teachers that make learning special. For children in Washington state, the Measurement of Student Progress, or MSP, tests for reading and math, mostly. In the weeks leading up to the rigorous exams, teachers try to squeeze as much detail into their students’ heads as possible, hoping, I guess, that some of it will stick by test time.

Imagine that you’re a fourth-grade teacher. Next year your evaluation and, presumably, your pay will be based in part on how well your kids did on the standardized test. The results, we’ll imagine further, aren’t pretty. Few passed. So you get a lousy report card. In your defense you say that you can’t rely on a single year’s worth of scores. Besides, your students’ gaps were too large. Shouldn’t the third and second and first grade teachers bear some of the blame?

Sorry. It doesn’t work that way. Whether or not you are judged mediocre of superb depends only on last year’s results. No excuses.

Teachers and principals know that this system sucks. Yet, they have no clout when it comes to benighted politicians, who always know better than educators, even though they wouldn’t survive ten minutes in a public school classroom. The legislatures continue to pile on the requirements while denying basic funding to the schools. The inevitable decline in public education falls exclusively on the educators; the elected officials, with fingers pointing, escape to do the bidding of the gun lobby and otherwise fail completely at their jobs.

I agree with Peter

March 31, 2013 by davald

Those who know me, and there aren’t that many, might be taken aback by my headline. I am referring to Peter Newland, with whom I strongly disagreed when we were both at the Snohomish County Public Utility District—he as a commissioner; I as a policy aide within the office. But that was a long time ago, and we’ve both moved on, though I didn’t go very far. I now occupy his previous seat on the board. He continues with his private businesses.

Newland offered his opinions on what to do with the site formerly occupied by Kimberly-Clark. The paper products company shut down the mill, which had been around for decades, then demolished the empty buildings. When I drove by the other day, there was nothing but rubble. What emerges will largely define what kind of place Everett will be for generations to come. (Photo from the Herald.)

The closure sent hundreds of employees to the streets. They were making family wages with good benefits. Sympathy for their plight and legacy may have induced the city council to designate a “water-dependent” usage. Newland believes that this is a mistake. He has an alternative in mind.

[The council’s decision] tries to preserve the status quo even though that ship has already left the dock. If Everett’s downtown core is ever to be vital again, we need to rethink our goals like successful, forward-looking cities and towns across America have done for as long as I can remember.

I share this sentiment. Everett does have an opportunity for a “fresh start” on the waterfront. In his vision, the site would be transformed into a mixed-use development in the manner of Baltimore, Maryland.

In the 1970s, Baltimore’s leaders realized the city would be in dire financial straits if they allowed their waterfront to languish. They decided to redevelop the city’s ramshackle inner harbor. Their years of work and millions of dollars of public and private investment have added residential and commercial space to an empty harbor and made Baltimore’s renewal an overall success.

Revitalization is hard. I tried for years in Marysville, Wash., a suburb of Everett. To make it happen requires bold leadership from both the public and private sectors, people willing to collaborate on a shared vision. I was not that catalyst for change. It appears no one else was either. So Marysville languishes still, with no conversation about what to do.

Newland wants such a conversation to begin in Everett, while there is still a chance. His goal is a lofty one:

If we put our minds to it we can make Everett the most livable city on the I-5 corridor.

Good for you, Peter. Let’s talk.

More dystopia

March 26, 2013 by davald

Depressing articles are not hard to find. Here’s one in today’s New York Times about the difficulties facing the so-called “millennials,” also known as Generation Y. Despite being more educated than previous generations, they don’t have as much money. Worse, their prospects for obtaining meaningful employment—a job that pays a decent wage, good benefits, and some security and stability—are few and far between. As a result, they can’t buy homes and many eschew cars, which helps explain why so many of this generation live in urban apartments, often with others; or they stay with their parents.

…Now that the economy has entered a steady but slow recovery, younger millennials wonder if they can make up that gap. Lisa Kahn, a labor economist at the Yale School of Management, studied the earnings of men who left college and joined the work force during the deep recession of the early 1980s. Unsurprisingly, she found that the higher the unemployment rate upon graduation, the less graduates earned right out of school. But those workers never really caught up. “The effects were still present 15 or 20 years later,” she said. “They never made that money back.”

Kahn worries that the same pattern is repeating itself.

And so it goes…

Spending and confidence

March 16, 2013 by davald

The austerians chant that achieving fiscal balance instills confidence. The idea, I guess, is that investors, the people with all the money, will be more likely to expand production if they can count on the government’s finances being in order. (Paul Krugman takes a swipe at an austerian obsessed with “confidence.”)

What austerians ignore or downplay is the role of consumer spending. If the Rest of Us aren’t buying things, mostly because we don’t have as much money as we used to or we’re busy reducing our debt, then the economy suffers. Consider this chart:

 

Since the 1980s, certainly, economic growth correlates with consumer spending. (The red line, right axis, represents GDP; the blue line represents consumer spending as a percentage of GDP.) GDP actually fell by two percent from 2008 to 2009.

Those economists who subscribe to the demand-equals-growth equation suggest that investors have no reason to be confident if goods and services will not be consumed at increasing rates. It is these economists who also point out that the private sector is hoarding its cash rather than investing it, again, because they lack confidence. What would restore confidence? Rising demand.

But how can demand increase if people don’t have any money? They need well-paying jobs, whose supply steadily declines. FDR understood this much, which is why he pushed for massive infrastructure development funded by the federal government. Think Grand Coulee. Today, there are too many austerians to create a new New Deal.

So, we’re stuck with a sputtering economy that manages to enrich the wealthy and impoverish the poor. Stupid stuff, says me.

Wages and errors

March 11, 2013 by davald

Writing stuff, regardless of the content and fora, leaves one vulnerable to exposure. One can be discovered as a fraud, say when talking about things in complete ignorance of the facts. Certainly being labeled as lazy is easy, since one is always just a few mouse clicks away from libraries-worth of knowledge. Then there are simple mistakes, which routinely affects bloggers, who are often in a hurry to get to the next post  or to run an errand or two. This is especially true if you blog for nothing and are thus responsible to no one else.

Today, Slate‘s Matthew Yglesias, who studied philosophy at Harvard, opined on the errors of “the Left” with respect to wages. He writes:

The left is rightly concerned about the real wages and incomes of ordinary working people. But the left fallaciously analogizes from a bargaining dynamic at a single firm to the entire economy.

Tsk, tsk, says Noah Smith, who holds a PhD in economics and works in academia. Ever so gently he rebuts Yglesias’s argument. Smith quotes Yglesias then shows his errors:

To raise real wages across the economy…what you need to do is make things cheaper. And that generally entails an accumulated series of events that make selected groups of people’s nominal incomes lower. If everyone could be chauffered around cheaply by autonomous cars, then the people who currently earn a living driving cabs and buses and trucks will lose out. If computers take over routine diagnostic work, then doctors will lose out. If online courses that serve tens of thousands of students displace community colleges, then community college instructors will lose out [Yglesias].

This is just not right. The process that Matt describes need not reduce nominal incomes. Suppose, first of all, that everyone had to stay in the same line of work forever (which is not true, but which is a necessary assumption if you want to use phrases like “cab drivers will lose out”). Now an industry-specific productivity shock comes along – someone invents a latte machine that makes better-tasting lattes, twice as fast, while using only half the milk. The real price (i.e. the relative price) of lattes will fall. The quantity of lattes sold will rise. The income of latte-makers (the # of whom, remember, is fixed!) equals the price of lattes sold multiplied by the quantity of lattes sold.

Will this income go up or down? Well, you can see for yourself, with a bit of Econ 101. Draw a supply-demand graph for the latte market. Model the productivity improvement as a rightward shift in the supply curve. Take the derivative of equilibrium income with respect to some level parameter of the supply curve. You will find that whether latte-makers’ incomes go up or down depends on the elasticities of demand and supply for lattes. In particular, if demand for lattes is very elastic, then the incomes of latte-makers will go up. (This result will also hold in general equilibrium.)

Okay, a bit on the technical side. But Yglesias probably should have known better, despite lacking a degree in economics. Smith concludes that Yglesias has it wrong about the Left confusing the dynamics of an individual employee in a single firm and the economy as a whole. Yet, Smith does believe that something’s not quite right now.

…The Left wants to increase labor’s share of income, and decrease the skewness of the wage distribution. How to do that is an open question, but those goals seem perfectly reasonable to me.

For more on labor’s declining share of income read this article. Note this embedded chart:

Houston, we have a problem.

Does the government owe me a job?

February 15, 2013 by davald

The short answer is no. If a professional athlete sustains a debilitating injury, few of us would argue that it is the government’s responsibility to find the man a new job, though he would likely receive some disability or unemployment benefits that may or not be permanent, but in any case far less than his previous salary.

So, if we might agree that employment for one is not government’s obligation, then it would seem to follow that the government has no role in facilitating work for the vast army of the unemployed, currently at around eight percent of the workforce.

But perhaps we’re looking at this the wrong way. We might, for example, “abstract up,” to view the economy as a whole, rather than as the sum of its constituent elements. Reprising a theme from a recent post, we could invoke the words of the Constitution’s Preamble, wherein the Founders thought that government should be about establishing justice, ensuring domestic tranquility, and promoting the general welfare. In so doing, it’s as if we’re turning the composition fallacy on its head: the government, in the view of Madison et al., has a duty to the whole and, by derivation, to the one.

Republicans and many Democrats insist that the private sector must provide the solutions. Some would go further to suggest that the government frustrates firms’ ability to hire and sustain workers. Among the obstacles cited are: too-high tax rates, cumbersome regulations, a rising federal debt. But our taxes are generally lower than those of other western industrial democracies. Regulations, as we witnessed during the housing bubble’s rise and collapse, are lax and few. However, the debt is rising.

Which brings us to the debate raging in Washington and on op-ed pages. Should the government incur even higher deficits to stimulate the economy?

Professors Krugman, DeLong, Bernanke, to name a few, hold that the lingering high unemployment rate bodes ill for economic growth both now and in the future. They often reference J.M. Keynes in describing current conditions as unique. Here’s Krugman, for instance, quoting Keynes on his description of the 1930s:

“a chronic condition of subnormal activity for a considerable period without any marked tendency either towards recovery or towards complete collapse.”

In other words, the economy is sputtering, operating far below its potential. Since the market is unwilling or unable to boost economic growth, Keynes—and his followers—believed that government is the only available entity that has the means to do so.

Monetary policy (printing money, if you will) doesn’t work too well against a zero lower bound in which interest rates are already as low as they can go (in real terms, they are negative).

That leaves fiscal policy. Can and should the government spend money that it does not have in order to employ more people and, in turn, increase demand for goods and services?

Those making the case would have to demonstrate, it seems to me, that the return on the federal investment exceeds either the costs of doing so or the opportunity cost of doing nothing. This would imply that there is a positive multiplier. That is, for every dollar the government spends, the economy realizes more than a dollar in value. Suppose further that increased economic activity increases federal receipts that keep pace with incremental spending, thereby preventing larger deficits, or, even better, revenues exceed spending, thus reducing the debt.

The government tracks both gross domestic product (GDP) and potential GDP. Here’s a chart showing the difference between potential and actual real GDP:

The curve since the Great Recession should give us pause. The economy is clearly underperforming. If Keynes’s description of the 1930s applies to our current situation, then it’s possible for the economy to continue indefinitely with higher-than-average unemployment and even rising debt.

Doing nothing or, worse, contracting government spending, would either prolong the sputtering or exacerbate “sub-normal activity.” Yet, that is the strategy championed by those Krugman calls “Very Serious People.” It rests on a true fallacy of composition: while it may be salutary for an individual to spend within current means, it may be disastrous for everyone, including the government on our behalf, to do the same.

Unfortunately, from the perspective of Keynesians and the unemployed, government spending has fallen recently, as the following chart shows.

And how has the economy done of late?

 

Pursuing fiscal constraint, as has been the norm in the UK, contracts, rather than expands, the economy.

The private sector, as I said, has not stimulated economic growth, despite low tax rates and minimal regulations. Firms need people to buy their goods and products (aggregate demand). But people need wages to make that happen. And wages require jobs.

It’s up to you, Washington, to get us out of this mess. Otherwise, we’re stuck.

The robots did it

February 11, 2013 by davald

Why is American society so unequal? Some blame technology: robots and other electronic devices have reduced the need for human labor, which means an expanding surplus of people with nothing to do.

In a recent op-ed for the New York Times Harvard economist Greg Mankiw writes:

Over the last several decades, for the most part, the wages of workers without any higher education have stagnated, while the wages of those with advanced degrees have risen. The main forces driving these trends are technological change, which tends to increase the demand for skilled workers relative to unskilled workers, and, to a lesser extent, international trade.

But economist Dean Baker begs to differ:

Mankiw asserts as a fact that technology is responsible for the upward redistribution of income over the last three decades, but it is not clear that the evidence supports his story. After all technology had a much larger impact in increasing productivity in the decades from 1947 to 1973 yet workers shared in these gains more or less equally.

A chart may suffice.

What about productivity?

Hmmm. When worker productivity was at its highest, in the post-War decades, workers took a larger share of income.

As I recall, there was a lot of new technology in those days, especially at the consumer level, from wide-scale adoption of the telephone and television to cars and freeways and airplanes. (As a three-month-old infant I accompanied my mother on a very expensive voyage from New York to San Francisco. I’m told that the plane was a propeller-driven DC 9. That would have been in 1947. Fast forward to 1969, the year I graduated from Berkeley. I traveled by jet, I tell you, from San Francisco to Philadelphia. In that period our family switched from a rotary telephone on a party line to a touch-tone, single-line device. We purchased a color television, and transistor radios became ubiquitous. My first official job was working with business-class mainframe computers in New Jersey; there were none until the 60s. I could go on. Oh, and they put a man on the moon.)

Dean Baker offers some alternative explanations for rising inequality:

The villains that some of us would point to are anti-union measures by government and businesses that have weakened workers’ bargaining power, trade policy that was designed to put less-educated workers in competition with people in the developing world while largely protecting the most highly educated workers, patent and copyright policy that increased the rents pulled out of the economy for these monopolies, and macroeconomic policy that has led to more unemployment in the last three decades than in the early post-war period. High unemployment tends to disproportionately hit less educated workers, both by having more impact on their probability of being unemployed and reducing their wages.

Wrong medicine

February 8, 2013 by davald

Republican political doctors prescribe austerity—always. The government, we are to believe, spends too much money, which adds to the fiscal debt. So, the remedy is to curb spending now and forever.

Paul Krugman rebukes John Boehner in his column this morning. Boehner said that Congress had to “deal with its spending problem,” and that he’s tired of kicking the can down the road. Krugman argues that given current recessionary conditions, marked by high unemployment and sputtering GDP, we should continue kicking the can down the road. Now is no time to tighten our fiscal belts. Besides, where was Boehner when his man in the White House, G.W.Bush, squandered the Clinton surplus by cutting taxes and launching two expensive wars?

In the above chart we’ll notice that federal spending as a percentage of GDP remains fairly flat since 1981, between roughly 20 and 25 percent of GDP. The ratio fell under Clinton, then began to rise sharply under Bush II. We’ll also note that GDP (black line) dropped at the end of 2007, the beginning of the Great Recession. That is, the denominator fell, so that even if spending levels stayed the same, the ratio would increase.

Since 2010, however, federal spending has begun to fall as a percentage of GDP. In part that’s because GDP has started inching upwards. But spending itself has also stabilized.

Would Boehner want the line to fall? Has that ever happened?

 

We can see that only twice since 1947 has federal spending dropped against the previous year. On both occasions, 1950 and 1954, the country was in a mild recession. Also, military spending fell in the latter year with the end of the Korean War.

 

Krugman:

Start with a basic point: Slashing government spending destroys jobs and causes the economy to shrink.

He cites the experiences of Ireland and the U.K., whose governments both cut federal spending only to see their countries experience dramatic contractions in GDP. Since Washington has also cut spending, with defense down by 22 percent in the last fiscal quarter, our economy continues to sputter.

Yet, despite the problems associated with austerity, if we are to believe Krugman, the European Union leaders just capitulated to demands of U.K. prime minister David Cameron; the EU nations will cut spending. Why would the EU take advice from Cameron, whose economy is doing much worse than ours?

It’s this kind of major disconnect between policy makers and at least some economists that I find disturbing. Shouldn’t there be some sort of consensus on the prescriptions? At what point do Cameron, Boehner, et al. reconsider their positions? Do they know something that Krugman, Stiglitz, DeLong, and Bernanke don’t know? What could it be?

Our self-loathing

January 23, 2013 by davald

The Bureau of Labor Statistics released data for 2012 on union participation rates. They’re lower. A few lowlights:

  • for all workers, 11.3 percent belong to unions
  • in the private sector the percentage is 6.6
  • for the first time the number of public sector workers unionized exceeded unionized private workers
  • the total number of workers belonging to unions fell to 14.3 million, down from 17.7 million in 1983, the first year the data were collected

I titled this post “our self-loathing.” I’m implying that we love our inequality, which is why we don’t do a damn thing about it. Bring on the suffering as we worship the rich.

We should not be surprised that there is a strong correlation between union participation rates and inequality, as this chart comparing nations reveals (source):

 

There we are at the upper left, so proud and so poor. We can’t be bothered with those union “bosses” who smoke smelly cigars as they steal from our paychecks. That would seem to be the image in workers’ minds as they cheer further erosion of labor rights and the ability to organize.

Will we ever learn? Oh, and who the hell is Joe Hill?
