The beatings must continue…

May 10, 2013 by davald

Trying to extract consensus from economists is like trying to get oil to play well with water. A New York Times reporter attempted to seek common ground between Larry Summers and Glenn Hubbard. He came away disappointed. President Harry Truman asked for a one-armed economist, believing that even within the individual the truth was divided.

Here’s a big secret. Economists are biased, despite protests to the contrary. As the Times journalist, Adam Davidson, discovered, from whence you come evidently determines what you study, and what you study makes all the difference.

Larry Summers was born of two academic economists and is the nephew of the late Nobel laureate Paul Samuelson (if you took economics in my generation you read Samuelson’s textbook; I have the 15th edition lying next to me). Glenn Hubbard began life in rural Florida, the son of public-school teachers. Summers is decidedly liberal; Hubbard the opposite.

For us laypersons how do we arbitrate the differences? What shall be our metric?

Economists on Hubbard’s side of the profession worry about deficits and debts and inflation. Those with Summers abhor high rates of unemployment and anemic economies. In both cases it seems that government plays a major role. The former group blames Congress for spending too much money and getting in the way of “the market.” Liberal economists like Summers urge the federal government to spend more money than it receives and would restrain market participants lest they ruin things for the Rest of Us.

My non-expert vantage suggests that Hubbard et al. hold fast to their principles across all circumstances. Krugman, Summers and others acknowledge special conditions, but would otherwise agree that deficits do matter, just not at the moment. Hubbard’s group appears to reject the uniqueness possibility.

Hubbard et al. have been warning of deficits and debts and inflation for the last few years, especially. If government debt continues to mount or if the Federal Reserve expands the monetary base consumer prices will soar as will interest rates. Well, both antecedents occurred. Yet there has been no inflation to speak of and interest rates remain near record-low levels. One might conclude that the conservative’s theories are wrong. One might.

Krugman, Bernanke, Summers and others argued the opposite. Relying on their models, which are based on Keynes’s work, they predicted that higher deficits and monetary expansion would not cause inflation or interest rates to rise. Again, they asserted that the economy was operating under special circumstances, namely a liquidity trap—in which those with capital tend to hoard rather than invest their dollars because of uncertainty about returns—and interest rates running up against a zero lower bound—in real dollars rates are at or below zero. Like Keynes before them they have urged the government to spend more money, preferably on long-term public assets like transportation infrastructure, to stimulate the sputtering economy, even if deficits rise.

Unfortunately, the failure of facts to conform with conservative nostrums has not induced Hubbard and others to revisit their theories. One apostle, economist James McCusker, even wrote a column in today’s Everett Herald titled “Discredited research doesn’t discredit need for cuts.”

The research to which McCusker refers is that of economists Carmen Reinhart and Ken Rogoff. They concluded that rising deficits and accumulated debt deter economic growth. They even went so far as to suggest that there was a threshold beyond which economies decline. That threshold was debt exceeding 90 percent of GDP.

McCusker, though acknowledging the serious mistakes of the researchers, essentially dismisses the criticisms while asserting that their basic point still holds: deficits are bad for the economy, apparently at all times. He writes:

The error in this research paper gave them [critics] what they believed was the perfect sword to slay the austerity dragon.

That belief was mistaken, though. Reports of austerity’s death turned out to be exaggerated.The reason is simple enough. Austerity, like stimulus spending comes in many different flavors. There is a big difference, for example, between an austerity program dictated by a country’s creditors and an independently developed government plan to reduce its dependence on unsustainable debt.

Of the former, I suspect McCusker has in mind conditions of high government interest rates that induce financial institutions to charge more for credit. That would effectively squeeze consumer spending, especially on housing, and dampen growth. Those conditions don’t obtain now. The latter is in play, with governments, especially at the state levels, slashing spending, ostensibly to reduce deficits (see chart below).

Then McCusker lets his ideological blinders misjudge Congress’s American Recovery and Reinvestment Act. He says that “an ill-conceived stimulus program can be totally ineffective at prompting economic growth, employment or private investment. As happened with that program, stimulus spending may instead end up as a kind of mammoth employment security program for some workers. To the extent that it preserved jobs during the difficult early days of the recession, it may not have been totally useless. But it most certainly was not an effective economic stimulus.”

I agree that the program was ill-conceived. Too much of it was in the form of tax breaks, which constrained federal receipts, adding to deficits. Far too little was devoted to public goods and services like transportation and education. Krugman et al. condemned the 2009 program as much too small given the size of the economy’s downturn following the collapse of the housing bubble. However, to assert that ARRA was “totally ineffective” is simply wrong. If Hubbard and other like-minded economists had their way there would have been no federal package at all.

As it is, total government spending has fallen steadily as compared to GDP. We are “practicing” austerity with nothing but misery to show for it.

 

I, for one, would distinguish between the likes of Hubbard and Summers on the basis of sympathy. Either you have compassion for those who are suffering in this dismal economy or you don’t. You can guess which is which.

Extended joblessness

April 25, 2013 by davald

I count myself fortunate that I have a job, though it was voters who gave me one. My wife is also working, albeit retiring this year. Our two children are gainfully employed, with steady prospects for continued employment. All good.

But things are not so good for millions of Americans devastated by the housing bubble collapse and near meltdown of the financial sector. They just can’t find work, no matter how hard or long they have tried. Many have simply given up.

Some of us react to the grim statistics by blaming the victims. They assume that jobs are plentiful. They also assume that would-be workers could fill those open positions were they willing to accept lower wages. Further, they blame extended unemployment insurance for removing the incentive to seek work. But they are wrong on each count. (I’ve written extensively on these topics. Just search the site for “jobs,” “employment,” etc.)

Now that the austerians have been thoroughly discredited, other voices are beginning to be heard. These include economists, several of whom appeared before Congress to talk about the long-term unemployed and their impacts on future economic growth. They talked numbers, including the “labor force participation rate,” which is at its lowest level in 35 years. Let’s take a look.

We see that the ratio is about at the same point as it was in the late 70s. Also, the trend is heading in the wrong direction, with no signs of abating.

I find it interesting that the level remained fairly steady from the end of WWII to the mid-60s. Then the ratio began an upward trajectory. The reason for the rising number will be obvious from this next chart.

Women went to work. Yet, even their participation rate reached a peak before tapering off in the Great Recession. What happened to men?

 

This is quite remarkable. Immediately after the war nearly 87 percent of men were working, my father and his brothers being among them. Since 1948, the labor force participation rate for men has fallen by 20 percent, from a high of 86.6 to last year’s 70.2.

Others are far better equipped to explain why men stopped working. Among the usual suspects: loss of manufacturing jobs, occupied by men, for the most part; an aging male population; the wrong skill sets for available jobs. Whatever the reasons, that so many men are out of work is surely being reflected in negative societal trends, including crime, incarceration, family instability, and so on.

The economists testifying before Congress are worried.

“The long-term unemployment rate underestimates the number of long-term jobless,” said [Keith] Hall, who’s now a researcher at George Mason University’s Mercatus Center, adding that the long-term unemployed are “helping to hold back economic growth.”

What can be done?

The economists urged more public-works spending, tax credits and subsidies to employers to bolster private-sector employment, all of which would spark more demand and activity in the economy.

Raise your hands if you think this batch of congressional delegates will heed this advice.

Rewarding wrong

April 15, 2013 by davald

One might think by now that those who have championed a particular set of policy initiatives, which we can place under the rubric of conservatism for now, and who have nevertheless been proven wrong in nearly every respect would find it difficult to either stay in office or get themselves elected. But one would be mistaken. The power of nonsense retains its ability to inflict increasing damage on the Rest of Us while enjoying our electoral support. Color us schizophrenic.

Among those policy initiatives are “austerity measures.” Governments must restrain spending at all costs, because spending is the problem. (Oh, and high taxes.) Europe’s political leaders can’t seem to get enough austerity, despite overwhelming evidence that tightening fiscal balance sheets exacerbates both debts and unemployment.

Here in the U.S. an entire political party preaches fiscal restraint in religious overtones for no good reason. Our unemployment rate, though not as high as most of Europe’s, remains stubbornly high, with millions of Americans unable to find jobs after months and even years of searching. Many have simply given up trying.

I confess to lots of head scratching over this rewarding-the-wrong phenomenon. I just don’t understand the rationales of those who vote against their economic interests, as in What’s the Matter With Kansas? Why are so many of us evidently comfortable with high unemployment and associated lost lives?

Working poor

April 12, 2013 by davald

The Bureau of Labor Statistics has a new report (pdf) on America’s working poor. To be quick about it, there are lots of them. Here’s a chart on “primary families,” which are defined as families with at least one person working at least half a year.

What the graph shows is the percentage of primary families who are poor despite a member’s working. So, in 2011 8.3 percent of those families live in poverty, up from 6.4 in 2007, just before the Great Recession. Among all persons the percentage of working poor in 2011 was seven, down slightly from  2010.

What’s going on? BLS takes a stab and comes up with a partial list. Among the culprits are the proliferation of part-time jobs and, more important, low wages. A good proxy for both would be so-called burger-flippers. Not only do they work less than full time, they don’t make much money, the minimum wage or perhaps a bit more.

It is a sad testament to our political culture that the needs of the poor go unmentioned let alone mentioned. Certainly the Republicans aren’t about to address the problem. They consistently resist raising the minimum wage and always champion the desires of employers over their past, present, and future employees. So it’s quite easy to jettison workers and deny them both benefits and job security.

While most modern nations have a labor party, America doesn’t. We have the Democrats, who seem just as eager to screw unions as their conservative counterparts, and it’s unions that brought us the eight-hour day, the end of child labor, the minimum wage, and a host of other benefits that are as niggardly as they are taken for granted.

Still not working

April 10, 2013 by davald

We’re told that the economy is recovering, albeit slowly. New jobs are being added, but overall unemployment remains high. Europe is worse, suggesting a widespread dysfunction. Let’s look at job openings.

If one looked at the curve starting at the end of 2009, one might think that things were improving. But despite the upward slope, we’ve still got a long way to go before we reach post-recession levels and further still to reach the Clinton numbers.

Now let’s look at the unemployed.

Again, the trend is improving, but we’re not close to where we were. If we divide the figures in the first graph by those in the second, we get this:

When Clinton left office (and I’m not necessarily praising anything he did here) there was nearly a one-to-one ratio between those out of work but looking and job openings. At the peak of the recession, the ratio had soared to nearly seven. It’s now down to around three. That is, there are three people out of work for every open job. Yet, again, we’ve still got a long way to go before we approach levels seen before G.W. entered the White House.

There’s wide disagreement about what, if anything, should or could be done to get people back to work. Some would argue that there is no role for government; the economy will right itself if left to its own devices. Others, including Paul Krugman and Brad DeLong, advocate for fiscal stimulus, even if it (necessarily) means increasing federal deficits.

One thing seems certain. The guy in the White House is the master of distraction. He exhibits no concern for the jobless and is forever willing to sacrifice the most vulnerable on the altar of “compromise,” which, for Obama, is a goal in itself. So, the president is all about attracting the Very Serious (in Krugman’s argot) as he sets out to screw the Rest of Us.

What a crock, this man.

Boeing going

April 10, 2013 by davald

This morning’s headline was about Boeing investing another billion dollars or so in its new South Carolina plant, which manufactures or assembles the company’s largest planes. But so does its Puget Sound facilities. Hmmm.

Writing for the Seattle Times in his inimical style Jon Talton paints a dour picture:

Our conceit is that the Puget Sound region has the talent, skills and sunk costs in facilities that offset its relatively higher costs. The unions have accepted contracts without strikes. It makes no logical sense to start all over again elsewhere, particularly since this quality workforce is a major driver behind record profits at Boeing. But that’s not the way Jack Welch’s acolytes think.

Talton gives our region a decade or less before Seattle becomes Boeing-less. Nor, he suggests, could we do anything about the wholesale transfer of the company’s production to a prominent right-to-work state. Talton:

And we couldn’t prevent it even if the Boeing employees here decertified their unions and we racked back our wage levels to those of the South. It doesn’t matter that Ray Conner, chief of commercial airplanes, is “a good guy” who joined the old Boeing as a mechanic. His bosses came from General Electric, sat at the feet of Neutron Jack and carry the McDonnell Douglas toxic culture — and American employees that are not expendable teammates are simply liabilities. At the moment, the executive suite needs our world-class engineers, machinists and technicians to fix the dog’s breakfast their outsourcing made of the Dreamliner. Don’t expect any such dedication in return. By the way, neither should South Carolina. That’s the Chicago way.

WFT WSJ

April 8, 2013 by davald

Among things that I would never buy is a subscription to the Wall Street Journal. I’m even less inclined to do so now that Rupert Murdoch owns it. It used to be said, by even Noam Chomsky, that one could rely on its reporting, though one should toss the editorial pages into the trashcan.

Dean Baker gives us another example of why we shouldn’t pay any attention to the self-important publication. In a blog this morning, Baker tells us in derisive tones:

Folks who follow the economy might have thought that cutbacks in government spending, the continued weakness of construction, or the large trade deficits were the causes of the slow recovery, but the WSJ has the real scoop: it’s workers going on disability.

Baker goes on to explain how the Journal gets it wrong, spectacularly so. First off, there are far more people actively looking for work than their are job openings. If, as Baker suggests, the Journal assumed responsibility for those on Social Security disability rolls and tossed the offenders to the street, “How exactly does this create more jobs? Are the companies that were already seeing many applicants for each job openings going to start offering more jobs when this ratio increases further?”

How, indeed.
