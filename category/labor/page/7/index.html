A dismal conclusion from the dismal science

April 2, 2014 by davald

Writing for the New York Times, Thomas Edsall tells us:

For those American workers who have been battered by foreign competition, the possibility of a prolonged free fall cannot be discounted.

Edsall reports on disparate answers to the question: Does international trade harm U.S. workers?

The Obama administration’s decision to restrict tire imports from China, [a group of economists] contend, “saved a maximum of 1,200 jobs” at “the total cost to American consumers from higher prices” of $1.1 billion in 2011. “The cost per job saved was at least $900,000 in that year. Only a very small fraction of this bloated figure reached the pockets of tire workers. Instead, most of the money landed in the coffers of tire companies, mainly abroad but also at home.”

Henry Ford had an epiphany of sorts way back when: his employees were also consumers. Thus, it was in his interests to pay those who produced his cars enough money to buy them. If Ford’s equivalence still holds, then what’s good for consumers may actually be bad for workers; we cannot consume if we lack paying jobs.

I know, not exactly breaking news. Here’s another obvious fact: what’s good for U.S. corporations may not be good for American’s general welfare. If the modern-day Ford Corporation can manufacture and assemble its vehicles cheaper in Mexico than in the United States, guess where it locates factories?

It may be inconvenient but nevertheless true that U.S. workers have no more right to a decent living than Ford’s Mexican workforce. Moreover, the lives of once-deprived populations are improving while those of America’s middle class slide, certainly in economic terms.

Welcome to the future.

Minimum wage

February 9, 2014 by davald

I need not add my two cents to the discussion about the minimum wage, which varies between those who would abolish it altogether and those who would push it much higher, beyond the proposal by Obama, $10.10, and the level adopted by SeaTac, Wash., $15/hour. The editors of the New York Times argue for raising it, as do Washington state representatives Mike Sells and Luis Moscoso.

The Times editors:

The minimum wage is one of the most thoroughly researched issues in economics. Studies in the last 20 years have been especially informative, as economists have been able to compare states that raised the wage above the federal level with those that did not.

The weight of the evidence shows that increases in the minimum wage have lifted pay without hurting employment, a point that was driven home in a recent letter to Mr. Obama and congressional leaders, signed by more than 600 economists, among them Nobel laureates and past presidents of the American Economic Association.

The newspaper includes this chart:

In the documentary Inequality for All, now-Berkeley professor Robert Reich includes a number of useful charts illustrating, among other things, the decline in workers’ wages compared with the growth in labor productivity. I put this together based on data from the St. Louis Fed:

We’ll note that something happened in the late seventies to enable wages to depart from their previous parallel trajectory with productivity. Reich, who has been beating the inequality drum for most of his professional career, which began with a stint in the Ford administration, sometimes wonders if he’s been a failure, since things have only gotten worse since his initial efforts to call attention to poverty amidst rising incomes among the very rich. He includes a chart from his colleague Emmanuel Saez, whom he interviews in the film.

That The top 10 percent represents about 400 households. They 400 households make as much money collectively as 150 million Americans.

If you are a Republican, for reasons that totally escape me, you embrace the status quo while vigorously opposing solutions that would make the poor and rich less so. They would have us worship the “job creators.”

Yet even Nick Hanauer, a very wealthy Northwest venture capitalist who admitted that he paid only 11 percent in taxes on his “eight-figure” income last year, dispels that myth: he is hardly a job creator. Indeed, it is the middle class that creates jobs by working and spending. Nor can Hanauer possibly spend all the money he makes, whereas his employees spend all they make and more, via credit purchases.

Reich calls this middle-class earning and spending the ‘virtuous cycle,’ something often discussed on these pages (e.g., here). A worker spends his income on goods and serves, which induces businesses to expand their operations and therefore hire more workers, who spend their money, and so on.

But we have a different cycle today, a vicious one that actually moves in reverse. A worker is laid off (say from the collapse of the housing bubble), who then has far less money to spend, if he or she has any at all. In turn, businesses contract their operations, and may even shutter their doors, resulting in more workers being terminated—and so on.

The economy, then, suffers from inadequate aggregate demand. Eighty years ago John Maynard Keynes told us the way out of the mess was to put people to work doing useful things, although even paying workers to dig a hole and refill it would be helpful. It was all about enabling people to spend.

Increasing the minimum wage is one way to put more money into the hands of those who would immediately spend it. Investing in public transportation and schools would also boost the economy.

The Republicans, however, want none of this, and insofar as they can block such initiatives, the poor will get poorer and the rich even richer. (Chart below from Saez. It illustrates the average incomes of each “fractile.”)

The water divide

January 15, 2014 by davald

We hear much these days about the division of economists into “salt water” and “fresh water.” The former refers to those who inhabit coastal academias; they tend to be more liberal and embrace Keynesian policies. The latter mostly reside at the University of Chicago; they are prone to reject fiscal and monetary stimuli.

We can see the division play out in this open letter to congressional leaders. I did not see any economist affiliated with Chicago. There are about a half-dozen Nobel laureates among the signatories—none close to Lake Michigan.

The letter urges Congress to pass an increase in the federal minimum wage to $10.10 by 2016, phased in over three years from the current $7.25/hour. Those working full-time at that amount would earn a whopping $21,000 a year.

Republicans, as a rule, oppose raising the minimum wage, or any minimum at all, because they say they’re worried about jobs. If employers must pay more, they’ll either lay off workers or shutter their doors—and we know how much Republicans care about workers. The letter avers that a higher minimum would have little, if any, effect on employment levels. They write:

In recent years there have been important developments in the academic literature on the effect of increases in the minimum wage on employment, with the weight of evidence now showing that increases in the minimum wage have had little or no negative effect on the employment of minimum-wage workers, even during times of weakness in the labor market. Research suggests that a minimum-wage increase could have a small stimulative effect on the economy as low-wage workers spend their additional earnings, raising demand and job growth, and providing some help on the jobs front.

I suspect that the economists will be largely ignored.

Dead on arrival

January 15, 2014 by davald

John Quiggin writes:

The fact that Obama is not a wild-eyed socialist, or even a traditional US liberal, but rather a moderate conservative may be a revelation in some Republican circles, but it is scarcely news to the rest of us.

Quiggin, in the same post, is taking on Ross Douthat, the New York Times‘ “official Republican columnist,” who has urged his party to offer programs that appeal to the working class. But as Quiggin notes:

It’s patently obvious, however, that there is zero support for this program in any of the leading factions of the Republican Party, either among the leadership or in the activist base. There isn’t a single program benefitting the working class, from Social Security to the Earned Income Tax Credit to unemployment benefits to food stamps that can command the support of more than a handful of Republicans in Congress, and those few are likely to be driven out before long.

The political asymmetry might trouble many. Feral conservatives have a home in the new Republican Party. Their less exuberant counterparts on the left have nothing, save for their ability to organize outside established institutions. As a result, earnest progressives often avoid elections, which helps, rather than impedes, the country’s rightward trajectory.

There was a time in America when the Democratic Party stood for the working class. Indeed, that was its base and major source of income. But with the steady decline in union participation rates, both power and financial contributions also fell, leaving the party to look elsewhere. It turned to Wall Street and corporate America, beginning with Clinton.

Today presidential elections cost more than a billion dollars. The Rest of Us don’t have any money, which leaves us without official representation nor any chance, really, of influencing legislation.

The hope, I suppose, is that there are enough people with a conscience serving in Congress and state legislatures who haven’t yet completely abandoned the shriveling working class. Still looking.

Or, maybe it’s possible to somehow align the interests of domestic capital with domestic labor. Can something be good for both Wall Street and the Rest of Us? Only under Clinton’s second term did the incomes of all quintiles rise. The economic pie grew along with the wages of the Rest of Us. The rich, as always, did even better (data from Saez).

Perhaps we can only look in the rearview mirror. Those relative halcyon days of the 50s and 60s may have been an anomaly in an otherwise persistent trend in divergence.

Downton Abbey syndrome

January 15, 2014 by davald

If there is one takeaway from the PBS series it’s the stark differences between nobility and “staff.” Lines must never be crossed, and formalities must always be practiced. Even as the British lords and ladies grudgingly accommodate changing times, appearances count no less.

In watching the episodes I, for one, gain a dramatic appreciation for what is meant by conservatism. Royalty staunchly defends the status quo against progressive forces. After all, the status quo is good for the wealthy and a severe impediment to everyone else.

In the Republican Party I witness that top-down legacy at work. It is alive and well, operating on multiple stages, tragically aided and abetted by those it systematically screws.

Yesterday, Washington state governor Jay Inslee stood before the legislature to challenge the current status quo. He professed  support for increased education funding, higher pay for teachers, and a raise in the state’s minimum wage. The reaction from the Downton Abbey contingent was as swift as it was predictable: dead on arrival.

President Obama triggers a similar response each time he speaks (but rarely acts) in favor of reducing the chasm between the rich and the Rest of Us. Republicans counter with ideology devoid of facts, understandable since we know the latter have a known liberal bias.

And so we sit, unable and unwilling to move toward an economy that treats citizens with dignity rather than as mere cogs in an impersonal machine. Our collective shame is that we enable the continuing debasement, failing to understand that modern-day lords and ladies have no intention of letting us walk up the stairs, save for tending to their every need and desire. How easily purchased, we are.

 

Berkeley economist Emmanuel Saez, upon whose data the above chart is based, writes:

…the Great Recession has only depressed top income shares temporarily and will not undo any of the dramatic increase in top income shares that has taken place since the 1970s. Indeed, the top decile income share in 2012 is equal to 50.4%, the highest ever since 1917 when the series start.

As the rich accumulate more and more wealth they wield more and more political power. That power is used, as always, to keep the Rest of Us in tow. Some of us are still needed for service, though at ever diminishing wages. Those deemed surplus have seen the floor beneath them gradually weaken. Food stamps. Unemployment insurance. Wages. Pensions. Public education. All in the crosshairs of the lords and ladies.
