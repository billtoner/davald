Going backwards

May 10, 2012 by davald

The Marysville School District, on the first day of Teacher Appreciation Week, announced that it would layoff some two-dozen teachers and many more support staff and specialists in response to further state cuts in education. If the numbers stick, and they typically don’t by the time September rolls around, the remaining teachers will have more students in their rooms. Nor will they have the support of counselors to deal with unruly behaviors. Students will be denied music programs, among others.

Marysville, of course, is hardly unique. Education budgets have been cut all across the country. But no place has been harder hit than California, which used to boast of the world’s best education systems, from kindergarten through graduate school. Now it ranks at or near the bottom.

This piece, written by a San Francisco teacher, provides dismal statistics.

The San Francisco Unified School District (SFUSD) opened contract negotiations by sending out over 500 layoff notices. It tried to split the union by attacking seniority, and then proceeded to demand even larger concessions by, for example, asking to raise class size in K-3 from 22 to 25 stuents.

We already rank highest in the number of K-12 students per teacher, with an estimated 20.5 students per teacher–the rest of the country averages 13.8, according to the California Budget Project. This will make things worse for students and educators trying to work in an already underfunded system.

I hate this shit.

McCusker enters a lion’s den…

May 4, 2012 by davald

…with predictable consequences. Which lion am I talking about and who the hell is McCusker?

James McCusker writes a weekly column for the Everett Herald. He is described as “a Bothell economist, educator and consultant.” In this morning’s column he enters the den of the Keynesian lion, Professor Paul Krugman. McCusker writes:

Conservatives claim that government stimulus efforts are ineffective — and they’re right, too.

Actually, they’re wrong, if you follow the arguments of Christine Romer, Krugman, Baker, De Long, and, of course, John Maynard Keynes. It was Romer, for example, the Berkeley expert on the Great Depression and its eventual recovery, who provided data in this report in favor of monetary expansion, a government stimulus effort. (The Federal Reserve, last time I checked, is a creature of the federal government.) Romer wrote:

Plausible estimates of the effects of fiscal and monetary changes indicate that nearly all the observed recovery of the U.S. economy prior to 1942 was due to monetary expansion.

She included this chart.

Of course, the recovery after 1942 is most certainly the result of increased government spending (fiscal stimulus) to fight a two-front war. We can see what happened to GDP in the following chart (in billions of dollars).

In his just-released book, End This Depression Now!, excerpted in the New York Review of Books, Paul Krugman writes:

The truth is that recovery would be almost ridiculously easy to achieve: all we need is to reverse the austerity policies of the past couple of years and temporarily boost spending. Never mind all the talk of how we have a long-run problem that can’t have a short-run solution—this may sound sophisticated, but it isn’t. With a boost in spending, we could be back to more or less full employment faster than anyone imagines.

On his blog Krugman has written about the austerity-fueled death spiral happening in Europe (here, for example). Germany, which experienced a stronger recovery following the Great Recession than certainly the peripheral countries like Greece and Spain, insists that all Eurozone nations adopt its economic strategy, which was to maximize exports. (However, all countries cannot be net exporters, since someone has to import, and therefore, buy the exports of other nations like Germany.) Yet, Germany’s insistence that countries tighten their fiscal belts has resulted in huge cuts in spending and massive layoffs. These measures have further reduced revenues, creating even more need to contract budgets, resulting in less revenue, and so on—leading to a death spiral.

To a lesser extent, austerity is underway in America, mostly in the states, which are forced to balance their budgets. With reduced tax revenues occasioned by the economic downfall, legislatures are cutting budgets, in some cases drastically. With the budget cuts come layoffs of public employees and reduced spending on public services like education and roads. The contractions further reduce revenues, leaving legislators with continued rounds of cutting. Thus, the states resemble most of the Eurozone countries.

In a prior post Krugman quoted remarks uttered in 2010 by Jean-Claude Trichet, then president of the European Central Bank:

As regards the economy, the idea that austerity measures could trigger stagnation is incorrect.”

Incorrect?

“Yes. In fact, in these circumstances, everything that helps to increase the confidence of households, firms and investors in the sustainability of public finances is good for the consolidation of growth and job creation. I firmly believe that in the current circumstances confidence-inspiring policies will foster and not hamper economic recovery, because confidence is the key factor today.”

Given recent history, Mr. Trichet comes across as a benighted idiot. Here’s a chart, based on OECD data, that shows GDP trajectories, starting in 2007, or just before the recession.

We can see that only Germany has increased its GDP over its 2007 level. All of the other nations, both the GISPI and the UK (which has its own currency), have not returned to pre-recession levels. Greece, of course, is in a death spiral, with no signs of recovery.

Keep in mind that all of these economies were growing steadily before the recession, and most governments had been reducing their debts. Moreover, the above chart tracks GDP against the 2007 level. Had the respective economies continued on their same pre-2008 trajectories, their GDPs would be above the 2007 figures.

Now, McCusker is not alone in his opinions. Indeed, he’s in good company, with the likes of Trichet, PM David Cameron, and the entire U.S. Republican Party. But he and others ignore considerable research on the effects of government stimulus.

Diminishing compensation

May 2, 2012 by davald

I may at times sound like Johnny-one-note, drawing attention to, if not belaboring, America’s gross income inequality. I do this because it’s true—big time. It cannot be denied by those in possession of the facts.

Via Matthew Yglesias, I link you to a report by Lawrence Mishel of the Economic Policy Institute. Mishel cites three major reasons for the rising inequality:

Income inequality has grown over the last 30 years or more driven by three dynamics: rising inequality of labor income (wages and compensation), rising inequality of capital income, and an increasing share of income going to capital income rather than labor income.

What’s particularly interesting, and something I’ve commented on before, is the divergence in productivity and labor compensation. While the former continues to climb, the latter broke off the curve in the 70s and has never returned. Mishel gives us the following chart to illustrate the phenomenon:

American workers are producing more but earning less, especially when one considers that the hourly compensation includes the One-percenters. Mishel does the detail work for us, breaking down compensation into segments. He helps us appreciate the details with another chart, this one focusing on trends post-1973.

These figures, of course, coincide with the Great Divergence. America was a decidedly different country in the first two decades following World War II. We were certainly more equal during the “relative halcyon days.” Opportunities for improvement were much better. Wages continued to rise. The economy grew at its fastest clip. All this began to change in the early to mid 70s.

Worse for the Rest of Us, as our wages stagnated or declined, the marginal tax rate on the wealthiest dropped. So, not only were the Rest of Us now struggling to pursue the American Dream, which has all but evaporated for most, governments’ ability to fund public services, especially education, declined. Why? Because tax revenues fell, in large part because the rich weren’t paying their fair share. As Mishel says, much of this had to do with the increasing percentage of income in the form of capital gains, which are taxed at a much lower rate than ordinary earnings.

Working less

April 30, 2012 by davald

The above chart, based on St. Louis Fed data, means something, though I’m not quite sure what. Certainly the average worker is putting in fewer hours each year. The number was 1,912 hours in 1970; it fell to a low of 1,732 in 2009, then rose a bit the following year.

We can also see that after a period of relatively stable numbers during the 80s, the number of hours worked climbed during the Clinton administration, only to fall precipitously under Bush II. We might ask, “What’s wrong with working less?” After all, that seemed to be a goal a while ago, experiencing more leisure time as technology lessened the need for us to work. Perhaps we were making more money per hour worked, in which case our standards of living may have remained the same or even rose a bit.

Hmm. Real wages increased by 12 percent under Clinton, seven percent under Bush II, and remained flat under Obama. The Great Recession had almost everything to do with the last period. Nevertheless, though we are working fewer hours, we’re not making any more money.

I realize that you’re not making that kind of money, benefits included. These are averages. The One-percenters skew everything. Still, we can see that over the Bush II years, continuing into the Obama administration, real compensation has remained essentially flat.

As I said, these numbers are skewed, and more so of late.

Keep in mind that the lowest Gini index is 0.25 in Sweden, and the highest exceeds 0.50 in some countries (e.g., Nicaragua, Mexico). The point of the above graph is to show that U.S. inequality rose steadily from 1970 to 1994, then has stayed somewhat flat since. (I include the graph and this brief discussion to illustrate the skewering effect.)

We also know that millions of Americans have lost their jobs following the collapses of the housing bubble and Wall Street. And the duration of unemployment has skyrocketed.

Over 40 percent of the unemployed have been out of work for more than 27 weeks, according to data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics, via the St. Louis Fed.

The picture is gloomy. The unemployment rate, though falling from its peak of 10.9 percent in October 2009 to about 8.6 as of March of this year, is much higher than the post-war average of 5.6 percent (excluding the Great Recession years). Those who have a job are working less, while their compensation remans flat or falling, if we exclude the One-percenters from the calculations. If you lost your job, you’re likely to be out of work for a much longer period than the post-war average of 13 weeks, excluding the Bush II years and the Great Recession.

Well, one predictable response to the doom and gloom from the GOP is more austerity. Yeah, that’s the ticket.

Public pain

April 26, 2012 by davald

Paul Krugman provides us with a simple chart showing government employment numbers under Clinton, Bush II, and Obama. For all the haranguing of the “socialist” president, real socialists cringe at numbers like these.

As for the peak in the purple curve, that’s the U.S. Census Bureau hiring thousands of people to conduct interviews. They came, they did their job, and then they departed—along with thousands of other government workers.

Krugman surmises that all told 1.3 million public sector jobs have been shed since Obama assumed office. Were people still employed, Krugman suggests, the unemployment rate would be closer to seven percent, down from eight-plus.

Moreover, the data show that workers tossed out of their government positions have not been picked up by the private sector. This report from the New York Times offers more grim news on that front.

The number of people seeking unemployment benefits in the United States remained stuck near a three-month high last week, a sign that hiring has probably slowed since winter.

Meanwhile, Fed chair Ben Bernanke sees no reason to do more to help the economy, despite his admission that the recovery will be a long-time coming and the unemployment rate will remain uncomfortably high. But there’s always inflation just around the corner, right?

Professor Bernanke chided Japan’s central bank for its failure to raise inflation expectations. Chairman Bernanke pledges to stick to the Fed’s stated inflation target of two percent. That’s not going to cut it, in Krugman’s judgment.

By the way, Bernanke responded to Krugman’s sharp criticism of his former boss, and Krugman posts it here. I’ll include an excerpt from Krugman’s blog:

I guess the, uh, the question is, um, does it make sense to actively seek a higher inflation rate in order to, uh, achieve a slightly increased pace of reduction in the unemployment rate? The view of the committee is that that would be very, uh, uh, reckless. We have, uh, we, the Federal Reserve, have spent 30 years building up credibility for low and stable inflation, which has proved extremely valuable, in that we’ve been able to take strong accommodative actions in the last four or five years to support the economy without leading to a, [indiscernible] expectations or destabilization of inflation. To risk that asset, for, what I think would be quite tentative and, uh, perhaps doubtful gains, on the real side would be an unwise thing to do.

God, he makes Greenspan sound like Shakespeare. Bernanke has been assimilated.

One more thing. If you glanced at the De Long/Summers paper you may have caught this:

…[expansionary fiscal policy] is self-financing. There are no costs. No future tax increases are needed to amortize the extra debt, because economic growth does it on its own.

It is, rather, austerity that requires future tax increases.

Don’t you just love economics?

Anomaly

April 25, 2012 by davald

 “The two great aims of industrialism — replacement of people by technology and concentration of wealth into the hands of a small plutocracy — seem close to fulfillment.”

— Wendell Berry, as quoted by Mark Bittman in the New York Times

In my recurring theme of the Great Divergence I contrast today’s increased concentration of wealth and stagnating wages for the Rest of Us against the relative halcyon days of the 50s, 60s, and mid-70s. I say “relative” so as to qualify my description; there was much going wrong during that period, not the least of which were civil rights struggles and two wars, one cold the other a Southeast Asia slaughter. Here I wish to focus only on the economy in my comparison.

I’ll begin with this reprised chart, which shows the annual change in real GDP from the end of WWII to 2011.

For us baby boomers the post-war era was a period of increasing incomes, job security, and I suspect a broad belief that the future would be better than the past. The above chart comports with this view.

Then, at the end of the 70s the economy declined rather sharply year-to-year vis-à vis the relative halcyon days that preceded. Much as been written about the causes of the economic downturn, with globalization and technological innovation serving as the usual suspects. I would certainly add financialization, as aggregate profits derived from the financial sector rose sharply while manufacturing profits fell.

Whatever the causes of the Great Divergence, I’ve often wondered if the tendencies at work today are normal and that the relative halcyon days were an anomaly. If true, we’re in deep trouble.

Should the Rest of Us not particularly care for the present outcomes (permanently high unemployment rates and increasing concentration of wealth) and these outcomes are systemic, then the only alternative is to change the system or replace it with something different, one that produces outcomes more satisfactory to the Rest of Us.

We’ve heard this before, of course. For example, there was a fellow named Marx who offered a comprehensive critique of capitalism, calling for the workers of the world to unite so as to break the chains that tether them to a system designed to siphon off wealth into the accounts of a few.

Quaint.

The biggest problem confronting those who would dare change or replace the current system is that the pernicious outcomes have compounded over time. Not only do the rich have most of the money, they also have most of the political power, which ensures that they get their way.

You’re right. I’m not at all optimistic about the future. The Rest of Us have become inured to troublesome outcomes. Indeed, we have enabled it. We have long since forgotten how things once were and have resigned ourselves to the way things are, as if the outcomes are predetermined. A few in our ranks even embrace the system and intend to do well by it. Good luck.

The rich and therefore powerful prefer that the Rest of Us eat our own. So they pit one set of workers, say those in the public sector, against those struggling mightily in the private sector. Why should government employees receive such “generous” benefits and “lavish” pensions?

This race to the bottom is sick. The road to change demands solidarity among the Rest of Us. The enemy should be obvious, and it’s not your neighbor. Yet, there are some who dare take pity on the rich, as if the One-percenters deserve every penny of their wealth, and tough shit for the Rest of Us.

It is probably much too late to mount a comeback. We long since passed the critical point, probably around the time of Reagan’s reign. Then it would have been easier to alter the trajectories. But we besotted masses were enjoying collective lunacy. Fire the air traffic controllers? Damn right! Reduce taxes on the rich? Well, of course! After all, they are the job creators. End Medicare as we know it? Certainly!

There is another reason why no revolution is at hand, whether or not a televised one. Most of us who want and need a job have one. Most of us are not starving. Most of us have a roof over our head. Most of us make enough money to pay our bills. Most of us have health insurance. In other words, the majority is doing okay, and we’ll keep silent about it.

Sympathy for others? Who needs it.

By all means, we must have more austerity

April 14, 2012 by davald

People are taking their own lives in Europe, mostly Ireland, Greece, and Italy, but surely this suicide contagion will spread, given the German-led call for greater austerity. After a few years of boom, citizens are confronting a major bust, which has taken its toll on individuals’ psyches.

Researchers say the trend has intensified this year as government austerity measures took hold and compounded the hardships for many. While suicides often have many complex causes, researchers have found that severe economic stress corresponds to higher suicide rates.

Are there any bright spots in Europe? Once again, we can look to the Nordic countries.

Social scientists say that some nations, like Sweden or Finland, avoided a rise in suicide rates in times of crisis because they invested in labor-market projects — initiatives to help get people back on their feet — instead of cash handouts.

Work provides dignity, a necessary emotion for well-being. Of course, it also provides sorely needed income.

The lesson for us here, still facing unemployment rates above eight percent, is to provide jobs. The private sector is busy hoarding money in this protracted period of uncertainty. It will have to be the federal government, since state and local governments are revenue-starved.

If that sounds Keynesian, so be it. Stop the suffering.

Pouvez vous dire des pauvres?

April 1, 2012 by davald

Even the French are feeling the pinch of broad European austerity measures. This is a depressing article from the New York Times, which reveals increasing marginalization of the people in a country known for its generous welfare state and haute cuisine. Consider some statistics from the Times:

  • In France, half the nation’s workers earn less than $25,000.
  • While the country seems to exude prosperity, the number of working poor is up from 6.1 percent in 2006, and experts predict it will grow.
  • Today, up to 120,000 people are living in French campgrounds, according to Observatoire des Inégalités, a social watchdog group. While it is not a new phenomenon, officials say it is accelerating.

But like the United States and most of Europe, increasing public debt along with widespread belief that governments must curtail spending have made matters worse, especially for the growing ranks of the poor. The Economist magazine also turns a spotlight on France:

The awkward truth is that France, the second-biggest economy in the euro zone after Germany, faces a public-finance squeeze. French public spending now accounts for 56% of GDP, compared with an OECD average of 43.3%: higher even than in Sweden. For years France has offered its people a Swedish-style social model of services, benefits and protection, but has failed to create enough wealth to pay for it.

The Economist‘s recommendation is for France to institute labor reforms (i.e., lower wages and benefits) to increase the competitiveness of its workers. Certainly its unit labor costs are much higher than those of Germany, as this chart from the magazine makes clear:

Moreover, France’s current account balance is negative, as is the U.S.’s. Germany is running a trade surplus of over $200 billion; France’s deficit is over $63 billion (Economist). By the way, the U.S. is running a trade deficit of more than $470 billion.

It could be a matter of timing: recession, high unemployment, along with debt overhang from better days. Perhaps austerity measures, which are understandable reactions to high public debt, may make matters worse. Perhaps further these are extraordinary times that undermine conventional wisdom. Paradoxically, the solution to excessive debt may be more of it.

Paul Krugman cites a new study by Paul McCulley. From the report (pdf):

Fiscal austerity does not work in a liquidity trap and makes as much sense as putting an anorexic on a diet. Yet, “diets” are the very prescriptions that fiscal austerians have imposed (or plan to impose) in the U.S., U.K. and Eurozone. Austerians fail to realize, however, that everyone cannot save at the same time and that in liquidity traps, the paradox of thrift and depression are fellow travelers that are functionally intertwined.

…

The importance of fiscal expansion and the impotence of conventional monetary policy measures in a liquidity trap have profound implications for the conduct of central banks. This is because in a liquidity trap, the fat tail risk of inflation is replaced by the fat tail risk of deflation. In turn, the fatness of the deflation tail is a function of the government’s willingness and ability to pump-prime, i.e. to borrow and spend.

McCulley concludes:

Unless fiscal stimulus is deployed with force to offset the loss of demand from private deleveraging in a liquidity trap, enduring economic slack, deflationary pressures and the risk of depression may arise. In the topsy-turvy world of liquidity traps, adherence to fiscal and monetary orthodoxies can be costly, and acting irresponsibly relative to orthodoxy can work.

But few policymakers will dare jettison conventional wisdom, believing that the present situation falls within normal business-cycle trends. They make excessive debt a moral issue, requiring stern medicine in the form of fiscal belt-tightening and, in effect, the persistent suffering of the poor and near-poor. They still fear inflation just around the corner, again because they fail to recognize just how extraordinary are the current circumstances. Liquidity traps, for example, are extremely rare.

As long as we continue to think that we’re either in a new normal or that the economy will simply recover spontaneously, we will eschew proper prescriptions, thus prolonging the misery.
