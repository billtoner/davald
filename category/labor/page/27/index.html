Feisty

September 9, 2011 by davald

The longshoremen temporarily blocked a train and closed ports. They’re angry, and it’s about time.

Meanwhile, back in the other Washington, President Obama delivered his long-awaited speech on “jobs.” David Brooks, writing in the New York Times, called him “feisty.”

The mainstream economic view is that we should combine near-term stimulus with long-range austerity. Up until now, the political system has been unable to perform this two-stage approach. Republicans won’t touch spending, and Democrats won’t touch entitlement reform.

The president clearly wants to give it a final shot. His tone on Thursday was feisty and will please Democrats. But the substance was heterodox and worth pursuing. In this moment of peril, the country needs an insurance policy against the double dip.

I do share one thing in common with Brooks: neither one of us is properly schooled in economics, but that doesn’t deter us from talking about it. We quickly part company, however, on the topic of “entitlement reform,” among a long list of other topics.

Only the ignorant would suggest that Americans as a whole are coddled by generous entitlements. We lack universal health care coverage. We lack retirement security. And now, for certain, we lack jobs that pay livable wages. The ones who are being coddled, as Warren Buffett pointed out, are the very rich.

But David Brooks won’t hear of taxing the wealthy.

If he [Obama] comes up with his own deficit proposal that pays for his programs with some serious entitlement reforms (and not merely with some boilerplate “let’s tax the rich” plan), then Republicans would be wise to work with him to make his growth ideas more effective.

Oh, and I think that David Brooks is coddled, too. He sure acts like it.

Family-wage jobs

September 6, 2011 by davald

In his new book, End-of-Loser Liberalism: Making Markets Progressive, economist Dean Baker writes:

A conscious goal of trade agreements like the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) is to make it as easy as possible for U.S. manufacturers to relocate their operations to Mexico and other developing countries. This has the effect of putting U.S. manufacturing workers in direct competition with low-paid workers in the developing world. The predicted and actual result of this policy is to eliminate jobs in manufacturing in the U.S. and to put downward pressure on the wages of the workers still employed in the sector.

The association is clear, if not also the causality. Consider the following chart, which represents the difference between U.S. exports (positive) and imports to the U.S. (negative)—also known as the current account balance:

The North American Free Trade Agreement, or NAFTA, became effective January 1, 1994. Almost immediately, the U.S. current account balance fell sharply.

Happy Labor Day?

September 5, 2011 by davald

Other nations have a labor party. We have only two political parties, both beholden to corporate interests. It’s no wonder that the working class has taken hit after hit, with no respite in sight.

E.J. Dionne tells it like it is. In a nod to the truth, we should dub today “Capital Day.”

He quotes Abraham Lincoln, the “first Republican president,” who pronounced labor superior to capital. That is, there can be no capital without workers. This is what Abe Lincoln said:

“Labor is prior to and independent of capital. Capital is only the fruit of labor, and could never have existed if labor had not first existed. Labor is the superior of capital, and deserves much the higher consideration.”

Dionne also quotes Pope John Paul II from his encyclical Laborem Exercens.

So it would take a brave man to point out that unions “grew up from the struggle of the workers — workers in general but especially the industrial workers — to protect their just rights vis-a-vis the entrepreneurs and the owners of the means of production,” or to insist that “the experience of history teaches that organizations of this type are an indispensableelement of social life.” [the italics in the original]

Workers are getting pummeled from all quarters. Unions, who represent just five or six percent of private-sector employees, have been thoroughly demonized.

The Seattle Times‘ editors joined the wrathful chorus. They blasted teachers, who have already been kicked in the gut, arguing that they should face real consequences should they dare withhold their labor to improve working conditions, or, more likely, prevent further deterioration.

The second problem is the threat of strikes. Get rid of them. Parents are tired of the threat hanging over their heads. Administrators are loath to call a union’s bluff because strikes do happen, despite the legal prohibition against them, outlined clearly by state Attorney General Rob McKenna.

“In Washington, state and local public employees do not have a legally protected right to strike,” McKenna said in a 2006 opinion.

Lawmakers ought to find a way to take the threat off the table. Union negotiators could holler strike, but fines and other penalties would start the minute they acted on their threat. What would remain would be the normal give-and-take in workplace negotiations.

Editors, go to hell. You wouldn’t last a day teaching kids. I write that as a former teacher and the husband of a career public school educator, who works harder with greater results than any opinion writer.

Can it happen here? Na.

September 2, 2011 by davald

The freedom to read anything leads me to a vast trove of opinions, and facts, and whatnot. Among my occasional reads is the Socialist Worker.

First of all, I find the term quaint, as I wonder if there are any—that is, working socialists. I’m sure that America has its share of socialists, but few, as far as I know, are on the job.

As to the question appearing in the title to this entry, the editors at SocialistWorker.org say yes, kind of. They’re talking about a revolution.

And why not? Revolution is in the air, just not over the United States. Middle eastern dictators are falling left and right, and even the stiff-upper-lip Brits have taken to the streets. Alas, the only people protesting in America these days are the crazy Tea Partiers, who seem to be mad about everything, especially socialists who don’t work.

The SocialistWorker, however, draws hope from the minor skirmishes that dot our landscape, including the recent, albeit short-lived, strike by Verizon workers. Borrowing a bit from Amory Lovins, the editors would like us to believe that a lot of smalls make a big. Amory is talking about distributed energy resources and not so much political revolution. The editors:

What individuals do now to build up networks of activists and political organization–in our schools, workplaces and communities–couldn’t be more important. By waging the important smaller battles of today, they are laying the basis for the larger showdowns of tomorrow.

So why not here? Well, one could write a book on the subject, and many have. But since we are a nation bereft of readers, the books and the editorials rarely find eyes.

Yet, there are the usual suspects to explore. In no particular order, here’s a brief list:

  • Americans, as a rule, have drunk the Horatio Alger Kool-Aid; if at first you don’t succeed, it’s your own damn fault
  • the rich are to be worshiped as role models, not as sources of government revenue
  • while the media may televise the incipient revolution, they’ll get it all wrong; besides, too many folks are watching American Idol
  • there may have been a class war, but the corporations won it a long time ago; they’ve got just about everything locked up, including a rather large police state, should they need it
  • the Republican crazies co-opted Jesus, who was all about getting rich and slamming the poor—didn’t you know?
  • admit it, you’d rather go apeshit over the Seahawks than join the revolution
  • Abe Lincoln was spectacularly wrong: the U.S. government is of, for, and by the corporations, who are, after all, just like you and me—Mitt Romney and the Supreme Court said so

I invite you to add to the list. I’m sure that it’s endless.

Joe Hill and me

August 29, 2011 by davald

The New York Times featured an interesting piece on Joe Hill, the fabled union organizer tried, convicted and then shot by a firing squad in Salt Lake City. What had Hill done? He was accused of killing a grocer and his son on Jan. 10, 1914. Hill denied the charge. No matter. The jury found him guilty, despite flimsy evidence, which many believed had been manufactured. Before facing a hail of bullets, Hill’s supporters from around the world protested his execution, including President Woodrow Wilson, who wrote two letters to Utah’s governor on Hill’s behalf.

He was guilty, all right, but not for murder. No, he was guilty of fighting the bosses by organizing their workers, inciting strikes, and drawing attention to labor’s miserable working conditions.

Hill never testified in his own defense. There were two reasons, one conventional, the other only recently exposed.

Like many in the courtroom, Hill thought the prosecution had no basis for conviction, and he believed that he shouldn’t have to prove his own innocence; that was the prosecution’s job. The scant evidence was circumstantial, and no one could prove that Hill had fired the shots. Actually, Hill had been shot, but not by the grocer trying to defend himself. Which brings us to the second reason.

Evidently Hill was trying to protect the reputation of a his girl friend, Hilda Erickson. Hill was shot and wounded by a suitor of Erickson, whom she had jilted in favor of Hill.

Quite by accident, a letter of Erickson’s was uncovered in a stack of papers, discovered by Hill’s latest biographer, William M. Adler. The Times:

Mr. Adler uncovered a long-forgotten letter from Hill’s sweetheart that said that he had been shot by a rival for her affections, undermining the prosecution’s key assertion. The book, “The Man Who Never Died,” also offers extensive evidence suggesting that an early suspect in the case, a violent career criminal, was the murderer.

That person was Frank Z. Wilson. The Times:

The police arrested Mr. Wilson the night of the murders after they found him walking without an overcoat near the grocery. They also found a bloody handkerchief on him.

Mr. Adler said Mr. Wilson had lied repeatedly to the authorities after they arrested him, but they soon released him for reasons that remain unclear. Mr. Adler also discovered that Mr. Wilson had used at least 16 aliases during his many arrests and convictions, several for robbing trains. He was later involved in the St. Valentine’s Day Massacre in Chicago in 1929, with a getaway car registered under an alias he often used.

As for the person who shot Joe Hill, the Times:

In the letter found by Mr. Adler, Hill’s sweetheart, Hilda Erickson, wrote that Hill had told her he had been shot by her former fiancé, Otto Appelquist — someone she had broken off with a week earlier and who had asked her “if I liked Joe better than him.” In her letter, she added, “I heard Joe tease Otto once that he was going to take me away from him.”

When he wasn’t busy organizing, Hill was writing songs for the International Workers of the World, the Wobblies. How appropriate, then, that an Alfred Hayes would write a poem about Hill, which Earl Robinson put to music in 1936: “I Dreamed I Saw Joe Hill Last Night.”

So, what does Joe Hill have to do with me?

As it happens, my grandfather was a political radical in his own right, with whispered rumors of Wobbly connections, if not also the Communist Party. When I was a boy I’d thumb through and listen to his modest collection of records—this would have been in the 50s. One such album was a medley of songs sung by Paul Robeson, a fellow traveler, it seems.

I have in my music library a couple of Robeson CDs. The first is a live recording of Robeson in 1949 at the Tchaikovsky Hall in Moscow. Among the songs was “Joe Hill.” In my opinion, this is the best rendition of the Hayes/Robinson composition. Accompanied only by piano, Robeson gives real passion and eloquence in a voice unlike any other’s. He skips the verse beginning with “From San Diego…” to deliver a dramatic, yet softer end. The Russians cheered mightily at its conclusion. (Robeson, by the way, spoke Russian among a dozen or more other languages.)*

But that’s not the more significant connection I have with Joe Hill. For I, too, organized, or at least tried to.

In 1977-78 I attempted to form a union at Pemco Insurance Companies down in Seattle. I did not succeed. And, while I was not killed by “copper bosses,” I was set up for dismissal. Yep, I was framed—just like Joe Hill—on trumped up charges, later confirmed by former colleagues. Rather than be fired for my organizing activities, I simply quit.

Oh, and there was likely a third reason that Hill declined to defend himself. He reckoned that he was more valuable to Labor’s cause dead than alive. Before his execution he said that followers should not mourn. They should organize.

“Joe Hill ain’t dead,” he says to me,
“Joe Hill ain’t never died.
Where working men are out on strike
Joe Hill is at their side,
Joe Hill is at their side.”

________

*  You can view a live recording of Joan Baez singing “Joe Hill.”

Retaliation?

August 23, 2011 by davald

Boeing has moved some of its Dreamliner work to South Carolina, whose governor has stated that she doesn’t like unions. The National Labor Relations Board, NLRB, filed a complaint against Boeing, charging the company with retaliating against its workers’ union. Fox News, predictably, bashed both the NLRB and the Obama administration for squelching jobs and punishing a U.S. manufacturer. (Boeing has actually added workers in both Puget Sound and South Carolina.)

Today, the New York Times Joe Nocera issued a broadside against Democrats. He writes:

Seriously, when has a government agency ever tried to dictate where a company makes its products? I can’t ever remember it happening. Neither can Boeing, which is fighting the complaint. J. Michael Luttig, Boeing’s general counsel, has described the action as “unprecedented.” He has also said that it was a disservice to a country that is “in desperate need of economic growth and the concomitant job creation.” He’s right.

Media Matters did a little research into the allegations. They write:

Labor Law Professor Secunda: Complaint Against Boeing Shows The NLRB Is “Carrying Out Its Congressionally Mandated Mission To Protect The Right Of Workers To Engage In Concerted Activity.” From a Seattle Times editorial by Marquette University Law School associate professor Paul Secunda:

The National Labor Relations Act gives workers the unequivocal right to engage in concerted activity — including the right to strike. Boeing stated publicly that it was moving production away from Washington because its workers there previously went on strike and could go on strike again in the future.

Such comments amount to an admission from the company that it was intentionally retaliating against employees and trying to limit their rights — a clear affront to the law that the NLRB is charged with enforcing.

At the end of the day, what we are seeing is the agency carrying out its congressionally mandated mission to protect the right of workers to engage in concerted activity for mutual aid and protection. The agency is simply enforcing the law, providing balance and fairness for workers and businesses alike. [The Seattle Times, 4/29/11]

Mr. Nocera’s publisher, the New York Times, carried this editorial on the NLRB complaint. They wrote:

The N.L.R.B.’s case rests on statements by Boeing officials that, it believes, prove retaliation. One Boeing executive told The Seattle Times that the main reason to put the new line in South Carolina was “that we cannot afford to have a work stoppage, you know, every three years.”

The paper’s editors, by the way, called the NLRB complaint “a welcome effort to defend workers’ right to collective bargaining.” I concur.

UPDATE (Aug. 23, 2011):

Economist Dean Baker takes on Nocera here. He writes:

The question in this case is whether the move to South Carolina is part of a threat against its unions. This involves an examination of the record of negotiations and discussions between management and Boeing’s unions. Without knowing this history, it is not possible to make an apriori assumption that the NLRB case has no merit, as Nocera does in his column.

Why unions matter

August 12, 2011 by davald

Over the last 50 years the union participation rate in the U.S. has steadily declined. Meanwhile, the marginal tax rate has fallen while the ratio of CEO pay to average worker wages has skyrocketed. (See chart below.)

Today, with the unemployment rate stubbornly refusing to fall below nine percent as corporate profits ascend, company managers are doing their best to ensure that workers make less, receive fewer benefits, and suffer job insecurity. In Wisconsin and a few other states thus far Republican legislatures and governors are rapidly undermining public employee unions while slashing worker benefits—all in the name of fiscal austerity.

The battle against unions and workers has entered the private arena in a big way, featuring Verizon employees. Faced with management’s proposal to gut employee benefit packages, 45,000 of Verizon’s represented workers walked off the job, in the largest labor strike in several years.

What’s at stake? For some commenters, this recent conflict has implications far beyond the Verizon workers. It may signal the beginning of a concerted corporate effort to eliminate unions altogether. The Socialist Worker writes:

THIS FIGHT is about the power of unions to protect jobs. Underlying Verizon’s attack is the same logic at work in the assault on public-sector workers. Political leaders across the country are attempting to pit taxpayers against government workers with claims that state budget crises are the result of fat pensions and paychecks of teachers and firefighters.

The magazine provides some salient facts:

  • According to the company’s propaganda, technicians make over $100,000 a year plusbenefits, but the reality is that hourly employees can only reach that level by working massive overtime–if it’s even available. The top base wage is $77,000 a year, which lags well behind profit growth, and a majority of workers earn far less.
  • “The CEO makes $55,000 a day, but they expect us to give back, when a lot us don’t even make $55,000 in a year,” said another striking worker in Manhattan.
  • Verizon is one of the giant U.S. corporations that that dodged paying any taxes at all for the last two years–and therefore contributed to government deficits at every level.
  • former Verizon CEO Ivan Seidenberg took home $81 million last year
  • Verizon’s revenues for this year are estimated at more than $108 billion. Net profits last year were $6 billion. Verizon’s top five executives alone received compensation of $258 million over the past four years.

The United States has become the most unequal society among the western industrial nations, according to OECD data. Corporate CEOs routinely take home tens of millions of dollars a year, with some hedge fund managers raking in a billion or more. Workers’ wages have stagnated or declined in real terms, as labor productivity continues to rise—companies are getting more bang for their labor buck.

It’s no surprise that the victory of the Haves correlates with the decline in union membership and the huge drop in the marginal tax rate. A famous Roman once said, “Divisa et vincere.” Our failure to stick together has made the Rest of Us easy pickings for the plutocrats.

And that’s why we need unions—now more than ever.

So much to do. So little will.

June 30, 2011 by davald

A recurring theme of these pages is the miserable state of both the economy and our political system, however defined. There are problems out there that demand urgent attention, but the folks who can make this happen are engaged in a hissy fit, spewing vitriol and untruths rather than taking a moment to reflect on why they’re even in office. First clue, it’s not to simply reward those who bought your ticket to the congressional dance. You really have a duty to “the people.”

Let’s take our nation’s infrastructure, for example. I think it’s a good one, because (a) it’s been so long neglected and (b) were our putative representatives to start paying attention the economy might actually recover. Quickly on the latter point.

This is a demand-constrained recession, which means that too many people lack sufficient income to spend on goods and services. They need jobs that pay “family wages.” If the government were to spend money fixing the things that are broken and building things that we’ll continue to need, people would be working once again. And when they work, they make money, which they use to feed, house, clothe their families, and pay taxes—just what the Economy Doctor ordered.

Each year for the last five the Urban Land Institute and the firm Ernst & Young produce a report detailing infrastructure needs. They’ve just issued their fifth. Here’s an excerpt:

In contrast with its global competition, the United states is lurching along a problematic course—potentially losing additional ground. After more than 30 years of conspicuously underfunding infrastructure and faced with large budget deficits, increasing numbers of national and local leaders have come to recognize and discuss how to deal with evident problems. But a politically fractured government has mustered little appetite to confront the daunting challenges, which include finding an estimated $2 trillion just to rebuild deteriorating networks. operating beyond their planned life cycles, these systems include roads, bridges, water lines, sewage treatment plants, and dams serving the nation’s primary economic centers.

Although President Obama ranks infrastructure as one of his administration’s top three “win the future” initiatives (together with education and innovation), the chances for setting and executing national priorities appear to be foundering in partisan debate over tax burdens and how to cut exploding government debt. Plans for transformational networks—regional high-speed passenger rail, a new electric grid tied to energy-saving technologies, and state-of-the-art satellite air traffic control systems to replace obsolete radar stations—will probably get delayed, pared back, or shelved.

Despite the nation’s unemployment woes, the vast job-creation potential of infrastructure projects is being sidetracked by concerns about government spending appetites and potential cost overruns. related benefits from reducing carbon footprints—energy efficiencies and greater independence from problematic foreign energy sources—are also failing to gain much traction. The overriding stumbling block to generating support for rebuilding the country’s infrastructure remains simple public resistance to paying more for these systems—either through higher taxes or user fees. Although informed voters have passed bond issues and even some sales tax increases for new projects, Congress perennially refuses to raise the federal gasoline tax or allow states to put new tolls on interstate highways, which could help ramp up funding for mass transit alternatives and repair existing highways and bridges. [added emphasis]

Oh, and this is a very good time for governments to be borrowing, since interest rates are so low. Thus saith The Economist. However, the magazine continues, there is a lingering belief that would-be bondholders fear that the government won’t pass along the costs of borrowing. The Economist says this is nonsense:

America is the world’s largest economy. Its citizens are among the richest and least-taxed in the developed world. American citizens will not move to other countries en masse if taxes go up (the equivalent of “losing market share”), and citizens can’t simply refuse to pay their taxes (the equivalent of “elastic demand”). Setting politics aside, there should be no question that the American government can “pass on its costs to consumers” if necessary. This ought to mean that debtholders will continue to trust the credit of the government, which should mean interest rates on its debt will stay low until growth picks up, which should mean that no extra tax increase to pay off debt will be necessary. [my emphasis]

That this magazine would offer these suggestions strikes me as almost amazing. After all, The Economist is proudly conservative. But what passes for conservative in the U.S. is closer to a weird hybrid of fascism and libertarianism, both inchoately practiced by amateurs residing under the loony umbrella of the new GOP.

It’s no wonder we’re in such a mess.
