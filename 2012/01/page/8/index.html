Good teachers

January 6, 2012 by davald

Like every reader of this post I attended school, though I skipped kindergarten, but not because I was brilliant; our family’s move occurred after the registration date. From grades 1 through 6 I had six different teachers. Since this was a very long time ago, I have only vague memories of them. All were female, with several newcomers joining veterans.

The one teacher I recall most fondly was Mrs. McKelvy, who taught sixth grade. She had a certain élan, an elegance that suggested to me that she belonged elsewhere, perhaps in fashion design or in the sitting rooms of high society. But she stood out from the rest as an educator, too. I attribute this to her ability to facilitate self-discovery among her students. She seemed to figure us out then exploit our varied interests and talents.

One rather strange classmate had long straight hair and loved horses, so much so that she imitated them in the classroom by galloping to and fro and neighing from time to time. Annoying. But she also loved to draw. Mrs. McKelvy encouraged her idiosyncrasies by first creating floor space, then providing butcher paper and crayons. And draw she did, rather elaborate yet realistic images of countless horses. I have no idea what happened to the horse-girl. However, I suspect that she found self-esteem and self-expression in at least one year of her schooling.

In my case, I must have shown an interest in music. So Mrs. McKelvy encouraged me to join the orchestra, as a string bass player. A couple of points here: I had no formal training in music, including note-reading; and, yes, we had an orchestra in elementary school! This was California in the golden days of education.

There was one other teacher, I think it was in third grade, on whom I had a crush. She was young and energetic, which helped. When she changed her last name via marriage about half way through, my little heart broke. Sad.

In high school there was Mr. Goltra, a graduate of Rutgers University in New Jersey. His head devoid of hair, he’d busily write formulas on the board, demonstrating trigonometry and elementary functions. My table mate and I would pound our fists and shout out, “Rutgers is proud!” Mr. Goltra beamed. He made math interesting, and I did reasonably well at it.

Now to my point: after spending so many years in the classroom I can remember fewer than three “good” teachers, from first grade through twelfth. And something else: there were few tests, and none induced fear and anxiety. How did I and hundreds of classmates learn without them?

I put scare quotes around good, because I have no idea if these three teachers added value to their students. And this seems to be the rage these days, “value-added teaching.” Several states have established criteria to grade teachers. The most controversial is test scores.  What makes a good teacher? Improved test scores. How does one improve test scores? With good teachers. Note the circularity.

Now comes a couple of researchers who believe they can demonstrate that good teachers have long lasting effects on their students. To wit, their graduates earn more money as adults. The New York Times:

Elementary- and middle-school teachers who help raise their students’ standardized-test scores seem to have a wide-ranging, lasting positive effect on those students’ lives beyond academics, including lower teenage-pregnancy rates and greater college matriculation and adult earnings, according to a new study that tracked 2.5 million students over 20 years.

Again, we’re back to a logical circle. If we define “good” teachers by their students’ test scores, then, it seems, to boost scores you’ll need good teachers. And how will you know if you have good teachers in the classroom? By their students’s test scores.

Here’s the tricky part. Aside from this definition, what are the ingredients of good teaching? What do good teachers do differently from their middling or poor colleagues? If we walked into a good teacher’s classroom, what might be the observable indicators of quality teaching?

On to an even stickier widget: Whatever qualities we might discern in good teachers, can they be taught to others? Could we create good teachers out of bad ones? Or, as one researcher suggests:

“The message is to fire people sooner rather than later,” Professor [John] Friedman said.

Ouch. Skip the training and go straight to the pink slip.

The New York Times previously asked the question: What makes a good teacher?

But what makes a good teacher? There have been many quests for the one essential trait, and they have all come up empty-handed. Among the factors that do not predict whether a teacher will succeed: a graduate-school degree, a high score on the SAT, an extroverted personality, politeness, confidence, warmth, enthusiasm and having passed the teacher-certification exam on the first try. When Bill Gates announced recently that his foundation was investing millions in a project to improve teaching quality in the United States, he added a rueful caveat. “Unfortunately, it seems the field doesn’t have a clear view of what characterizes good teaching,” Gates said. “I’m personally very curious.”

Aren’t we all?

Lacking an answer to the question are we left with the great sort? That is, school systems could track teachers over a number of years, collect and analyze data, then produce results similar to the aforementioned study. My guess is that there would be a normal distribution, with one tail of superior teachers and the other tail lousy teachers, with everyone else clumped around a mean—all determined by students’ test scores over time.

So, please tell me. How did I learn anything without all those tests? Or, maybe I’m dumber than a brick.

Macro gets attention

January 5, 2012 by davald

If you visit the economics blogosphere you’ll quickly notice that there is sharp disagreement between what some have called the “freshwater” and “saltwater” economists. The former roughly describes the University of Chicago, led perhaps by Robert Lucas, Jr., a Nobel Prize winner. The saltwater folks occupy the two coasts, Princeton and the Boston constellation on the Atlantic and Berkeley on the Pacific.

These disparate groups also happen to align either for or against Keynesianism, however defined of late. Princeton’s Paul Krugman is the most outspoken champion of Keynes’s theories applied to today’s economic situation.

Recently there’s been quite a bit of chatter regarding a paper written by Chicago’s John Cochrane, who argues that there is no such thing as fiscal stimulus. Here’s an exchange, of sorts, between Brad DeLong, a macroeconomist at Berkeley, and Cochrane.

Now I’m not going to weigh in on this squabble, for a couple of reasons: I’m a lesser mortal than the disputants, and many have already offered their opinions and arguments. I do want to touch on one point made by Cochrane:

The inflation that will result from a trillion dollars of money permanently dropped on the economy, and the real economic dislocation of such a major inflation, is frightful to contemplate.

Cochrane wrote this in 2009. Since, the Federal Reserve has effectively tripled the money supply, adding an additional two trillion dollars. Yet, inflation remains low.

So I thought I’d take a look at two sets of data: M2 and CPI. I calculated the annual change in each then plotted the outcome on a chart.

Now it’s true that between 1960 and 1980 inflation coincided with money supply. But that’s certainly not the case over the last 30 years. Indeed, despite a dramatic jump in the supply of money in 2009, the economy experienced deflation—that is, prices fell; the first time since 1955.

The broader question may be this: Why haven’t changes in the money supply since 1980 affected the Consumer Price Index?

American “exceptionalism” debunked

January 5, 2012 by davald

Americans derive a perverse sense of pride in their self-delusion. They refuse to believe obvious truths (e.g., nearly anything scientific) while embracing falsehoods like angels.

Among the latter is the myth of social mobility. Against the evidence, too many of our citizens rationalize rank inequality of outcome because each of us has equal opportunity.

Along the way to our misguided notions we lazily accept the shibboleth that “socialist” Europe—with all of its taxes, generous public services, and history of feudalism—squelches the entrepreneurial spirit and denies inhabitants the chance to get ahead.

Well, it just ain’t so. As the New York Times headline tells us, “[It’s] Harder for Americans to Rise From the Lower Rungs.” Harder than whom?—you may ask. From the linked study (pdf):

Mobility is lower in the U.S. than in the U.K., where it is lower again compared to the Nordic countries. Persistence is greatest in the tails of the distributions and tends to be particularly high in the upper tails: though in the U.S. this is reversed with a particularly high likelihood that sons of the poorest fathers will remain in the lowest earnings quintile. This is a challenge to the popular notion of ’American exceptionalism’. The U.S. also differs from the Nordic countries in its very low likelihood that sons of the highest earners will show downward ’long- distance’ mobility into the lowest earnings quintile.

Why is this? The New York Times:

One reason for the mobility gap may be the depth of American poverty, which leaves poor children starting especially far behind. Another may be the unusually large premiums that American employers pay for college degrees. Since children generally follow their parents’ educational trajectory, that premium increases the importance of family background and stymies people with less schooling.

We may think of the latter in terms of a steadily rising conveyor belt. The offspring of the privileged already occupy a spot. Many will not have to lift a finger to remain on board; they have earned their money the old-fashioned way—via inheritance. Others, say those with supportive, educated parents, are provided with the necessary conditions for the upward rise. Many more will have to struggle mightily to stay with it.

There’s another conveyor belt, this one headed downward. It’s really easy to remain on the belt. You can’t really fall off this one. The occupants are already poor from birth and are likely to remain so, as the study reports. Their parents lack education and probably a steady job paying adequate wages. The schools they attend are old, dilapidated, and ill-supplied. Teachers there try their best to engage unmotivated pupils; many give up. The children live in squalid conditions and suffer food insecurity.

Some on the upper belt fall off. They may drop into a kind of purgatory, occasionally getting a break to jump back on the rising conveyor. But the longer they stay in this in-between realm, the more difficult it is to rejoin their former peers; the gap grows larger over time. Others who fall land on the lower belt, from which they may strive to reach purgatory. It is nearly impossible to get from the lower to the upper belt.

The American middle class used to be larger and more confident. They did not need college to land family-wage jobs. With their incomes they could afford a home, a decent car, a vacation, and an occasional trip to a restaurant. They could also set some money aside and perhaps even help pay their children’s college expenses. That was my family.

Today’s middle class has shrunk; its occupants worried about the future. They are in this purgatory of unrealized dreams and diminished opportunity. If they are laid off, chances are they will be out of work for many months if not years, and if they do find another job it will be at a much lower wage. They risk falling to the lower belt, forever doomed.

It really doesn’t have to be this way, as the Nordic countries attest. We are supposedly a free citizenry living in a democracy. We could decide our futures. But it seems that too many of us have become fatalists, as if the savage inequalities are just part of “the system,” just the way things are.

A pervasive sense of profound futility looms large. That should bother us all.

Meanwhile, next door in Montana

January 4, 2012 by davald

The Montana supreme court followed common sense in thumbing its nose at the U.S.’s top justices. They’ve decided that Citizens United will not be applied in Big Sky country.

In 1912 the citizens of Montana passed an initiative, the Corrupt Practices Act, that prohibits corporations from spending money on political campaigns or parties. Last Friday’s ruling upholds the constitutionality of the act. Here’s Montana’s chief justice:

“With the infusion of unlimited corporate money in support of or opposition to a targeted candidate,” wrote Chief Justice Mike McGrath, in a 5-2 decision, “the average citizen candidate would be unable to compete against the corporate-sponsored candidate, and Montana citizens, who for over 100 years have made their modest election contributions meaningfully count would be effectively shut out of the process.”

Wow!

But not so fast. It is expected that the decision will be appealed to the U.S. Nine, who are expected to overturn Montana’s supreme court. That doesn’t make it right, though. Why tip all the scales in favor of the One-percenters?

Ask your iPhone

January 4, 2012 by davald

I have a well-deserved reputation as a technophile. My in-home office has been jokingly called ‘NORAD,’ as if I could launch the next world war from my chair. In addition to my desktop computer, HD display, scanner, printers, wireless routers, and a myriad of external hard drives (along with four internal hard drives), I have an iPhone and an iPad and an Apple TV. Yep, I’m all Cupertino. And there is no doubt that my personal setup vastly exceeds most office workers’ technology.

Indeed, there is a new trend in town. It’s called ‘the consumerization of IT.’ Employees’ personal devices are better and more user-friendly than the employer’s standard issue. Increasingly, companies have decided to accommodate rather than resist the invasion.

As some of you know I sit on the board of the Snohomish County Public Utility District, PUD for short. I’ve been openly touting the praises of non-Windows gadgets and software, Apple in particular, of course. I’d like to think that my affection for Jobs’s creations had nothing to do with it, but I am happy nonetheless that PUD management is dipping its collective toe in the non-Redmond pool. To wit, the utility is adopting iPhones (for “security,” don’t you know). This month the board will begin using iPads rather than bulky binders to access meeting packets. Information will be immediately sent and updated to our tablets, so that we commissioners are assured of receiving the latest information and data.

I’ve already been using my own iPad for packet materials. All of a meeting’s documents are available online. I use the iPad during the board meetings. I don’t have to carry around the binder, which would be a nuisance given that I walk to and from the PUD. My colleagues are understandably wary of abandoning the binders in favor of the new technology. But with a few hiccups, I’m certain that they will get the hang of it in short order. Will the PUD adopt other Apple products? Not my call, though I wouldn’t be surprised if it happened.

It is widely rumored that Apple will announce in New York an agreement, and perhaps some new technology, regarding educational textbooks, the iBook application, and who knows what else? Steve Jobs had long believed that students and teachers need not tote a bushel full of books and papers. Why, he thought, we could put all this stuff and more on an iPad, which weighs just a bit over a pound. Everything, including the Internet, would be instantly available by simply swiping the tablet face then tapping on an icon.

I should also mention that Jobs loathed teachers’ union. Not so much because he was necessarily anti-labor, but because he thought that stubborn unions were stuck in the past and resistant to change; and technology is all about change.

Well it seems that some Apple people have been busy in Idaho, of all places. They, and other technology companies, successfully lobbied/persuaded that state’s governor, superintendent of schools, and the legislature (all or mostly quite conservative Republicans, as is the entire state population) to adopt a far-reaching technology initiative, replete with laptops and tablets. Moreover, high school students will be required to take some online courses in lieu of a traditional classroom-cum-teacher.

Not all teachers are happy with this, as this article reports. The New York Times:

To help pay for these programs, the state may have to shift tens of millions of dollars away from salaries for teachers and administrators. And the plan envisions a fundamental change in the role of teachers, making them less a lecturer at the front of the room and more of a guide helping students through lessons delivered on computers.

This change is part of a broader shift that is creating tension — a tension that is especially visible in Idaho but is playing out across the country. Some teachers, even though they may embrace classroom technology, feel policy makers are thrusting computers into classrooms without their input or proper training. And some say they are opposed to shifting money to online classes and other teaching methods whose benefits remain unproved.

As much as I like technology, especially the Apple variety, I detest top-down decision-making on educational issues. I especially loathe far-removed politicians dictating what happens in a classroom. This is so un-Finland-like.

Yet, research supports the proposition that technology boosts academic performance. Here’s an article (pdf). You can find more on, of all places, the Internet! Heck, my brain would be simple mush without the worldwide web. Just about anything and everything is available through your favorite browser.

But does technology facilitate thinking? Here’s what one teacher told the Times:

 “I’m teaching them to think deeply, to think. A computer can’t do that.”

That makes sense, doesn’t it? We don’t lack for information. The challenge is figuring out what to do with it, and that involves thinking about something not just retrieving data.

Neither Idaho’s governor nor legislators can know students, discern their strengths and weaknesses, then fashion appropriate curricula and pedagogy to develop successful learners. It is the classroom teacher who is in the best position to make appropriate judgments about the use of technology, whether it helps or hinders student progress.

I’ve always wondered why there are no people in government or education who engage in the most obvious behavior: asking the classroom teachers what they need to do their job. Instead we get presumptuous dictators.

Boring is good

January 4, 2012 by davald

I do not lack a federal guarantee on my bank deposits. That’s because I’m a proud member of the Rest of Us, which means, inter alia, that I’m not wealthy enough to be uninsured.

But suppose you are fortunate to have more than $250,000 in a financial institution. The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation generally limits coverage to the quarter million, but not higher, and it does not cover mutual funds, stocks, and annuities, for example.

Fine and dandy.

Now what happened in the run up to the housing bubble collapse? Here is where things get complicated.

Following the repeal of the 1933 Glass-Steagall Act in 1999, the bright line between investment and deposit banks evaporated. The latter were permitted to invest or use as collateral depositors’ money in the great American casino, also known as Wall Street. Although the FDIC continues to insure that first quarter million, the newly created financial institutions, by combining investing and depositing, expose the entire banking system to possible collapse.

John Maynard Keynes wrote of “animal spirits,” really the unchecked, herd-like appetite for bigger returns on investments. Put more simply, if a group of entities is making a whole lot of money doing X, everyone wants the opportunity to do X. As stock prices kept rising, the conventional deposit bankers wanted in on the action—that is, to play on Wall Street. (chart source)

From roughly 1982 to 2000 the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose about 1,400 percent, from 777 to 11,723 points. What an enticement for deregulation and to open up this vast treasure trove to even the local bank on the corner. Congress, a wholly owned subsidiary of Wall Street, obliged by passing the Gramm–Leach–Bliley Act in 1999, which removed the separation between conventional deposit banks and investment institutions. Stock prices, which had fallen during the first few years of Bush II’s term, soared to their highest ever in 2008, reaching 14,165 points (Dow Jones).

The greed did not end with the repeal of Glass-Steagall; competition ensued over the financial instruments themselves, which became ever more complicated. And all these sophisticated schemes would be based on the value of homes, always rising never to fall. Or so it was believed by the people playing with everyone’s money.

When real estate values began to fall in the mid-2000s, the entire house of financial cards fell with them. (source)

The late economist Hyman Minsky suggested some explanations for the causes of financial bubbles. From Wikipedia:

Minsky proposed theories linking financial market fragility, in the normal life cycle of an economy, with speculative investment bubbles endogenous to financial markets. Minsky claimed that in prosperous times, when corporate cash flow rises beyond what is needed to pay off debt, a speculative euphoria develops, and soon thereafter debts exceed what borrowers can pay off from their incoming revenues, which in turn produces a financial crisis. As a result of such speculative borrowing bubbles, banks and lenders tighten credit availability, even to companies that can afford loans, and the economy subsequently contracts.

See the “animal spirits” in there? We just experienced “speculative euphoria,” this one based on real estate. Minsky believed that we should be worried during times of prosperous excess, and the warning signs were there. Just look at the chart. Key to Minsky’s argument was that these moments are endogenous to the markets themselves and not triggered by external events. The bubbles and subsequent collapses have everything to do with individual players’ quest for financial gain. Greed is dubbed good, so let the games begin.

I have no truck with those who wish to play with their own money and assume the risks of investing as well as the rewards—though I would impose a transaction tax. But I emphasize their own money. They should not be allowed to backstop their gambles with depositors’ accounts. I also believe that investors should establish their own private insurance to cover their misfortunes. No more bailouts on the public’s dime.

It should be clear from recent history that we need to resurrect “boring banks,” as Professor Amar Bhidé proposes here. Among other things, Congress should (but it won’t) repeal the Gramm–Leach–Bliley Act and restore Glass-Steagall. Otherwise we will have enshrined moral hazard: heads I win, tails you lose. If banks continue to take risks knowing that they get all the rewards when things end well and a bailout when things sour—we’ll see more bubbles.

Prof. Bhidé goes further:

…we need to take away the reason for any depositor to fear losing money through an explicit, comprehensive government guarantee. The government stands behind all paper currency regardless of whose wallet, till or safe it sits in. Why not also make all short-term deposits, which function much like currency, the explicit liability of the government?

Guaranteeing all bank accounts would pave the way for reinstating interest-rate caps, ending the competition for fickle yield-chasers that helps set off credit booms and busts.

Wall Street relies on our collective tendency to forget. After a while normalcy returns and we all get complacent. Meanwhile, the gamblers get itchy, leading to another round of “speculative euphoria.”

When will we ever learn?

The sense of doom widespread

January 3, 2012 by davald

Why, just across the pond, albeit a rather large one, comes this observation, in response to the European fuss over the euro and debt:

But in a sense we are all democratic fatalists now. One of the most striking things about this crisis is that the basic divide it has revealed is not between fundamentally different political views of the future, but simply between optimists and pessimists. Looking back on all the hundreds of articles and commentaries I have read, this is the theme that dominates. Either we’re fucked or we aren’t. Either we’re almost out of time or we’ve always got more time. The optimists believe that democracy will be all right because it has always been all right. Indeed, in every crisis of the past hundred years the people who have written off established democracies have been made to look like fools: the doom-mongers always overstate their case because they mistake short-term difficulties for long-term weaknesses. The pessimists believe that this time it’s different: European democracies have finally got themselves into a bind from which there is no obvious means of escape. American democracy is not far behind on the road to ruin. But the pessimists aren’t proposing any alternatives to democracy, they simply want us to know that its luck may finally have run out.

That’s what makes this crisis for democracy different from any that have gone before. We might be worried that the Chinese are stealing a march on us with their neat, streamlined, ruthless, election-free politics. While we’re squabbling and tinkering, they are deciding what needs to be done and then doing it. But no one in the West is seriously advocating that we should ditch our democracies and adopt Chinese autocratic capitalism. We want the system we’ve got, because we know it’s the least bad one on offer. In the past, democracies in crisis have always had to fear being swept away by some plausible ideological alternative. The current argument between the optimists and the pessimists has all the hallmarks of an ideological dispute but without any of the content. We don’t have an alternative. The fear is that the political system we’ve relied on in the past might not be up to the task at hand, but it’s the only one we’ve got. You’d think that would make it easier for us to fix it. My fear is that it’s going to make it harder. It makes it more likely that we will drift along with our fate, and into the unknown.

I wonder if an ascendant China, which dispenses with all our democratic claptrap, will rule the world—not via its military, since America is still Number One!—but through its particular combination of autocracy, financial acumen, an increasingly well-educated elite, and literally millions of people desperate to work long hours under horrendous conditions to survive.

We would have no desire to replicate the Chinese system, however defined. As the writer of the linked piece suggests: we’re stuck with our own history of conflicting ideologies, endless bickering, egregious political machinations—all marinated in an obsessive greed. We may be overtaken by a resurgent Asia and succumb to global warming, but we’ll go down squabbling to the last bitter rhetorical fusillade.

Good luck with this, Bay Area

January 3, 2012 by davald

I grew up in the Bay Area, specifically Concord, Calif. (Mention of this typically invokes puzzlement. But when I say that my hometown neighbors Walnut Creek the mental lights return.) The eastern part of this region is quintessential sprawl. Over the last 60 years massive housing tracts have gobbled up agricultural land. They are interconnected by thousands of miles of asphalt, enabling monumental traffic jams each weekday morning and evening as mindless workers take the journey from their garages to San Francisco offices or blue-collar venues along the waterfronts, then return at the end of the day. And the days are long, with commute times easily exceeding one hour each direction.

You can appreciate that this haphazard arrangement between suburban sleep and metropolitan employment wastes time, money, energy, and the environment. There’s got to be a better way.

To the rescue, however too little or too late, is a new initiative called Bay Area One. As this article suggests the challenge is “daunting.” That ostensible challenge is to reduce transportation-related carbon emissions. The trick is to build up rather than out, curbing reliance on private automobiles, a remarkably inefficient means to get from A to B.

Another 2 million residents are expected to live in the Bay Area by 2035, with most of them getting around by automobile, the source of an estimated 41 percent of greenhouse gas emissions in the region.

Planners say they want to steer new development toward public transit corridors and stations and ease reliance on the auto.

Bay Area One is a joint effort of the Metropolitan Transportation Commission and the Association of Bay Area Governments. Neither possesses the authority to direct new patterns of urban development. But they do hold the purse strings. Judicious distribution of transportation dollars, it is hoped, will lead to increased population density situated near and around public transit hubs.

Not everyone is on board. Opposition comes from—who else?—real estate and home-building entities.

“They base their utopian model on high-density housing with shops underneath, no parking, but a lot of cycling and walking,” said Heather Gass, an Alamo real estate saleswoman, in a blog post critical of the growth plan. “What these people don’t seem to understand is that people move to the suburbs to get away from this type of urban lifestyle.”

Invoking the Big Brother specter, Gass adds: “At least the Soviet Union’s big plans were for five years. This plan will transform our area for 25 years.”

These are the same folks who fought Washington state’s Growth Management Act, which attempts to confine population growth to urban centers. If you find yourself on I-5 trying to make your way to Seattle you can reach your own conclusion as to the Act’s success.

We still have suburban tracts, but they tend to be smaller than before. The housing industry has managed to create greater density, but not in the cities so much as in the suburbs—bigger houses on tinier lots. We still lack adequate public transportation; so there is no viable alternative to the automobile—thus, more cars and traffic congestion. Nice try, Olympia.

The Bay Area has B.A.R.T., a rapid transit system that alleviates some highway congestion. But it’s hardly adequate, as the following anecdote underscores.

My brother in law was a San Francisco cop who lived in Antioch, about 50 miles from Baghdad by the Bay. As a police officer he was able to ride B.A.R.T. for free. However, to get a seat he drove his car into Concord, where he caught an outbound train. When it arrived in Antioch, the terminus, he simply remained for the trip into the city.

There is always a chicken-egg syndrome in new urbanism. Attracting existing or would-be suburbanites into downtowns—like Everett, where I currently reside—requires amenities (e.g., entertainment, restaurants, public safety). But the amenities can only be built and sustained with more city dwellers. Who goes first?

Neither cities nor suburbs were deliberately planned; they evolved. Seattle, for example, began as a shipping port, since it was on the water and proximate to raw materials—lumber, for the most part (“skid row“). The growth of suburbia coincided with the Eisenhower-era federal highway act and its thousands of miles of cement corridors. As it became more expensive and probably dirtier and scarier to live in cities, young couples sought a cheaper and cleaner alternative to raise families. Since land cost less as one got farther from the city, suburbia sprouted on dirt once farmed. (At the same time agriculture became more efficient requiring less acreage, which freed up large, easily convertible areas for houses and roads.)

Now many of us recognize that suburbia is unsustainable—too many carbons. And those carbons are becoming ever more costly at the pump and to the environment.

But old habits die hard. Moreover, there are substantial financial interests wedded to the status quo (e.g., oil and car companies) along with new generations pursuing their “American dream.”

My guess, hardly sanguine, is that even if government efforts to change our inherited arrangement succeed they will be too little and much too late. Our whole economy runs on oil, and oil is our doom.

The great macro divide: salt and fresh

January 2, 2012 by davald

But it was inevitable that freshwater economists would find themselves trapped in this cul-de-sac: if you start from the assumption that people are perfectly rational and markets are perfectly efficient, you have to conclude that unemployment is voluntary and recessions are desirable.

— Paul Krugman

The Great Recession, besides bringing misery to millions, has cast a skeptical eye on the economics profession. How could so many putatively bright people get things so horribly wrong? The few who issued dire warnings about the expanding housing bubble were dismissed as “misguided.” But the bubble collapsed anyway, evaporating some $13 trillion in household wealth, according to Krugman, and leaving 15 million Americans without work or grossly underemployed.

It used to be said that the sun revolved around the earth. Those who denied what seemed obvious were ostracized or even imprisoned. Those clinging to the geocentric model assumed that God was behind it. Since humans were at the apex of his creation, it must be the case that the planet upon which men and women lived was at the center of the universe. Theory, however wrong, trumped telescopic observation-cum-arithmetic.

Likewise many in the economics profession cling to their cherished notions of rational agents and perfect markets. When things go terribly bad, as they have since the bubble collapsed, the theory can’t be wrong. Rather, “unemployment is voluntary and recessions are desirable.”

If you have wondered at all about what policies, if any, should be in effect to spur economic recovery, you would benefit from a reading (or re-reading) of Krugman’s lengthy September 2009 essay (click on the above link). You may not be persuaded by Krugman to alter whatever views you may have of macroeconomics or the role of government in the economy, but at least you’ll gain an appreciation for the sharp divide in the profession and some understanding of the dispute.

As for the profession, Krugman leaves us with this advice:

So here’s what I think economists have to do. First, they have to face up to the inconvenient reality that financial markets fall far short of perfection, that they are subject to extraordinary delusions and the madness of crowds. Second, they have to admit — and this will be very hard for the people who giggled and whispered over Keynes — that Keynesian economics remains the best framework we have for making sense of recessions and depressions. Third, they’ll have to do their best to incorporate the realities of finance into macroeconomics.
