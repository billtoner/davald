It’s demand, stupid

January 14, 2012 by davald

To supplement my most recent post on Eurozone difficulties, I’ll add Krugman’s observation here. He quotes from S&P’s explanation for its downgrading France’s debt.

We also believe that the agreement [the latest euro rescue plan] is predicated on only a partial recognition of the source of the crisis: that the current financial turmoil stems primarily from fiscal profligacy at the periphery of the eurozone. In our view, however, the financial problems facing the eurozone are as much a consequence of rising external imbalances and divergences in competitiveness between the EMU’s core and the so-called “periphery”. As such, we believe that a reform process based on a pillar of fiscal austerity alone risks becoming self-defeating, as domestic demand falls in line with consumers’ rising concerns about job security and disposable incomes, eroding national tax revenues [my emphasis].

Only Greece, however, could be rightly accused of “fiscal profligacy” before the Great Recession. Debt-to-GDP ratios were actually falling in the periphery prior to the housing bubble collapse.

It’s been said (by the Financial Times‘ Martin Wolf) that if a country should wish to join the Euro, it will have to be okay getting in bed with Germany, since Germany essentially dictates financial policy for the Eurozone. Krugman quotes a report on German chancellor Merkel’s response to the downgrading:

German chancellor Angela Merkel has called on eurozone governments speedily to implement tough new fiscal rules after Standard & Poor’s downgraded the credit ratings of France and Austria and seven other second-tier sovereigns.

Well, I guess she really didn’t pay attention to S&P’s reasons for the downgrade. Fiscal austerity, as Keynes said, is not the prescription for recessionary disease.

Societal symbiosis

January 14, 2012 by davald

New York Times columnist Charles Blow makes a good point in his essay this morning. He first quotes Elizabeth Warren, running against Scott Brown in Massachusetts:

“There is nobody in this country who got rich on his own. Nobody. You built a factory out there, good for you. But, I want to be clear: you moved your goods to market on the roads the rest of us paid for. You hired workers the rest of us paid to educate. You were safe in your factory because of police forces and fire forces that the rest of us paid for. You didn’t have to worry that marauding bands would come and seize everything at your factory and hire someone to protect against this because of the work the rest of us did. Now look, you built a factory and it turned into something terrific or a great idea. God bless. Keep a big hunk of it. But part of the underlying social contract is you take a hunk of that and pay forward for the next kid who comes along.”

That is the corporate Contract With America: societal symbiosis. We create a society in which smart, hard-working people can be safe and prosper, and they in turn reinvest a fair share of that prosperity back into society for posterity.

Everyone benefits.

Increasingly, however, the rich, having received all of the breaks (including a massive bailout of Wall Street), want to deny support for the Rest of Us, whether it be education, job security, or better transportation. Their message: I became wealthy, so what’s your excuse?

In a recent column George Will intoned:

When taxes are levied not to efficiently fund government but to impose this or that notion of distributive justice, remember: Taxes are always coerced contributions to government, which is always the first, and often the principal, beneficiary of them.

Yet he seems to echo Blow’s point, albeit through the back door. He first condemns a “redistributive” government, then concedes the following:

The tax code, government’s favorite instrument for distributing wealth to favored factions, has been tweaked about 4,500 times in 10 years. Generally, the beneficiaries of these changes are interests sufficiently strong and sophisticated to practice rent-seeking.

Not only does redistributionist government direct wealth upward…

He goes on to suggest that government somehow “siphons power into itself” as it transfers more dollars from the Rest of Us to the wealthy. Though a proud beneficiary of government largesse, he nevertheless protests “the distribution of wealth produced by consensual market activities.”

Since the start of the Great Divergence “societal symbiosis” no longer obtains. More and more of society’s wealth has been siphoned off by the One-percenters, who have become so financially and politically powerful that they are able to operate the levers of Congress to produce favorable treatment.

Will dismisses such talk of “social contract” or “societal symbiosis.” He writes:

The left’s centuries-old mission is to increase social harmony by decreasing antagonisms arising from disparities of wealth — to decrease inequality by increasing government’s redistributive activities.

So? He’s evidently ignored the Nordic experiment, which does precisely that. The Scandinavian countries enjoy lower unemployment rates, better education (all unionized, of course), and even greater social mobility. It’s easier for a middling Norwegian to ascend to a higher stratum than it is for an American.

For Will, it’s all about “consensual market activities.” And if such activities lead to greater inequality, so be it. If they should threaten economic collapse, as they just did? So be it as well?

You want models?

January 13, 2012 by davald

Whether or not our political leaders take global warming seriously (and they’re not), global warming continues, mostly as a result of combusting coal. But there’s another specter threatening under seas and tundra: methane “burps.”

The climate researchers tending to the blog site Real Climate have given us an interactive model. We get to put in some numbers, push the calculate button, and, voilà, instant graphs.

CH4, methane, is an especially pernicious greenhouse gas, over 20 times more potent than CO2. Huge pockets of the gas are trapped under frozen tundra and cold seabeds. But as the climate warms, the tundra thaws—same with the oceans. The resulting burps promise further catastrophe.

Have a nice day.

I feel a little bit better

January 13, 2012 by davald

Several weeks ago I started writing a post that was pure speculation, and it was about Apple, the company. It had occurred to me, without much evidence at all, that Tim Cook, who replaced Steve Jobs, would move differently on three fronts: employee relations, supplier labor, and the environment.

Having attended Michael Daisey’s one-man show, The Agony and Ecstasy of Steve Jobs, I was appalled by the working conditions at Foxconn, Apple’s principal supplier of iPhones and iPads. Nor was Apple faring well within the environmental community. Greenpeace, in particular, gave the company black marks for its production processes.  I believed then that Jobs could easily afford a kinder, gentler Apple, one that respected both labor and the environment.

Well, my speculation about Tim Cook may have been correct. He just sent an email to employees announcing significant changes. Among the highlights:

  • the release of a supplier responsibility report, which provided details on over 200 audits of supplier processes and policies, in particular the use of child labor (Daisey’s revealed that young teenagers were employed by Foxconn to assemble by hand hundreds of iPhones per day; after they developed disabling injuries from repetitive tasks, they were discarded then replaced with another batch.);
  • Apple has just joined the Fair Labor Association, the first technology company to do so;
  • Tim Cook is pushing for a “greener” Apple.

However, Tim Cook is no Steve Jobs, and here I mean that as a compliment—with respect to how he relates to employees. He comes across as modest, unassuming, and gracious; traits absent in Jobs. Almost immediately after assuming the reins, Cook told Apple employees that the company would match their charitable giving. From Walter Isaacson’s biography, we learned that Jobs was prickly at best on the topic of philanthropy, shunning invitations by Bill Gates and Warren Buffet to join their endeavors.

It’s about time, Apple. Now keep it up.

The philosophy—or is it the science?—of economics

January 13, 2012 by davald

Given the collective egg on economists’ faces these days—having relied on outmoded theories to tell the world that everything was fine, only to see the biggest economic collapse since the Great Depression—one might think it a good idea for those in the profession to conduct a little soul-searching. In particular, why didn’t Prof. X or Prof. Y see the train wreck coming, and why aren’t they taking a very close look at both theories and observations?

It seems that a few professionals are doing this very thing, however disorienting. Noahpinion offers us a brief glimpse into the fretful journey.

For macro, though, I fear that many of us are still telling ourselves that deduction is all we need – that we should keep putting “theory ahead of measurement,” that we should continue to believe in theories after the data has rejected them. That needs to stop, or we’ll never get anywhere. I like to think that the awarding of last year’s Nobel prizes to two macro empiricists – one who created an alternative to DSGE, and one who rejected a ton of rational-expectations models – is a sign that other people have realized this as well. But it’s going to be an even more uphill battle than in micro. Macro has much worse data, after all. It’s almost certain that, unlike in micro – where additions like Prospect Theory often represent only moderate tweaks to existing frameworks – most or all of our existing macro theories will turn out to be not even close to right. But we have no choice but to reject those models, or we will eventually fade into noodling irrelevance.

Welcome, economist, to the desert of the real.

Refreshing.

Blessed be brown fat

January 13, 2012 by davald

It seems that regular exercise turns white fat brown, which is a good thing, say researchers. Brown fat is associated with lower obesity and diabetes rates.

Brown fat, as many of us have heard, is physiologically desirable. While white fat cells are essentially inert storehouses for fat, brown fat cells are metabolically active. They use oxygen and require energy. They burn calories.

Until recently, it was thought that human adults did not have brown fat, that we lost our stores after babyhood. But beginning in 2009, a number of studies showed that grown-ups do harbor brown fat. Some people just have more than others.

And it may be that irisin, and exercise, partially determine how much brown fat each of us contains, the new study suggests. In perhaps the most compelling of the many separate experiments detailed in the Nature paper, the scientists injected irisin into white fat cells removed from mice. Afterward, genetic changes in the cells signified that they were browning. The fat cells also increased their respiratory rate, an indication that they were burning more energy.

So, get thee off thy duff. Or, maybe we’ll eventually take a pill to transform our fat and skip exercise altogether.

This is why I read Egan

January 13, 2012 by davald

His essay this morning is entitled “A Murder at Paradise.” The New York Times‘ Timothy Egan poignantly reminds us of both beauty and tragedy, commingling at the foot of Mount Rainier on New Year’s Day. A disaffected Iraqi veteran shot two bullets into park ranger Margaret Anderson, leaving a widower and two young children.

In the West, most people live in sprawling urban centers not far from the public-land playgrounds that shape so many lives on the sunset side of the United States. When the worst of our world spills over into that other one, it is people like Anderson who hold the line.

She was asked to do so on New Year’s day, which dawned Technicolor bright on Mount Rainier, the 14,411-foot volcano that can get more snow in some years than any other place on earth. The biggest draw is Paradise, more than a mile above sea level at the high point of the main park road, named by an early visitor who gushed, “This must be what Paradise is like.”

iPhone envy

January 13, 2012 by davald

My wife’s iPhone slipped out of her hands landing on the hardwood floor. No apparent physical damage, but the darn thing wouldn’t work, no matter what buttons I pushed. So we took a little trip to the Alderwood Apple store to meet a “genius.”

The genius went through the same routine as I, with the same result. Nada. He then read with his young eyes the serial number off the sim tray (I had to use a big magnifying glass at home). The phone was in limbo: about halfway between the warranty expiration and the upgrade date.

We were then handed off to another Apple employee to consider options. I had another brain fart, giving the individual my phone number rather than my wife’s. She accessed the account using the MacBook Air. It appears that an upgrade was at hand. Splendid. Now for the choices. Get another 3GS, a 4, or the new iPhone 4S. My wife shrugged, then selected the newest and greatest. Bless her. She added a protective case for this one and Apple Care for two years of free service. The salesperson began entering the numbers for the transaction. This time I got the phone numbers straight. Sorry. It’s my phone that’s eligible and not my wife’s. Now what? Well, you could fork over $500 at a minimum, even for another 3GS. But as Steve said, “One more thing.” The employee said that we could walk on over to the AT&T store inside the mall. It’s possible that a “swap” could be had.

So walk we did. We were not disappointed.

The AT&T guy said “sure,” we could purchase the iPhone using my eligibility status and switch the numbers. Then, come this November, when my wife’s now-damaged phone would be upgradable, I could purchase the newest and greatest, likely an iPhone 5.

Now, of course, I have iPhone envy. But there’s a rumored iPad 3 just around the corner. Neat.
