Under God

January 18, 2012 by davald

It’s amazing what one can accomplish with a lot of money to spend. Why, the One-percenters have been at it for a very long time, but much in earnest since the Collapse of 1929.

Imagine yourself back then, now struggling to put food on the table along with nearly everyone else in the Great Depression. You’ve lost faith in corporate America, rightly viewed as the problem rather than the solution. The banks were worst of all, busy foreclosing on homes and denying people credit or arrangements.

Well, the One-percenters didn’t just sit back and take it. They organized. But first, what message to spread and who should spread it? Wouldn’t you know that surveys showed that a plurality, if not a majority, of Americans placed their trust mostly in clergymen of the decidedly Christian variety.

As for the message, how about “one nation under God”? Kevin Kruse, writing for the New York Times:

During the Great Depression, the prestige of big business sank along with stock prices. Corporate leaders worked frantically to restore their public image and simultaneously roll back the “creeping socialism” of the welfare state. Notably, the American Liberty League, financed by corporations like DuPont and General Motors, made an aggressive case for capitalism. Most, however, dismissed its efforts as self-interested propaganda. (A Democratic Party official joked that the organization should have been called “the American Cellophane League” because “first, it’s a DuPont product and, second, you can see right through it.”)

Realizing that they needed to rely on others, these businessmen took a new tack: using generous financing to enlist sympathetic clergymen as their champions. After all, according to one tycoon, polls showed that, “of all the groups in America, ministers had more to do with molding public opinion” than any other.

The One-percenters kept at it, all the way to 1953, persuading Congress to include “under God” in the Pledge of Allegiance, written by a socialist, by the way. But they didn’t stop there, of course. Just ask our present-day members of Congress who feeds them.

Internet opposition to congressional initiatives

January 18, 2012 by davald

I admit to not following these matters very closely. But as anyone who has attempted today to use Wikipedia or conduct a Google search can attest, there’s rather sharp opposition to at least two pending bills. One is known as SOPA; the other as PIPA. (You can catch a summary of them here. Usually I’d consult Wikipeda, but the site is down in protest.)

SOPA stands for Stop Online Privacy Act. PIPA for Protect IP Act. If you happened to access Wikipedia today you are invited to enter your zip code, as noted above; the folks at Wikipedia deliver contact information on one’s congressional representatives. In my case they are: Senators Maria Cantwell and Patty Murray and Congressman Rick Larsen. So, I did my civic duty, sending very brief notes to Cantwell and Larsen; Murray’s site must have been overloaded, because I was unable to access it.

I received one reply so far, from Ms. Cantwell. I’m sure that it’s the same letter that you’d receive should you contact her. Here ’tis:

Thank you for contacting me about the internet streaming of copyrighted material. I appreciate hearing from you on this issue.

On May 12, 2011, Senator Leahy (D-VT) introduced S. 968, the Preventing Real Online Threats to Economic Creativity and Theft of Intellectual Property (PROTECT IP) Act. While I am supportive of the goals of the bill, I am deeply concerned that the definitions and the means by which the legislation seeks to accomplish these goals will have unintended consequences and hurt innovation, job creation, and threaten online speech and security. On November 17, 2011, I signed a letter along with Senator Ron Wyden (D-OR) objecting to the bill as it is currently written.

On December 17, 2011, Senator Wyden introduced the “Online Protection and Enforcement of Digital Trade” (OPEN) Act (S. 2029), of which I am an original co-sponsor. The bill has been referred to the Senate Finance Committee, where it is currently awaiting further review. The OPEN Act is a more effective approach to stopping foreign web sites that are found to be primarily and willfully used to infringe intellectual property rights. The OPEN Act builds on the existing legal framework used by the International Trade Commission for addressing unfair acts in the importation of articles into the United States, or in their sale for importation, or sale within the United States after importation.

Our trade laws have yet to catch up to deal with the global digital economy. The OPEN Act recognizes that the Internet has created new opportunities for foreign products to reach the U.S. market and that there is little difference between downloading a pirated movie from a foreign website and importing a counterfeit movie DVD from a foreign company. For those foreign web sites that are determined after an investigation to be primarily and willfully infringing, the International Trade Commission will issue a “Cease and Desist” order. The “Cease and Desist” order may also be served on financial intermediaries that provide services to that foreign web site, compelling financial payment processors and online advertising providers to cease doing business with the foreign site in question.  This would cut off financial incentives for this illegal activity and deter these unfair imports from reaching the U.S. market.

The OPEN Act addresses the same challenges as the PROTECT IP Act, while protecting freedom of speech, innovation, and security on the Internet. The challenge of rogue web sites is one that many nation’s face. The United State has always been seen as a leader on Internet issues. Laws we establish in the United States regarding the Internet are likely to be used as models around the world. And because the Internet is global in nature, it is important that we carefully consider how the laws and policies we adopt in this area may be received and translated by other countries.

Thank you again for contacting me to share your thoughts on this matter. You may also be interested in signing up for periodic updates for Washington State residents. If you are interested in subscribing to this update, please visit my website at http://cantwell.senate.gov. Please do not hesitate to contact me in the future if I can be of further assistance.

Sincerely,
Maria Cantwell
United States Senator

 

Truth be told, I’ve never downloaded an illegal copy of music or video. While I’m aware of bitTorrent, I’ve never used it. But it seems that many people are engaged in placing illicit content on the Internet and many more are downloading it illegally. The government, as always, wishes to protect the property of private corporations, which explains in part our nation’s draconian copyright and patent laws, which likely do more harm than good to the economy by keeping costs artificially high (e.g., prescription drugs).

As for the OPEN Act, you can read a comment here by folks at the Electronic Frontier Foundation, a group dedicated to keeping the web open. That means, among other things, ensuring that there’s limited government interference with the Internet. While EFF judges OPEN better than the above alternatives, it presents us with “the bad”:

The provision for entering temporary or preliminary relief arguably does not require (or contemplate) participation from the targeted site (notably, neither does the Federal Rules). It does require notice to the site before implementation, but we’d much prefer a process that includes both sides, especially because temporary or preliminary relief as contemplated by the OPEN Act can potentially silence fully legal speech. (Sec. 2(f)(2)).

The OPEN Act lacks a public interest provision that would require the ITC to consider the effect on the public interest that its order might have. The rules governing patent actions at the ITC require it to take into account the public interest; unfortunately, the ITC doesn’t always take that requirement seriously. To preserve the balance between intellectual property claims and the public interest, the OPEN Act should expressly mandate that the ITC make findings on the effect that a cease-and-desist order would have on the public interest.

The vigilante provision still lives, which is our biggest complaint about the OPEN Act. The legislation’s current draft includes language that can be read to provide immunity for financial transaction providers and Internet advertising services who cut off payment and services to a site based on a “reasonable belief based on credible evidence” that the site is dedicated to infringing activity and the services do not otherwise breach their contractual relationships with the sites. Unfortunately, the draft legislation fails to define “a reasonable belief based on credible evidence,” creating a scenario where providers could proactively cut off services to avoid potential liability, even if their “reasonable belief” is only based on notice from the big content industries. (Sec.2(j)).

Whatever your attitudes about these measures, today matters came to a head on the Internet. In addition to Wikipedia and Google, even Microsoft, which has forever complained about piracy, joins the protest; it opposes SOPA “as currently written.” As much as I can tell from searching the net, Apple, as is its wont, reminds silent.

The White House has issued a statement that, while expressing support for the protection of intellectual property, finds the competing bills problematic. Here’s an excerpt:

Let us be clear—online piracy is a real problem that harms the American economy, and threatens jobs for significant numbers of middle class workers and hurts some of our nation’s most creative and innovative companies and entrepreneurs. It harms everyone from struggling artists to production crews, and from startup social media companies to large movie studios. While we are strongly committed to the vigorous enforcement of intellectual property rights, existing tools are not strong enough to root out the worst online pirates beyond our borders. That is why the Administration calls on all sides to work together to pass sound legislation this year that provides prosecutors and rights holders new legal tools to combat online piracy originating beyond U.S. borders while staying true to the principles outlined above in this response.  We should never let criminals hide behind a hollow embrace of legitimate American values.

What are those principles?

  • Any effort to combat online piracy must guard against the risk of online censorship of lawful activity and must not inhibit innovation by our dynamic businesses large and small.
  • We must avoid creating new cybersecurity risks or disrupting the underlying architecture of the Internet [emphasis in the original].

Join the protest?

Corporate tax rate

January 18, 2012 by davald

Despite its Republican Party advocacy, reducing the U.S. corporate tax rate may have some merit. Compared to Europe, America’s tax rate is high. Consider this chart from KPMG:

But we should keep in mind that the U.S. rate is “statutory”; the effective tax rate of many, if not most, U.S. corporations is less, in some cases substantially less. Indeed, through a myriad of loopholes and exemptions some corporations actually receive money back from the U.S. Treasury. One such study, albeit dated and reprinted here, found that the average real tax rate for Fortune 500 companies was less than 20 percent, which is half the statutory rate.

According to this New York Times article there is broad support for reforming corporate taxation, but achieving consensus on the details remains elusive. While many concede that the statutory rate is too high, those who enjoy the advantage of various loopholes favor the status quo. The Times:

Arguably, the United States now has a corporate tax code that’s the worst of all worlds. The official rate is higher than in almost any other country, which forces companies to devote enormous time and effort to finding loopholes. Yet the government raises less money in corporate taxes than it once did, because of all the loopholes that have been added in recent decades.

The Times reports that the existing tax structure includes perverse incentives, which retard economic growth.

The problem with the current system is that it distorts incentives. Decisions that would otherwise be inefficient for a company — and that are indeed inefficient for the larger economy — can make sense when they bring a big tax break. “Companies should be making investments based on their commercial potential,” as Aswath Damodaran, a finance professor at New York University, says, “not for tax reasons.”

Instead, airlines sometimes buy more planes than they really need. Energy companies drill more holes. Drug companies conduct research with only marginal prospects of success.

Inefficiencies like these slow economic growth, and they are the reason that both conservatives and liberals criticize the corporate tax code so harshly. Mitch McConnell, the Republican Senate leader, says it hurts job creation. Mr. Obama, in his State of the Union address, said that the system “makes no sense, and it has to change.”

A question begs itself: Should corporations write bigger checks to Uncle Sam? Or, in the alternative, might we lower corporate tax receipts and boost personal income taxes?

A Republican would surely oppose each, since conservatives always and everywhere believe that taxes are too high. Democrats are likely all over the map, with some answering yes to one or the other.

Perhaps a different perspective is in order. We know that the U.S. is running up a huge federal debt and it continues to run annual budget deficits. The mantra of the day is to cut spending rather than raise revenues. There appears to be little support for incurring more debt as part of fiscal stimulus, even though some economists, notably Paul Krugman, argue that this is precisely the prescription to cure the present disease, occasioned by diminished aggregate demand.

There’s no question that government receipts have lagged behind expenditures, especially of late. From above we know that corporate tax revenues have declined over time, notwithstanding the comparatively high statutory rate. We also know that the marginal tax rate on personal incomes has fallen dramatically since the 50s, and that overall income tax rates have declined. Consider this chart, which I’ve posted before:

Remember the good old days of the 50s? As the above chart suggests, from a fiscal position, they were indeed good—spending and revenues tracked together. (The shaded columns represent Republican administrations.) Then, like just about everything else in our society, things began to change dramatically with Ronald Reagan’s first term. Spending, as always, continued to rise. But tax receipts fell behind, yielding, for the first time since WWI, significant federal deficits.

During his presidency, Bill Clinton persuaded Congress to raise rates on personal income. Doing so actually produced a surplus. But it was only temporary; Bush II sharply reduced tax rates. It’s been downhill from there, as the chart plainly shows.

Having looked at this for a bit I believe the following are in order:

  • reduce the statutory tax rate to the Euro average (about 21% in 2010, according to KPMG)
  • eliminate most, if not all, tax loopholes and exemptions; create proper incentives, but give some leeway to smaller startups
  • restore the Clinton-era tax rates on personal income

While I’m at it, I would also jettison fossil-fuel subsidies and impose a carbon tax. That’s a good start, I think.

Two passages

January 17, 2012 by davald

As I get older, news about others turns negative. In place of weddings and births, obituaries reign. A few thoughts on two recent deaths. I did not know either person well.

The first was Bob Hayman. He and his wife and young family were briefly neighbors in Marysville. They had met at Stanford, where he received a degree in fish biology, I think. Makes sense since he earned his living as a fish biologist. The family moved to Seattle some time ago, ironically a few blocks from my daughter’s residence on the same street.

When I was much younger I’d jog. And I do mean jog, rather than run. No marathons in these bones. I set out in the early mornings back then, usually before dawn. Invariably I’d be startled by the sound of bouncing shoes on the pavement. It would be Bob, a serious and proficient runner who literally bounded as he sprinted past me.

He seemed to be a private man, much less gregarious than his wife. He appreciated his children and had turned their home into a playground of sorts for their kids.

I don’t know what ailed Bob. I presume it was cancer, since his circulatory system must have been excellent; all those miles each day.

My thoughts go out to Barbara, his wife, and their children. He was much too young to die.

Today I learn that Walt Notaft passed away, after a long struggle with cancer. He regularly attended PUD board meetings. But he didn’t just sit there, he cheerfully contributed with thoughtful comments and questions. As a retired Boeing engineer he got the technical stuff, so his contributions were well-informed. He was also wont to praise the utility for its “prudence,” what has become a favorite expression of mine and I think my colleagues. However defined, we seem to have it at the PUD, judging by our record of achievements, if I can boast for a moment.

Having often been the lone member of the public sitting in the audience for every board meeting, in these past several months Walt’s attendance became conspicuously sporadic, then non-existent since the end of last fall.

My thoughts extend to his wife, Karla, who contacted the PUD this morning with the sad news.

Be gone, Austerians

January 17, 2012 by davald

Austerity isn’t working, nor should anyone have expected it to, at least those who should know better—like economists. But economic thinking has entered what Paul Krugman calls “the dark ages” of macro. Lessons learned 50 years ago having been discarded in favor of ideologically based models; elegant but essentially worthless.

Jeff Madrick, writing for the New York Review of Books, chronicles the follies of European austerity.

On the last day of 2011, a headline in The Wall Street Journal read: “Spain Misses Deficit Target, Sets Cuts.” The cruel forces of poor economic logic were at work to welcome in the new year. The European Union has become a vicious circle of burgeoning debt leading to radical austerity measures, which in turn further weaken economic conditions and result in calls for still more damaging cuts in government spending and higher taxes. The European debt crisis began with Greece, and that nation remains the European Union’s most stricken economy. But it has spread inexorably to Ireland, Portugal, Italy, and Spain, and even threatens France and possibly the UK. It need not have done so. Rarely do we get so stark an example of bad—arguably even perverse—economic thinking in action.

As I was saying…

January 17, 2012 by davald

My discussions about the state of the economy and, in particular, the growing disparity in wealth has been buttressed by a recent talk given by Alan Krueger, Obama’s new head of his Council of Economic Advisers. You can watch it here.

Among his major points is that wealth inequality retards economic growth. If more of us had more money we’d likely spend it, increasing the amount of economic transactions, which would lead to more jobs. Krueger talks a bit about the “marginal propensity to consume” (MPC). The rich spend less of their income than the Rest of Us, a completely obvious deduction. Yet, there is overwhelming evidence that the rich have siphoned off more and more wealth from everyone else. It should not be a surprise, then, that aggregate demand has fallen over the last few years; it would have fallen even further had more of us not taken on more debt.

Krueger presented several interesting charts. A couple stand out. The first one shows how, by implication, the increasing fortunes of the very rich have elevated the income of the median household. Over time a smaller number of households fall within the bottom 50 percent. The middle class has shrunk.

The other was what Krueger called “the Great Gatsby” chart. It was a scatter-plot graph, with the vertical axis showing intergenerational earnings elasticity and the horizontal the Gini coefficient. (You can download Krueger’s text and slide presentation from the Center for American Progress here or the slideshow only here.)

Thus, the greater a country’s inequality (Gini index) the harder it is for a poor man’s child to become rich and, ironically enough, the more difficult for a scion to lose rank. If your parents are poor in America, so are you and so will your offspring. Meanwhile, generations of the rich and powerful will likely remain so.

And speaking of the rich, their share of total income has risen to historic levels, the highest since just before the Crash of 1929. Krueger tells us that the share going to the top one percent is equivalent to the bottom 44 percent of us. If we had that money instead of the rich, an additional half a trillion dollars would be added to the economy each year, which was less than Obama’s fiscal stimulus package, much of it in the form of tax breaks.

Krueger offers several policy prescriptions:

  • allow the Bush II tax cuts to expire
  • adopt the “Buffet rule”: no one making a million dollars or more should pay a lower tax than the middle class (Romney, by the way, recently said that his effective tax rate is 15 percent, implying that most of his wealth is subject to the capital gains tax.)
  • extend the payroll tax and unemployment benefits (the latter providing the most bang for the buck)
  • increase federal spending on infrastructure and education

None of these are part of Mitt Romney’s plan to boost the economy, by the way. According to the New York Times, here’s Mitt:

  • immediately cut the corporate tax rate to 25 percent, down from 35 percent
  • make the Bush II tax cuts permanent: that means about $600 for the Rest of Us and literally millions for the top 0.1%, where Romney comfortably resides
  • repeal Obama’s health care plan (which is based on Romney’s plan when he was governor of Massachusetts)
  • reduce environmental regulations: drill, drill, drill to create jobs; climate change be damned
  • cut $20 billion from the federal budget, for starters

I should end with a comment of Krueger (who’s also a Princeton economist when he’s not working at the White House). He emphasized that the Bush II tax cuts did nothing to spur the economy; indeed, they probably worsened the situation. When Clinton raised taxes, the economy soared. Also, the “broadland” years featured much higher marginal tax rates, higher union participation rates (a counter to capital’s power), economic growth, and rising incomes for the Rest of Us.

Let’s return to that brighter future. More of the same ain’t getting us nothing.

Low inflation: coincidence or planned?

January 17, 2012 by davald

Among the more remarkable observations one can make about the economy over the last few decades—in addition to the Great Divergence—is inflation; or, to be more precise, lack thereof. It seems that no matter what happens to the overall economy, through booms and busts, inflation remains low. This could be coincidence, but I wonder.

Bill Moyers upon his return to PBS interviewed Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson, the authors of Winner-Take-All Politics. They argue that the growing inequality in American society cannot be explained by globalization or technological innovation. Other nations sensitive to each have not experienced the huge transfers of wealth from the Rest of Us to the One-percenters. Rather, the gap between the Haves and Have-nots, they argue, is the result of a concerted effort by the wealthy operating through newly sprouted conservative think tanks, conventional pro-business organizations (e.g., the Chamber of Commerce), persistent and massive lobbying, combined with political campaign contributions.

Hacker and Pierson, both academics, described the period between the end of WWII and the end of the 70s as “broadland,” an era that saw the incomes of all quintiles rise, with the lower strata rising even more than the upper. This all began to change during Ronald Reagan’s presidency, but had the support of both Democratic and Republican presidents since, culminating, perhaps, in the Bush II tax cuts, which transformed budget surpluses into now-enormous deficits. (Moyers posts a salient graphic here. I’ve reprised one of mine below)

Within this context of growing inequality, is it possible that government policies, along with those of the Federal Reserve, have achieved the goal of modest inflation at the expense of the Rest of Us? More to my topic, might the huge increase in wealth among the top one percent be partially explained by historically low inflation rates?

The wealthy, as we know, own a disproportionately high percentage of financial assets, including stocks and bonds. Inflation erodes the value of those assets. Thus, it is in the interest of creditors to keep the Consumer Price Index stable and low.

Notice the dip in the bottom four quintiles from roughly 2000 to the present. In other words, 80 percent of U.S. households experienced a decline in their real incomes. We, as debtors, have an obligation to creditors, mostly One-percenters. With low inflation rates the diminishing value of earned income exacts a toll.

In these days of astronomical wealth, when fortunes are measured in billions, higher inflation makes the rich notice. For each billion dollars of wealth, inflation of five percent represents a $50 million hit in real wealth. If I’m a billionaire holding mostly financial paper (as opposed to tangible assets), inflation causes me to be poorer. (Hold the tears.)*

Consider this:

Income and wealth disparities  become even more  absurd  if we look at the top 0.1% of the nation’s earners— rather than the more common 1%. The top 0.1%—about 315,000 individuals out of 315 million—are making about half of all capital gains on the sale of shares or property after 1 year; and these capital gains make up 60% of the income made by the Forbes 400.

Now, how do federal policies keep inflation low and stable? One way is to restrict the supply of money.

We may recall from a previous post that there appears to be a correlation between the velocity of money and inflation. Here’s a chart that I posted then.

Velocity equals nominal GDP divided by money supply. It’s a measure of a dollar’s transactional frequency; or how much it circulates in the economy.

The Federal Reserve, which controls the supply of money, has a dual mandate: to somehow create conditions to assure full employment and to manage inflation, the latter, again, through currency volume in circulation. Since roughly 1980 both unemployment and inflation rates were relatively low—until the Great Recession, which resulted in millions of workers losing their jobs.

The Fed, it could be said, was apparently doing its job; at least for a while. Then things began to unravel with the collapse of the housing bubble. But inflation actually declined, becoming negative in 2008. In response, the Fed caused a dramatic increase in the supply of money.

There is some research (pdf) that suggests the economy may expand with the money supply. That’s what Christine Romer concluded from her study of the first FDR term. We also know that in response to the historically high inflation rates during Carter’s last years in office, then-Fed chair Paul Volcker squeezed the supply of money; inflation fell dramatically in the early 80s, but at the cost of higher unemployment. The chairman chose lower inflation over jobs.

There’s something else going on. While the incomes of the Rest of Us have stagnated or fallen since 1980, economic growth seems to be falling as well.

So we have these interesting trends, all happening in parallel:

  • lower inflation
  • lower economic growth
  • lower marginal and capital gains tax rates
  • stagnating incomes for the Rest of Us
  • skyrocketing wealth for the top one percent

We can add to this list a swelling federal debt.

Perhaps only a simpleton would dare to connect these dots. Well, call me simple.

MLK was dangerous

January 16, 2012 by davald

Meek men don’t get assassinated. That fate is reserved for those who dare to pester the rich and the powerful. Martin Luther King annoyed the hell out of our nation’s elite. It was not for his stand against racism, though he played a significant role in the eventual passage of the Civil Rights Act. Rather, MLK condemned the fortress of capitalism, calling for a massive redistribution of wealth.

In a 1968 Memphis speech he called it “criminal” that a nation so rich would force men and women to work for “starvation wages.” He advocated a guaranteed income, and it was Richard M. Nixon who pushed Congress to pass relevant legislation; the idea of a basic income has been championed by those on the left (Karl Marx) and the right (e.g., Frederich Hayek and Milton Friedman).

Here are some quotes of King that appeared in a Socialist Party USA pamphlet:

The dispossessed of this country — the poor, the white and Negro — live in a cruelly unjust society. They must organize a revolution against that injustice, not against the lives of the persons who are their fellow citizens, but against the structures through which society is refusing to take means which have been called for, and which are at hand, to lift the load of poverty.

…

We must rapidly begin the shift from a ‘thing’-oriented society to a “person”-oriented society. When machines and computers, profit motives and property rights, are considered more important than people, the giant triplets of racism, materialism and militarism are incapable of being conquered.

…

I am now convinced that the simplest approach will prove the most effective — the solution to poverty is to abolish it directly by a now widely discussed measure: the guaranteed income.

In 1965 Martin Luther King wrote a letter for Pageant magazine. The essay was titled “The Bravest Man I Ever Knew.” That man was Norman Thomas, the leader of the Socialist Party. In a preface he apologized for being unable to attend the 80th birthday of Thomas; King was on his way to Oslo to accept the Nobel Prize for Peace. Here are some excerpts from the essay:

I can think of no man who has done more than you to inspire the vision of a society free of injustice and exploitation. While some would adjust to the status quo, you urged struggle. While some would corrupt struggle with violence or undemocratic perversions, you have stood firmly for the integrity of ends and means. Your example has ennobled and dignified the fight for freedom, and all that we hear of the Great Society seems only an echo of your prophetic eloquence. Your pursuit of racial and economic democracy at home, and of sanity and peace in the world, has been awesome in scope. It is with deep admiration and indebtedness that I carry the inspiration of your life to Oslo.

…

During our historic March on Washington in the summer of 1963, when 250,000 Negro and white Americans joined together in an outpouring of fellowship and brotherly cooperation for a world of freedom and equality, a little Negro boy listened near the Washington Monument to an eloquent orator.

Turning to his father, he asked: “Who is that man?”

Came the inevitable answer: “That’s Norman Thomas. He was for us before any other white folks were.”

…

Yet America has never fully utilized Thomas’s great abilities. He has been a marvelous unofficial ambassador-at-large to our friends in Europe, Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Would it not make sense to make him our official representative to the United Nations?

In his column today Paul Krugman acknowledges Dr. King’s contribution to ending legal sanctions against blacks and other people of color, though racism still casts a pernicious pall over our society. As an economist, also a Nobel Prize winner, Krugman concludes that de jure racism has given way to economic classism. Krugman:

Economic inequality isn’t inherently a racial issue, and rising inequality would be disturbing even if there weren’t a racial dimension. But American society being what it is, there are racial implications to the way our incomes have been pulling apart. And in any case, King — who was campaigning for higher wages when he was assassinated — would surely have considered soaring inequality an evil to be opposed.

King spoke of the growing chasm between the Haves and the Have-nots. In one of his speeches (you can view it yourself by clicking on the last link) he said that America spends over $320,000 for “each enemy we kill in Vietnam,” whereas we spend only $53 for each poor person in the War on Poverty. Krugman:

Mitt Romney says that we should discuss income inequality, if at all, only in “quiet rooms.” There was a time when people said the same thing about racial inequality. Luckily, however, there were people like Martin Luther King who refused to stay quiet. And we should follow their example today. For the fact is that rising inequality threatens to make America a different and worse place — and we need to reverse that trend to preserve both our values and our dreams.

Dr. King “refused to stay quiet,” for which he paid the ultimate sacrifice. MLK, rest in peace.

Profligacy: a follow up

January 14, 2012 by davald

I said in my previous post that the peripheral nations of the Eurozone had been reducing their debt-to-GDP ratios before the Great Recession. Krugman provides a nice graph here about Italy and Spain. (Data in the chart below from the World Bank, via the St. Louis Fed, and Eurostat.)

Two years before things went sour in 2007, all of these countries were reducing their government debt. Since 2007, all of the above countries have increased their debt, especially Ireland. Its ratio jumped 160 percent from 2001 to 2010.

But look at Ireland. It was the model of fiscal behavior, steadily reducing its already low debt-to-GDP ratio. In 2007, its real estate bubble burst then panic set in. The government chose to make the country’s banks whole, while imposing severe contractions on the Irish people.

Despite the lack of economic growth Chancellor Merkel wants more austerity. Yet, look at Germany’s debt. A glass house, Ms. Merkel?
