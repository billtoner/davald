God’s traders

June 14, 2012 by davald

Economists will tell you that mutual exchanges between willing agents increases wealth. The inherent logic, they say, is both simple and obvious. Agent A has something that B wants, and vice versa. So A trades with B, making each better off. Writ large, that’s the market economy.

Let’s stick with the simple for a moment. We might imagine that I have skills that an employer needs. So the employer hires me. I get money while the employer benefits from my skills, or whatever. I’m better off and so is the employer.

I go to the grocery store. I need provisions. I pay the grocer for the items I’ve stuffed into my cart. The grocer is happy that I parted with some of my money, and I get a week’s worth of food and beverage. Our aggregate wealth has increased.

Firm A is good at making x and not so good at making y, while Firm B is good at making y but not so good at making x. One has a comparative advantage over the other. If each focuses on doing what it does better, then the collective wealth improves.

So far, so simple. But what about the financial sector, or Wall Street for short?

Over the last few decades the financial sector has seen its profits rise while the manufacturing sector’s profits have fallen.

What, exactly, does the financial sector do, other than deliver enormous paychecks to a relative few? Keeping with the simple, how do we explain the exchanges mentioned in the first couple of examples above in a Wall Street context? What or where do we find the comparative advantages?

Of course, Wall Street is anything but simple, as we’ve come to learn. The financial instruments developed by PhDs in mathematics and physics have become so complex that few understand them. But I should think that they have one thing in common: they are conditional algorithms. If this happens, do that. For instance, a change in temperature in the Midwest could trigger a buy or sell or a put or a call on agriculture futures. I suppose, really, that an action can be tied to any number of possible circumstances. Inherent in the conditionals is a presumed correlation between one or more events.

Recently JP Morgan lost billions of dollars in a complicated scheme (well, they’re all complicated). It appears that someone outside the firm was closely tracking a particular trade or set of trades by some JP Morgan employees. When JP Morgan turned left, others turned right. As it happens, turning left proved to be the wrong direction.

What’s really going on here and defines what Wall Street and other financial trading hubs is betting, plain and, I dare say, simple. I imagine that I’m a trader (a bettor) on Wall Street. I notice that a particular stock is lower than I think it should be. So I place a bet on the stock, believing that its price will rise. What if I’m wrong and the stock goes even lower? Well, I could hedge against this happening. I persuade another trader to provide me with insurance against a potentially bad bet. The other trader exacts a premium for the insurance. That other trader may himself seek a hedge. And so on? There’s nothing to prohibit a long series of hedges based on the initial trader’s bet. Regardless of which direction the stock price goes, there will be winners and losers, though the wins and losses will be mitigated by virtue of hedging.

But notice that there is no mention of an underlying asset of the particular stock, call it Stock A. We don’t know what the company associated with Stock A makes or provides. For the trader it doesn’t matter.

Here’s an example to illustrate the point: the price of housing. It was assumed as an article of faith by far too many that the price of houses only rises; it does not fall. The implicit assumption is that the population grows, people need a place to live, they buy houses. Home buyers also believe that they are making an investment, since the value of their real estate will increase over time. Since the demand for housing increases with population growth, or so it is assumed, then the price of houses will keep rising.

We can then say that the underlying asset in mortgages and the financial derivatives upon which they are, or were, based is the value of the home. However, as we have witnessed, housing prices do fall. The expression ‘house of cards’ seems particularly apt, since a significant chunk of the edifice of financial instruments was in the form of house prices, always expected to rise.

The late economist Hyman Minsky wrote about “speculative euphoria.” When things are going good, everyone wants to get into the act. Housing and its financial edifice attracted more and more traders, who bundled up the mortgages in increasingly complex instruments then sold them to other traders, who then bundled and sold to another set of traders. And so on, creating a burgeoning mushroom of presumed financial value. As more and more individuals put their money into these instruments, their prices, and therefore their value, rose, attracting even more investors.

Here’s the peculiar thing about these financial mushrooms. Their collective value is many times greater than the value of the underlying asset. How can this be? I suspect that the differential has everything to do with the notion of value.

What is the value of x? It is the price that one or more individuals is willing to pay for it. An ancient painting fetches several millions of dollars at auction. The value of something bears little resemblance to its costs, which is certainly true of rare art, say.

Assume that you are a banker. You’ve just lent me $100,000 to buy a house. You hold my mortgage—for a moment, anyway. You, the banker, are in business to make money. So you sell the mortgage to an investor, or one who represents a group of investors. The price the investor pays is greater than $100,000—or why else sell it? Now you’ve cleared your books of the accounts receivable, the mortgage, and pocketed some extra money, along with divesting risk.

In turn, the investor bundles the mortgage he’s just purchased with several other financial instruments. Each of these, including the mortgage, is assumed to have an underlying asset, though to the investors, it really doesn’t matter. The objective is to turn these instruments over, holding them only for a short while, then selling them to others for a profit. Each transaction drives up the value of the traded instruments, since each player in this series of transactions is charging the buyer more than the player paid for the instrument. When the notional value of each transaction is summed the result can be three to four times the total of financial assets.

We can begin to appreciate this mushrooming effect as a combination of “speculative euphoria” and the corresponding rising value (i.e., the price that individuals are willing to pay). That original house upon which I took out a mortgage for $100,000 is still the same house. But that mortgage generated many times the initial value of the mortgage.

Back to Wall Street traders. While it can be said that they are exchanging one thing for another (e.g., the notional value of a financial instrument for payment to the holder of the instrument), which could be construed as trading, Wall Street, as John Maynard Keynes described it, is one big casino where trillions of dollars are at stake at any given time.

For the Rest of Us, this means that we are at the mercy of betting. If enough “traders” guess right, we can breathe a sigh of relief. If too many guess wrong, shit happens—as in a Great Recession.

It hasn’t always been like this. The increasing volatility occasioned by financialization of the economy has placed all of us at risk.

But for all their shenanigans, Wall Street’s mucky-mucks believe that they’re doing “God’s work,” and that we simply don’t understand their enormous contribution to the Rest of Us. Appearing before the Senate Banking Committee yesterday JP Morgan’s Jaimie Diamond admitted that there were “negatives,” including, “you know, greed, arrogance, hubris, lack of attention to detail.” Yet, he keeps insisting that he and his brethren should not be constrained. The loss of billions should be treated as bygones, an aberration along an otherwise glorious path of wealth creation.

The editors of the New York Times chastised members of Congress for their abject failure to impose the regulations contained in the Dodd-Frank bill, in particular the Volcker Rule, which would restore a bit of Glass-Steagall. Don’t the editors know that when Diamond et al. address Congress they’re appearing before chattel? After all, this is the best Congress money can buy, and Wall Street is where the money is.

Sunset Falls [u][u2][u3]

June 13, 2012 by davald

[u3] I was trying to take pictures while listening to all the facts and figures. It seems that I got a few items wrong, as AGM Kim Moore discovered. In the interest of accuracy, here goes:

  • on fish handling 10,719 was the record one day haul of pink salmon (9/14/2009)
  • entrance to ladder rather than intake to ladder
  • transform Sunset Falls AREA into a better place than it is now [davald: the falls themselves will not be altered]
  • the trap and haul was built in 1958 by Department of Fisheries and not Puget Power and Light; the land was previous owned by Puget Power and Light and intended as a hydroelectric project
  • more than one home can see the intake [davald: a few will be able to glimpse a side view of the intake]

[u2]Video at the end.

[u]Correction below.

The Snohomish County PUD has a rather simple resource strategy: capture all cost-effective conservation and, if necessary to meet increasing load, develop renewable resources in our own backyard. This strategy allows us to comply with Initiative 937, reduce financial risk, add resources in small increments to match load growth, impose a smaller ecological footprint, and keep costs, and therefore rates, stable.

Over the last five years the PUD has broken its own historical records for annual conservation savings. Last year the utility saved 10.1 average megawatts.

On the resource front we’ve been exploring tidal power in Puget Sound, installing solar voltaic panels on rooftops, constructing new low-impact hydro projects (we’ve got mountains and rain in Snohomish County: a good combination for letting water and gravity generate electricity), and engaging in serious efforts to add a geothermal project to our portfolio.

As always, the bulk of our power—over 80 percent—comes from the Bonneville Power Administration. With the exception of the output from the state’s lone nuclear power plant (Columbia Generating Station), all of that electricity is generated by a series of hydroelectric projects along the Columbia and Snake rivers, including Grand Coulee.

We’ve begun exploring another hydro project, this one at Sunset Falls. It appears to be an ideal site. But by merely announcing our intentions to investigate we’ve encountered opposition from some of the local residents and various non-governmental groups. Unfortunately, much of their rhetoric is inaccurate, often wildly so.

This morning I joined my commission colleagues, the general manager, the clerk of the board, and the PUD’s assistant general manager in charge of generation in a visit of Sunset Falls and surrounding environs. We commissioners wanted to see for ourselves what the fuss might be about.

The falls are certainly spectacular. Here’s one shot I took from the shoreline opposite the falls.

Near the center of the photo is an existing intake structure and fish ladder. Puget Sound Power & Light The state constructed both about 50 years ago. The investor-owned utility, now known as Puget Sound Energy, did not build a hydroelectric facility. The Department of Fisheries uses the structure to capture adult salmon swimming upstream. The fish cannot negotiate the falls; they must be trucked up stream, where they spawn. The juveniles then make their way downstream and over the falls then eventually out to the Pacific Ocean.

Here is a closeup of the capture tank. Fisheries workers use nets to pull the fish out of the tank then place them onto a single, rather ancient truck. I was told that as many as a thousand adult salmon a day may be transported. Should we proceed with the project, we’ll greatly improve this structure to enhance both the aesthetics of the site and the capturing and transporting of the salmon.

Some of the opponents of the project, whose details are far from being finalized, imagine that the utility will construct a giant concrete dam to impound the water for hydrogenation. Hardly. The plans thus far involve constructing an inflatable weir across the stream, reducing flows by approximately 2,500 cubic feet per second. (Flows can exceed 80,000 during extreme flooding. This morning, I was told, the river was flowing at 6,500 cfs.)

Here’s a slide from an October 2011 presentation to the board. It’s a plan view of preliminary designs for the intake. The inflatable weir is the balloon-like object near the middle; the intake is at the bottom left.

I’ve been informed that only one local resident would be able to see the intake structure and the weir, which would be inflated during low-water periods. During the winter when the PUD would need the electricity, the weir would likely be deflated, since the water flows will be at their highest. (The weir would inflate and deflate automatically according to river flows. It will be monitored remotely in real time and can be operated via computer if necessary.)

Here’s a preliminary layout of the proposed project.

You’ll notice that the water flows around a peninsula. At the left-most point of that peninsula the PUD would construct the inflatable weir that would connect with the side opposite, where the intake structure would be located. The diverted water would flow downhill through a constructed tunnel under ground to the site of the current fish trap structure. There the PUD would build a powerhouse that contains the turbines for generating electricity.

Here’s a picture of the intake area from a location just to the right and near the same shoreline.

To the left of the picture is the tip of the peninsula. The water flows around the peninsula from the picture’s left to right, then toward the camera. It continues to Canyon Falls, which is above Sunset Falls.

As I said at the outset, the falls are magnificent. The PUD’s proposed project will have no effect on the appearance or operation of the falls, since the utility would divert only a fraction of the river flow above. No resident, with the possible exception of one, would see any difference after the project’s completion. That one resident, as I mentioned above, would have a view of the intake structure.

Construction, itself, is a different story, of course. Building things makes noise. There will be trucks and crews on site for a number of months.

But once the project is finished, the entire site will be visually improved. The power house, for example, will be built so as to resemble the existing rock formation the power house will replace. The Tulalip Tribes and government fishery agencies support the PUD’s plan to greatly improve the trap-and-haul component of the proposed project.

Nevertheless, the PUD, as always, will be especially sensitive to the concerns of those who now oppose the project. We’re still in the preliminary phases, gathering and studying data, meeting with local residents and other stakeholders. We have a well-deserved reputation for negotiating in good faith, accommodating the wishes of others. I can make this guarantee: should the project proceed, the PUD will transform Sunset Falls into a better place than it is now.

  • new MacBook Airs, but without Retina displays—same price, more features and power, available now

Meanwhile, most of the growth in European sales among S&P’s 500 is attributable to Apple. What recession?

A final comment on the allegedly brilliant Wall Street traders. They never have or ever will understand Apple. The stock fell 1.2 percent after this morning’s event concluded. Apple keeps blowing out estimates with each quarterly earnings report, yet the experts undervalue the stock. How can a company so large (the largest in market value) grow so much?

Perhaps there’s something about the company’s attitude. Here’s MacWorld quoting Apple CEO Tim Cook at the close of this morning’s keynote:

“Only Apple could make such amazing hardware, software, and services. We are so proud of these products, as they are perfect examples of what Apple does best. To make a difference in the lives of so many people around the world. The products we make, combined with the apps that you create, can fundamentally change the world. About the “Rest of Us”

June 11, 2012 by davald

How presumptuous of me to speak of the Rest of Us, as if it’s possible to lump the 99-percent into one, homogeneous category. The only commonality is that we who occupy this category make less than a quarter-million dollars a year. Otherwise, we’re very different from one another in nearly every respect.

Clearly Republicans belong to this group, as do Democrats, and Independents, and socialists, communists, libertarians, and no doubt adherents to a dozen more political ideologies. There are lawyers and doctors and teachers and firemen and accountants and professional athletes and real estate agents and columnists. Indeed, we should include every American in the Rest of Us.

With the exception of the One-percent. Why the exclusion? Increasingly, the top one percent of Americans in terms of income and wealth disproportionately control or influence circumstances that affect the Rest of Us. In our country, especially, money matters.

So, when I use the term ‘the Rest of Us’ I do so only to suggest, aver, or argue that when it comes to political and economic issues we are in a struggle with the very rich, whether or not we acknowledge it; and, I dare say, most would not, which is a big part of the problem. To the extent that we can set aside all that divides us and begin to appreciate the one thing we do have in common, namely, that we are not One-percenters, the more we can effect outcomes to our benefit, achieving, perhaps, a kinder and gentler nation.

OKC vs. Miami

June 11, 2012 by davald

I suppose that I shouldn’t care who wins the NBA championship, with the series beginning tomorrow night in Oklahoma City. After all, sports should be taking a back seat to all the misery I write about. But I’ll be watching, anyway.

Dave Zirin, writing for The Nation magazine, argues that I should be rooting against the Thunder and, thus, for the Heat. Why? Because the OKC owners blatantly stole the Sonics from Seattle, while telling all the world in official terms that they were doing everything to keep the team in the Emerald City. They must be punished. Zirin quotes OKC co-owner Aubrey McClendon:

We didn’t buy the team to keep it in Seattle, we hoped to come here…. We started to look around and at that time the Sonics were going through some ownership challenges in Seattle. So Clay, very artfully and skillfully, put himself in the middle of those discussions and to the great amazement and surprise to everyone in Seattle, some rednecks from Oklahoma, which we’ve been called, made off with the team.

Notwithstanding Zirin’s advice, I nevertheless find myself pulling for the team formerly known as the Sonics. First of all, I greatly admire the two remaining Seattleites: Kevin Durant and Nick Collison. Both are quiet professionals who work their butts off each game to secure victory. You won’t find their names associated with this or that scandal. Indeed, they seem to genuinely practice “NBA cares,” wherein basketball players “give back to the community.” Yes, Collison didn’t immediately endear himself to Oklahoma City fans when, after the move was announced, he mentioned matter-of-factly that he was going to miss Seattle and the Pacific Northwest, where his laid-back, no drama style meshed easily with the trees and the rain and the waters of Puget Sound. I’ve never been to Oklahoma, but I suspect that it would induce climate shock to a Seattle transplant.

I also suspect that if Seattle is to secure another franchise it will likely have to do it the OKC way: purloin an existing team. The ongoing favorite target is Sacramento’s Kings, who originated in Rochester, New York, almost 90 years ago. If I condemn McClendon and co-owner Clay Bennett for “stealing” the Sonics, I should not countenance the acquisition and removal of the Kings. Besides, NBA teams have a habit of relocating over the years. The Lakers used to be in Minneapolis. The Warriors have been in Philadelphia and San Francisco then Oakland, but soon returning to Baghdad by the Bay. The St. Louis Hawks moved to Atlanta. The Grizzlies started in Vancouver, B.C. The aforementioned Kings began as the Rochester Royals, before relocating to Cincinnati then to Kansas City, where they took the name “the Kings.”

As for the Heat, I have nothing against them. I’ve always liked Pat Riley from his playing days, then as coach of the Lakers and their “showtime” games, featuring Magic Johnson and Kareem Abdul-Jabbar. Lebron James is clearly in a class by himself, combining power, speed, agility, and field-goal accuracy. We can add intensity and tenacity, as evidenced in the just-concluded war against the proud by aged Celtics. The knock on the Heat is that they have always been expected to win, given their fearsome threesome, in James, Wade, and Bosh.

I look forward to lots of excitement and remarkable displays of athleticism. The money is probably on the Thunder. But championship series are full of surprises. Just ask the Heat, who were defeated by the Mavericks to win last year’s finals.

Besides, we should all take a break from the doldrums. It’s good for the soul.

Meritocracy and government

June 7, 2012 by davald

Michels’s grim conclusion was that it was impossible for any party, no matter its belief system, to bring about democracy in practice. Oligarchy was inevitable. For any kind of institution with a democratic base to consolidate the legitimacy it needs to exist, it must have an organization that delegates tasks. The rank and file will not have the time, energy, wherewithal or inclination to participate in the many, often minute decisions necessary to keep the institution functioning. In fact, effectiveness, Michels argues convincingly, requires that these tasks be delegated to a small group of people with enough power to make decisions of consequence for the entire membership. Over time, this bureaucracy becomes a kind of permanent, full-time cadre of leadership. “Without wishing it,” Michels says, there grows up a great “gulf which divides the leaders from the masses.” The leaders now control the tools with which to manipulate the opinion of the masses and subvert the organization’s democratic process. “Thus the leaders, who were at first no more than the executive organs of the collective, will soon emancipate themselves from the mass and become independent of its control.”

— excerpted in The Nation

Hayes is talking about Robert Michels, a German social theorist, who, born of wealth, nevertheless embarked on a political journey, moving further and further left. In each case he found that organization leads ineluctably to oligarchy, rule by a few.

In our own political systems, more properly described as representative government, we ostensibly select those we prefer to govern us. Hayes goes on to say about our current Congress, especially, that we are increasingly represented by elites beholden to monied interests, from which many, if not most, of them spring.

Hayes begins this lengthy excerpt chronicling his own experience as a young boy scoring well enough on a three-hour exam to gain entrance to Manhattan’s most elite educational institution, Hunter College High School, free to those few who attend. The only criterion for admittance is the score on a once-administered three-hour exam. As pure a meritocracy as we have in America. Or, at least it was. Over time, Hayes reports, New York City’s wealthy denizens invested in private tutors and test-taking classes for their young children. And the preparation and investments worked, so that the demographics of the school changed over time, becoming whiter and richer.

Meritocracy, Hayes suggests from his reading of Michels, requires adherence to two principles: difference and mobility. The first assumes that we have vastly different capabilities, which should be not only acknowledged but embraced. The second principle, mobility, assumes that, though we are different, we should all have equal opportunity to compete for the more attractive positions.

I’ll step in here with some comments. The above sounds an awful like conventional conservative thinking: the role of government is to ensure equal opportunity, but also to recognize that the better should prosper and the worse should suffer. After all, that’s what markets are all about. Scarce goods are distributed on the basis of merit rather than, as liberals would argue, to ensure fairness. Hayes has his own take:

But this ideal, appealing as it may be, runs up against the reality of what I’ll call the Iron Law of Meritocracy. The Iron Law of Meritocracy states that eventually the inequality produced by a meritocratic system will grow large enough to subvert the mechanisms of mobility. Unequal outcomes make equal opportunity impossible. The Principle of Difference will come to overwhelm the Principle of Mobility. Those who are able to climb up the ladder will find ways to pull it up after them, or to selectively lower it down to allow their friends, allies and kin to scramble up. In other words: “Who says meritocracy says oligarchy.”

America, for better or worse, and I believe strongly that it’s the latter, is enthrall to “The Iron Law of Meritocracy.” Born rich, you’re likely to stay that way. On the other end of the scale, you should have picked more successful parents.

In such a pseudo-merit system, those who are first when the metaphorical race begins are far more likely to finish first. They will have taken every advantage to ensure that once rich they will leave the world rich. Hayes:

But the Iron Law of Meritocracy makes a different prediction: that societies ordered around the meritocratic ideal will produce inequality without the attendant mobility. Indeed, over time, a society will become more unequal and less mobile as those who ascend its heights create means of preserving and defending their privilege and find ways to pass it on across generations. And this, as it turns out, is a pretty spot-on description of the trajectory of the American economy since the mid-1970s.

Notice the dates. They are too familiar to readers of this blog. They signal the start of the Great Divergence, a term that is both apt and increasingly popular. It coincides most sharply with Ronald Reagan’s occupation of the Oval Office.

While I doubt that he was the architect of the Great Divergence he certainly allowed it to happen, giving it a big nudge, certainly, with the wholesale firing of the air-traffic controllers. He also spoke the language of “meritocracy,” singing the praises of the private sector and its inbred social Darwinism while defaming government as “the problem” rather than the solution.

It has become all too fashionable to denigrate government as did Reagan. Conservatives would go further than crusade against it; they spend their waking hours conjuring up new ways to give effect to their attitude. Government, they say, must be put on a diet. It must be denied revenues, because its expenditures frustrate the “Iron Law of Meritocracy,” nurturing the undeserved at the expense of the privileged.

I hope that you see what’s at stake here, especially in light to what’s been happening in conservative-controlled states like Wisconsin and Ohio, where public employees are under siege. Government, for all its ills, is about the only mechanism available to rein in the extremes, rendering both the rich and poor less so.

Okay, this sounds like socialism! Put down your swords for a moment, wipe off the spittle, and allow yourself to be mentally transported to northern Europe. If you don’t like government doing things on behalf of the Rest of Us, you’ll hate Finland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, et al. Their governments are huge in comparison to the U.S.’s And what do their citizens get in return? Free education. Free health care. Scant poverty. Far less inequality. Higher living standards.

Well, yes, education and health care are not exactly “free.” The citizens of these lands pay higher taxes for these benefits, in most cases willingly if not enthusiastically.

But the Nordic people largely embrace the exchange: higher taxes and larger government for those things we Americans increasingly expect the private sector to provide, in accordance with the “Iron Law of Meritocracy.”

Mark my words. The more we support, either by action or inaction, the shrinking of government, the more the Rest of Us will suffer and the more wealth the already wealthy will siphon off from a protracted, sputtering economy.

Having issued this caveat, I quickly offer another. There are many obvious reasons why we should distrust our government, though not necessarily the concept of government. Congress is the home of “institutional corruption.” And as Hayes points out, regulatory capture is rife in D.C., with federal agencies serving as the source of future employees for the firms they are supposed to regulate.

I’ve got to believe that the Founding Fathers, Abe Lincoln, and certainly Teddy Roosevelt would be aghast at what has happened to the federal government, especially the deluge of billionaire dollars ensuring that the Rest of Us get screwed as the “malefactors of great wealth” extract greater shares of GDP. They would be appalled by Citizens United, which brought unbridled bribery into the open and onto the pages of the U.S. Code.

But in particular for those who cobbled together the Constitution, they would be horrified by how the very concept of democracy as they conceived it has been greatly sullied beyond their recognition. Of course, they wanted no part of monarchy, and none of the associated pomp and circumstance. And they also preferred a limited, less intrusive government than the one they had endured under King George. And they also believed in the entrepreneurial spirit, about fashioning a respectable life through dint of labor and discipline.

However, the virtues of self-reliance and self-actualization cannot be practiced today by the Rest of Us. Those attitudes are reserved for the extremely privileged, for the most part, and unavailable to the masses. We’ve also lost our sense of innovation and imagination, if we take economist Tyler Cowen’s word. With the Great Divergence we may have entered a protracted state of stagnation.

You see, handing everything over to the plutocracy has been bad for business. The plutocrats know no limits. If left to their own appetites and devices, they will cause the whole damn thing to come crashing down on the Rest of Us. After all, they came awfully close to doing that just a few short years ago. They must be constrained for their—and our—own good.

Who regulates, or at least is supposed to? Government, preferably one whose agents are themselves virtuous.

Who can lift the poor? Government.

Who can restrain the malefactors? Government.

Who can provide education, health care, and guaranteed retirement income? Government.

Who can enable scientific research? Government.

But that kind of government, now just a mental figment, can be realized only by a citizenry committed to those achievements. Yet, it’s proved to be far too easy to manufacture the consent of the governed. Actually, we’re pretty damn cheap.

Why do we collectively put up with this? Is the challenge too large? Have we already lost? Did we ever struggle in service to the Rest of Us?

I guess that’s why some people feel the need to occupy something. Doing something—anything—is better than nothing. Lean out your window and scream that you’ve had enough.

We’ve recently witnessed real revolutions. After suffering a long train of autocratic abuse, citizens of the Middle East erupted, literally taking to the streets then occupying them. Something or perhaps several things sparked the collective outrage.

What might be the spark that sets off the Rest of Us? How low do we have to go before we scream “enough”?

Or, horror of horror, perhaps most Americans are quite comfortable with the growing inequality, the decline in public education, the erosion of our public infrastructure. Perhaps they have other things to do than to arrest these developments. Maybe, just maybe, they like not only oligarchy but the plutocrats who run it.

Spark. Where are you?

Share the pain

June 7, 2012 by davald

The Wisconsin vote to retain Governor Walker bodes ill for public sector workers in that state and elsewhere. In particular, workers’ benefits are in jeopardy. We may wonder why voters support this assault. The Seattle Times‘ editors may provide a clue:

WISCONSIN matters. Tuesday’s vote in that state to support its Republican governor, Scott Walker, was a vote against the public-employee unions and their power to insulate themselves from the effects of the Great Recession.

Note that last phrase: “their power to insulate themselves from the effects of the Great Recession.” People are suffering. So should public employees. Walker, the editors implied, did us a favor by denuding public-sector unions, and Wisconsin voters said “yeah.” That’s the clear message the editors wish to deliver, and they warn elected officials in Washington state to ignore that message at their political peril.

Note also that the editors summarily dismissed the real power, the immense financial contributions from billionaires outside the state. Walker outspent his opponent 7-1, if not more. Such influence, the editors imply, can be ignored. Rather, the real problem before us are those public employees and their “exorbitant” benefits, in the words of New York Times columnist David Brooks.

Should we speak of the unequal influence of big money we are charged with engaging in “class warfare.” Well, there is a war going on, and the Rest of Us are not winning.

Yet, insofar as we wish to punish public employees, we turn a blind eye to the far greater problem of corporate dominance of the political process. The very rich have accumulated vast fortunes, far more than they could ever need. Their spending hundreds of thousands of dollars on multiple political campaigns is not only a pittance against their bank accounts. They are making investments that, history proves, pay off handsomely.

I also fault the Times‘ editors for advocating squeezed state budgets.

The voters of a Democratic-leaning state have backed a program of dramatic economy in state government.

The editors cannot have it both ways, desiring a robust recovery while pushing for “dramatic economy.” The latter denies the former.

GDP and further musings

June 6, 2012 by davald

While the U.S. economy grew in both 2010 and 2011, the rate was small, at three and one percent, respectively.

We’ll note that the trend is in the wrong direction, at least for the Rest of Us. Yet, as the economic engine appears to be losing its steam, the incomes of the very rich continue to rise. They have figured out a way to extract a greater share of wealth even as the wealth of the nation is growing at a slower rate, especially since the mid-80s.

Statistics, of course, can be misleading. While over eight percent of us are officially unemployed, that means that over 90 percent of us still have jobs. Surveys, like a recent one in Wisconsin, suggest that a large majority of Americans are either better off now or have stayed the same as compared with a few years ago. And, let’s face it, one can go about their lives and never encounter the downtrodden. Moreover, if you’re a conservative, you can take comfort in the belief that one’s station is all about individual effort. So, regardless of which end of the economic scale one sits, you deserve to be there.

I’ll go out on a sociological limb here. I think it’s much easier to be a conservative than a liberal. You don’t have to think as much. Indeed, you may not have to think at all. You don’t have to care about anyone else, save perhaps for your immediate family. You’re damn sure about your worldview, and could not be bothered by pesky facts. There’s right and there’s wrong, with no shades of moral gray. Chances are you’re white and heterosexual. You delight in pledging allegiance to the flag and the bible. America is always number one; and if we should kill a few thousand non-Americans, it’s their own damn fault for getting in our way.

Shit. Sign me up. I need a rest.

Scott Walker and money

June 6, 2012 by davald

Yesterday the people of Wisconsin voted. By a seven-point margin they chose to retain governor Scott Walker. I should think that Walker believes he now has a strong mandate for what he has done so far and what he will likely do henceforth.

In skimming the accounts of yesterday’s election results, I was struck by a couple of things. First, a map showing the counties of Wisconsin. Red-filled counties represented a pro-Walker vote; blue depicted the anti-Walker vote. Nearly the entire state was covered in red. Only the major urban areas were blue.

The second item was the money. Walker outspent his opponent more than seven to one. When the Super Pac dollars are finally counted, the margin will surely rise. We can thank the Supreme Court for the latter.

The New York Times provides a comprehensive breakdown of the Wisconsin vote. A few takeaways:

  • There were more Republicans than Democrats who voted, with Independents not that far behind the Dems.
  • Only a third of the voters lived in households in which at least one member belonged to a labor union.
  • Ninety-three percent of those supporting the Tea Party voted to retain Walker.
  • A majority of those with college degrees voted against retaining Walker.
  • The suburbs and rural areas voted overwhelmingly in favor of Walker.

Now, the results may have been more than about money and declining union participation rates. Many Wisconsinites, it seems, objected to the recall as a matter of principle, believing that such an extraordinary measure should be reserved for more serious offenses than breaking public sector unions; and since two-thirds of the voters had no connection to labor unions, this rationale appears plausible.

I have no idea why urbanites tend to be liberal Democrats rather than conservative Republicans. But if the former expect to have their way in the future, the cities must grow and the suburbs contracted. Or is there no causal connection between one’s political views and where one lives? On that note, do cities make people liberal or do liberals prefer urban centers?

Which leads me back to what I call ‘the politics of dirt.’ This is no way to govern ourselves, folks. If you happen to be a rural liberal, you’re shit out of luck when it comes to being represented in state and local governments. Same for a conservative urbanite. How much better this would work—self-government, that is—if we could vote for ideas rather than on the basis of geography.

Remember that Gore won the popular vote. That he didn’t gain the White House is simply bizarre and, more to the point, reflects a flawed system in which a minority of the people prevailed.

At any rate, the Wisconsin vote disturbs me. It may be a harbinger of more bad things for the Rest of Us. It certainly proves that billionaires matter.
