When you don’t have Siri

June 21, 2012 by davald

I’ve still got the iPhone 4—no S. A couple of weeks ago I was doing my usual walk, listening to music via my iPhone using Sennheiser headphones with buttons and a remote microphone. I occasionally take phone calls on my walks, and everything works splendidly. The phone rang (I’ve got a Glenn Gould ringtone), it was someone wanting to deliver some furniture to my son, who happens to live in the same building as I do. The store had my phone number rather than my son’s. So after I hung up, I pushed the pause button on my headset, holding it until I heard a beep or two. I then said, “Call [my son].” The voice answered, “Mobile or work?” I said, “Work,” and I was immediately connected.

So this morning I received a phone call. It was the PUD’s general manager reporting on an issue. After we’d finished, I said that I’d see him next Monday, as I recall my calendar having Monday, rather than the usual Tuesday, as the day of the next board meeting. I was mistaken.

After we’d completed the conversation, I held down the pause button again to get the beeps. I then said, “When is my next board meeting?” I thought that if it could place a phone call it might be able to answer the question. I was wrong.

The voice came back, “Playing Misuko Uchida.”

Oh, well. I’ll have to wait for the next iPhone this fall.

Obscure but amazing

June 21, 2012 by davald

My son sent me an email recently about a couple of items he saw on television. One was on the Cincinnati Reds’ Joey Votto; the other on the NY Mets’ R.A. Dickey. I’ll do them in order.

Votto is the 28-year-old All Star first baseman for the Reds. He’s been in the majors since 2007. His lifetime batting average is .318. Only once, during his sophomore year, did he fail to reach .300; he hit only .297. So, here’s the amazing, but obscure, fact about Votto. He’s hit only one foul ball into the right or left field stands—in his entire career! What does that mean?

Votto strives to hit every pitch back through the middle. He doesn’t try to pull or go inside-out. Rather, he meets the ball with his bat nearly perpendicular to the pitch’s flight path. If he gets just a piece of the ball, chances are, overwhelmingly so, that the ball will go either straight down in front of him or to or over the backstop. His style of hitting, which I would heartedly recommend to any young ballplayer, is the polar opposite of, say, Ichiro Suzuki, the master of fending off one pitch or another by slapping it foul and into the stands—or the dugout, or the on-deck circle, or any other place but fair.

My wish for all the Mariners batters: emulate Votto.

Now on to R.A. Dickey. We used to have him, you know, here in Seattle. Now he toils for the team that drafted me. He became the first Mets pitcher to record two consecutive one-hit shutouts, barely missing a no-hitter in the first game, when third baseman David Wright could have been charged an error, although a very tough one, to be sure. Dickey, who is 37, throws a knuckleball, almost exclusively. And what a knuckler it is. The amazing thing about his is that it may rotate only once after it leaves his hand and crosses the plate. My son saw a video demonstrating this fact.

As a former pitcher I can attest to this pitch, since all of us throwers, at one time or another, experimented with the knuckle ball. Like any good fisher person talking about the one that got away, mine was spectacular. Ask any poor schmuck who had occasion to catch me in the bullpen; one did so without his protective cup, and he was sorry that he’d forgotten it that day.

The amazing thing about my knuckleball is that the only time I used it in a game was in an exhibition between the Cal Bears and the Oakland A’s in the Coliseum before a massive crowd of 2,000, or just under the A’s average attendance in most years.

I was scheduled to start the next day against Santa Clara, always a good baseball team, so I would pitch just one half-inning. Now most players, I should think, would view this as a lifetime opportunity, going against a major league team, the same team that in a few short seasons would record three consecutive World Series championships. But I was a head case who always struggled to be in the here and now while on the mound. My mind was elsewhere. And when I was physically elsewhere, my mind was back on the mound. Go figure.

During my warmup, I tried the knuckleball. It was working splendidly, dancing like a small school of fish. It was my turn to take the hill to face Rick Monday, Tom Reynolds, and, I think, Joe Rudi. Monday was the first player ever drafted, going number one in the 1965 debut draft. I was back aways—closer to 700, but I forget.

I started Monday out with my knuckleball. It was knuckling, all right. But no where near the plate. I could tell that Monday was miffed. The A’s on their day off would prefer to be anywhere but playing a bunch of college kids. Each Oakland batter came to the plate ready to tear the cover off the ball. Monday wanted nothing more than to have me throw the ball down the middle. After throwing three consecutive balls, all knucklers, I delivered a straight one down the middle. Every Oakland hitter had the green light that evening. So Monday swung mightily, connecting on the sweet spot. His one-hop line drive nearly drove our diminutive second baseman into the right field wall. But he caught it, stumbled a bit, then threw to first in plenty of time. One out.

I dispensed with the knuckleball for the next two hitters. I retired both on popups, as I recall. A curve ball got Reynolds. Don’t know what pitch did it with Rudi, or whoever it was.

Now, back to misery.

Inflation and unemployment

June 20, 2012 by davald

The Federal Reserve acted with a big shrug today, sticking to low-inflation targets while ignoring the unemployed. Remember, it has a dual mandate: full employment and low inflation. However, the mandate may be difficult to achieve.

When the economy is humming along (in my lifetime I’ve seen it do this, but not so much of late), the unemployment rate typically falls. This is generally a good thing. But with economic growth often comes inflation.

So, let’s look at a chart showing inflation and unemployment rates.

During the relative halcyon days of the first post-war generation the unemployment rate generally stayed below six percent, even dropping below four percent a few years. In the 70s, however, both unemployment and inflation rates rose, giving us “stagflation.” After Treasury Secretary Paul Volcker tightened the monetary screws, inflation fell dramatically during Reagan’s terms, although unemployment remained stubbornly high. The economy did relatively well under Clinton, when both unemployment and inflation were low. Since the Great Recession we’ve had high unemployment, but very low inflation, even negative in 2008.

Now let’s look at inflation and GDP.

Here the correlation is much stronger. When the economy is growing, as it was during the 50s through 70s, inflation also rose. Since Reagan left office, both inflation and economic growth have declined.*

In one of his posts today Matthew Yglesias wonders about posing a hypothetical question to Ben Bernanke. Suppose, Yglesias imagines, that the inflation and unemployment numbers were reversed. That is, the unemployment rate was less than two percent and inflation was at 8.2. What would Bernanke do? Here’s Yglesias:

The central bank clarified that it is fanatically committed to holding inflation close-to-but-below two percent, and while it’s happy to speak many words about output and employment it basically doesn’t care. Bernanke had various remarks to make about housing policy and mortgage finance and I agree with some of those sentiments. But recall that if we had massively expansionary fiscal policy and cleaned up the mortgage situation and suddenly started adding 400,000-500,000 jobs a month that prices would go up. With 8 percent inflation and 2 percent unemployment, you can’t curb inflation unless you’re willing to tolerate some joblessless. With 2 percent inflation and 8 percent unemployment, you can’t curb joblessness unless you’re willing to tolerate some inflation. If millions of people get jobs, get their own places to live, start commuting, etc., the prices of some stuff will go up. If you want to keep prices low, you have to condemn us to years more of mass unemployment.

Actually, aside from the weak correlation between inflation and unemployment, as the first graph above suggests, I doubt that there’s much the Fed can do to directly boost employment. It may even be unable to affect it indirectly. The economy is barely limping because demand is too low, as I’ve mentioned here many times. That’s not my opinion. It is the considered judgment of Mssrs. Krugman, Baker, DeLong, et al.—along with John Maynard Keynes.

To his credit, Bernanke did urge Congress to engage in fiscal strategies that don’t frustrate economic recovery. Fat chance of that happening.

Those who hold lots of cash and financial instruments abhor inflation, since rising prices erode their accumulated wealth. (To be sure, those living on fixed incomes, like pensioners, also benefit from low inflation. But they are not the real winners in a low-inflation economy.) Now it’s increasingly clear that the slowdown in economic output has not inhibited the rich getting more so. As the economy stagnates, the One-percenters have figured out ways to maintain their nearly exclusive control of the nation’s wealth. But the sputtering economy is bad for the Rest of Us. Our wages have fallen. Our net wealth has declined. We’re losing jobs.

Austerity’s victors

June 20, 2012 by davald

While the U.S. unemployment rate remains stubbornly high by historical standards, it’s not nearly as high as the rates in Spain, Greece, Italy, and the other peripheral countries of the Eurozone. As national governments receive fewer revenues occasioned by the economic downturn, and as the number of citizens needing public assistance rises, something’s got to give. The increasing anxiety and desperation of suffering people manifests themselves in political polarization and, worse, a turn to radicalism.

In Greece, which is at the bottom of the Euro pit, another round of elections resulted in a center-right coalition claiming victory, however narrow. Its leaders are willing to let its citizens take it in the shorts to comply with German austerity conditions, meaning lower wages, fewer benefits, and slashed government payrolls.

But a very far right political party, Golden Dawn, gained 18 seats in the new parliament, after receiving a scant 0.23 percent of the vote in 2009, insufficient to garner representation. The party’s campaign theme: “Let’s rid this country of the stench.” It proposes to deport one million immigrants, bar the immigration of Muslims, and bury land mines along the Turkish border to deter would-be immigrants. It would also halt the austerity measures. For The Socialist Worker, Shaun Harkin writes:

That Golden Dawn was able to maintain its newfound support is an indication of the depth of political polarization in Greece: the crisis is so acute and the situation so volatile and uncertain that this scourge of humanity will continue to pose a serious threat as long as the situation drags on.

Golden Dawn, of course, has been emboldened, viewing its vote as an endorsement of its message and violent attacks on immigrants. Taking advantage of the new media attention, party leader Nikolaos Michaloliakos boldly took the opportunity to spew a denial of the Holocaust in May, claiming, “There were no ovens–this is a lie…There were no gas chambers either.”

During the twilight of the Weimar Republic in Germany, similar austerity measures were underway. They were the price the country was forced to pay by England, America, et al. for “starting” the first world war. (The causes were a bit more complicated than that, of course.)

In his book The Economic Consequences of the Peace, written in 1919, John Maynard Keynes issued a sharp warning to the victors:

Moved by insane delusion and reckless self-regard, the German people overturned the foundations on which we all lived and built. But the spokesmen of the French and British peoples have run the risk of completing the ruin, which Germany began, by a Peace which, if it is carried into effect, must impair yet further, when it might have restored, the delicate, complicated organization, already shaken and broken by war, through which alone the European peoples can employ themselves and live.

His fair warning went unheeded, nor has his ghost fared particularly well in current circumstances. Keynes provides insights into the conditions that led to war and why Germany could not withstand the harsh reparations. Here’s a particular interesting observation that resonates today following the Great Recession:

Europe was so organized socially and economically as to secure the maximum accumulation of capital. While there was some continuous improvement in the daily conditions of life of the mass of the population, Society was so framed as to throw a great part of the increased income into the control of the class least likely to consume it. The new rich of the nineteenth century were not brought up to large expenditures, and preferred the power which investment gave them to the pleasures of immediate consumption. In fact, it was precisely the inequality of the distribution of wealth which made possible those vast accumulations of fixed wealth and of capital improvements which distinguished that age from all others [original emphasis].

Sound familiar? We’re repeating history’s mistakes with predictable consequences. Keynes cites one particular provision of the Reparations Commission that contributed to Germany’s post-war economic deterioration:

There remains a third provision more sweeping than either of the above, neither of which affects German interests in neutral countries. The Reparation Commission is empowered up to May 1, 1921, to demand payment up to $5,000,000,000 in such manner as they may fix, “whether in gold, commodities, ships, securities or otherwise.” This provision has the effect of intrusting to the Reparation Commission for the period in question dictatorial powers over all German property of every description whatever.

The onerous measures imposed on Germany radicalized the German electorate. Economist Christian Stögbauer of the University of Munich published a paper (pdf) wherein he associates the push to polar opposites with the crisis of the Great Depression exacerbated by the penalties imposed by the Reparations Commission. He writes:

The most obvious evidence for the economic crisis in Germany was the increase of unemployment from about 1.1 million in May 1928 to about 6.1 million in February 1932. Unemployment decreased only slightly in 1932 and stayed at a very high level. In January 1933 the number of people on the dole again passed the 6 million threshhold (Institut für Konjunkturforschung, 1933, p. 15). Over the same period, the percentage of unemployed people receiving insurance benefits fell from 83.2 % to 37.8 % (Adamy and Steffen, 1982). The unemployed (and their families), whose unemployment benefits had expired, had no choice other than to rely on public relief, which provided a bare minimum level of subsistence. Coincidentally with the increase in unemployment, wages were drastically cut back and partial lay-offs also increased dramatically. As a result, weekly nominal per capita income diminished from about 31 Reichsmarks in 1928 to about 25 Reichsmarks in 1933. However, because of unmistakable deflation, real income increased by about 5 %. The agricultural sector, the single largest employer with 30.5 % of the labor force, faced drastically declining prices, especially for dairy products and livestock whose price decreased by over 50 % from 1928 to 1933. The result was a debt crisis and numerous foreclosures (Brustein, 1996, p. 66). Although unemployment figures were much lower in agriculture – especially in regions with small farms – hidden unemployment was substantial. Regional variations in the extent of indebtedness were also very high, partly because large grain farms with high wages bills and social security obligations were concentrated in the East.

He includes this chart (NSDAP stands for National Socialist German Workers Party, or Nazis):

We’ll note that as incomes fell and unemployment rates rose, the fortunes of Hitler advanced. Here’s a chart showing the electoral results of the latter years of the Weimar Republic:

Until the Sept. 1930 elections, the Nazis were a no-show. But look at how quickly their power rose in subsequent votes. Although the communist party’s support also increased, it pales in comparison. (I have not included all of the parties, of which there were as many as 40 over the Weimar period.)

Hitler also had a thing about “stench.” In his case he was referring to Jews (and communists and gypsies). We know about his “final solution.”

Although the Socialist Party prevailed in the recent French elections, the fascist National Front garnered nearly 18 percent of the popular vote. In Hungary, a very far right party, Fidesz, secured two-thirds of that country’s parliamentary seats. The New York Times:

Democracy here is dying not with a single giant blow but with many small cuts, critics say, through the legal processes of Parliament that add up to a slow-motion coup. And in its drift toward authoritarian government, aided by popular disaffection with political gridlock and a public focused mainly on economic hardship, Hungary stands as a potentially troubling bellwether for other, struggling Eastern European countries with weak traditions of democratic government.

If you’re not getting a bit upset by this stuff so far, think of what could happen should the Euro collapse and the zone disintegrate into its status quo ante. The problems of Greece could easily spread to all of Europe, save, perhaps, Germany and the Nordic countries. That means widespread high unemployment, declining wages, diminished benefits, and sharply reduced public services. Could long-standing animosities resurface with horrific results?

Europe had always been a continent at war with itself. After the devastation of WWII, countries banded together so as to put their sordid pasts behind them. With the best of intentions they negotiated a common currency and eliminated (most) trade barriers between them. Things worked reasonably well until the Great Recession. Then the defects of a common currency without a common fiscal union painfully revealed themselves. The whole experiment may collapse.

In its wake will be the conditions that have already led to political extremism and polarization, even in a more solid France, but notably in Greece and Hungary. Spain, we will recall, was also under fascist rule, both during and after WWII. It could return.

We would do well to keep in mind that even Hitler, for all of his dastard ideology and actions, was legitimately elected by the German people. If enough of us begin to break under pressure, we can go apeshit.

After all, we’re busy electing our own brand of right-wing ideologues. They’re called ‘Republicans.’

On taxes: Too good to be true?

June 20, 2012 by davald

An opinion from Bloomberg News:

What if we told you there’s a way to make the U.S. tax system fairer, get corporations out of politics, improve our global competitiveness, bring home more than $1 trillion in offshore earnings, increase wages, end the double taxation of company profits and improve returns for many shareholders?

You’d say we were dreaming. But all this could be achieved if Congress would agree to a simple deal: Lower marginal rates, tax long-term capital gains and dividends at the same rate as regular income, and eliminate the corporate-income tax.

At first blush I don’t like the idea of lowering the marginal rate, which is near its lowest historical point already. During the relative halcyon days of post-war America, the rate was at or near 90 percent; Kennedy dropped it substantially. Yet, despite the high rate, we were experiencing strong economic growth and “the age of the great compression”—the rich weren’t quite as rich and the poor quite as poor.

However, I could be persuaded to change my view in light of this phenomenon: an increasing share of the One-percenters’ wealth is in the form of capital gains, or investment earnings. The Bloomberg editors propose to tax these as regular income, rather than the current 15 percent.

Then I look at the numbers again.

How low can you go? Corporate tax revenues have steadily declined since the end of World War II, while individual income tax revenues held roughly steady for the most part, peaking during Clinton’s second term, then falling sharply with the Bush tax cuts (data from NIPA tables).

All of which brings me to this point: despite lowering tax rates on the wealthy (both their regular and investment incomes), economic growth has stalled.

Give the bastards more money in the form of tax cuts, and they either keep it for themselves or use the subsidy to generate more income, little of which gets circulated in the economy for the benefit of the Rest of Us.

Public jobs

June 20, 2012 by davald

Conservatives, if they are anything, they are zealous. That holds especially so with respect to government, which, as Ronald Reagan intoned, is the problem. That attitude looms large in America, not so much elsewhere. But I happen to live here, so I have to deal with it; and so do you.

One conservative strategy, hardly secret, is to turn the electorate against their governments at every level. We know about Grover Norquist’s obsession: to shrink the federal government to a size that can be poured down the bathtub drain. A “think tank” with the acronym A.L.E.C. is working closely with Republican elected officials, and those running for office, to focus on shrinking state and local governments. Among the group’s initiatives is to cut the number of public sector jobs and to dramatically curtail public employee benefits, especially pensions. We’ve seen this play out in several states, including Wisconsin and Ohio.

The New York Times‘ Joe Nocera devoted his column yesterday to ALEC taking over cities in Rhode Island and elsewhere. He reports that ALEC’s objective of squeezing state and local budgets is being realized, leading to draconian measures. Say a town, which used to rely on revenue sharing, is cutoff from further state funding. Since it’s so difficult to raise taxes and since the usual sources of revenue are being pinched (e.g., property taxes), the town faces municipal bankruptcy. Or, in the alternative, it is put into receivership by the state, whose governor appoints a receiver. The receiver, according to Nocera, has blanket authority to wield an ax to the town’s budget, cut jobs, eliminate public pensions, force employees to pay for all of their healthcare insurance premiums, and so on.

Yet, ALEC seems superfluous. Because of declining tax revenues, cities and states have been forced to cut their workforces and employee benefits without the help of ALEC*.

The New York Times reports that state and local government cuts, in addition to exacting more misery from those who lose their jobs, are also impeding economic recovery. The Times:

Government payrolls grew in the early part of the recovery, largely because of federal stimulus measures. But since its postrecession peak in April 2009 (not counting temporary Census hiring), the public sector has shrunk by 657,000 jobs. The losses appeared to be tapering off earlier this year, but have accelerated for the last three months, creating the single biggest drag on the recovery in many areas.

The chart above, based on FRED data, shows how dramatic these cuts have been. The Times:

If governments still employed the same percentage of the work force as they did in 2009, the unemployment rate would be a percentage point lower, according to an analysis by Moody’s Analytics. At the pace so far this year, layoffs will siphon off $15 billion in spending power. Yale economists have said that if state and local governments had followed the pattern of previous recessions, they would have added at least 1.4 million jobs.

Nevertheless, conservatives insist that their anti-government ideology is correct, though tragically unproven. Cutting employment, regardless of whether its private or public, reduces aggregate demand. This recession and anemic recovery are all about declining demand. When we don’t spend money, everybody hurts—well, save for the One-percenters.

Hmmm. Maybe that’s it. The conservatives care only about the fortunes of the rich. The Rest of Us be damned.

Why?

June 19, 2012 by davald

In trying to make sense out of our world, and, in particular, the present state of the United States, I find myself invariably asking Why? Or, to be more precise, How? That is, why or how did America become so vastly different from other modern industrial countries? When I ask why or how, I have in mind two phenomena: the rise of the conservative movement; and increasing political polarization. They are related.

I am not a frequent visitor to YouTube, but this afternoon I happened upon a couple of clips. One features economist Paul Krugman speaking some time ago in San Francisco. The other is a TED talk by Richard Wilkinson, a British researcher on economic inequality and its deleterious effects on health, both physical and mental.

Watching the Krugman talk, I was struck by his opening point. The period I have called ‘the relative halcyon days,’ defined as the first post-war generation in the U.S., did not gradually evolve. Rather, as Krugman says, it happened very quickly, perhaps in as few as seven or eight years, from the late 1930s to the end of World War II. Moreover, it was the result of deliberate federal policy. Krugman cites several causes or factors that created “the great compression,” when inequality was at its lowest point in our history. These are:

  • the New Deal programs, which pumped millions of dollars into building dams and roads and schools and bridges and so on;
  • historically high marginal tax rates (above 90 percent);
  • war-time controls on prices and wages;
  • Social Security and unemployment insurance; and
  • union-favorable legislation.

On the last point, Krugman said that during the great compression union participation rates tripled, reaching 30 percent, about the same as Canada’s. By the way, Canada’s did not later decline as the rate did in the U.S. It’s still around 30 percent.

The “great compression” did not last, as readers of this blog site can attest. Beginning with Ronald Reagan’s first term, the great compression gave way to the “great divergence.”

So, how did this happen? And why did the U.S. chart a path sharply different from its counterparts in Europe, Japan, and Canada?

It seems to me that the forces of greed are nearly universal. There must be Europeans who want more even though they have enough. They want lower taxes, fewer regulations, and a less generous welfare state. They’d also prefer no unions. Yet, they did not prevail. Once the social welfare democracies were established following the war, successive governments, whether right or left, sustained them.

Today, the United States is the most unequal of this cohort of nations. Private sector union rates are in single digits. We enjoy no universal health care system. Tax rates are the lowest. Government spending as a percentage of GDP is also lowest.

Remarkably, the conservative agenda has come to fruition, almost completely unraveling the “great compression.” And the Republicans have only begun.

But this huge inequality comes at great cost, though we choose to ignore the consequences. Here is where Richard Wilkinson comes in.

I’ve mentioned him before, for example here. His TED talk reprises highlights of his research, wherein he describes briefly inequality’s adverse effects on health and well being.

Straightaway Wilkinson dismisses the significance of a country’s total wealth. There is no correlation between richness and health—in the aggregate. What’s important, Wilkinson’s research shows, is the distribution of wealth within a country or state or even a city. Each chart of his slideshow illustrates these points. (By the way, Wilkinson advised his audience, which, he assumed, included a number of Americans—his talk was in Scotland—that if they wished to live the American Dream, move to Denmark.)

In one chart he shows several countries along the x-axis, ordered by the differential between the richest 20 percent and the poorest 20 percent. The Nordic countries’ ratio is less than four. The ratio of the U.S. is more than twice as large, just below Singapore’s, which has the highest Gini index of the selected countries.

As you would imagine, the U.S., being the most unequal of the countries in our cohort, also suffers the highest rates of mental and physical illness. We’re more violent. We trust our neighbors less. We grudgingly treat government as a necessary evil, with more emphasis on the noun than the adjective.

Wilkinson had one slide showing levels of cortisol, a chemical released during stress. The highest stress occurs in situations Wilkinson calls ‘social evaluative.’ None of us likes being judged, ridiculed, measured, or bullied by others—to be socially evaluated. It should not surprise us to learn that unequal societies create more opportunities for social evaluation. We lack social capital. Our differences are magnified, especially in the U.S. We are polarized, feeling less and less sense of belonging. So, our lives are riddled with stress, and too much stress is bad for your health.

Knowing this you’d think we’d want conditions to change, perhaps more like those that existed during the relative halcyon days of the 50s, 60s, and 70s. You’d think that we would be open to another great compression, with higher taxes on the rich and greater support for the Rest of Us. We’d want that kinder, gentler nation.

Instead, we are nothing of the sort. We’re mean, peevish, anxious, intemperate, hateful, spiteful, boorish, and always envious. Again, there’s a real good chance that November’s elections will deliver more of the same.

Feeling the pinch

June 19, 2012 by davald

The New York Times tells us that even those fortunate enough to have jobs are seeing their wages and hours cut, making it increasingly difficult to pay the bills. For example, an African American woman was driving a passenger van that brought in nearly $1,500 a month. The Times:

When she began driving a passenger van last year, she earned $9 an hour and worked 40 hours a week. Then her wage was cut to $8 an hour, and her hours were drastically scaled back. Last month she earned just $233. So Ms. Woods, who said that she had been threatened with eviction for missing rent payments and had been postponing an appointment with the eye doctor because she lacks insurance, has been looking for another, better job. It has not been easy.

Others interviewed by the newspaper report working multiple jobs to make ends meet. We may recall then-president G.W. Bush congratulating a woman from an audience who said that she had three jobs. He never had a real one.

Meanwhile, a Republican candidate running for a Wisconsin seat in the U.S. Senate chides a reporter for writing too much about poor people. The Huffington Post:

Then, pointing to a reporter in the audience, [Eric] Hovde said he would love to see the press stop covering sad stories about low-income individuals who can’t get benefits and start covering issues like the deficit more frequently.

“I see a reporter here,” he said. “I just pray that you start writing about these issues. I just pray. As it happens, the press as a whole is not reporting all that much about the plight of the poor. The Huffington Post:

In May 2011, National Journal looked at the nation’s five largest newspapers and counted how many times “unemployment” or “deficit” appeared in their headlines or first sentences. The analysis found that unemployment was covered significantly less than the deficit.

“Mentions of unemployment have been dwindling since they spiked to 154 in the month ending August 15, 2010; over the month ending Sunday, there were 63,” wroteNational Journal, adding, “That major newspapers and other media outlets have covered the deficit with greater intensity in recent months should come as no surprise given the focus of the politicians and policymakers they cover. The declining mentions of unemployment are perhaps more surprising, as the issue remains salient for millions of Americans.”

What about deficits? Dick Cheney, when he was vice president, said to then-Treasury Secretary Paul O’Neill: “You know, Paul, Reagan proved that deficits don’t matter. We won the mid-term elections, this is our due.” Cheney was talking about the $550 billion tax cut on the table. The HP includes this graphic:

Eric Hovde and other Republicans exercise selective memory. When they are in charge of Washington, they dismiss deficits and debt. When they are striving to regain control, they want to frighten the hell out of us, arguing that the rising debt will bring devastation.

Do deficits matter? Over time, yes, and we should prefer surpluses. These are hardly ordinary times, however. Now is not the occasion to cut spending. We can only wish that Congress had rejected Bush’s tax cuts. We’d be in much better shape now.

You know, Japan has been running high deficits for decades. Although it’s in a period of prolonged economic stagnation, with only modest GDP growth, the accumulating debt has failed to devastate.

Nor is austerity working in Europe, as we know too well. It’s not working for the states, either. It’s certainly not helping the passenger van driver.

Why do we continue to elect and re-elect clowns like Hovde? This November, they could be in charge of everything. Now that will be devastating.

Soiling our own nest

June 19, 2012 by davald

Well, soiling is much too mild. We’re on a direct course of massive environmental destruction, the consequences of which we’ve yet to consider. I wrote a bit about this here. Now comes Media Lens with a more elaborate report on global warming, the artificial threshold of another two degrees centigrade, and the gargantuan role played by corporations, including mainstream media, in both pursuing the economic juggernaut and frustrating efforts to educate and mobilize the public. (There is much about this last point here.)

Media Lens asks what became of the green movement, launched some 50 years ago by Rachel Carson’s Silent Spring. Why are there no massive protests, as there were in the 60s and 70s, first to end the Vietnam War then to focus attention and action on preserving the environment, action that led to the EPA, the Clean Water and Clean Air laws, and a host of federal and state environmental protection measures?

Media Lens quotes Edward O. Wilson:

We’re destroying the rest of life in one century. We’ll be down to half the species of plants and animals by the end of the century if we keep at this rate.

Why aren’t you young people out protesting the mess that’s being made of the planet? Why are you not repeating what was done in the ‘60s? Why aren’t you in the streets? And what in the world has happened to the green movement that used to be on our minds and accompanied by outrage and high hopes? What went wrong?

Media Lens suggests that corporate media have changed the subject, constantly.

The trouble is that most of what the public hears about politics, including environmental issues, comes from the corporate media. This is a disaster for genuine democracy. As discussed in a recent alert, the media industry is made up of large profit-seeking corporations whose main task is to sell audiences to wealthy advertisers – also corporations, of course – on whom the media depend for a huge slice of their revenues. It’s blindingly obvious that the corporate media is literally not in the business of alerting humanity to the real risk of climate catastrophe and what needs to be done to avert it.

I also think there’s an issue of psychology that factors into the lack of protest. The Vietnam War was real and deadly, splattered across television screens and newspapers’ front pages. We were dying; hundreds of thousands of Southeast Asians were being slaughtered. Also, we young men at the time had a very good chance of joining in the macabre mayhem. (A college student, I received two deferments, then the draft gave new meaning to lucky.) The images of Love Canal and a river literally on fire inflamed the public, bringing outrage to the streets, culminating in Richard M. Nixon’s signing major federal legislation that actually worked to clean the air and water and protect endangered species. In the 60s we saw uniformed police officers fire-hosing young blacks non-violently protesting segregatioon southern streets. Ugly, systemic racism televised. So we protested by the millions, losing leaders to assassinations. Again, laws were passed, change happened, though not as quickly and as thoroughly as we had wished.

But a changing climate fails to deliver urgency. Sure, there is this or that violent storm, possibly the product of increased warming. It seems a bit hotter this year. More wildfires in California and droughts across the Southwest. Eroding shorelines of distant islands may bother some, yet not enough to force us to the streets.

Ironically, it is the scientists who are more alarmed than the public and certainly the politicians. Those who know most also worry the most. Meanwhile the largely ignorant populace is all too happy to have the subject changed.

Besides, a war against carbon? Corporations need carbon.
