A picture of a bully

October 19, 2012 by davald

Timothy Egan’s column carried this photo taken during the last presidential debate.

 

Egan’s accompanying words, as always, worth the read. For example:

To take just a couple of examples, here was Romney explaining the benefits of his tax program, the breaks that you’ll get on your stock dividends and mutual funds. As he outlined it with all the mercenary gusto of the visiting suit with a PowerPoint, Romney said, “Every middle-income taxpayer will no longer pay any tax on interest, dividends or capital gains.” And, a bit later, “If you’re getting a statement from a mutual fund or any other kind of investment you have, you don’t have to worry about filing taxes on that.”

No kidding. In Romney’s world, and throughout his own tax return, the money earned from money — as opposed to money earned from working — is the chief source of wealth. And it’s already taxed at a lower rate than middle-income earnings. Getting rid of those taxes altogether does nothing for the warehouse manager, schoolteacher or insurance sales person taking home a salary and being taxed at a full rate for making a living. But it’s great for someone living off mutual fund dividends.

As I was saying…

October 18, 2012 by davald

Here is philosophy professor Gary Gutting:

How, then, should we choose our president? Typically, I propose, by voting for the party rather than the person. All we can reasonably expect is that the country will probably move in the ideological direction of the winning candidate’s party. This is especially true now, when even the President is so dependent on the political and financial support of the party’s base. Many voters, of course, have a fixed party allegiance. Others will judge that at least for the next four years we should tilt in a particular partisan direction. In any case, since there’s no good way to predict who will turn out to be the “better man” — that’s mostly a matter of luck — the thing to do is vote for the party you think should be in power for the next four years.

Never throw this pitch—

October 18, 2012 by davald

—a low, inside slider to a left-handed batter. I know from experience, with my errant toss to Chris Chambliss, who promptly smashed a line drive over the right-field fence for a walk off home run.

But that’s what Matt Cain threw yesterday. And he threw it to the man who replaced an injured Carlos Beltran, a former Giant. Matt Carpenter, son of a Texas high school baseball coach, golfed Cain’s pitch into the right-field bleachers for a two-bagger and all the runs the Cardinals would need. The miserable Giants mustered nine hits but only a single run, leaving far too many teammates stranded.

Further irony, and baseball is full of them, as any Mariners fan can attest, is that the Giants could have retained Beltran, who is hot just at the right moment. Instead, they stuck with Bay Area-born Nate Schierholtz, who was then dealt to the Phillies in mid season for Hunter Pence, who gives new meaning to the term “free swinging.”

We’ll have to see tonight if Tim Lincecum has really rebounded. Keeping my fingers crossed.

Scatological freedom

October 18, 2012 by davald

The U.S. Court of Appeals tossed out Seattle’s attempt to restrict the distribution of Yellow Pages, an ancient relic of a directory that I haven’t seen, let alone used, in at least a dozen years. That so many have been dumped unwanted on city porches prompted “the Seattle City Council [to vote] two years ago to crack down on unwanted deliveries that piled up in people’s recycle bins and apartment-building lobbies. To pay for the program, the city charged a $100 fee to each of the three companies that distribute the books in Seattle, plus a per-book charge and a disposal fee for unwanted directories that ended up at the recycling center.” (Seattle Times) The court held that the council had violated the First Amendment.

This got me to thinking.

Suppose that I felt the need to walk past my neighbors and yell “Shit!” My neighbors would likely disapprove, but I could argue that I was simply expressing myself, much as advertisers do in yellow pages.

Let’s take this further. Instead of just saying the words, why not actually take a dump, leaving a very tangible form of expression. After all, what’s the difference  between a pile of Yellow Pages and a pile of shit? Just exercising my freedom, wouldn’t you say?

Battle for the idiots

October 17, 2012 by davald

I wanted to watch the Yankees lose to the Tigers, having long since soured on political theater, of which debates are Exhibit A, and not in a good way. But my wife preferred to see the sparring between Romney and Obama. So, we compromised, switching from exasperation to Verlanderian glee. (I hate the Yankees.)

Last night’s Hempstead, NY, ruction yielded more frustration. Answer the damn question, would you! Earnest idiots (my description of “undecideds”) queried the two candidates, which only served as yet another entree to endless encomia of the personal variety. Well, you see, “my plan” will usher in Nirvana, so you won’t have to worry any longer about your concerns, from immigration to unemployment to AK-47s.

Today’s newspapers were filled with predictable palaver about who won and why. Obama was “feisty.” Romney “parried.” Whatever. A few reactions of my own.

The two candidates spent considerable time on how they would reinvigorate the U.S. economy. Both lamented the loss of manufacturing jobs to China, in particular, a favored whipping boy. Romney claimed that as president he would make America “attractive” again to capital investment. Obama emphasized education, especially on technology and engineering.

Before he died Steve Jobs was asked by Obama about all those jobs devoted to the manufacturing and assembling of iDevices. Jobs told the president that those jobs “aren’t coming back.” Why not? Jobs replied that Apple needs thousands of technical workers with adequate engineering skills to facilitate massive production, and those workers don’t exist in the U.S. in sufficient quantities. China, however, had them in spades.

Assembling iPads and iPhones and Galaxy gadgets would not appeal to the average American, which is why we’re content to let undocumented Hispanics pick our tomatoes. Judging by the long queues of applicants outside Foxconn campuses it would appear that thousands of Chinese would prefer to assemble rather than pick. Thus, I’m skeptical of Jobs’s explanation. Even if a U.S. firm could quickly aggregate enough technicians to run a Foxconn-like production behemoth in, say, Alabama or Mississippi, I doubt that there would be the requisite number of workers to insert this tiny component here, then repeat ad nauseam—unless they were “undocumented workers” from foreign lands. How silly of me.

Obama mentioned renewable energy on several occasions. Wouldn’t you know that China is now the world’s major producer of solar panels? Recently, it became the largest wind energy provider, according to Wikipedia. Damn those Chinese. (I am being facetious.)

Lost from everyone’s agenda is climate change or, more specifically, what to do about it. During the 2008 campaign Obama pledged to address global warming, no doubt as part of one of his “grand bargains.” Of course, the Republican would hear none of it. They dug in, and Obama quickly retreated, never to mention the looming catastrophe again.

Last night Obama touted oil and natural gas extraction while praising coal—always clean, naturally. WTF? Both candidates seemed to step over the other to become the drill-and-mining king. What happened to carbon? Forget about it.

Forgive me, but how can Obama continue to function as Romney’s punching bag on the economy? Yes, the president foolishly predicted that his deeply flawed stimulus package would lower the unemployment rate to less than six percent by now. But he dared not blame the bastard Republicans for thwarting every legislative proposal to employ more workers, including the Bring Jobs Home Act.

On the topic of AK-47s, I was forced to laugh listening to Romney’s chattering about husband-cum-wife households somehow ensuring against the acquisition and use of weapons that Obama reminded us were designed for soldiers in the battlefield.

The assembled Hempstead idiots got what they deserved.

The peace dividend

October 17, 2012 by davald

With the collapse of the Berlin Wall and then the Soviet Union there was much talk of a “peace dividend.” The dollars presumably earmarked to defend against “the communist menace” would now be available to build schools, roads, and, well, a better society. It didn’t happen, of course. The financial interests tied to guns and bombs quickly dashed any notions of diverting Pentagon dollars to improving American lives.

But we see from the above chart that defense spending did stabilize after Clinton took office, or just after Russia reentered our vocabulary. Then along came 9/11 and two wars. Military brass and their benefactors drooled.

This next chart tracks annual changes in defense spending from the previous year. We’ll note sharp increases during G.W. Bush’s initial term and another spike just before he left office. Spending has dropped on a year-over-year basis since Obama became president.

 

The point here, of course, is that Congress always seems to find a way to maintain a bloated defense budget, regardless of circumstances. As for the underlying rationale, it makes one wonder.

During last night’s presidential fisticuffs Obama said that he would use dollars devoted to prosecuting the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan to fund education and infrastructure, which he opined were necessary to reinvigorate the national economy.

Both the Everett Herald and the Seattle Times ran an AP “fact check” on last night’s debate. Here is a relevant excerpt:

OBAMA: “Let’s take the money that we’ve been spending on war over the last decade to rebuild America, roads, bridges, schools. We do those things, not only is your future going to be bright, but America’s future is going to be bright as well.”

THE FACTS: What Obama didn’t mention is that much of the money that has been paying for the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan was borrowed. In fact, the government borrows nearly 40 cents for every dollar it spends. Thus using money that had been earmarked for wars to build schools and infrastructure would involve even more borrowing, adding to the federal deficit.

Like most of the Hempstead blather, promises of a new peace dividend will not be realized.

Output gaps and unemployment

October 16, 2012 by davald

Arthur Okun, JFK’s principal economics advisor, discovered a relationship between the unemployment rate and the economy’s output gap—the difference between potential and actual gross domestic product. Here is the Wikipedia description of Okun’s Law. I’ll include a scatter-plot of the relationship.

 

In the chart below I have taken the difference between potential and actual real GDP and overlaid the unemployment rate.

 

 

We’ll note a fairly close relationship, with a couple of exceptions. During the last year’s of the Clinton administration the economy was actually exceeding its potential (the negative values in grey). In Reagan’s first term the unemployment exceeded nine percent; however, the output gap, though high, did not reach the unprecedented levels of the Great Recession.

Unsurprisingly, when the economy is doing well unemployment is low. If too many people aren’t working, the gap between potential output and actual GDP widens.

I have frequently referred to the “relative halcyon days” following WWII. It has also been called ‘the Great Moderation,’ a period of low unemployment and robust economic growth. Then the Great Divergence hit. Let’s compare the average unemployment rate for the two periods.

 

I’ve written previously about reduced marginal tax rates, an explosion of wealth accumulation at the very top, and declining GDP growth during the Great Divergence. In this period workers’ wages have stagnated with higher unemployment rates.

Why is this so? I’ll offer some opinions in a subsequent post.

Washington state governor

October 16, 2012 by davald

Rumor has it that Democratic gubernatorial candidate Jay Inslee pales in side-by-side comparisons with his opponent, Republican Rob McKenna. Whether he’s being interviewed by editorial boards or various interest groups Inslee appears weak, ill-prepared, and unimaginative. McKenna, on the other hand, comes across as bright, energetic, and quick.

Now, as I said, I’m relying only on vague rumors here. While I have met Inslee and seen McKenna only on television, I have no personal experience for rendering the above judgments. I’m in the same position as most voters: dumb and dumber.

Yet, we have a problem in this either-or proposition based on personality only. When it comes to governor, for example, the position for which Inslee and McKenna are vying, there would seem to be two important questions: (1) What would each candidate want to do if elected? (2) What could each candidate do?

We should remember candidate Obama, with his rather bold set of promises, versus President Obama. While the latter has his share of accomplishments, they are but mere shadows of the hopes and dreams he encouraged and facilitated before assuming office. He disappointed many of his supporters, in large part because his opposition desired to achieve that effect, blocking legislative initiatives designed to advance his goals and objectives.

As a Republican, McKenna would surely wish to adopt the austerity regimen championed by his party’s faithful. That is, he would endeavor to cut government spending and lower taxes, all the while proclaiming that these two strategies—and perhaps these alone—would make us all prosper.

In the Voters’ Pamphlet McKenna rails against the state’s “burdensome tax system and redundant regulations,” which, he implies, impede economic growth. He “knows the private sector creates jobs, not government.”

Certainly the recession took a major toll on public-sector employment. State and local governments have continued to shed jobs, including teachers.

Moreover, the percentage of the total workforce employed by Washington state governments has also declined.

It seems difficult to argue that “bloated” governments are the problem, the reason that the economy is lagging. What about taxes?

People and businesses buying things and services represents nearly half of all tax revenue. However, when people lose their jobs, their wages are cut, and firms contract or shut down retail sales fall, which means that the state receives fewer dollars to fund schools (about 40 percent of expenditures) and other social services. Taxes on businesses (B&O) deliver much less revenue to the state, suggesting that they have less effect than McKenna suggests.

What McKenna doesn’t or can’t grasp is that the economy—whether national, state, or local—depends on those retail purchases. And it doesn’t matter whether people are working in the public or private sector. It matters that they have a job with sufficient income to buy food, shelter, clothing, and so on.

Moreover, governments can create jobs, provided they have adequate revenues. They can employ teachers, health care and transportation workers, and technicians to monitor and protect the environment. In turn, those public employees will spend their money, generating taxes.

Inslee, according to the Voters’ Pamphlet, has a “mission”: “to create a stronger and growing economy for Washington.” He would do so by emphasizing “innovations” and “revolutions” in technology and aerospace (read Boing). Inslee has long advocated renewable resources and would surely direct public resources to facilitate their development.

I’m going to reveal a bias here. I am not very excited about Inslee. He lacks administrative experience, most of all. Nor do I care for his government-based approach to renewable resource development; I would prefer a carbon tax, then let the innovators innovate.

Yet, I prefer Inslee to McKenna, and that choice has almost everything to do with just two facts. First, that McKenna is a Republican immediately disqualifies him from consideration. Why? Well, just turn on the television or open up a newspaper to learn what the GOP is about these days. The party’s ideas are crazy, false, or demonstrably wrong in effect (for example, that lower taxes necessarily spur economic growth).

Second, neither candidate can achieve much of anything without legislative support. Since I generally prefer the policies of Democrats to Republicans’ I want an Olympia with Democratic control. If McKenna wins and the opposition party retains majority standing, then he’s not likely to carry out his agenda—which would be a good thing. However, should Inslee prevail under the same circumstances, then those policies I prefer stand a better chance of becoming the law of the land, which is an even better thing.
