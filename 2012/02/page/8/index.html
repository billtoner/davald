Pragmatism trumps principle

February 8, 2012 by davald

The New York Times editors rightly condemn Pres. Obama’s flip-flop on campaign financing. After first rebuking the Supreme Court justices for their Citizens United decision, in an awkward moment of a State of the Union address, Obama has given principle the finger. The editors:

Two years ago, while delivering his State of the Union address, President Obama looked the Supreme Court justices in the face and told them they were wrong to have allowed special interests to spend without limits on campaigns. “I don’t think American elections should be bankrolled by America’s most powerful interests,” he said. “They should be decided by the American people.”

On Monday, the president abandoned that fundamental principle and gave in to the culture of the Citizens United decision that he once denounced as a“threat to our democracy.”

We’ve witnessed blatant hypocrisy before, so many times that counting them would require a roomful of abacuses. Indeed, as a fledgling candidate for the White House in early 2008, Obama said that he would rely on public financing and attendant restrictions. Then he performed a 180, raising a record amount of cash to propel him into office.

The emergence of Super PACs in response to the utterly ridiculous court decision was clearly foreseeable, which is why Obama initially criticized the Supremes. That was then.

He’s tossed “the American people” under the bus, who never really decide elections anyway. One would have to be an imbecile to deny that money equals speech. And the higher a candidate’s pile of cash (both direct and indirect) the more “speech” he or she wields. In turn, that speech induces the reptilian brain to choose one versus another. As I quoted here:

But in three dozen interviews across Iowa and New Hampshire over the past few days, voters readily acknowledged that their decisions would be driven as much by personal chemistry and biography as by political positions and policy.

Amassing millions of dollars is all about conveying “personal chemistry” that appeals to voters’ baser instincts. A thumbs up or thumbs down determined by a candidate’s appearance more than his or her “political positions and policy.”

I can personally attest to this. In 1963, when I was 16 years old, our family motored over to Martinez, Calif. from our home in Concord, a short, ten-mile trip. The purpose? To see JFK standing on the back of a caboose as his campaign train wended its way across the U.S. I recall the anticipation of the assembled crowd as it waited and waited for the train to pull in then stop before them. Finally Kennedy stepped onto the deck to enormous, deafening applause. His face was well-tanned. His tie was purple. There were a number of women in attendance. I overheard several swoon, “He’s so handsome. He’s got my vote.” A tie and a face made the difference. JFK’s views on Vietnam and the arms race failed to register.

Advertising a candidate has little or nothing to do with rationality or “positions.” It has everything to do with selling. And we Americans are conditioned to respond to imagery and sounds, being inundated each day by television commercials, billboards, newspaper ads, and the like.

Obama knows this, I’m sure, and he also knows that he needs to raise and spend a whole lot of money to sell himself while disparaging the opposition. The Supreme Court changed the rules. Goodbye to principle.

A Darwinian Left

February 8, 2012 by davald

That’s the title of one of philosopher Peter Singer’s books. It seems apropos of my posts on conservatism and liberalism, for Singer, like Jonathan Haidt and George Lakoff, believes that the right has something to teach the left.

In discussing Haidt’s remarks to Bill Moyers I mentioned that the University of Virginia social psychologist asserts that conservatives have a more accurate view of human nature. Maybe, maybe not. Lakoff disagrees, but Singer falls in with Haidt.

Singer, before making his mark as a prominent ethicist at Princeton, was a student of Marx’s philosophy, though he certainly avoids the label ‘Marxist.’ He seems more comfortable in the world of moral philosophy, specifically his version of utilitarianism, marinated with socialist compassion.

In the book, A Darwinian Left, Singer writes, first quoting Marx from his Theses on Feuerbach (VI):

“…the human essence is no abstraction inherent in each single individual. In its reality it is the ensemble of the social relations.”

It follows from this belief that if you can totally change the “ensemble of social relations,” you can totally change human nature.

Singer suggests that this point forms the core of Marx and most of the traditional left’s political philosophy. But, Singer says, “The Left needs a new paradigm.”

And what might that be?

I may not do justice to Singer here, but, with Haidt, he asserts that human nature is such that individuals will fall into hierarchical arrangements, no matter the circumstances. It is a consequence of Darwinian evolution. Thus, Marx got it wrong, says Singer, for believing that egalitarian societies will emerge from a rearrangement of the “ensemble of social relations.”

The new paradigm for the left, then, begins with a rejection of Marx’s core principal and an acknowledgement that humans eventually form stratified groups, with superiors and subordinates arrayed on one basis or another. In U.S. society, the dominant organizing principle is money. But human societies have always been sorted by color, that most salient of characteristics. We can add to this religion and no doubt a host of other categories that tend to distinguish one group of individuals from another.

In Chapter 5 of his book Singer sets out what a new Left would reject and what it would embrace. I’ll mention a few of each.

  • The Left should reject the notion that humans are essentially good (contra Rousseau) and infinitely malleable (contra Marx).
  • The Left should reject the belief that humans can live in a state of indefinite harmony without war or famine. There will always be conflict between and among us.
  • The Left should not assume that all differences in social or economic status are the result of deliberate discrimination. Some of us, by our inherited abilities (Darwin) will rise to the top (of whatever), some will fall to the bottom, and the rest in the middle.

Meanwhile, the Left should:

  • develop a better understanding of human nature;
  • not infer, as conservatives do, that what is “natural” is therefore morally “right”;
  • appreciate that individuals will compete for fame and fortune in a condition of scarcity, and economics is all about the distribution of scare goods;
  • understand that notwithstanding the innate tendency to compete, individuals will prefer cooperation if they perceive the likelihood of mutually beneficial outcomes; the Left should push for structures and conditions that promote cooperation and “channel competition into socially desirable ends.”

In my previous discussion of Haidt I failed to draw attention to one category that sharply distinguishes the liberal from the conservative. Liberals score highest on the category “care and compassion,” far higher than conservatives, who tend to rank the same in each of the six categories. (You’ll need to go to Moyers website to see the breakdown.)

Singer wants us to embrace our empathetic tendencies, or at least that’s his prescription for a new “Darwinian Left.” But we should always keep in mind that egalitarian utopias are only figments of imagination; our human nature prevents them from forming or certainly enduring.

Nor should we ignore the fact that cooperation is at least as natural as competition. The challenge is to build a society in which the winners compensate the losers through cooperative structures and mechanisms. As Singer puts it, “We need to think about how to set up conditions in which cooperation survives.”

I take from this, though I found no reference to this by Singer, that a more Nordic-like society gives greater expression to the new Left paradigm than our existing winner-take-all system. For whatever historical reasons, and I’m ill-equipped to name them, Sweden, Finland, et al. have more egalitarian societies than the U.S., which is near the top of the inequality scale. Moreover, Nordic citizens also have a stronger regard for government than Americans do, and they evidently view their respective governments as an embodiment and facilitator of cooperation. But these Nordic countries also have market economies, which entails competition between and among private firms. The combination of competition and cooperation is manifested in strong social welfare societies that mitigate the extremes of wealth and poverty.

I think we can—and should—follow Singer’s advice. However, we don’t have to reinvent the wheel. Precedents already exist in northern Europe.

The downside of growing up

February 7, 2012 by davald

Recessions spur our appreciation for economic growth. We don’t have any right now, or at least not very much. To boost employment, the economy must grow. In our case, and in most industrial nations, growth depends heavily on people buying things. But making and consuming and eventually throwing away stuff, however necessary for our current well-being, jeopardizes the long-time health and happiness of the planet and its inhabitants.

We’ve seen these charts before, depicting the earth’s carrying capacity, or biocapacity, along with ecological footprint, typically defined as the total of: energy production and consumption, which includes resource depletion; and waste disposal, including plain old garbage and harmful emissions.

As it turns out, and this has been going on since the mid-70s, we’ve become too big for our planetary britches. About 40 years ago the total of human activity exceeded the planet’s ability to sustain us and other species. Something has to give. (data source)

Millions of the world’s inhabitants are paying the negative externalities of energy-intensive economic growth. The payments are in the form of disease, malnutrition, dehydration, and death—in addition to a downright shitty existence. Because the gap between footprint and capacity is widening, we have guaranteed that future generations of species, including our own, will continue to suffer, perhaps at greater and greater rates.

Each day we unconsciously make a Hobson’s choice between growth and sustainability. And we almost always choose the former, perhaps because we have to in the short term. Nor can the countercultural choice of the individual for the latter overcome the aggregate choice for the former. It’s not easy being totally, completely, and globally green. Indeed, we’ve so far found it impossible.

Such a legacy.

I’m trying, I tell ya

February 6, 2012 by davald

I make a modest effort to understand things, using William Zinsser’s prescription “write to learn.” So I do a lot of writing, which is based for the most part on my reading, which, I hope, leads to learning a thing or two. This seems to be working with respect to economics, a subject I touched on only briefly in college, some 30 to 40 years ago. And I think economics, the study of, is important these days, with so many people out of work, the rich getting richer, and millions being tossed from their homes.

If you read Dean Baker’s blog, you’ll soon discover that newspaper reporters and columnists, even those who write on economic issue, lack a basic understanding of Macro 101. Baker is repeatedly and acutely annoyed by Robert Samuelson, who writes for the Washington Post.

So, Samuelson betrayed his ignorance once again, according to Baker, in a piece today. Baker:

Almost no one who wrote about the economy for a major news outlet [like Samuelson] was able to recognize the $8 trillion housing bubble that was driving the economy before it burst. Because people who write about the economy are not held responsible for the quality of their work, none of the people who missed this huge bubble were held accountable for this failure. Remarkably, many of them even now do not understand the bubble.

Baker also chastises reporters for letting politicians, especially, off the hook for, well, dumb-ass remarks. In this case, he brings Thomas Edsall to task for his New York Times column. Edsall invokes Newt Gingrich and Mitt Romney:

Newt Gingrich has been explicit in this species of anti-Democratic critique,describing President Obama as “the best food stamp president in history.”

“Mitt Romney takes up the same theme, with more subtlety, warning that Obama and the Democratic party are fostering a European-style welfare state with a growing ‘contingent of long-term jobless, dependent on government benefits for survival.'”

Baker argues that Edsall should have exposed Romney’s b.s., but didn’t. And why bother with Gingrich, who’s going to the moon. Baker:

In fact, the generous European welfare states of northern Europe do not have a growing “contingent of long-term jobless, dependent on government benefits for survival.” Germany, the Netherlands, Sweden, Denmark, Austria and Norway, the countries with the most generous welfare states, all have lower unemployment rates than the United States both short-term and long-term.

Take that, Mitt.

But wait. Mitt and other Republicans are busy crafting and foisting moral narratives on us all, the kind that resonate with the American people, who are evidently not all that interested in reason and facts.

Baker has carved out a niche for himself with his blog “Beat the Press.” The media’s economic ignorance keeps him very, very busy.

There, you, too, may have learned something about the housing bubble.

As Europe goes so goes Washington state?

February 6, 2012 by davald

European leaders are trying, and failing, to snatch an austerian victory from Eurozone defeat. Today it was announced that Greece will cut 15,000 government jobs in a bid to dramatically cut its federal spending, a condition imposed by core Eurozone leaders.

Paul Krugman asserts that the strategy “is not going to work.” Slashing government spending along with wage-paying government jobs will exacerbate the revenue problem, since there will be fewer workers paying taxes, which the Greeks were never very good at collecting anyway. Lower revenues will add to the deficit, all other things being equal. In turn, the core leaders will demand more belt tightening, and so on.

Europe, including its core countries, is far from escaping recessionary woods. The Eurozone could suffer negative growth, and significantly so, should its austerity measures fail, as Krugman predicts.

Suppose that happens. How exposed is Washington state? Here’s a graphic prepared by Chase, using U.S. Department of Commerce and Wells Fargo Securities data (via Krugman).

Notice those states with the “highest risk.” Yep. There’s Washington, with 2.8 percent of its GDP in the form of exports to European countries. My guess is that Boeing and Microsoft are in there, perhaps along with agricultural products, including wine.

Washington’s 2011 GDP was $313 billion, according to this report (pdf). Its European exports, then, would be about $8.9 billion. If Europe falls into another recession, as is widely speculated, wages and consumer spending will decline, leaving American products on the shelves.

Should Europeans sharply curtail their purchases of Washington stuff, our companies will produce less, leading to layoffs or wage reductions or both. That means fewer revenues for Olympia, resulting in further budget cuts.

More dismal economics, I’m afraid. Unless, of course, you’re Mitt Romney, should he emerge as Obama’s challenger, which gets more likely with each primary result.

Speaking of conservatives

February 6, 2012 by davald

I’ve got a couple of recent posts on conservatism, one here and the other here. I readily concede my bias; I have strong liberal tendencies. But I confess difficulty trying to understand the conservative mind, so much so, that I believe I’d find it impossible to even have a conversation with someone on the extreme right, although I’ve been able to successfully converse with someone from the polar opposite, who happens to be my doppelgänger. As I’ve also mentioned previously, Berkeley cognitive scientist George Lakoff endeavored to discover the conservative mind, coming up with a set of moral metaphors distinguishing those on the political right from those on the political left.

In last night’s Bill Moyers & Company we’re introduced, at least I was, to Jonathan Haidt (pronounced ‘hite’), a social psychologist with the University of Virginia. He’s just written a book (available for pre-order at the usual outlets) entitled Righteous Mind: Why Good People are Divided by Politics and Religion. His effort resembles Lakoff’s, although from the perspective of a psychologist rather than a linguist/cognitive scientist.

Haidt has discovered a pronounced split between left and right across six categories. These are:

  1. care, or compassion
  2. liberty
  3. fairness
  4. loyalty
  5. authority
  6. sanctity

The professor has devised an online test, which he calls a “morality quiz.” I took it, and here are my results:

The green bars represent my scores. The blue and red depict those of liberals and conservatives, respectively. It seems that I’m certainly not a conservative, and I’m a bit more to the left than the average liberal.

Haidt acknowledges that we are a sharply divided country today, with each side “demonizing” the other, accusing our opposites of being and doing “evil.” Once you get to this point, he suggests, the ends justify the means. We see this playing out in Congress, with the Republicans strongly and stubbornly adhering to what they believe to be their core principles.

To illustrate this rigidity Moyers showed an excerpt from 60 Minutes, wherein Leslie Stahl is interviewing House Speaker John Boehner. It’s almost, but not quite, funny, as Boehner insists that he will “not compromise.” When Stahl insists that the art of governing is all about compromise, Boehner says that the goal of both sides is to find “common ground.” Isn’t that compromise?—Stahl counters. No, we will not compromise, Boehner insists.

America wasn’t always this way. When Bill Moyers was working for Lyndon Johnson in the White House, there were such animals as “moderate Republicans,” whose votes were necessary to secure passage for the Civil Rights and Voting Rights acts. There was still evidence of this species well into Nixon’s administration, whose domestic agenda was more liberal and effective than either Carter’s or Clinton’s.

But Johnson also foresaw the loss of the southern Democrat with his signing of the aforementioned acts. The South will switch to the Republicans, he predicted. And so it was. (Haidt thinks that southerners used to be Democrats because Lincoln was a Republican. Now that the Democrats had finally completed Lincoln’s emancipation agenda, might was well join the GOP.)

Haidt allows that he used to be a solid liberal Democrat. But he’s gradually moved to the center because of his research findings. He’s now agnostic on the liberal-vs-conservative preference. For one, he is now more inclined to accept the lessons of karma: there should be consequences for our actions, both good and bad. Thus, the conservative principle of just deserts appeals to him. Also, he claims, the conservatives have a more accurate picture of human nature. From the interview:

When I began this work, I was very much a liberal. And over time, in doing the research for my book and in reading a lot of conservative writing, I’ve come to believe that conservative intellectuals actually are more in touch with human nature. They have a more accurate view of human nature.

We need structure. We need families. We need groups. It’s okay to have memberships and rivalries. All that stuff is okay, unless it crosses the threshold into Manichaeism [my link]. So I think that it would be very difficult to run a good society without resting much on loyalty, authority and sanctity. I think you need to use those.

Moyers showed Haidt several photographs of Occupy Wall Street and Tea Party rally signs. What do they tell us?—asks Moyers.

JONATHAN HAIDT: So what I’m hoping my book will do is kind of give people almost a decoding manual so they can look at anything from the other side and instead of saying, ‘See, this shows how evil they are,’ you say, ‘Oh, okay, I see why they’re saying that.’ All right, so, let’s take, ‘Stop punishing success, stop rewarding failure.’

BILL MOYERS: I remember seeing that at one of the early Tea Party rallies.

JONATHAN HAIDT: So that’s one version of fairness. Fairness adds proportionality.

BILL MOYERS: What do you mean?

JONATHAN HAIDT: Well, if people work hard, they should succeed. If people don’t work hard, they should fail. And if anyone bails them out, that is evil. You should not bail people out who have failed, especially if it’s because of lack of hard work, something like that. So as the right sees it, government is evil because it keeps punishing success, with redistributive policies, okay, take from the successful and give to the unsuccessful.

And it keeps rewarding failure by giving out welfare and other payments to people who aren’t working. So what I’ve found is that fairness is at the heart of both Occupy Wall Street and the Tea Party. But because the words have different meanings and they relate to additional moral foundations, that’s why they’re really, very, very different moral views.

There was a lot of empathy and caring at Occupy Wall Street. So this sign, “I can’t hurt another without hurting myself.” This is part of the ethos on the left, this is why you get a lot of Buddhists and sort of the Christian left.

It’s a lot of emphasis on care and compassion. When they talk about fairness, it’s in particular, fairness, that will benefit the weak and the poor. So, here’s a sign, “Marching for the meek and weary, hungry and homeless.” “Tax the wealthy, fair and square,” as though because they’re hungry and homeless people, it’s fair to take from them and give to them. Now, I think there are really good arguments for why we need to increase tax rates on the top. But simply saying, ‘Some have and some have not, therefore it’s fair,’ that’s not a moral argument for most Americans.

BILL MOYERS: And what’s the conservative moral position on this?

JONATHAN HAIDT: The conservative moral position is the Protestant work ethic. It’s karma.

So, how do we break the great divide? Haidt:

One is demonization. When you have people saying, you can disagree as much as you want, but when you start saying, “They’re only saying that because they’re, you know, they’re a racist or they’re in bed with this company,” or, and even though sometimes that might be true. But we are so prone to dismiss other people and demonize their motives that we’re usually going to be wrong about that. So if we could begin to see this in each other and even challenge each other and say, “Hey, you’re demonizing.” Like, just, you know, disagree with them but stop attributing bad motives to the other side. So if ten years from now people sort of recognize that and could call each other out on in, that would at least be some progress.

The other one is corruption. Until we develop a massive groundswell of public revulsion at the fact that our Congress is bought and paid for, not entirely of course. Many of them are decent people. I don’t want to demonize. I’m sorry. But the nature of the institution is such that they’ve got to raise tons of money. And then they’re responsive to those interests. So perhaps there’s some norms that we could develop that will put some pressure on Congress to clean up its act.

Haidt avers that the Democrats, the liberals, don’t understand what the Republicans do: that winning the hearts and minds of the voter requires a compelling narrative, one that embraces moral tenets that have broad appeal. And he does mean moral. Politics is religion, he adds. The principles espoused or should be espoused are entwined with what Haidt calls ‘sacralization.’ Haidt:

Politics is about sacredness. Politics is about offering a vision that will bind the nation together to pursue greatness. And Republicans since Ronald Reagan have been really good at that.

Moyers mentions a passage from Haidt’s book that took him aback. It had to do with reason. Here’s Haidt:

But they [liberals and Democrats] sacralize reason itself, as though reason is this noble attribute, reason is our highest nature. And if we could just reason, we will solve our problems. All right, that sounds good on paper. But given all the stuff I just told you about what psychologists have discovered about reason, reasoning is not good at finding the truth.

I, too, did a mental double-take on this. But Haidt might be on to something. We liberals contend that we’ve got reason and the facts on our side. Yet, we’re not winning the electoral arguments. Something’s missing, and it may very well be that strong moral—politics-as-religion—narrative.

Now, having watched the program and pondered its contents, I have no idea what such a liberal narrative would look like. Haidt says that we can’t talk about fairness, as if we’d got that market cornered. Republicans also have a sense of fairness, but it’s expressed in Aesop’s fable about the ant and the grasshopper, suggests Haidt. The ant is industrious, disciplined, and persistent, looking ahead and preparing for the future. The grasshopper sits idly by, mocking the busy ant. When winter comes, the ant is ready for it; not so the grasshopper. Aesop’s lesson: be the ant and take no pity on the grasshopper. That’s only fair.

I should think that appeals to compassion could work, and they’ve worked in the past. Identifying groups as victims resonates (e.g., women’s rights, workers’  rights, civil rights for minorities, gay rights). Are the 99-percenters being victimized by the One-percenters? That’s certainly the message of OWS; yet I doubt that it gains much traction on the right.

The right believes strongly that fertilized human eggs are victims of abortion. Destroying the egg is tantamount to murder. The abortionist, then, is evil and must be stopped by any means necessary.

The left responds with sympathy for women with unwanted pregnancies. Thus, whether or not to abort is the woman’s right to choose. But what about the right to life of the fetus?

Haidt concurs that the Haves have too much and the Have-nots too little. The rich should pay higher taxes, notwithstanding karma. What is a compelling argument for the government as Robin Hood, in which the winners compensate the losers?

Wall Street must be reined in. What’s the argument? I think we’re back to just desserts: the rich should be revered and envied for their success not emasculated. But it’s not fair, you say. That doesn’t resonate, remember?

Lakoff, the Berkeley scientist, concluded that the liberal worldview was preferable to the conservative, since it delivered better outcomes. For instance, the rich wouldn’t be quite as rich and the poor quite as poor. Also, a stronger sense of society would emerge under more compassionate political leadership. Unlike Haidt, Lakoff urged liberals to take up the fight, as if it were a holy one, to defeat, if not figuratively neuter the modern, take-no-prisoners conservative.

Big question: Do we have the votes?

Words from the prophet Dorgan

February 6, 2012 by davald

On his January 28th show Bill Moyers focused on the Glass-Steagall Act, what it did, how it came to be repealed, and the foreseeable disaster that ensued. He interviewed John S. Reed, formerly with Citigroup, and Senator Byron Dorgan (D-ND).

On November 4, 1999, Senator Dorgan addressed his chamber, which was likely almost empty. He urged his colleagues to vote against the repeal. In so doing, he uttered a prophecy.

“I’ll bet one day somebody’s going to look back at this and say, ‘how on earth could we have thought it made sense to allow the banking industry to concentrate through merger and acquisition to become bigger and bigger and bigger,’” Dorgan said. “How did we think that was gonna help this country?”

He spoke financial institutions becoming “too big to fail.” Yes, he used those words. He also said, “I think we will in ten years time look back and say we should not have done that.”

No matter, the Senate voted 90-8 and the House 362-57. A beaming Bill Clinton signed the bill, Gramm-Leach-Bliley, handing the first pen to Phil Gramm.

However prescient Dorgan’s remarks, Moyers’s interview of John Reed was even more fascinating. Reed reveals the rationales behind the Glass-Steagall repeal and the merger between Travelers and Citibank. Reed was then-CEO of the latter.

As Reed tells it, many of his bank’s customers (not the Rest of Us) were taking their investment business to insurance companies (e.g., Travelers and AIG) and Wall Street brokerages, because the Glass-Steagall Act prohibited commercial banks from gambling depositors’ money. Reed was approached by Travelers’ CEO, Sandy Weill, who convinced him that a merger between the two entities would allow the emerging financial institution to provide a complete portfolio of services, enabling them to retain those customers who would otherwise depart. One problem: Glass-Steagall prohibited the merger.

Financial institutions have lots of money, which they lavish on members of Congress and propaganda machines. It also helps to have true believers in government, those who thought Glass-Steagall not only quaint but counterproductive. Then-Federal Reserve chairman Alan Greenspan, who seems to have learned economics at the feet of Ayn Rand, blessed the move to abolish Glass-Steagall. Joining him were Clinton’s Treasury secretary Robert Rubin and his economic advisor Larry Summers. Needless to say the Republican embraced repeal, and it was three GOP senators who co-sponsored the repeal legislation. Also working behind the scenes was Travelers’ Sandy Weill, who boasted of having phone conversations with Clinton, who assured him before the fact that Glass-Steagall would die, allowing the merger between Travelers and Citicorp. As Moyers put it, “The fix was in.”

Here is the fascinating part of Reed’s interview, something that I should have thought of before, because it now seems so obvious. With Glass-Steagall gone, the commercial-cum-investment entities like Citigroup would now have risk-free money to invest on Wall Street. Why risk-free? Because the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, a wholly owned subsidiary of the federal government, would guarantee the dollars deposited in the commercial side of the merged entity. Citigroup and others could place all sorts of bets knowing that if they guessed wrong the government would cover the losses. This is a classic case of moral hazard.

The new mergers, as Dorgon predicted, created financial colossi too big to fail. Not only did the new joint institutions have FCIC as a back stop, by expanding to enormous size and thereby controlling trillions of dollars in financial assets, they posed a monumental threat to the entire global economy should things go south. And that’s precisely where they went.

In a classic Rolling Stone magazine article, Matt Taiibi described the largest colossus, Goldman Sachs:

The world’s most powerful investment bank is a great vampire squid wrapped around the face of humanity, relentlessly jamming its blood funnel into anything that smells like money.

That’s an apt metaphor, “vampire squid.” The financial sector is one enormous system of tentacles attached to every facet of the economy, both at home and abroad. A jolt to one part of the system reverberates throughout the whole.

When asked by Moyers if the bailout of Wall Street was the right thing to do, John Reed quickly said yes. Not because he had any affinity to Citigroup; he had long since retired before the collapse of Lehman Brothers. Rather, the financial institutions had such a stranglehold on the international economy that without a massive infusion of cash everything would have collapsed, making things far worse than they are. (This is essentially Paul Krugman’s opinion, too.)

Reed enthusiastically supports the Volcker Rule, which is part of the Dodd-Frank bill. Originally, the rule would have prevented proprietary trading by commercial banks, the effect of Glass-Steagall. However, it was weakened considerably by Scott Brown (R-MA), who held Dodd-Frank hostage in the Senate to secure his amendment. In the final legislation banks are now allowed to invest in hedge and private equity funds. The weakened Volcker Rule becomes effective July 21 of this year, unless Republicans succeed in eliminating it altogether.

Should Republicans gain control of the White House and Senate I would expect them to repeal Dodd-Frank and with it the Volcker Rule, however modest its effect. I also expect that the Republicans would restore all those conditions that allowed for, if not facilitated, the housing bubble and further capital accumulation.

Then it’s here we go again.

Educating China

February 5, 2012 by davald

My two children graduated from the University of Washington, with my daughter then acquiring three graduate degrees, including law, also from U.W. They both received good educations at an affordable cost, the virtues of a top-ranked public institution.

But times are changing. Faced with diminishing revenues, the legislature has been busy cutting the budget in order to balance it. The university has taken big hits from Olympia, compelling the regents to raise tuition. While still a bargain compared with Harvard or Yale, the higher tuition forces more students into debt, which can be staggering after four years or more. The state legislature has not kept pace with population growth, failing to expand existing or building new facilities. Thus, competition for admission has skyrocketed. With too few slots and too few dollars from the legislature, the university has recently opened its doors to Chinese students, who evidently have the means to pay treble the in-state tuition rate.

In 2006, according to the New York Times, just two percent of the student population were from foreign countries. This year the percentage is 18, and most of this cohort comes from China—who comprise 11 percent of the 5,800 freshmen.

Nationwide, higher education financing has undergone a profound shift in recent years, with many public institutions that used to get most of their financing from state governments now relying on tuition for more than half their budgets. But legislators and taxpayers still feel deep ownership of the state institutions created to serve homegrown students — and worry that something is awry when local high achievers, even valedictorians, are rejected by the campuses they have grown up aspiring to.

One does not have to be xenophobic to appreciate that Washington’s future economic health, not to mention the well-being of its citizens, is at risk if the state does not provide sufficient educational opportunities for its high school graduates. Already businesses complain that many U.S. applicants lack requisite skills.

Here’s Boeing’s CEO in a Northwestern University address:

So it is absolutely critical that we address the tightly related issue of education and the readiness of our workforce. I’m sure it’s no surprise to you that technology-based companies face an impending skills shortage. This is a global circumstance, by the way; no single nation can produce enough creativity, talent, or knowledge to meet today’s marketplace challenges alone. But the problem is growing acute in the United States, where many seasoned and skilled workers are close to retiring, and insufficient numbers of capable workers are being prepared to replace them. I emphasize “capable,” because in the United States today we face a skills shortage, not necessarily a labor shortage.

Ironic, it would be, if Boeing and other businesses began hiring China-born graduates of U.S. public universities.

Had you heard? There’s something called a Super Bowl.

February 4, 2012 by davald

Tomorrow’s the big game. I know, that doesn’t do justice to the spectacle, which commands a wider audience than the Pope, for sure. I will be among the football faithful, despite my team’s ignominious defeat. That would be the 49ers.

So, which team will win, the Patriots or the Giants? I have no skin in the game, no bets on the line, no stakes to worry about, and all those other gambling cliches. I’m Chauncey Gardner on this, “I like to watch, Ben.”

Neither team rolled over their playoff opponents. But for an overtime fumble by the Niners’ punt returner, the Giants might not have survived; and Eli Manning barely did. Seeing his uniform pulled out of place, mud all over his arms, hands, and face, I was reminded of this famous photo, via the Smithsonian (Ironically, perhaps, Y.A. Tittle played for both the 49ers and the Giants; it was for the latter that he received a bloody head.):

Yet, the Giants kicker had to make the chip shot, which he did convincingly. On to the Super Bowl, New York.

As for the Patriots, they too enter the big game on a lucky break. The Ravens all-pro placekicker botched his chip shot, which would have tied the game sending it into overtime, just like the 49ers and the Giants.

But tomorrow is a blank slate. And there won’t be mud, or rain, or sleet, or snow—the game’s indoors, hosted by our newest right-to-work state. The players are none too happy about this. The game, however, will go on.

I’m stalling, I know. I favor the Giants, although I don’t know why. Brady can be a piece of art when he’s at the top of his game, which is most of the time. In truth I really don’t care who wins. I just hope for a close one, played well.

I’ll be ready with appropriate food and beverage. I know that I won’t be blogging then.
