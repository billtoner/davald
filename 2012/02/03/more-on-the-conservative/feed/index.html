Our physical, mental, and emotional limits create what we CAN’T do, thus limiting us to certain stations in life.

I saw several janitors the other day talking and laughing in the hallway around 7:30 in the morning. They seemed like some of the happiest people I’d ever seen, much happier than many of the professors with PhD’s. Can we honestly say that guy running the vacuum cleaner would be as happy drudging through essays written by freshmen for a comp course? Probably not.

The veil of ignorance, was nonsense. Any person who doesn’t have a comprehensive doctrine or any idea of his station in life is not a person at all. He rather resembles a computer. Rawls overlapping consensus idea is more of a cop out than anything else.

Fairness would be great. But nature is not so fair in endowing people. Yes, we should make their life better, and provide them with more options. But we also need to consider options that help increase their chances of happiness.

I agree, somewhat, on your point about “stations in life.” However, there are fewer and fewer attractive opportunities in America for those lacking tertiary education. Is this “structural,” as many argue, or is it the product of deliberate strategies (e.g., so-called ‘free-trade agreements,’ immigrant restrictions on highly educated foreigners)? Germany, for example, has strong apprenticeship programs, where high schoolers learn skills then join the firms upon graduation. Wages are higher than paid by McDonald’s, for example.

Rawls, though stopping far short of pushing egalitarianism, appreciated that fairness was key. In his assumed “original position” we wear “veils of ignorance,” not knowing of our relative skills or inherited traits and fortunes. We would then arrange society in a Pareto optimum manner, ensuring that any decision does not make the least advantaged worse off. Thus, the rich could get richer, but not at the expense of everyone else.

I do disagree that each of us is meant to occupy certain positions. By whom?

]]> By: flippantgadfly https://davald.wordpress.com/2012/02/03/more-on-the-conservative/#comment-140 Sat, 04 Feb 2012 00:13:11 +0000 http://davald.wordpress.com/?p=2362#comment-140 I think you, as well as Corey Robin, have a false understanding of conservatism. It is not about feudalism or enslaving one class to another. it is the appreciation that some people are meant for certain stations in life.

While I am far from a rote conservative, I find it impossible to believe that an egalitarian society is possible. This nonsense is what leads to people going to college who should not be there. There are certain people, like myself for example, who simply cannot grasp complex mathematics. I should, therefore, not be forced to study calculus. Why then, should someone who is simply not able to learn things in a college setting, urged to go to college when he could achieve a much better quality of life by picking up a trade that utilizes his innate abilities? Rather than suffering through years of self depreciation in college, he could simply attend trade school for anywhere from 6 months to 2 years and be set in a job he might actually enjoy. This isn’t feudalism in any sense. Certain people are just more naturally endowed to do certain things, even John Rawls agrees with this.
