My point, made frequently on these pages, is that the modern Republican Party has moved far to the right of its predecessors. As Bruce Bartlett told Jon Stewart recently, there would be no room for Ronald Reagan in today’s GOP; and Bartlett worked under Reagan.

Compromise, which used to be called “the art of politics,” seems impossible in Congress, not because the Democrats have refused to concede points, which they have done all too often, in my judgment. It’s all about Republican intransigence. This was brought home at one point during the GOP presidential primary, when no single candidate would allow any tax increase regardless of how much the federal budget would be axed.

Friedman quotes Paul Quinn of New Zealand’s conservative National Party: “I will explain to you how our system works compared to yours: You have Democrats and Republicans. My Labor opponents would be Democrats. I am a member of the National Party, and we would be … Democrats” as well. For New Zealanders, compromise is the rule rather than the exception, since their differences are much smaller than those in Congress.
