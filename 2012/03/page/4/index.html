Renewables have not displaced fossil-fuel generation

March 20, 2012 by davald

Washington is different, the Snohomish County PUD even more so—in this respect: the majority of the electricity is generated by renewable resources, mostly hydroelectric plants along the Columbia River. There is only one coal plant still operating (Centralia), and that will be phased out over the next decade and a half. Natural gas facilities contribute a bit over ten percent (pdf) of electricity generation. With the passage of Initiative 937 (pdf), both private and public utilities with more than 25,000 customers (the PUD has over 300,000) must populate their supply portfolios with increasing percentages of renewable resources, save for new hydro, which does not qualify, for dubious reasons. The resource of choice is decidedly wind power.

Utilities are scrambling to meet the initiative deadlines. This year the percentage of the supply portfolio that must be in the form of qualifying renewables is three percent; it jumps to nine percent by 2016; and reaches its maximum of 15 percent by 2020. The PUD is well ahead of the curve.

Things are different for the U.S. as a whole and for over 100 other countries. A study by University of Oregon professor Richard York concludes that almost all of the renewable resources built over the last 30 years have been swallowed up by increasing demand for electricity; the green resources have not displaced coal and natural gas.

York found that each kilowatt-hour (kWh) of electricity generated from non-fossil-fuel sources displaced only 0.089 kWh of that from fossil fuels. To put this in perspective, displacing one kWh of fossil-fuel electricity would require over 11 kWh of electricity from alternative sources.

It’s not easy being green.

Oops, Mr. Daisey

March 20, 2012 by davald

I sat in the sold-out audience to be entertained by Michael Daisey, and entertained I was. He is a master story-teller. His subject for the evening was Steve Jobs and Apple, Inc. Titled The Agony and Ecstasy of Steve Jobs, the performance revealed the dark side to all those “insanely great” products emanating from Cupertino geniuses. I wrote about it here.

Well, as you’ve no doubt heard, Mr. Daisey played footloose with facts, embellishing for the most part, lying for the remainder. The revelations of deceit followed widespread praise for his work, including a recent link to Mr. Daisey’s website and monologue script by Robert Kuttner. It seems that Mr. Daisey made things up about working conditions at Foxconn, the principal assembler of iPhones and iPads.

Nevertheless, I suspect that the tens of thousands of workers who stand for hours in an assembly line are laboring under circumstances we’d find repellant: low wages, long days, repetitive tasks. Boring work, to be sure. But for these Chinese workers assembling gadgets beats the alternative, which is either toiling in the fields or doing nothing. Indeed, when Foxconn recently announced the need for more workers, thousands of eager youths joined a long queue outside its factory doors.

I believe Apple can and must do better. It was no doubt stung by the New York Times series on working conditions in southeast China’s “special economic zones.” Daisey, of course, didn’t help matters. Tim Cook, Apple’s CEO, responded by stepping up audits of its suppliers, denying the worst of the alleged abuses, and committing the company to improving its supply-chain conditions. Also, Foxconn has just boosted wages by as much as 25 percent. It’s adding “lifestyle services managers” to “ensure that the workers’ quality of life is fair and enjoyable within reason.”

Tim Cook is not Steve Jobs, in a good way, I think. The autocratic and mercurial Jobs likely gave not a whit for Chinese laborers. Nor was he obsessed about Apple’s environmental footprint, although he took steps to eliminate the more toxic elements from the company’s “post-PC” gadgets after Greenpeace gave Apple a thumbs down. Cook, however, strikes me as a genuinely decent man, concerned about both working conditions and the environment. (Apple’s new server farm in North Carolina will be powered by the country’s largest solar array.) While he’s no Bill Gates in this department, not long after he assumed the reins Tim Cook established a charitable giving program; the company will match employees’ contributions to charities of their choice.

For his part, Michael Daisey is sticking to his narrative, albeit with some recent modifications to reflect the backlash. He defends his work as a testament to the “broader truth.” Much of what he speaks about in his performance is true; workers toil under harsh conditions, they succumb to repetitive motion disease, they have even died on the job.

I wrote the following after attending Daisey’s Seattle performance.

Michael Daisey told his audience that we Americans made Shenzhen, insofar as we have demanded relatively cheap gadgets. Upon leaving the theater we were handed a piece of paper entitled “What happens next?” Daisey wrote, “That lies in your hands.” He asks at the bottom that we “tell others.”

This is just a monologue [he wrote]—a single voice telling a story of a single experience. But if I have opened the door for you, consider opening a door for others. We do not like to think about China and its implications in our country, but that silence can only exist if we are complicit with it. Talking about it, thinking about it when making purchasing decisions, and understanding it is not just symbolic. They are the first seeds of actual change. Do not be afraid to plant them.

Pass the shovel.

I see no reason yet to pronounce all is well with Apple and those who make its products. It can and should do more.

Some ABCs of the NBA

March 18, 2012 by davald

Portland

Losing to any team by 42 points suggests deep problems. The Portland Trailblazers, in their Madison Square Garden date with the Knicks, looked horrible and played worse. The resurgent Knickerbockers, sans just-resigned coach Mike D’Antoni, seemed bent on proving that their recent doldrums had nothing to do with themselves. It was all about their supervisor. The Trailblazers have done the same, managing to parlay that ignominious defeat into the ouster of their once-sainted coach and former prized member of the erstwhile Seattle Supersonics, Nate McMillan. Poor Nate. His days were numbered following the premature retirement of Brandon Roy, a floor leader par excellence. His leadership gone, Nate discovered that his players had a “chemistry” challenge, and he wasn’t up to the task of facilitating a team Kumbaya.

Portland finally gave up on its former top pick, Greg Oden, who has played only 82 games since he was selected number one in the 2007 draft—ahead of Kevin Durant. Bad choice. With Oden gone, the team has some extra cash to pursue talent. Can you say “rebuilding”? I doubt that we’ll see very much very soon from the Trailblazers.

Los Angeles

The Lakers, that is. They sent Derek Fisher to Houston, where he has yet to surface. Will he retire rather than play for the Rockets? Age had taken a toll on the point guard, who had become a defensive liability with sporadic scoring, mostly poor. Luke Walton was sent to Cleveland. He suffered through a strange tenure as a Laker, spending most of his time on the bench these days. I suspect that if given the opportunity to play more minutes, his talents could shine. He possesses great court sense, can pass the ball with any big man, and can shoot. Or not. We’ll see soon enough. In exchange the Lakers acquired Ramon Sessions, whom they’ve eyed for some time. He, too, saw his minutes dwindle after the Cavaliers made rookie Kylie Irving their new star. Sessions is just 26 years old and is in his fourth NBA season. His addition may vault the Lakers into the championship, since every team will have a match-up problem against them.

That other LA team hasn’t been doing so well since the injury to Chauncey Billups. That may be just a coincidence. After all, the Clippers have Chris Paul and Mo Williams to deliver the ball to superhuman Blake Griffin. But they’ve lost the lead in their division to the Lakers, who will only get better (see above).

Orlando

So, Dwight Howard will stick with the Magic—for another year, anyway. Despite putting up the numbers night after night, I really don’t see Howard taking his team deep in the playoffs. Call it ‘attitude.’ He’s not hungry enough, lacking the intensity of a Kevin Garnett or a Joachim Noah or even an Andrew Bynum, who is having the best season of his still-young career. And winning teams are indeed hungry.

Odds and ends

I like that Sacramento has established former Husky Isaiah Thomas at the point. Diminutive, certainly, but also crafty, spunky, and quick. He can shoot as well as he passes. Another former Husky, Nate Robinson, is now the Warriors starting guard. He brings energy, toughness, and experience. His challenge is always about control. Can he exercise appropriate discretion, or will he play chaotically, as has been his history?

It appears that Seattle can check off New Orleans as a possible transfer to the Emerald City. A long-term deal is in the works to keep the team there for years. I don’t think Commissioner David Stern would have allowed the Hornets to depart from that hurricane-ravaged metropolis. Next?

Statins and exercise

March 16, 2012 by davald

While I am hardly a marathoner, I do put in four to five miles of vigorous walking each day. But not without some muscular discomfort. I also take statins, as many in my cohort do (older men with a—previous?—weight problem).

Then I came across this article in the New York Times. It seems that there is a correlation between statins and painful exercising.

For years, physicians and scientists have been aware that statins, the most widely prescribed drugs in the world, can cause muscle aches and fatigue in some patients. What many people don’t know is that these side effects are especially pronounced in people who exercise.

Research has shown that when people stopped taking statins their aches and pains disappeared. Citing researchers the article suggests that those who have low risks of heart disease halt their medications.

Well, having passed a stress test a couple of years ago (“your heart and vascular system are just fine”), I decided to remove statins from my list of meds, starting last night. I’ll test the hypothesis later this morning.

Dr. Krugman exposes Republican fallacies

March 16, 2012 by davald

I often wonder where Paul Krugman finds the time to debunk G.O.P. aversions to facts, what with his teaching duties and global traveling. Here he takes on the conservative claims that we could lower oil prices today and solve the unemployment problem if only we let the drillers drill without environmental constraints. The problem: they are already drilling without environmental constraints and we still suffer from high unemployment. Krugman:

…As a result, U.S. oil production has risen significantly over the past three years, reversing a decline over decades, while natural gas production has exploded.

Given this expansion, it’s hard to claim that excessive regulation has crippled energy production. Indeed, reporting in The Times makes it clear that U.S. policy has been seriously negligent — that the environmental costs of fracking have been underplayed and ignored. But, in a way, that’s the point. The reality is that far from being hobbled by eco-freaks, the energy industry has been given a largely free hand to expand domestic oil and gas production, never mind the environment.

Strange to say, however, while natural gas prices have dropped, rising oil production and a sharp fall in import dependence haven’t stopped gasoline prices from rising toward $4 a gallon. Nor has the oil and gas boom given a noticeable boost to an economic recovery that, despite better news lately, has been very disappointing on the jobs front.

Eating our own

March 15, 2012 by davald

I guess it must be resentment. For how else to explain the war on government workers?

One example from New York, where the state legislature has sharply curtailed public pensions, a proposal from “Democratic” governor Andrew Cuomo. The Times quotes Danny Donahue, the president of the state’s largest public employees union:

“This deal is about politicians standing with the 1 percent — the wealthiest New Yorkers — to give them a better break while telling nurses, bus drivers, teachers, secretaries, and laborers to put up and shut up.”

In our state, Olympia legislators threaten to significantly modify health care insurance for teachers under the guise of saving money. Currently, each district’s teachers’ union bargains with its respective school district for wages and benefits. Among the latter is health insurance coverage. The principal sponsor for Washington’s attempted overhaul is another nominal “Democrat”—Steve Hobbs.

Yes, I understand that Governor Gregoire is required by law to submit a balanced budget proposal; and usually the legislature complies, although it is free to incur deficits. Since a Tim Eyman initiative, passed by the voters, essentially prevents Olympia from raising taxes to pay for public services, the only option left is to cut. Education and social services compose two-thirds of the state budget. So these are obvious targets for the slashing.

Of course, we’re familiar with what other states have done or are trying to do to curb labor in general and public workers in particular, removing collective bargaining, reducing pay and benefits, or both. Nor should we forget that states have been forced to lay off millions of workers since the start of the Great Recession.

In his Rolling Stones essay economist Jared Bernstein decried the downward trend in union participation rates:

In an economy like ours, where unions are relatively weak, and where forces like globalization and technical change reduce the bargaining clout of the 70 percent of the workforce that’s not college educated, full employment is the best, if not the only, friend of the working class. That’s because the lower your income, the more full employment helps you.

With the exception of Iceland, those countries with the highest union density rates have lower unemployment and greater equality than in the U.S. John Kenneth Galbraith used to write about labor providing a necessary counterforce to capital. That’s when more than a third of the workforce belonged to a union.

One need not be a socialist or a communist to appreciate the concept of solidarity. The Rest of Us could use that now more than ever, as Wall Street and the other One-percenters kick labor when it’s down. This is no time to be eating our own.

Besides, since almost all of state government is devoted to teaching and helping others, why not push for the best government possible; and that means highly qualified public workers receiving good wages and benefits. If we keep chiseling away at the public sector, the Rest of Us, who rely heavily on government services, will only be cheating ourselves.

The prison pitfall

March 15, 2012 by davald

Ginandtacos.com suggests that there is a “bull market” for prison privatization. Budget-squeezed states are finding it difficult to reject upfront cash from the likes of Corrections Corporation of America.

Private prison giant Corrections Corporation of America recently sent an offer to 48 states offering immediate cash payments in exchange for 20 year contracts on the state prison system. Some states will undoubtedly take them up on the offer, either falling for the promise of savings through “efficiency” or reasoning that the current crop of politicians will be long gone by the time the piper requests payment. The offer has an interesting caveat, though – CCA requires the states to guarantee 90% capacity in the prisons for the duration of the contract.

Those states that have accepted previous offers saw their incarceration costs skyrocket.

In California, for example, prisons were 2% of the budget in 1980 but now consume a full 10% of every dollar the state takes in. Texas is now spending $6,000,000,000 annually on its carceral [sic] empire, nearly 8% of a state budget facing a $20 billion shortfall.

Only in America.

The jobs deficit

March 15, 2012 by davald

Jared Bernstein makes the case for full employment in this Rolling Stones essay. He writes:

Moreover, when I look at the statistical relationship between periods of tight versus slack job markets, I find that for every point the unemployment rate was below full employment, low incomes went up 1.3 percent per year in real terms, middle incomes went up 0.8 percent, and high incomes were unaffected.

That last point – that the incomes of the wealthiest families are statistically uncorrelated with full employment – might surprise you.  But think about the current recovery.  The unemployment rate has been way too high for way too long.  Yet what growth there’s been has bypassed low- and middle-income families on its way to the top of the scale.

Okay, how do we get to full employment?

…in times of elevated joblessness, like the present, stimulus measures such as infrastructure investment and fiscal aid to states could help get us closer to full employment.  Aid to states is particularly important in preserving decent middle class jobs that are both important to the teachers and police who hold those jobs as well as to their communities.
