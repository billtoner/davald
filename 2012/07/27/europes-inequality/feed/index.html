]]> By: James Galbraith https://davald.wordpress.com/2012/07/27/europes-inequality/#comment-864 Sun, 29 Jul 2012 14:46:49 +0000 http://snohomishobserver.com/?p=3871#comment-864 Thanks for your interest in this work.

It is important to be consistent. But note that inequality data for the US do take account of differences between states, since inequality for the US is measured for the US population as a whole. Whereas, inequality data for Europe — such as you show here — are generally given on a country-by-country basis, and the inequalities between countries are simply not factored into the measurements.

What we did was to add the within-country inequalities to a measure of inequality between countries, in a mathematically-consistent way, so as to develop a measure of inequality across Europe as a whole. Comparing this measure to the US, you find that the inequalities in Europe are greater.

Note that the measures in question are measures of wage inequality, not inequality of incomes as a whole. The latter are harder to measure and compare, in part because the definition of “income” in tax records depends on tax codes and tax compliance, which differ from country to country. However, the economic arguments relating inequality to employment are all about wages, not incomes, so this is the appropriate basis for comparison in this case.

Lots of details in the working papers at the UTIP web-site, address below.
