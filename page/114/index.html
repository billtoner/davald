Inequality is good for you. Not. [u]

October 26, 2012 by davald

Okay, that’s not the explicit message the rich convey, but it’s close. Without our plutocrats the Rest of Us would be in even worse shape. You buy that, don’t you?

There should be no question about America’s inequality. It’s high and getting higher. Nor should there be any question that the extremely wealthy, the top 0.01 percent of households, extract the bulk of income from the economy. Let’s revisit some data (from Emmanuel Saez).

But despite the wealth gap, the Rest of Us haven’t fared so well. We see this clearly in the following chart:

 

Notice that flat line at the bottom. That’s the Rest of Us. And, yes, the average income of the top 0.01 percent surpassed $7 $19 million before the Great Recession. In 2010 it fell back to a piddly $5.9 $16.3 million. How can they possibly manage? Oh, and these numbers do not include capital gains, which comprise most of the super rich’s income.

Keep in mind that these charts reflect inflation-adjusted dollars. Going back to 1917, the earliest data year, the income of the bottom 90 percent has hardly budged.

Nobel Prize-winning economist Joseph Stiglitz tells New York Times readers that inequality actually stunts economic growth. He also reminds us that social and economic mobility in the U.S. is the lowest of all advanced industrial democracies. Then Prof. Stigltiz sets out to debunk five essential myths promulgated by Romney, the Republicans as a whole, and the capitalist class. All of the following are false:

  1. America is a land of opportunity.
  2. Trickle-down economics works.
  3. The rich are “job creators.”
  4. The costs of reducing inequality are so great that “we would be killing the goose that lays the golden egg” should we try to raise the incomes of the bottom 90 percent and lower them at the top.
  5. Because markets are efficient and self-regulating the government should not meddle in the economy.

In a recent Harper’s essay (subscription required) Jeffrey Madrick emphasizes the importance of raising taxes, especially on the wealthy. Americans pay about 26 percent of their income on taxes of all forms. The OECD average is 38 percent. Yet higher tax rates do not impede growth. Madrick:

…the high-tax economies grow about as fast as ours does, sometimes faster. Prosperous Denmark, Norway, and Sweden have tax rates well above 40 percent.

Suppose Americans were to pay the OECD average. Madrick says that over 10 years tax revenues would increase by $16 to $17 trillion. The Budget Control Act, which, if allowed to trigger, would reduce the deficit by only $1.5 trillion over the same period.

Now here’s a statement that’s sure to rankle the Randians. “Better government, not weaker government, is the path to prosperity.” The whacky Tea Partiers would prefer no government, except for a bloated defense and a larger criminal justice system. But if we are to make the investments necessary to restore the economy and, I dare say, “compete” with other countries—or at least improve our trade balance—then our governments need more dollars. Here’s Madrick:

Politicians and ideologues are playing a cruel game by keeping serious tax increases off the table, but it is especially hypocritical to do so in the name of fiscal responsibility. America’s budget problem is a revenue problem, not a spending problem. The current national conversation about tax hikes is a fine example of political deference to the rich and powerful. It is not good economics.

So, ladies and gentlemen, our first order of business is to raise taxes. The rich will do just fine, by the way, and the Rest of Us will benefit from a solvent Social Security system, better transportation, greener energy (Madrick recommends a carbon tax), and world-class schools.

Unless, of course, you really enjoy misery.

Red and blue and geography

October 25, 2012 by davald

I’ve been perpetually confounded by America’s divisions. In particular, why is the U.S. mostly “red” and “blue” only along the coasts and upper Midwest? I shared my befuddlement here.

The noted psychologist Steven Pinker has some thoughts, posted in the New York Times this morning. After briefly surveying the literature, including the hypotheses of George Lakoff and Jonathan Haidt, Pinker suggests that what separates us has much to do with how human nature was tamed. Pinker:

If this history is right, the American political divide may have arisen not so much from different conceptions of human nature as from differences in how best to tame it. The North and coasts are extensions of Europe and continued the government-driven civilizing process that had been gathering momentum since the Middle Ages. The South and West preserved the culture of honor that emerged in the anarchic territories of the growing country, tempered by their own civilizing forces of churches, families and temperance.

One especially provocative opinion cited by Pinker that should give us pause.

The historian Pieter Spierenburg has suggested that “democracy came too soon to America,” namely, before the government had disarmed its citizens. Since American governance was more or less democratic from the start, the people could choose not to cede to it the safeguarding of their personal safety but to keep it as their prerogative. The unhappy result of this vigilante justice is that American homicide rates are far higher than those of Europe, and those of the South higher than those of the North.

We could explore a couple of counterfactuals. Suppose, for example, the early colonists and their offspring deferred to the indigent populations rather than slaughtering them. The wholesale capture of relatively pristine real estate propelled a premature geographical expansion far from nascent seats of government, law, and order. Meanwhile, those who remained behind, within or closer to the country’s initial urbanization, eschewed the rugged individualism and rough justice practiced by migrating pioneers of the sparsely populated Wild West and pre-industrial South. In a word, the original urbanists were more “civilized.”

Imagine that a stubborn few, the “patriots,” declined to “sever the bands” with England and, instead, followed Canada’s lead. The British monarch maintained control over our northern neighbors, which may have allowed Canadians to gradually develop their own parliament—though still tethered to the Queen—and formal system of justice. We tend to think of Canadians as more peaceful than we are, and, indeed, our homicide rate 2.6 times as high. Besides, we like to go to war on any pretext, beginning with an armed revolt against the King. Ironically, perhaps, the Canadians did not share our antipathy toward “tyranny.” They did not succumb to the false dichotomy between liberty and death, and they may be better off as a result.

Alas, we’re stuck with ignorance armed to the teeth. But we’re free!, goddammit.

Apple harvest

October 24, 2012 by davald

Apple revealed one new device and several upgrades to its current lineup. The long-anticipated iPad mini made its debut, sporting a smaller screen but with the same aspect ratio as its bigger sibling, so that all iPad apps will run on it without modification. It weighs just under 11 ounces.

The pundits were out in full force. It seems that Apple now has a large target on its backside, the almost inevitable outcome of success. So the mini triggered predictable gainsaying, from a too-high price ($329 at the low end) to lack of Retina display. Others faulted the display’s glare as compared with the new Kindle offerings.

I suspect that consumers will ignore the criticisms and gobble up the mini as fast as Apple can make them. I’m sure that schools will now see better financial metrics with the lower price vis-a-vis the larger iPad. We may eventually see the demise of students as beasts of burden; they’ll wear clothing with big pockets to accommodate the mini-cum-digital-textbooks.

The fourth generation iPad promises to be twice as fast. It now comes with the new Lightning power cord and port. The changes, then, are mostly internal.

I’ve never owned a Mac mini, but it sounds intriguing. Such a small package with so much power; and it uses only 11 watts of electricity in idle mode. Apple says that it’s the most energy-efficient desktop computer on the market. You can plug it into a giant HDTV if you like via its HDMI port.

A DeLorean flux capacitor? Not quite, but how about a “fusion drive”? Apple engineers have figured out a way to get near flash-drive performance out of an SSD+hard drive combination. The specs dazzle:

 

You can find this drive in the new iMacs as an optional configuration. I’m guessing that the top-of-the-line model screams past anything else out there.

Tim Cook announced that Apple had sold 100 million iPads. Impressive.

Here comes Christmas, but not for me. I’m falling behind the cycle. Something about inadequate dollars.

Charter schools

October 23, 2012 by davald

Washington voters are asked to decide whether or not the state will finally have charter schools, after several failed efforts to make them a reality. I’ll get to the point. I’m opposed.

Before I discuss my reasons for opposing the ballot measure, I need to say a few words about the miserable state of public education. In a word, it sucks. For several decades now—and this is especially true in my home state of California—school budgets have been squeezed; so much so that the teachers and administrators can’t possibly do their proper jobs. Educators lack resources, time, and high-quality raw materials—as in learning-ready children.

Against this awful status a bunch of very rich people want to stick the camel’s nose inside the educational tent. This latest effort, which the Seattle Times‘ editors describe as “thoughtful and limited,” possesses neither trait. (Also, I find it odd to praise something because it’s “limited.” We didn’t mind the torture, because it stopped.)

Here are a few caveats to voters.

  • The devil will be in the details, and the details will emerge only in submissions to either the yet-to-be-established “charter school commission,” which will authorize and superintend some charter schools, or existing local school boards. The applicant, a non-profit organization, will submit a plan that includes an overview of the proposed school and a financial roadmap. Should either the commission or the school board authorize the charter school, the non-profit applicant will be awarded an initial contract of six years.
  • The initiative’s advocates stress “flexibility.” Therein lies the rub. Each charter school board will have the authority under its own contract to “hire, manage, and discharge any charter school employee.” Not surprisingly, there is no mention of unions or labor protections. We can understand the Washington Education Association’s opposition.
  • While the advocates seem to go out of their way to emphasize “at-risk students” and that the charter schools will be open to all, there is one provision that stands out. It’s under Section 203 and reads in part:

“[A charter school board may] solicit, accept, and administer for the benefit of the charter school and its students, gifts, grants, and donations from individuals or public or private entities…[except religious groups].”

Hmmm.

In the brochure that I received in today’s mailbox I see that the effort is being financed by Bill Gates, Alice Walton (of the Walmart family), Mike and Jackie Bezos (Amazon), and Nick Hanauer, a local venture capitalist who earned a degree in, gulp, philosophy. He was an early, major investor in Amazon. Why would such wealthy individuals want public charter schools, when their own children can and do go elsewhere?

Bill Gates, we know, has got his money in a lot of projects, with education being just one. He attended private school as a child then dropped out of Harvard. His wife seems to have pushed him into foundation work, and, to his credit, he’s donated billions of dollars to apparently worthwhile efforts, in particular to the eradication of diseases. I’m going to assume here that charter schools is his latest do-good foray into education, having pushed small schools for several years, with absolutely zero results.* I’ll even concede for argument’s sake that the other wealthy benefactors have a noble basis for pushing charter schools. They may even care about the fate of at-risk children, many of whom no doubt shop with their parents at Walmart stores.

But what we don’t hear from these individuals is the necessity of fully funding public schools via a progressive tax system. All children deserve better. Yet they get the shaft, because too many of us deprive state and local governments of the revenues they need to properly educate students.

One more thing, as readers of this blog will recognize, the focus of educational reform in the U.S. is grossly misplaced. The charter schools measure joins a host of other initiatives that emphasize after-the-fact solutions to improving learning. Research does show that quality teaching is vital. So let’s start producing quality educators before they enter the classroom.

Moreover, and equally familiar, is that our society has fallen criminally short of providing an array of public services that ensure that children grow up safe, secure, and capable of learning. Too many suffer from food insecurity, inadequate shelter, and lack of health care.

This charter school measure is just one more distraction from what’s really needed. Worse, if it passes, the artificial limit of 40 total charter schools (and no more than eight new ones per year), will surely be eliminated.

Consider that troubling provision about gifts, grants, and donations. Gates et al. are free to lavish all their generosity on charter schools to their liking, while allowing conventional schools to starve. Will we not see one more manifestation of Haves and Havenots, this time in our public school system?

And those gifts, grants, and donations will come with strings attached. Do it my way, or risk losing my “generosity.”

There is nothing public about this at all.

Vote no on Initiative 1240!

_________

*  In principle I don’t like the idea of someone having so much influence on public agendas. Cash-strapped school boards, in particular, have a difficult time saying no to Microsoft and, now, the Gates Foundation.

How we work

October 23, 2012 by davald

We recognize that we do not live in our fathers’ economy. My dad toiled for 35 years at the Richmond refinery of Standard Oil/Chevron. He raised a family of seven, including five children, on the union wages earned.

He did not aspire to have his three sons join him among the tanks and pipes and pervasive odor of sulfur and oil. We would pursue other lines of work.

My career, such as it is, featured stints at teaching, insurance adjusting, forensic consulting, policy analysis, and, finally, holding political office. Along the way I’ve always written stuff.

One brother, after 20 years in the Air Force, is a lawyer in small town in southern Idaho. My other brother trained as a chef and made a living around food, before a series of weird events conspired to make his life literally intolerable.

My one surviving sister is a court reporter. The other was a nurse until she contracted a fatal cancer. (My brother just sent a reminder that today is the 25th anniversary of her death.)

As you can see, none of us siblings took employment cues from our father. And none of us spent an entire working career at one company.

I’ve written several posts on the Great Divergence, the period beginning in the mid-70s that saw the incomes of the top one percent skyrocket while wages of the Rest of Us stagnated or declined. The kind of job that fed and housed a family of seven—without the need for a college degree—has diminished in number. Let’s take a look at a few occupational categories since 1975.

Employment in manufacturing and “durable goods” sectors has steadily declined as retail trade, leisure/hospitality, and financial jobs have increased. But what’s happened since the Great Recession?

All of the listed sectors experienced declines in employment. However, with the exception of construction, which contracted the fastest and remains stuck far below pre-recession levels, employment numbers have risen since the end of 2009, Obama’s first year in office. Yet none of the occupational categories have seen employment levels return to pre-recession states, save for “leisure and hospitality.”

By the way, the above chart goes a long way in explaining why the persistently high unemployment rate is not “structural”—too few people with requisite skills, as even Bill Clinton suggested during the Democratic convention. The debt overhang, combined with a housing glut, impedes the construction sector, a key indicator of the economy’s overall health.

Both presidential candidates have trumpeted their ability to create jobs, especially of the manufacturing kind. That would be nice, but here is another disturbing set of data.

All of these countries, including Germany, which has a reputation for building things, have shed manufacturing jobs since 1975, the U.S. most of all. Neither France, Germany, nor Japan gained as a result of our losses.

We may wonder about China. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics estimates (pdf) that there could be nearly 100 million Chinese manufacturing jobs. The average hourly wage: $1.36. If you’re looking for a reason as to why manufacturing will not return to the U.S.—they were ever here in such large numbers—proceed no further.

But all is not well in China, either. It, too, is feeling the effects of the global recession, as we’ll note in the following chart:

 

Happy days have returned in spades for the über-rich. They may never revisit the Rest of Us. Of course, in Romney’s world, we can and should all be just like him. All we need are the right attitudes.

The Giants and the face

October 23, 2012 by davald

In my previous “holding my nose” post I had initially checked “sports” as a category. I had meant to say a few words about switching between the Giants-Cardinals game and the last presidential debate. I squeezed in only a few moments of the latter, during commercials of the former. I have my priorities.

Of the debate I happened to catch Obama’s retort to Romney about the number of vessels controlled by the U.S. Navy. I gathered that Romney had criticized Obama for allowing the fleet size to shrink to WWI levels. Obama, as I recall, upbraided Romney for failing to appreciate how the world and technology have changed. We don’t need as many ships as we used to. Nor do we have as many “bayonets” as before, although he could have used the term “muskets.”

Another sneak at the debate revealed Romney’s recurring theme. He will restore America’s “leadership.” Heck, it worked for Reagan, and recent polls suggest that it may work again—so is the state of the U.S. voter’s mind.

Leadership, I infer from the blather, has a lot to do with being “strong” and “forceful.” That’s what Mitt said. Just look at this face, he seemed to be beckoning the viewer. Now look at the other guy. Romney’s countenance has the virtue of not sitting atop John McCain’s aging, gnarled body.

Oh, boy.

Holding my nose

October 23, 2012 by davald

On these pages I vowed not to vote for Obama. My reasons were many and obvious. Here is one example. Yet, it is nearly inevitable under our system of government and winner-take-all electoral procedures that I, for one, confront every four years a choice between extreme evil and something slightly less so. This year is no different.

The increasing closeness of the presidential debate draws into sharper relief the horror of a Republican White House, especially one aided and abetted by GOP majorities in both houses. Mitt Romney scares the hell out of me.

As I sit down with my ballot this evening I will grudgingly renew my subscription to Obama, sorely wishing that I had a genuine alternative. In marking my ballot for the incumbent, I will take some comfort from the endorsement by the editors of The New Yorker:

The choice is clear. The Romney-Ryan ticket represents a constricted and backward-looking vision of America: the privatization of the public good. In contrast, the sort of public investment championed by Obama—and exemplified by both the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act and the Affordable Care Act—takes to heart the old civil-rights motto “Lifting as we climb.” That effort cannot, by itself, reverse the rise of inequality that has been under way for at least three decades. But we’ve already seen the future that Romney represents, and it doesn’t work.

The reëlection of Barack Obama is a matter of great urgency. Not only are we in broad agreement with his policy directions; we also see in him what is absent in Mitt Romney—a first-rate political temperament and a deep sense of fairness and integrity. A two-term Obama Administration will leave an enduringly positive imprint on political life. It will bolster the ideal of good governance and a social vision that tempers individualism with a concern for community. Every Presidential election involves a contest over the idea of America. Obama’s America—one that progresses, however falteringly, toward social justice, tolerance, and equality—represents the future that this country deserves.

Public jobs

October 22, 2012 by davald

The New York Times‘ editors take on both Romney and Obama for their rare agreement on the assertion that “government does not create jobs.” What were the candidates thinking?

 

Lest you think that public sector employment has somehow crowded out private-sector hiring, consider the following chart:

 

The ratio between government and private sector employees is declining. The upward tick in 2010 represents temporary U.S. Census workers.

 

Nevertheless, the slash in government employment does adversely effect the economy as a whole. The Times editors:

Public-sector job loss means trouble for everyone. Government jobs are crucial to education, public health and safety, environmental protection, defense, homeland security and myriad other functions that the private sector cannot fulfill. They are also critical for private-sector job growth in two fundamental ways. First, the government gets its supplies from private-sector companies, which is why Republican senators like John McCain have been frantically warning about the dire effects on job creation if Congress moves ahead with planned military spending cuts. (Republicans insisted upon the cuts as part of their ill-advised showdown over the debt ceiling.) Second, government spending on supplies and salaries reverberates strongly through the economy, increasing demand and with it, employment.

But Republicans—and now it seems Obama, too—believe that government cannot create jobs, despite overwhelming evidence to the contrary. That attitude, as the editors suggest, impedes economic recovery while eroding support for public services that benefit everyone. To see just how bad these government cutbacks have been on the economy, the editors link to this report.

Watch housing

October 22, 2012 by davald

The collapse of the housing bubble ushered in the Great Recession, or, as Paul Krugman calls it, ‘the Lesser Depression.’ Setting aside the reasons for the bubble’s expansion and its sudden burst, we can see from the following chart that when housing starts begin to fall a recession is imminent.

In his column this morning Krugman discusses the importance of leverage, or debt, in the recovery, or lack thereof. He writes:

Why is recovery from a financial crisis slow? Financial crises are preceded by credit bubbles; when those bubbles burst, many families and/or companies are left with high levels of debt, which force them to slash their spending. This slashed spending, in turn, depresses the economy as a whole.

When households are deleveraging (paying off debt) they are not spending as much. The chart below shows real personal spending per capita indexed to January 1, 2000 (= 100).

 

When people aren’t spending, firms aren’t producing. When firms aren’t producing, they’re not hiring. When they’re not hiring, people don’t have a job. When people don’t have a job, they don’t have money to spend. When people don’t have money to spend…well, you get the idea.

The good news, I suppose, and a point made by Krugman is that there is a recovery going on. It’s just a very weak one. Also, we can appreciate the enormous gap between where per capita spending would have been had it continued along the 2000-to-2007 trend line and where it is today.
