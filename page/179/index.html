I like it

December 14, 2011 by davald

The Everett Herald‘s editors have broached an interesting alternative for the soon-to-be-shuttered Kimberly-Clark plant. They suggest converting it to produce hemp-based paper products. The hemp could be grown in Washington state.

Why do we cling to the notion that paper products must come from trees? At least two sustainable alternatives exist: Hemp and bamboo.

Industrial hemp, the blue-collar cousin to marijuana that has no psychoactive properties, has been grown for at least 12,000 years for fiber (textiles and paper) and food. (Thomas Jefferson drafted the Declaration of Independence on hemp paper, according to the North American Industrial Hemp Council.) It was grown commercially in the U.S. until the 1950s.

If it’s good enough for Tommy Jefferson, it’s good enough for the Rest of Us. Better to keep the plant running then to shut it down and cast several hundred workers to the street.

Gregoire didn’t get the memo on Finland

December 13, 2011 by davald

Against Finland’s successful education model Governor Christine Gregoire proposed an expanded teacher (and principal) evaluation system. Instead of the current two evaluation categories—satisfactory and unsatisfactory—the governor proposes four: unsatisfactory, basic, proficient and distinguished. This is so un-Finnish.

Had Gregoire read any of the recent reports and studies of Finland’s wholesale reform of its education system she would have proposed something entirely different, something more before-the-fact than after. One major takeaway from the Finnish model is facilitating superior teaching by selecting only the best and the brightest candidates for teacher training. How might this look?

In the U.S., unlike Finland, college education is very expensive, saddling graduates with long-term debt that frustrates their net earning power. Thus, students avoid lower-paid professions like teaching in favor of more lucrative careers, as in Wall Street. Finland pays for all of the teacher training, which should remove a significant impediment to attracting talented education candidates. Boosting the salaries of teachers would certainly help.

As the pool of prospective, top-notch candidates expands, universities can afford to be and should be much more selective, as they are in Finland. (I got into a teacher-certificate program, so that’s all you need to know about selectivity in the U.S.) The presumption in the Finnish model is that deploying society’s best and brightest in the field of education translates into superior academic performance by students. Experience has confirmed as much. Once hired, teachers should be given sufficient resources to be effective.

Yet, I cannot emphasize enough the overwhelming influence of socioeconomics in student populations. As I commented earlier, “poor students test poorly.” Governor Gregoire joins most political leaders in avoiding the obvious—eradicating or mitigating poverty—to target teachers after they’ve made it to the classroom. The Finnish experience tells us that it’s more important and effective to concentrate the state’s efforts on bringing highly capable candidates to the teaching profession in the first place.

In the New York Times article that I linked to previously, we find this excerpt:

“The first six years of education are not about academic success,” he [Dr. Sahlberg] said. “We don’t measure children at all. It’s about being ready to learn and finding your passion.”

Think about this for a moment. In Washington state’s schools, young five-year-olds about to enter kindergarten are subjected to a battery of tests to evaluate their readiness to learn. That’s right. We test them before they start their first day of school.

Well, we’ve gone assessment crazy. Everyone in education is tested, from pre-schoolers to principals. So, everyone in education is operating under increasing pressure to perform. Is it any wonder that so many teachers drop out?

How different the experience if very talented teachers were able to help young children get ready to learn and to find their passions. Gosh, school might just be a place to enjoy rather than endure.

More on Finnish schools

December 13, 2011 by davald

You can get into trouble around here by throwing around the word socialism. Our principal propagandists, aided and abetted by a subservient school system dedicated to inculcating the appropriate civic virtues (read the free market), have ensured that we react viscerally to the mere mention of the word.

So, there is no way we’d hear an American politician utter these words:

“The primary aim of education is to serve as an equalizing instrument for society.”

Damn commie. Actually, the speaker is Pasi Sahlberg, a Finnish educator and author. He has been globe trotting these days, talking to different groups who are anxious to find out more about the Finnish education model, which differs from ours as crocodiles to turnips.

It wasn’t always this way, as I wrote about here. The New York Times reports on D. Sahlberg’s recent visit to Manhattan, his views on education, and a bit on the huge change Finland made in both the structure and operation of its schools, which had been fair to middling—and also uneven, just as ours is in the U.S. Here’s what Stanford professor Linda Darling-Hammond told the Times:

“Thirty years ago, Finland’s education system was a mess. It was quite mediocre, very inequitable. It had a lot of features our system has: very top-down testing, extensive tracking, highly variable teachers, and they managed to reboot the whole system.”

I’ll just list the elements of Finland’s educational reform as culled from the Times article:

  • all teachers must have master’s degrees, paid in full by the state
  • teacher-education programs are highly selective; it’s more difficult to gain entrance than it is for law or medicine
  • teachers are given broad latitude to design curricula and pedagogical approaches
  • children do not start school until after they reach the age of seven
  • there are no tests at the elementary level; indeed, no standardized tests at all until the age of 16, when, like the rest of Europe, the result will help determine whether a student goes on to university or into vocational training
  • there is no homework
  • teachers spend about four hours in the classroom each day and the state pays them for two hours a week of planning time

Of course, Finland is a very different country than ours. It’s much, much smaller (only three percent the size of the U.S.). The population is about 5.5 million; the U.S. about 313 million. Only five percent of its people are foreign-born, and poverty is quite low, only a fifth of our rate.

By the way, to those who like to bash teachers’ unions, as both Bill Gates and Steve Jobs had, 96% of Finnish teachers belong to one. Evidently working collectively does not detract from learning. Finnish students have routinely scored at or near the top in international assessments. The Times:

Dr. Sahlberg said another reason the system had succeeded was that “only dead fish follow the stream” — a Finnish expression.

Finland is going against the tide of the “global education reform movement,” which is based on core subjects, competition, standardization, test-based accountability, control.

“Education policies here are always written to be ‘the best’ or ‘the top this or that,’ ” he said. “We’re not like that. We want to be better than the Swedes. That’s enough for us.”

This may shock you, but I tend to agree with those on the Right who wish to abolish the U.S. Department of Education. The kind of changes made by Finland stand a better chance on a smaller scale, like in Washington or Oregon.

Actually, let’s go all the way down to the classroom. Hire great teachers, give them sufficient resources, then get out of their way. It’s working in Finland. Why not here?

Gridlock yields nothing

December 13, 2011 by davald

Unlike many Americans, I find our system of government woefully inadequate to the tasks at hand. The Founding Fathers’ fear of genuine democracy coupled with their antipathy toward all things royal conspired to produce a federal government that doesn’t work. Indeed, it cannot work.

So, I was naturally drawn to this article in The American Prospect by Harold Meyerson with the provocative title: “Did the Found Fathers Screw Up?” His answer is decidedly affirmative. Meyerson:

Yet, three years into the worst recession since Roosevelt’s time, a countercurrent, every bit as American as our bias for action, has swept over us. Twenty-five million Americans are either unemployed or underemployed, and the average duration of joblessness stands at record highs. Consumers are too deep in debt to consume; our producers produce and our investors invest abroad. To remedy all this, the federal government today tries … nothing.

Again, because it can’t. Meyerson:

The root cause of all this inactivity is our peculiar form of democracy. While most democracies are governed by parliamentary systems, our Founders opted for a presidential system, which they consciously booby-trapped with multiple veto points to impede decisive legislative action and sweeping social change.

The most recent standstill involves Obama’s attempts to fill the head position of the new Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, which Elizabeth Warren set up but was viewed too liberal (who isn’t?) by the current GOP senators. After being rebuffed, Obama selected Richard Cordray, a former Ohio attorney general. No you don’t, said the Republicans.

While they have no truck with Cordray personally, they really don’t like the bureau itself. They prefer that it not be, which is why they refuse to let anyone run it. Republicans are all about protecting the people from financial shenanigans, right?

Well, Obama could put Cordray in charge during the upcoming congressional recess. But that’s not going to happen either.

While presidents have the power to make temporary, or recess, appointments without lawmakers’ approval after Congress has formally adjourned, congressional aides in both parties say they don’t expect Obama will get that chance.

It seems that under Harry Reid, the Senate has never been officially in recess. If only a single senator sticks around, the body is considered open for business, thus blocking any recess appointments.

If I ran the circus for a day, I’d immediately abolish the Founding Fathers’ colossal mistake and replace it with a new structure with these ingredients:

  • unicameral parliament of 100 members
  • a prime minister rather than a president
  • proportional representation with a ten percent electoral threshold for seats
  • no judicial review by the Supreme Court of the new parliament’s laws

Does it ever occur to Americans to ask the question: “If our governmental system is so great, why hasn’t any other country adopted it?” The answer is clear. Our government can do nothing, by design. As Meyerson says:

Americans angered by the failures of our political system should be angered at the failures of our governmental system as well. The problem isn’t that we’re too democratic. It’s that we’re not democratic enough.

We certainly don’t care for the Congress, as polls indicate. According to Rasmussen Reports:

Ratings for Congress now match the lowest levels ever recorded and a solid plurality continue to believe that most Members of Congress are corrupt.

I’d love to start over. Agreed?

County roads

December 13, 2011 by davald

The squabbling continues between the county council and the executive. Though he approved most of the council’s budget, executive Aaron Reardon vetoed a $5-per-year increase in property taxes to pay for roads. The council is expected to override the veto.

The Herald quoted Reardon:

“I do not believe now is the time to raise our residents’ property taxes.”

This is silly posturing.

Assume, for the moment, that the council believes that road improvement and expansion are important, if not also necessary, to facilitate safe transportation in the unincorporated areas. Let’s also assume that residents are asked how much they’d be willing to contribute to the county’s road fund. Would they really balk at $5 a year, or $0.42/month? Would they find this increase an unbearable burden?

The Herald:

Councilman Dave Gossett devoted several meetings during the past year to scrutinizing the executive’s long-term roads planning. He has noted that about a third of all road and bridge projects in the county’s six-year plan have either been delayed or cancelled.

If you’ve ever traveled the county’s roads, and you don’t have to go too far out, you’ll encounter stretches of hair-raising danger. One assumes huge risks driving a car along, say, 67th, which connects Marysville to Arlington. There are no shoulders, and the adjacent drop off has got to be at least ten feet. Make the slightest turn of the steering wheel and one could quickly plunge into a deep ditch, causing all sorts of injuries, even death.

A few months ago a group of Marysville Getchel High School students were on their way from school to cross-country practice at Marysville Pilchuck High School. The driver appeared to have been traveling too fast for the conditions. A consulting traffic engineer determined that the safe speed was no more than 18 mph, although the road is signed for 35 mph. The Herald:

[Juan] Mendoza was behind the wheel of a 1997 Honda Civic, headed west on 108th, just before 2:30 p.m. when the car left the roadway, went down an embankment and hit the trees. The road is well traveled by students from both Marysville Getchell and Marysville-Pilchuck high schools.

The road, which I’ve traveled, is incredibly steep. Moving east to west, as were the boys, by simply letting one’s car coast yields speeds much higher than 35 mph—more like 50 or so.

The son of a former Marysville school board member sustained massive injuries that may leave him permanently disabled. His family has filed a $70 million suit against the county for failure to build and maintain a safe road. The Herald:

The claim states that [Lars] Kundu suffered brain and spinal injuries in the crash, as well as broken bones. He has returned home but has been unable to attend school, said his mother, Nola Kundu. He has been receiving home tutoring for a math class but has been unable to continue taking college courses through the Running Start Program.

Their attorney, Ann Deutscher, is quoted:

“It’s a roadway where they [the county] know children are going to be driving, as well as members of the community, and they have chosen to do nothing.”

I seem to recall years ago when I lived in Marysville a rumor to the effect that the county had done a cost-benefit analysis regarding the inadequate roads. County officials reckoned that it would be cheaper to pay jury awards than to make the roads safe.

Whether or not the rumor was true, that calculus is in play. If it were the other way round, we’d have safer roads.

I’d pay an extra five dollars a year to prevent death. How about you?

The Beast rams St. Louis

December 13, 2011 by davald

Pathetic, wouldn’t you say? The St. Louis Rams, sporting the worst offense in football, showed why last night. I almost felt sorry for them.

Did you catch the attempted on-side kick by former Seahawk Josh Brown? It failed to travel the obligatory ten yards. To whom was he directing his bile? There were no mirrors present.

Joe Montana had spindly legs. But Sam Bradford’s are even punier. It’s as if a lineman could blow on them to break a fibula.

The folks up in the ESPN booth let the world know what they thought of the St. Louis ineptitude. Jon Gruden proclaimed that the Rams couldn’t score a touchdown in a “double-header.” All three—Gruden, Mike Tirico, and Ron Jaworski—could hardly wait for next Monday’s matchup: Pittsburgh at San Francisco.

Go, 49ers.

Tell a good story

December 12, 2011 by davald

Judy Lightfoot tells us about Elizabeth Warren, who is running against incumbent Scott Brown to represent Massachusetts in the Senate. Warren is offering a simple, truth-filled narrative about the Great Divergence. Lightfoot:

Warren told the story, which is becoming familiar around the country, in Seattle Friday night before a crowd of people with $40 tickets to a joint fundraiser with Sen. Maria Cantwell at the Paramount Theatre. The story begins in the Great Depression, Warren said, “a dark point in America’s history,” a colossal bust in the boom-and-bust cycle that had caused panics, bank crashes, and the destruction of ordinary American lives and livelihoods every 15 years or so since the 1790s.

But then “America said we can do better,” Warren said. We instituted FDIC insurance, banking rules, and the SEC to enforce those rules. We also “invested in ourselves and built America’s middle class” by putting money into education K-through-grad school (“for every dollar we put in, we got five dollars back in taxes”) plus into infrastructure like highways, transit, a power grid, and “research, research, research.” Thus “our country got richer, and families got richer right along with us. It worked for more than half a century.”

Sadly, the story goes, about 30 years ago we lost our way. A deregulated financial industry and unfair taxation have “staggered America’s middle class,” said Warren. “G.E., a profitable company with worldwide operations, pays zero taxes.” At the same time, we starve the infrastructure that our millionaires depended on for growing their enterprises, “and we tell young people ‘You’re going to have to take on debt to get an education.’ ”

Pass it on.

Tidbits prompted by Harper’s

December 12, 2011 by davald

I’m rarely disappointed in Harper’s Index, which extracts the bizarre from the merely mundane. Here’s one from the January 2012 issue:

Percentage of all Americans who consider themselves part of the top 1 percent of U.S. earners: 13

Have you gotten yourself off the floor yet? Using 2006 data, one would need an income of at least $104,000 to be in the top ten percent of earners. To reach the coveted one percent, you’d have to make over $375,000 a year. It follows that the top 13 percent threshold is somewhere south of $100,000, a far cry from the nearly $400,000 to be a true One-percenter.

Here’s another tidbit:

Percentage of workers with only a high school diploma who received employer-based health insurance in 1979: 70

Percentage of workers with a bachelor’s degree or higher who receive it today: 66

You see, the Great Divergence yielded lower benefits for the Rest of Us, along with lower wages.

This makes eerie sense, when you think about it:

Minimum number of weapons confiscated from visitors to the Statue of Liberty last year: 11,448

Get it? Liberty = freedom—to bear arms. The uniquely American take on the notion of liberty.

For a sign of the new military, consider this:

Amount the Defense Department has requested for new Gray Eagle and Reaper drones and parts in 2012: $1,908,600,000

That’s right, almost two trillion dollars—for drones. Gosh, the proposed 2012 budget for federal education spending is a bit more than $121 billion. Another example of U.S. priorities.

I’ll leave you with one more:

Date on which Obama said that donations from lobbyists “don’t contribute to the public interest”: 8/4/2007

Minimum amount Obama’s 2012 campaign has so far accepted from bundlers connected to lobbyists: $2,800,000

America, love it or leave it.
