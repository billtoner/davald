That extra twist

September 15, 2011 by davald

The Mariners’ Jason Vargas pitched a gem last night. It was his second superb outing in a row, after a string of big-time losses. What’s the difference?

Well, it seems that Vargas has twisted his torso a bit more toward second base, pausing a bit, then driving toward the plate. His fast ball has picked up some velocity as a result, and it appears that batters are having a more difficult time picking up the ball. Combined with excellent location of his pitches, Vargas has actually dominated the hitters—for two games, anyway.

I can relate to the twist.

I got the nod many, many years ago to face Santa Clara at Cal’s Evans Field. Santa Clara always had a good baseball team, stocked with hitting and pitching. The afternoon lacked an auspicious beginning. No matter what I threw it was shellacked. Fortunately, the line drives were either foul or right at Cal fielders.

Now I had a rather bad habit of experimenting with my delivery, often depending on what pitcher I had watched on TV. On that afternoon, realizing that nothing was working, it occurred to me in the third inning, as I recall, to adopt the motion of a pitching mate, who happened to be left handed, just like Vargas. Mike Baldwin, the teammate, had an exaggerated twist and pause, which yielded remarkable results. Why not copy?

Remember, this is Berkeley. I was immediately transported into “the zone” after aping Baldwin’s delivery. I went from whack to whiff. Santa Clara could no longer hit my stuff. Fast ball? Swing-and-a-miss. Curve ball? Forget about it.

Perhaps it’s like fishing reports, but I believe that I completed the game with a one-hit shutout. I doubt that there are any extant records to discredit my recollection. You’ll just have to trust me on this.

It’s all relative

September 15, 2011 by davald

Both the New York Times and the Washington Post have been running articles on wealth and poverty. The Post has focused on the widening gap between the very wealthy and the Rest of Us. As I’ve written, the Great Divergence began in the 70s during the reign of Nixon. The Times has told us that the poor are more plentiful and getting poorer as a result of the Great Recession.

There are some on the right who are quick to argue that today’s poor are much better off than even the non-poor of previous generations. In absolute terms, yes. But it’s all relative.

That is, today’s poor have much less wealth than today’s rich—astoundingly so. That deep chasm turns out to be important for the wellbeing of all of us.

Offering a diagnosis

September 14, 2011 by davald

Yesterday I linked to a column written by a professor of economics. He told his readers that the Keynesians are wrong in that they cite reduced demand as a cause of the current crisis. People aren’t spending enough to induce more production and, therefore, more jobs. The economist wrote that reduced demand is a symptom. The causes are yet to be determined.

Well, guess who offered a theory on causes? Karl Marx.

Now, before you go apeshit, try to read this post that nicely sums up the reasons for the bad economy, in the opinion of two economists, one being Marx the other being Nouriel Roubini.

Roubini told the Wall Street Journal:

Karl Marx had it right. At some point, capitalism can destroy itself. You cannot keep on shifting income from labor to capital without having an excess capacity and a lack of aggregate demand. That’s what has happened. We thought that markets worked. They’re not working. The individual can be rational. The firm, to survive and thrive, can push labor costs more and more down, but labor costs are someone else’s income and consumption. That’s why it’s a self-destructive process.

This makes intuitive sense, when you think about it. It is the nature of the firm to maximize profits—the difference between revenues and expenditures. Firms can’t set their own prices. Therefore, the firm strives to reduce costs, usually by increasing productivity. Either it incorporates technological solutions or it lowers its labor expenses, or both. In an economy constrained by reduced demand, firms typically shed jobs. That’s happening now, which further depresses aggregate demand.

Writ large, a firm’s desire to keep its employees’ wages down is self-defeating. Henry Ford understood as much.

The Great Divergence means too many unproductive dollars; they’re hoarded rather than spent. If the Rest of Us had higher incomes we’d spend the money on goods and services, which keeps firms busy and thriving and workers employed. A virtuous rather than vicious cycle.

QED

On the other side of the pond

September 13, 2011 by davald

Each day brings news of European troubles, influencing stock markets around the globe. In Europe, as in the US, there are disputes over basics, from how we got into this mess (and Europe is in a mess) to what steps can and should be taken to recover.

On these matters, I really enjoyed Matthew Yglesias’s perspicacious gloss of the Eurozone fundamentals and what might be done. Take a look. As always, depressing stuff, so forewarned.

The combination of monetary union, hard money thinking at the ECB, and refusal to engage in substantial fiscal transfers is a business class wet dream. You have asymmetrical mobility—capital, goods, and high-end professional labor are super mobile while working class labor is largely fixed. With exchange rates fixed and the central bank inflexible, the only way to respond to macroeconomic shocks is to cut wages and social welfare benefits. They’ve created an institutional setting under which not only is the nominally socialist government of Spain pursuing a hard-right agenda of austerity and low wages, but they are correct to do so not because an austerity and low wage agenda is a good thing but because the institutional environment of Europe leaves them with no alternative.

So we’re basically stuck. The story of the west for the past 150 years or so has been the parallel development of capitalist markets and democratic politics. The E.U. has constructed a setting in which not only have markets escaped the bounds of democratic politics, but also one wherein strengthening the political forces at the center itself counts as an elitist anti-populist project. This is nothing smart people on the European left aren’t aware of and thinking about, but very little headway was made before the crisis and now, again, the practical business of crisis-management is being carried out entirely as a discourse between two different elite agendas.

Another example of why economics is not a science

September 13, 2011 by davald

Via Matthew Yglesias we get this spittle from an academic economist. He’s actually a professor at George Mason University in Virginia.

The ease with which he disses Nobel-Prize winners astonishes. I mean, who the hell is Donald Boudreux? He writes:

This Keynesian explanation is adolescent. Lazily identifying the symptom as the underlying problem, Keynesians then craft a “theory” that shows just how inadequate spending can in fact cause inadequate spending. How clever of them!

It’s understandable that many people untutored in economics fall for this nonsense. Just as many untutored in geography naturally think the Earth is flat (looks that way, doesn’t it?), many untutored in economics, upon seeing businesses closing up and workers being laid off, conclude that the problem is inadequate spending (looks that way, doesn’t it?).

Well, as Yglesias points out, such luminaries as Krugman, Stiglitz, Summers, and even Milton Friedman fell “for this nonsense.” Which brings me to a larger discussion.

Economists desperately crave recognition as scientists. Yet, the fact that they cannot agree on fundamental theories or, gosh, facts, should deeply disturb the Rest of Us. We’re hurting, and the profession is arguing the equivalent of how many angels can dance on a pin.

UPDATE (Sep. 13, 2011):

As I was saying…

Via Noahopinion I found this piece apropos of the above. I should apologize, on one hand, for my broadside against economists. The author, “Sean,” tells us that the social sciences are “harder” than the natural sciences. I agree, in this respect: economists or sociologists or anthropologists have difficult research subjects, namely us. We are a confounding species, subject to both passion and reason. Thus, we are not all that predictable. Or at least that’s what the economists are discovering.

On the other hand, I’d encourage social scientists to be much more humble and circumspect in their opinions. And I really don’t care how much math you have in your model; you’re still stuck with homo sapiens.

Sean writes:

So my advice to economists who want more respect from the outside world would be: make it much more clear to the non-expert public that you have a reliable, agreed-upon set of non-obvious discoveries that your field has made about the world. People have tried to lay out such discoveries, of course — but upon closer inspection they don’t quite measure up to Newton’s Laws in terms of reliability and usefulness.

You know, we’re in really deep do-do.

The Great Divergence and the withering of empathy

September 13, 2011 by davald

We are, of course, entitled to our own opinions. Some might go further to argue that we’re equally entitled to our own facts, which belies ignorance of the the term ‘fact.’ However, it’s always been the case, and seemingly more pronounced in these times, that passions rule reason, as Hume suggested. In other words, what we may lack between our ears is more than offset by the quantity of our guts.

In response to my recent post on “So Why is America So Great?,” a reader offered these words:

Okay, best national defense in the history of earth.  That’s a plus, though it is a bloated bureaucracy.

This is obfuscation.  The issue is medical care, not health care.  There’s more MRI machines in Philadelphia than in all of Canada and England . . . combined.

The richest (owned houses, cars tv’s, cable, autos, electronics, health) and fattest “poor” in the history of humankind, and with a standard of living higher than the average (not “poor”) person in, say, Paris or Rome or Madrid.

The rich keep doing what made them rich and the poor (the few who remain such for as much as a decade) keep doing what made them poor.

My congratulations and admiration to the billionaires.  keep up the good work.

I really don’t know what to make of this. But it’s clear that the writer and I live on different moral planets. It also seems as if presidential candidate Ron Paul lives on the same planet as the commenter.

Last night emcee Wolf Blitzer asked the congressman, “Are you saying that society should just let him [a quite sick man near death who opted not to buy private health insurance] die?” Several members of the audience yelled out, “Yes!”

Despite having studied several years’ worth of philosophy, I would not expect to put forth an argument that would persuade Mr. Paul and the commenter to change their minds. Rather, I suspect that we either have empathy or we don’t. It’s not so much a matter of the gut as a function of two other factors: evolutionary biology and early childhood development.

I read the Wikipedia entry on empathic concern. Consider this excerpt from the article:

Human beings are strongly motivated to be connected to others.[4] In humans and higher mammals, an impulse to care for offspring is almost certainly genetically hard-wired, although modifiable by circumstance.

To be fair to Congressman Paul, in answering the question he recounted his professional medical practice and charitable works. He implied that he cares about others. But he also said something to the effect that freedom means assuming personal responsibility for your own welfare and not relying on “socialism.”

One common thread that runs through all of the Republican candidates for president is abhorrence for governmental programs, especially public social programs like Medicare, “Obamacare,” and even Social Security, although there seems to be some retrenchment from the latter after Texas governor Rick Perry called it a “lie” and “a Ponzi scheme.” And while Ron Paul is decidedly libertarian in his views, which places him a bit more out there than others in his cohort, he believes with his competitors that government should keep its hands off the private sector, which brings me back to my commenter and his praise of billionaires.

I happen to think that highly unequal societies—and the U.S. is at or near the top of this category—are unhealthy societies. There is considerable research to support this view. At the forefront of this research is Prof. Richard Wilkinson of the UK. With Kate Pickett, a professor of epidemiology, he has written his latest book, entitled The Spirit Level: Why Greater Equality Makes Societies Stronger. Here’s an excerpt from the dust jacket:

Further, more unequal societies are bad for everyone within them—the rich and middle class as well as the poor.

Interesting. As it happens, inequality is bad for your health, even Mr. Paul’s and Mr. Buffett’s.

Wilkinson and Pickett show that inequality tracks with the following syndromes:

  • obesity rates
  • morbidity rates
  • mortality rates
  • and a host of psychological diseases.

Consistent readers of my posts know that I focus on inequality. For example, I recently wrote about what I call the ‘Great Divergence.’ It began in the 7os and has continued unabated, enlarging the gap between the extremely rich and the Rest of Us. (Coincidentally, the Washington Post has begun a series on inequality, replete with charts and graphs.)

I am deeply disturbed by the brazen selfishness of the Tea Party, as evinced by members of the debate audience. Likewise, all of the Republican presidential hopefuls exhibit what I believe to be a perverse disregard for the wellbeing of others. We will recall that at the previous Republican debate some in the audience cheered governor Perry’s unseemly record of state executions.

Robert Reich, now teaching at my alma mater, talks about America being two societies. One of the very rich and the other populated by the Rest of Us. (He also wrote the foreword to the Wilkinson book.) The Great Divergence may have already reached a point of no return. The very rich and their odd supporters have become increasingly inured to the misfortune of others, no doubt for a very simple reason: they have no exposure to the Rest of Us. There is no overlapping of these disparate societies. Yes, we know of the rich. We see them on TV and in magazines. But they do not, perhaps cannot, know us.

And that’s a problem, because the rich have all the money, and money translates into political power, which is put to the further service of the rich, and so on. Yet, rather than inducing a revolution we get “admiration.”

I don’t get it.
