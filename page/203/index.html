Predictive theories

August 22, 2011 by davald

Milton Friedman, of all people, argued that the value of an economic theory lay in its ability to predict human behavior. That is, what would people, in the aggregate, likely do under particular circumstances. This is somewhat ironic to the extent that some of Friedman’s own theories fail to account for what the heck is going on today.

Today, of course, we’re in a mess, arguing over what, if anything, can and should be done about it. One intrepid economist, whom I often cite, appears to have been prescient about not only the diagnosis but also the cure.

Paul Krugman was recently asked by a few readers whether he had anticipated the protracted economic stagnation. In particular, did he express concerns over the efficacy of Obama’s stimulus package. He linked to three separate columns he penned for the New York Times.

The first prior assessment was made in early January 2009, a couple of weeks before Obama assumed office. He wrote:

But Mr. Obama’s prescription doesn’t live up to his diagnosis. The economic plan he’s offering isn’t as strong as his language about the economic threat. In fact, it falls well short of what’s needed.

Bear in mind just how big the U.S. economy is. Given sufficient demand for its output, America would produce more than $30 trillion worth of goods and services over the next two years. But with both consumer spending and business investment plunging, a huge gap is opening up between what the American economy can produce and what it’s able to sell.

Krugman believed that the stimulus package was only a third of what was needed to close the output gap. That is, instead of $800 billion (nearly half of which was in the form of tax cuts, which are not all that stimulative), Obama should have proposed and Congress should have adopted a measure of almost $2.5 trillion. Even then, it seems, despite Democrats controlling both houses, Obama was seeking a pragmatic, bipartisan solution. He and his political advisors thought that any package had to be less than a trillion dollars and much of it had to be tax cuts in order to woo Republicans.

His second and third previous columns are more of the same, although he ups the amount the stimulus should have been to almost $3 trillion.

But there was one point he made that caught my attention back then, in early 2009. Krugman wrote:

So here’s the picture that scares me: It’s September 2009, the unemployment rate has passed 9 percent, and despite the early round of stimulus spending it’s still headed up. Mr. Obama finally concedes that a bigger stimulus is needed.

But he can’t get his new plan through Congress because approval for his economic policies has plummeted, partly because his policies are seen to have failed, partly because job-creation policies are conflated in the public mind with deeply unpopular bank bailouts. And as a result, the recession rages on, unchecked.

O.K., that’s a warning, not a prediction. But economic policy is falling behind the curve, and there’s a real, growing danger that it will never catch up.

Well, that’s precisely what has happened, although Obama has never come forward to propose further stimulus, especially after the Republicans “shellacked” the Democrats in 2010. Worse, Obama sold the actual package on the promise that it would reduce unemployment and spur the economy.

He’s cooked his own goose with several different recipes.

In praise of cities

August 22, 2011 by davald

After spending a majority of our lives in the suburbs, my wife and I moved downtown. No, it’s not Paris or Manhattan. It’s Everett, barely a bump on the urban scale.

We had grown weary of having to drive cars everywhere and spending weekends pulling the damn weeds. Besides, who needs all that space?

Now we walk: I to work; both of us to restaurants, the library, to the occasional Aqua Sox game. No more weeds.

Scientific American devotes a special issue to the city—the good, the bad, a bit of the ugly. But more important, argues the magazine, cities could be so much more, if they were given a level playing field.

In matters of housing, education,transportation, the environment and social services, existing rules and spending priorities give cities a raw deal. Cheap gas, highway subsides, tax incentives for home ownership, complacency over urban education and the apportionment of legislators all give preferential treatment to suburbs and rural areas. Even national leaders who should be cheerleaders for an evenhanded urban policy have faltered. Barack Obama, the most urban president since Theodore Roosevelt, skewed the stimulus bill toward more dollars for rural America. The five least populated states got twice as much money per capita as the rest.

The magazine’s editors believe that cities must be our future, because sprawl is unsustainable, both economically and environmentally. They write:

The basic issue is fairness. Why should government policy favor owning over renting, driving over mass transit, or kids in one school district over another? The current incentives encourage people to settle in the outskirts when they might otherwise prefer to live downtown—a bias that makes little sense even when you leave out its environmental costs. And those costs are enormous. To keep our carbon emissions in check, we will need to edge closer to our neighbors. From the perspective both of simple fairness and of rational, science-based public policy, eliminating the incentives for citizens to spread out should be our goal.

Some more inconvenient facts

August 20, 2011 by davald

It has become the standard mantra of conservatives that the economy is in the doldrums because of excessive federal spending. Hewing to this line serves as a narrative glue binding the different shades of the GOP, twinned with a pledge not to raise taxes, even should the ratio of cuts to revenues exceed 10-to-1.

Now no one ever accused Republicans of letting facts get in their way. Indeed, they can yelp whoopers ’til the frogs cease to croak, then simply ignore or slough off the gaffes should a proximate sentient being mention the error. However, the Rest of Us should not shirk from our responsibility to record the blunders and, if possible, dissuade the weak from following the idiots down the dark holes of ignorance.

About that spending (chart from the St. Louis Fed):

 

Writing for ProPublica Michael Grabell does more service to our cause (how presumptuous) by dispelling several myths that are also part of the GOP’s narrative glue. He throws a few glancing blows at mainstream Democrats on the stimulus’ effects, for balance’s sake, I guess.

But I must take issue with his comment on the belief that the stimulus should have been bigger. He calls this “a red herring.” Well, no. There are many competent economists who argue that, given the size of the housing bubble collapse, among other factors, the stimulus really should have been much larger. I emphasize ‘should,’ which is different from ‘could.’ Yes, the politics were horrible, and it’s clear that Obama refused to accept the advice of his own economic advisors, Christine Romer being one, to push a larger stimulus package. So, it’s doubtful that a divided Congress would have acceded, as Grabell points out. That does not detract from the argument that the package should have been larger.

It’s become more obvious with each report on the dismal economy that Congress and Obama were wrong. I’d go further.

It was not only the puny size of the stimulus that failed to overcome the recessionary dynamics. It was also its limited duration.

Economic players, both consumers and producers, crave stability and predictability. Had Congress built in an indefinite stimulus, keyed to obvious metrics (e.g., the unemployment rate), the economy, I would argue, would be recovering much faster. Once growth returned, ease back on the stimulus. (Sweden does this.)

Yes, there is a time for austerity. Keynes said that it was appropriate during “boom” cycles, but hardly during recessionary periods, such as we’re in now. Austerity only makes matters worse.

In retrospect, at least, it was a humungous mistake to cut taxes, as Bush did. Launching two wars—without raising revenues to pay for them—added to the burden. The debt, which had been growing sharply since Reagan’s tax cuts, was being paid down during the Clinton administration and headed toward an actual surplus. (Graph from the Washington Post)

By the way, notice how little the stimulus (2009 Recovery Act) contributed to the problem. The tax cuts ($1.7 trillion) and the two wars ($1.3 trillion) are responsible for a huge chunk of the current deficits.

The solutions to our fiscal problems, as I’ve suggested previously, are relatively simple in concept, however difficult to achieve in this political cauldron:

  1. end the Bush-era tax cuts (better yet, get them back to the pre-Reagan days)
  2. end the wars
  3. invest in public infrastructure (e.g., fix dilapidated schools, non-automobile transportation sector)
  4. adopt Medicare for all
  5. eliminate sprawl-inducing subsidies (e.g., mortgage deductions, oil subsidies)

Piece of cake, I say.

A simple remedy

August 19, 2011 by davald

For many of us, the problems we currently face are easily remedied. What’s in the way is not a lack of proper ideas but political will. Indeed, we have at least one party intent on throwing the entire economy under the bus rather than see another Obama term.

Writing for the London Review of Books professor Corey Robin offers a serious yet familiar solution to what ails us. He writes:

Had politics not intruded and had Obama heeded the advice of centre-left economists [among them, his own advisors and Prof. Krugman], these are the steps he might have taken. First, do nothing about the debt, at least not now. The debt can be significantly reduced only if the economy improves. The best way to encourage that is for the government to spend, which will add to the debt in the short term but reduce it in the long term through revenues generated by growth. Second, once the economy is healthy, increase taxes, particularly on the wealthy. As a share of GDP, revenues are at their lowest levels since 1950. With the exception of a brief interregnum in the late 1980s and early 1990s, top marginal rates are the lowest they’ve been since 1931. Corporate taxes in the US are the lowest of any OECD country. The notion that taxes shouldn’t be raised, not only to fund necessary and desirable expenditure but also to cut the debt, runs counter to common sense. Last, cut military spending. As the economic journalist Doug Henwood has observed, if the US simply returned to the spending levels of 2000 – when ‘the Pentagon didn’t have to hold bake sales’ and spending was 3.7 per cent of GDP as opposed to the current 5.4 per cent – it could save $3.6 trillion in the next decade, 72 per cent more than the debt-ceiling deal negotiated by Obama and Congress will save.

But, as he condemns the measures taken by Bush II, Robin excoriates Obama, who, he believes, holds much responsibility for prolonging a stagnant economy. It was Bush, as Robin reminds us, who cut taxes and launched two wars on borrowed money. Instead of paying off the massive U.S. debt, as would have occurred by merely perpetuating the Clinton policies, Bush added more to it than any president in history. As for Obama’s failures, here’s Robin:

Instead, Obama and Congress took the opposite path, which was paved 40 years ago by the anti-tax philosophy of the American right. In February 2010, Obama convened a bipartisan commission to balance the budget by 2015, effectively making debt reduction a top priority. After the November midterm election, when the Republicans took back the House with the help of the Tea Party, Obama froze the pay of federal workers and endorsed a more aggressive austerity programme. Cuts were proposed and tax increases dismissed: not just once (with the extension of the Bush tax cuts in December) but twice (with the first phase of the debt deal, which eliminates $900 billion solely through spending cuts) and now potentially a third time (with the second phase of the deal, which eliminates an additional $1.2 trillion solely through spending cuts if a congressional committee can’t produce an alternative package of tax increases and spending cuts by November). While the deal does include defence cuts – though it’s unclear if these are cuts or simply slower rates of growth, and whether and how most of them will happen – Obama’s latest comments, and those of his defence secretary, suggest they could be traded for benefit cuts.

Robin considers the widely divergent interpretations of Obama’s character, giving partial credence to all, while ultimately dismissing them as almost beside the point. These, too, are familiar to readers: Obama’s “preternatural aversion to conflict”; the now-evident fundamental flaws in our federal political system; and that Obama is not the progressive that many had hoped for.

However, the current problems, Robin suggests, should be viewed within a wider, historical context. Populations facing similar situations—war and associated fiscal crises—”have catalysed politically progressive advances and even precipitated revolutions.” The focus has now shifted to the few genuine progressives among us who allowed the present tragedy to unfold over decades. Robin concludes:

And here Democrats like Obama and his defenders, who bemoan the stranglehold of the Tea Party on American politics, have only themselves to blame. For decades, Democrats have collaborated in stripping back the American state in the vain hope that the market would work its magic. For a time it did, though mostly through debt; workers could compensate for stagnating wages with easy credit and low-interest mortgages. Now the debt’s due to be repaid, and wages – if people are lucky enough to be working – aren’t enough to cover the bills. The only thing that’s left for them is cutting taxes. And the imperialism of the peasants.

That last phrase, by the way, comes straight from Karl Marx.

Who needs whom?

August 19, 2011 by davald

I was recently treated to an email exchange that seems to have ended in a vitriolic rant against “the system.” The rest of the posts echoed a similar theme: it really doesn’t matter whom one votes for, the result will be the same—bad for us and very good for the well-heeled. The solution, as far as I can divine from the exchange, is to forget about electoral politics and concentrate on building a better future with like-minded individuals voluntarily providing the necessities of life to the self-ostracized group. Or something like that.

One of the exchangers linked to a webpage. The author announced that “we don’t need them.” “Them” being the rich and powerful and all who serve their interests, including both political parties, the corporate media, and the millions of voters who enable their hold on the Rest of Us.

As I write this I’m in front of my computer display with NORAD-like processors, hard drives, and so on. I’m surrounded by the accoutrements of our modern, technological economy. In other words, I benefit directly from “the system,” as much as I, too, would like to start all over. So, it’s rather hypocritical of me to bash that which has satisfied my needs as well as several wants.

That said, I wholeheartedly concur with the assessment that it really doesn’t seem to matter who sits in the Oval Office and the seats of Congress. The Rest of Us get screwed in a very bipartisan fashion. Instead of apologizing for our elected candidates or, pray tell, seeking to improve the system from within, the apparently more rational approach would be to first declare your independence from “them”—after all, who needs them?—then work with fellow travelers to live autonomously, freed from the vast, powerful tentacles of the military-industrial-political complex.

What might this alternative future look like? One of the posters cites Noam Chomsky. Asked that very question, Prof. Chomsky reportedly responded: “I don’t know how it will look, but it will be different. It will be up to you and on a local level what meets your needs. It will look different all over, but it will be different from what it is now.”

I must now confess that I’m either too old, too tired, or both. I lack the will and the energy to create my own world apart from “the system”—even with a little help from my friends.

On the other hand, we might try to understand that the rich and the powerful need us, even should we decide that we no longer need them. We can imagine what would happen should a critical mass of the Rest of Us withhold our time, energy, and skills from “the system.”

Well, it’s actually happening right now. It’s called unemployment, and it’s hardly voluntary.

The global economy, as it turns out, needs us minions to buy its products and services. If we no longer can, because we lack adequately paying jobs, then the rich and the powerful worry. They lose confidence. They decline to invest. They accumulate cash, then sit on it.

It took the Great Depression to effectively rattle “the system,” resulting in the most expansive government in our nation’s history. The New Deal programs, had they progressed, just might have morphed into the social democratic societies enjoyed by our counterparts across the pond. But something got in the way. Something that took people’s minds off building a better society. Something that finally got us to work.

That something was, of course, World War II. And that was no accident.

Rebound

August 18, 2011 by davald

Suppose that energy conservation actually increases overall energy consumption, what some call the “rebound effect.” In the mid-18th century, British economist William Jevons suggested as much in his much praised book The Coal Question. His straightforward argument: increased energy efficiency yields lower unit costs of energy, which, in turn, induce greater consumption via supply-and-demand principles. This has come to be known as the Jevons Paradox.

Jevons focused on coal and emerging technology that improved energy output relative to inputs. We can imagine a mill owner relying on steam power for manufacture. A new and improved steam-making machine would require fewer units of coal, the basic fuel of Victorian England. The coal-cost component of his product, then, declines. Does the mill owner boost production and thereby increase his purchase of coal? That was Jevons’ conclusion, based on his own observations at the time.

More recently David Owens wrote a piece for the New Yorker magazine (subscription required). He resurrected the Jevons Paradox in the context of energy conservation achieved through constantly improving technology. Here is an excerpt from the abstract:

The problem with efficiency gains is that we inevitably reinvest them in additional consumption. Paving roads reduces rolling friction, thereby boosting miles per gallon, but it also makes distant destinations seem closer, thereby enabling people to live in sprawling, energy-gobbling subdivisions far from where they work and shop.

Owens cited a statistic: the U.S. now uses as much electricity just to cool buildings as the country consumed for all end-uses in 1955. He concluded that good intentions lead to hellish consequences.

But were Jevons and Owens right? It depends, as we’ll soon see.

I know a little bit about the Snohomish County Public Utility District, since I worked there as a policy analyst and now serve on its board of commissioners. I therefore have direct access to lots of data. Keep in mind that the PUD is achieving more conservation than ever before, while its retail rate has remained fairly stable in real terms.

If Jevons and Owens’s conclusion obtained, then we would expect that customers would use more electricity, both individually and in aggregate, as efficiency measures proliferated. Efficiency, according to Jevons and Owens, would result in lower unit costs and thus increased consumption. More on this relationship below.

So let’s take a look at the PUD’s historical conservation numbers. The chart below shows the average megawatts of energy saved as a result of efficiency measures.

Now, if the Jevons Paradox were in effect we would expect to see per capita consumption rise, all other things being equal. The evidence contradicts.

However, it’s true that total electricity consumption has increased. But that’s because the population also rose.

Another aspect about electricity is its price elasticity of demand, which is very low. That is, prices have to dramatically change to effect consumption. During the late 70s and early 80s, the PUD’s rates jumped upwards of 500 percent, and consumption fell—but not nearly to the same extent. Also, we can appreciate that electricity is an essential commodity and that people tend to use only what they need. It’s not as if customers will keep their lights on for longer periods just because rates might fall.

That said, the economics differ for electricity-intensive industries like aluminum smelters. The operators are more sensitive to electricity prices because electricity comprises the bulk of a smelter’s operating costs. Here, the Jevons Paradox may follow the economics of mid-19th-century coal-intensive industries.

It appears, then, that for SnoPUD customers at any rate, the steady and significant increases in energy savings has not resulted in higher consumption rates. Indeed, it seems that conservation is achieving what it’s supposed to: reduce consumption of electricity.

I’d like to speak to the matter of conservation lowering costs, which is almost a premise for Jevons. While it’s true that energy conservation is far cheaper than almost any generating resource, for utilities that deliver electricity, conservation boosts pressure on rates. Here’s why.

The rate that each of us pays the utility for a kilowatt-hour of electricity is simply the quotient of revenue requirements divided by units of electricity. Utilities, especially in the private sector, tend to do far less conservation than is potentially available because of what’s called ‘lost revenues.’ Conservation, by definition, reduces units consumed. Utilities earn their money be selling more, rather than less, electricity.

But this calculus also has a timing component. If a utility expects to need additional supplies of electricity to meet growing demand it has several options. It can build its own generating resource. It can purchase electricity from the market. Or it can develop or pursue demand-side resources, including conservation. Insofar as a saved kilowatt-hour costs less than a generated one, then the utility’s revenue requirements would be less than had it pursued one or both of the other options.*

In the PUD’s case, demand has continued to grow, as the chart above shows. The conservation savings achieved have deferred the need to acquire new generating resources and help explain both the per capita consumption drop and stable real rates.

In Owens’s New Yorker piece he argued that increased efficiency (e.g., a Prius’s gas mileage vs. a Hummer’s) would lead to increased consumption. The driver of the Prius may take longer trips, thus negating the efficiency gains. My wife and I happen to own a Prius. Yet we drive the same number of miles as before. Necessity dictates consumption of an essential commodity.

But Owens counters. While I may not drive more miles because I own a Prius, I will likely use the gas savings for other, non-vehicle purchases. Since my transportation budget shrunk I can increase, say, my entertainment spending, perhaps dining out a bit more often.

Or, and this is important I think, I could save the dollars I don’t spend on gas. Indeed, the dollars saved via efficiency need not be spent at all. They can all be deposited in banks.

One final point I should address. If not energy conservation, what then? Unless aggregate demand for energy remains flat, we’ll need new resources. Removing conservation from the available options leaves us only with generating resources: coal, natural gas, hydro, wind, solar, etc. Not only are these resources more expensive, they require transmission lines to deliver the kilowatt-hours to end users. Those, too, are expensive. And if we’re concerned about carbon emissions, we’d like to stay away from coal and natural gas.

In the end, I am not persuaded by Owens’s argument, given the PUD’s history or my own experience. Through efficiency measures I’ve deployed, including how I choose to live, both my gasoline and electricity bills are lower than they would otherwise be. Have I spent the saved dollars elsewhere? Probably. But I’ve also managed to put some money in the bank.

__________

*  The economics differ between investor-owned and consumer-owned utilities. The former are regulated by utility commissions, which must authorize rate adjustments. Also, IOUs, as they’re called, receive a return on capital investments. Thus, if they build a generating facility, they are assured a return in excess of expenditures. The publicly owned utilities are prohibited from making profits. They have no shareholders that must be compensated.

Upon further analysis…

August 17, 2011 by davald

…the economic news is worse than previously reported. That’s one takeaway from this article in the New York Times.

The Bureau of Economic Analysis renders judgments about the state of the economy based on the “best available data.” Unfortunately, the data themselves are largely projections or estimates; and too many policymakers rely on the initial BEA reports. The Times:

When the government announced in April that the economy had grown at a moderate annual pace of 1.8 percent in the first quarter, politicians and investors saw evidence that the nation was continuing its recovery from the depths of the financial crisis. The White House called the news “encouraging” and the stock market extended its bull run.

When calculating economic growth, the government makes an educated guess about spending on construction and cellphone bills. An estimate of growth in the first quarter was changed after a revision of the value of vehicles at dealerships and spending on imported oil.

Three months later, the government announced a small change. The economy, it said, actually had expanded at a pace of only 0.4 percent in the first quarter.

In other words, the initial estimate was off by -78%.

It would behoove policymakers and politicians to generally ignore the early reports, since the underlying data, as it turns out, are ephemeral and wrong. Don’t get too excited, then, whether up or down.

On the other hand, overall the news is grim, which should lead to some attempts to correct. I say “should.” But I doubt seriously that we can expect any real solutions before, let’s say, November of next year.

And speaking of entitlements…

August 15, 2011 by davald

Ironies abound. Here in Washington state voters rejected a splendid opportunity to impose an income tax on only wealthy individuals, something Oregon recently did. I scratched my head in search of answers. Was it really a fairness issue, as Seattle Times‘ columnist Danny Westneat argued on the eve of the initiative vote? Please.

In today’s New York Times we’re treated to another irony: a very rich man wants to be taxed more heavily. The Rest of Us choose to coddle the rich while some of the rich say that’s unfair—to the Rest of Us. Warren Buffett writes:

These and other blessings are showered upon us by legislators in Washington who feel compelled to protect us, much as if we were spotted owls or some other endangered species. It’s nice to have friends in high places.

Last year my federal tax bill — the income tax I paid, as well as payroll taxes paid by me and on my behalf — was $6,938,744. That sounds like a lot of money. But what I paid was only 17.4 percent of my taxable income — and that’s actually a lower percentage than was paid by any of the other 20 people in our office. Their tax burdens ranged from 33 percent to 41 percent and averaged 36 percent.

If you make money with money, as some of my super-rich friends do, your percentage may be a bit lower than mine. But if you earn money from a job, your percentage will surely exceed mine — most likely by a lot.

Buffett wants to send a message to the new super committee of twelve, which is charged with the task of reducing the federal deficit. The Republicans on the committee will surely reject any proposal to increase tax revenues. But Buffett argues otherwise:

…I would leave rates for 99.7 percent of taxpayers unchanged and continue the current 2-percentage-point reduction in the employee contribution to the payroll tax. This cut helps the poor and the middle class, who need every break they can get.

But for those making more than $1 million — there were 236,883 such households in 2009 — I would raise rates immediately on taxable income in excess of $1 million, including, of course, dividends and capital gains. And for those who make $10 million or more — there were 8,274 in 2009 — I would suggest an additional increase in rate.

My friends and I have been coddled long enough by a billionaire-friendly Congress. It’s time for our government to get serious about shared sacrifice.

Amen, Brother Warren. By the way, his taxable income last year was about $40 million.
