It’s about jobs, stupid

August 15, 2011 by davald

Will Republicans thwart every effort to create jobs? Evidently, yes, if they believe continued high unemployment rates can be blamed on Obama and the Democrats. To what end? To regain the White House and control of the Senate.

The bigger question: Will enough voters be suckered into this narrative?

Imagine Michele Bachman in the Oval Office, aided and abetted by a Republican Congress. Scary stuff.

The New York Times editorializes on jobs. The editors:

By a large margin, Americans have told pollsters that job creation is more important than budget cuts. Yet Republican leaders are wedded to austerity and appear to think that high unemployment will hurt President Obama politically more than it will hurt them, so they will likely resist efforts to create jobs, no matter how great the need.

Without more jobs, both the economy and the budget will deteriorate further. It is past time for Mr. Obama to send a jobs plan to Congress that has popular appeal, one that he can use to try to shame Republicans. He will need cooperation from the Senate, which should bring one jobs-related bill after another to the floor, forcing its members to approve jobs initiatives or go on the record to show that they just don’t care.

Going down

August 13, 2011 by davald

Conservatives steadfastly stick to their mantra of spending and tax cuts. They believe, against considerable contrary evidence, that austerity yields economic growth. In an era of surplus, maybe. But surely not now.

The data are drawn from the Bureau of Economic Analysis, which is part of the U.S. Department of Commerce. Real GDP in chained 2005 dollars is represented by the blue line. It has been steadily rising, with one major exception: the 2008-2009 recession. The reddish orange line depicts the annual change in real GDP. I’ve added a trend line (dotted black) to show the 5-year moving average of the annual change.

We’ll note that annual growth rates have steadily fallen and significantly so since Bush II entered office. The biggest decline, from January 2008 to January 2009, was -3.5 percent, the sharpest drop since the Great Depression. The drop corresponded with a dramatic reduction in tax revenues and a sharp increase in military outlays to fund two wars.

The numbers suggest that failing to collect sufficient receipts to offset spending bodes ill for economic growth.

By the way, here is the Economist magazine on what needs to be done:

In America Congress must rouse itself to provide both fiscal support in the short term—extending temporary payroll-tax cuts and unemployment insurance, and investment in infrastructure—and a credible plan to cut the deficit in the medium term through tax reform and tackling entitlements.

Where have I seen this before? Oh, that’s right, here—save for the entitlements part

Why unions matter

August 12, 2011 by davald

Over the last 50 years the union participation rate in the U.S. has steadily declined. Meanwhile, the marginal tax rate has fallen while the ratio of CEO pay to average worker wages has skyrocketed. (See chart below.)

Today, with the unemployment rate stubbornly refusing to fall below nine percent as corporate profits ascend, company managers are doing their best to ensure that workers make less, receive fewer benefits, and suffer job insecurity. In Wisconsin and a few other states thus far Republican legislatures and governors are rapidly undermining public employee unions while slashing worker benefits—all in the name of fiscal austerity.

The battle against unions and workers has entered the private arena in a big way, featuring Verizon employees. Faced with management’s proposal to gut employee benefit packages, 45,000 of Verizon’s represented workers walked off the job, in the largest labor strike in several years.

What’s at stake? For some commenters, this recent conflict has implications far beyond the Verizon workers. It may signal the beginning of a concerted corporate effort to eliminate unions altogether. The Socialist Worker writes:

THIS FIGHT is about the power of unions to protect jobs. Underlying Verizon’s attack is the same logic at work in the assault on public-sector workers. Political leaders across the country are attempting to pit taxpayers against government workers with claims that state budget crises are the result of fat pensions and paychecks of teachers and firefighters.

The magazine provides some salient facts:

  • According to the company’s propaganda, technicians make over $100,000 a year plusbenefits, but the reality is that hourly employees can only reach that level by working massive overtime–if it’s even available. The top base wage is $77,000 a year, which lags well behind profit growth, and a majority of workers earn far less.
  • “The CEO makes $55,000 a day, but they expect us to give back, when a lot us don’t even make $55,000 in a year,” said another striking worker in Manhattan.
  • Verizon is one of the giant U.S. corporations that that dodged paying any taxes at all for the last two years–and therefore contributed to government deficits at every level.
  • former Verizon CEO Ivan Seidenberg took home $81 million last year
  • Verizon’s revenues for this year are estimated at more than $108 billion. Net profits last year were $6 billion. Verizon’s top five executives alone received compensation of $258 million over the past four years.

The United States has become the most unequal society among the western industrial nations, according to OECD data. Corporate CEOs routinely take home tens of millions of dollars a year, with some hedge fund managers raking in a billion or more. Workers’ wages have stagnated or declined in real terms, as labor productivity continues to rise—companies are getting more bang for their labor buck.

It’s no surprise that the victory of the Haves correlates with the decline in union membership and the huge drop in the marginal tax rate. A famous Roman once said, “Divisa et vincere.” Our failure to stick together has made the Rest of Us easy pickings for the plutocrats.

And that’s why we need unions—now more than ever.

Add Dean Baker to your blog roll

August 12, 2011 by davald

Economist Dean Baker, one of the first to acknowledge the housing bubble then foretell the consequences of its inevitable collapse, calls out the media on its economics reporting, or lack thereof. As he scolds he also entertains, mostly with sarcasm. This morning he has much material to criticize, including this New York Times piece featuring the wisdom of former Treasury secretary Robert Rubin. Baker writes:

Rubin also was a big proponent of removing government restrictions on the financial system. He helped to push through the repeal of Glass-Steagall. He also nixed Brooksley Born’s efforts to regulate derivatives like credit default swaps.

After his years in the Clinton administration he took a top position at Citigroup where he earned over $100 million. When he left the bank in 2008 it was on government provided life support. It had been at the center of the subprime crisis, securitizing hundreds of billions of dollars of subprime mortgages.

Presenting Rubin as just a wise man who used to be Treasury Secretary, as this NYT article does, is like presenting the Unabomber as simply a man who lived in a small cabin in Montana.

I would hope, against substantial evidence to the contrary, that reporters tasked with covering economic matters would read Baker’s frequent posts. They might learn a thing or two, especially about how lousy they are at their jobs.

Those “liberals”

August 11, 2011 by davald

It’s hardly scientific, but I suspect that we can gain a sense of what types of people live in our community from the letters they write to the local newspaper. One such letter caught my attention while reading this morning’s Herald.

The writer, who lives in Arlington, was responding to a piece the newspaper ran on the proposed coal-shipping terminal near Bellingham. The idea is to transport Wyoming coal to China by rail, passing through cities like Marysville situated along the tracks. Opponents of this project, including Marysville’s mayor, object on a couple of grounds: (1) the additional train traffic; and (2) enabling the combustion of coal, the main source of greenhouse gases. (We should appreciate that it really doesn’t matter where the coal is burned; the gases and their effects become global problems.)

The writer:

With 14 million of our citizens unemployed, a stock market in steep decline, and an economy that makes a morgue look like it’s in the business of recovery, any thinker with good sense and an equal amount of goodwill should see that, far from a moral shame, coal coming from the west and through the Puget Sound is a benefit to our economy.

I noticed that not once did the writer mention global warming or greenhouse gases. Perhaps, like Senator Inhofe, he believes that it’s all a “hoax” perpetrated by the same “liberals” who oppose the coal transport. After all, “any thinker with good sense” should understand the economic benefits of mining, shipping, and burning coal. It’s all plusses with no minuses. I suppose that even the mayor of Marysville should suck it up for the economy. What’s a few extra trains, after all.

This because of that

August 11, 2011 by davald

Even casual readers of stock market reporting can’t help but be struck by both the headlines and the body text. Bloomberg News, for example, almost always writes something to the effect: “The Dow Jones fell 50 points as scientists discovered the moon is really made of cheese.” Well, not the last part, but the pattern is always there. What galls me about this kind of language is that the writers or editors believe that they know what caused x. They don’t, of course.

This morning economist Dean Baker registered a similar complaint. His headline: “The Chicago White Sox Sunday Defeat Sent Stock Market Tumbling.” He’s being sarcastic. He goes on to write:

Time to beat up on really really bad news reporting. The stock market doesn’t tell people why it does what it does. We have commentators who bloviate on what they think caused the market to rise or fall, but they don’t really know and they could be completely wrong.

Baker adds that aside from shoddy journalism such attempts to link stock market movements to a particular event muddy the policy waters.

This point is important because many political actors, including National Public Radio, are trying to use the debt downgrade as an argument for cutting Social Security and Medicare. Their argument will be furthered if they can claim that the downgrade had enormous consequences for the stock market, since so many people involved in political debates (i.e. columnists, policy wonks, reporters, congressional staffers) have substantial amounts of money invested in the stock market.

About that downgrade…

August 10, 2011 by davald

 

Paul Krugman reminds us that if investors were really concerned about the government’s willingness and ability to repay bondholders they’d be either bidding up the yields or buying something else. But as we can see from the Bloomberg chart above, investors are happy to put their money in U.S. treasuries, assigning very little risk premium.

Krugman writes:

But here’s the thing: it wasn’t just the WSJ. Pundits, Very Serious Politicians, and more have spent the past two years plus doing everything they can to make the deficit the center of public discourse, to focus all our fears on the attack by bond vigilantes that was supposedly just over the horizon.

And now it turns out that what really terrifies the markets, let alone the suffering unemployed, is the prospect of a second Great Depression — a prospect that has become much more likely thanks to the utter wrongness of elite policy priorities.

Great work, guys.

Politics, as usual

August 10, 2011 by davald

Robert Reich:

Which gets me to the President. Even though the President’s two former top economic advisors (Larry Summers and Christy Roemer) have called for a major fiscal boost to the economy, the President has remained mum. Why?

I’m told White House political operatives are against a bold jobs plan. They believe the only jobs plan that could get through Congress would be so watered down as to have almost no impact by Election Day. They also worry the public wouldn’t understand how more government spending in the near term can be consistent with long-term deficit reduction. And they fear Republicans would use any such initiative to further bash Obama as a big spender.

So rather than fight for a bold jobs plan, the White House has apparently decided it’s politically wiser to continue fighting about the deficit. The idea is to keep the public focused on the deficit drama – to convince them their current economic woes have something to do with it, decry Washington’s paralysis over fixing it, and then claim victory over whatever outcome emerges from the process recently negotiated to fix it. They hope all this will distract the public’s attention from the President’s failure to do anything about continuing high unemployment and economic anemia.

If the nominal leader of the Democratic Party says essentially the same things as the opposition, then the horrible mess we’re in will likely last a very long time. Think Great Depression and Japan.
