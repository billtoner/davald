Creeping Comcast [u]

March 11, 2013 by davald

I just received my latest bill from Comcast. It is just over $300, about $20 more than the previous month, and at least $100 more than one from last year. What happened?

My basic, albeit bundled, TV package is about half the total. Then come the ad-ons.

I’ve got “Extreme 50” Internet, which provides a consistent 61 Mbs download speed. I’m okay with this, though I should be content with something slower and cheaper. It’s one of my few vices, fast Internet, along with an occasional pastry and a shot or two of good Scotch. With recent technical problems, I fell for the “Signature Support” option, at $9.95 a month. Peace of mind, I guess. I’ve also been paying about $4 for a “service protection plan,” which would seem to cover the same thing.

So, why did I order Streampix and the Sports Entertainment package? They cost me $5 and $10 a month, respectively. I have yet to access either one.

I’ve got to do some trimming. Yet, try to delete services online. Can’t be done. You have to call. And guess what? As long as I’m prepared to hold for “at least 15 minutes” (which would likely run much, much longer), I might eventually succeed. But I don’t want to wait 15 mins. After all, I could easily add services via the Internet. Why not be given the opportunity to cancel them in equal fashion?

I’ll “chat” with a customer representative. Ah, there’s the fine print: can’t be used to delete services. How about writing a letter? There’s no address given to accomplish that.

It occurs to me that Comcast makes it so easy to add and so difficult to subtract. By design, I’m sure.

I’ll cut the cord. With my Apple TV I have Netflix and Hulu+, the latter delivering quite a bit of day-old content with commercials. But Hulu doesn’t carry all the networks, for sure. I could also buy TV series via iTunes, including Game of Thrones and Homeland, which I have watched religiously.

Sports. That’s the final hook. Opening day of MLB is just around the corner, as are March Madness and the NBA finals. In the fall, I’ve got football and basketball. Tough to give those up.

On the other hand, if I make no changes I’ll wind up paying $3,600 for TV, fast Internet, and a landline I rarely use. This must be America, right? Or, I’m just another big fool who got caught up in “creeping Comcast.”

UPDATE (Mar. 11, 2013):

Okay, I was too impatient. I just needed to push a different option in calling Comcast to get me to the right person. It took me just a few moments to cancel the stuff I don’t use. But the bill is still way too high.

Ad hominems abound

March 11, 2013 by davald

Suppose you publish a fact-based column that describes America’s unequal society. You’ve cobbled together statistics, read a few academic reports, then fashioned a descriptive narrative incorporating the data. At the end of your piece you write something like this:

Things may have to get worse before change happens. One thing is clear: Our situation is unsustainable and un-American.

Perhaps after your column appears you sit back hoping for reasonable reaction. You would be wrong.

Jon Talton dared to point out the obvious: A few people in the U.S. make a whole lot of money while most have seen no increase in real wages or even marked declines in their incomes. Instead of beginning a dialogue he essentially opened the door to the loonies, who seem to be both plentiful and abusive.

Here’s Talton:

If you’re a hedge-fund manager, software engineer or elite surgeon, good for you. Odds are you came from a moneyed family. The rest of us are likely to be struggling, a situation that has been made much worse by the Great Recession.

Loss of economic mobility in America is a growing concern across the ideological spectrum. The data keep growing and the message is grim.

Readers of my blog know that there is nothing extraordinary in Talton’s words. America is far more unequal than our Nordic counterparts, for example, and citizens of Sweden, Norway, et al. enjoy more generous public services and much better odds of climbing up their respective socio-economic ladders than we do in the U.S. One may not bear to read the facts and figures, but the message is nevertheless clear.

However, if you come upon the words with a rigid predisposition to believe that Americans get what they deserve and that the only thing between rags and riches is hard work and discipline, then you are likely to simply call people names. Here’s a brief sample of the comments to Talton’s column (there are nearly 400 as I write this):

You socialists need to remember the grand soviet socialist experiment, or go visit Cuba. Those are real life examples of what happens to economies structured in alignment with your beliefs. In a nutshell, everyone ends up in permanent poverty.

” With union membership decimated and only around 7% in the private sector, there is little to dampen the corporate forces to keep labor costs low. ”

DUH..!!! It’s called Capitalism and works a hell of a lot better than Communism…….

Jesus, Jon, are you writing a FINANCIAL column, or a POLITICAL column?

Move your column to the political op-ed page, please.

I’m tired of whiners like Mr. Talton. Blame someone else for their situation. He is blind to his own myoptic shortsightedness…. he has no topics other that whoa me… whoa me…. waaaa… I’m middle to upper middle class and I work my butt off. I don’t whine. I work hard and I am always learning. Those that are lazy complain. Those that aren’t lazy and work can succeed here. No wonder that immigrants that are motivated can become wealthy while little whiners like Mr. Talton snivel and complain all day… oh those terrible rich people. Whoa me… What a loser you are Mr. Talton. No other country offers what the US does, but you can’t be lazy and a whiner. Guess you’re out of luck Mr. Talton. Maybe if you expanded your writing scope you might deserve a raise. Don’t blame the wealthy. Blame yourself and make things better for yourself. Quit asking for handouts.

Gee. Another leftist shill sniveling about class warfare.  

…You are a self-proclaimed business expert, right? I want my check!!!! You are not paying your fair share, Jon Talton!!!! You are a greedy person and all you care about is yourself.

To be fair, not all the comments were of this nature; some were even laudatory. And if you’ve scrolled through comments sections before you are familiar with the back-and-forth exchanges between the commenters, none of which contribute to serious debate or understanding.

I, for one, find all of this rather depressing. Most disheartening are the apologists for the rich and the system—however defined—that makes them so.

Austerity inevitable?

March 11, 2013 by davald

James McCusker, writing for the Everett Herald, flippantly dismisses those who emphasize the negative impacts of austerity while suggesting that austerity was an unavoidable option. He writes:

Government budget cuts are painful and whatever their long-term effects might be their initial impact is hurtful to an economy. And the deeper the cuts, the more hurtful they are. No one ever questioned that.

The part that is missing from the picture, though, is that the European countries that adopted austerity programs did so because they had no other choice. Their credit spigots had been turned off and no one was going to lend them any more money. Their only source of funds to keep the lights on was the central bank, and it wasn’t about to underwrite their unrealistic, government supported cradle-to-grave lifestyles [my emphasis].

But England had a choice, certainly, and the Euro itself prevents peripheral countries like Spain, Greece, and Italy from controlling their own monetary destinies. (I’ll have more to say on the UK below.) Moreover, the austerity measures adopted by those countries have only exacerbated their immediate post-bubble conditions. Nor can we hardly blame generous social welfare benefits for the problems; other European countries with “cradle-to-grave” lifestyles rejected austerity regimes and their economies are doing well, thank you very much.

Let’s use government social spending as a proxy for “cradle-to-grave lifestyles.” Here’s a chart from the OECD comparing a few nations.

The European countries above spend a much higher percentage of their income on public services than we do, but don’t all have high debt-to-GDP ratios. They are able to sustain their “cradle-to-grave lifestyles” without austerity. Indeed, those nations who rejected the belt-tightening prescriptions of the IMP and European Commission, for example, have generally done better than those who adopted fiscal austerity.

Spain, which is now experiencing crushingly high unemployment rates, was reducing its government debt-to-GDP ratio before the Great Recession; Germany, the symbol of financial prudence, was moving in the opposite direction (source: FRED).

Even today, Spain’s central government debt is far less than ours, which is inching toward 100 percent of GDP. Nevertheless, as a condition for securing loans from Europe’s core nations (mostly Germany on behalf of its lending banks), Spain’s government was told to cut spending. We should keep in mind that German money flowed generously to Madrid when times were good, leading to grand projects in the private sector, including beautifully paved roads to nowhere. (For more on this, read Neil Paumgartner’s depressing piece in The New Yorker. An International Monetary Fund report goes into greater detail on capital flows.)

As for McCusker’s assertion that “no one ever questioned” the downsides to austerity, he hasn’t been paying attention. There were many people on both sides of the pond, some notable economists, who predicted that government austerity was the answer to economic woes. There would be no pain, only glory.

David Cameron’s UK is a strong case in point, wherein he championed fiscal cuts. They would instill investor confidence and stimulate the economy. Paul Krugman has spent some time of late criticizing Olli Rehn, the vice president of the European Commission. He first quotes Rehn from a couple of years ago then adds his comments:

“Europe’s recovery in the real economy has taken hold and is becoming self-sustaining.”

Rehn is still in place at the European Commission, and is still telling us that austerity will work any day now. And he’s not alone. The economics team at the OECD that told us in May 2010 not just that Europe needed fiscal austerity, but that the Fed needed to raise rates 350 basis points by the end of the year to head off inflation is still issuing reports. And then there’s Cameron/Osborne.

In a blog post recently Krugman argues, citing evidence, that austerity has achieved the opposite effect championed by its advocates. As it happens, cutting federal spending in recessionary periods actually increases a country’s central government debt.

This outcome may seem counterintuitive. If a government is spending more than it receives, the obvious solution to reaching fiscal balance would be to reduce expenditures. After all, that’s what we are expected to do with our household budgets. Why shouldn’t the government do likewise? Krugman:

Suppose that a government imposes fiscal austerity in a realistic fashion, with spending cuts getting steadily deeper relative to baseline over a period of several years. If the negative impact of these cuts is fairly large — which all the evidence coming in suggests is the case under current liquidity-trap conditions — and if the country starts from a fairly high level of debt — as the austerity countries do — something alarming is likely to happen. Instead of falling, the ratio of debt to GDP is likely to rise for years.

In part this is because a weaker economy shrinks revenues, offsetting a large part of the direct austerity. What pushes it over the top is the weakening of GDP, which increases the ratio.

It’s the revenue part of the equation that is often ignored. Declining economic output shrinks tax receipts. Thus, the ratio of debt-to-GDP rises rather than falls when the overall economy sputters.

Moreover, if all indebted households cut spending to diminish their obligations, aggregate demand takes a hit, as J.M. Keynes warned when he wrote about the “paradox of thrift.”

He also argued that during prolonged contractions like the Great Depression, businesses are reluctant to expand production since too many people lack sufficient income to buy the additional output. Indeed, under such conditions firms are more likely to jettison workers, thereby further dampening demand. To the rescue, suggested Keynes, must come the federal government.

Though a simple message, it is of course controversial because of Republican orthodoxy. The GOP, we should recall, strenuously opposed FDR’s New Deal initiatives using the same specious arguments one hears today.

And so millions of people unnecessarily suffer under a misguided and fundamentally flawed ideology. But, as McCusker would have it, that’s the price of fiscal rectitude.

The economics question to be asked, then, is not whether government budget cuts are painful. Of course they are, just as cutting our household budget reduces our standard of living, or “lifestyle.” The real question, though, is whether austerity programs are more painful than the alternative, which in the case of many European countries was total economic collapse.

McCusker, read your Keynes.

It’s supposed to be cold out there

March 7, 2013 by davald

Imagine that humans did not discover fossil fuels, in which case they didn’t burn coal or oil or natural gas or otherwise emit huge amounts of carbon dioxide. Would the temperatures be colder, warmer, or about the same as they are today?

As it happens, the planet had been in a long cooling trend before we started combusting carbon. The natural earth cycle would have produced far colder temperatures than we observe now.

This article in Mother Jones, adapted from a Science magazine piece, looks at Michael Mann’s famous hockey stick. In light of new data, the stick has been revised, and it’s not good news for the planet or future generations of anything.

 

Another thing to notice is that the climate moves up or down gradually—until the last century. Then we humans discovered that burning fossil fuels would utterly transform our lives. We quickly accelerated out of the horse and buggy and into automobiles fueled by refined petroleum. We learned that burning coal could generate vast quantities of electricity, and that electricity allowed us to become truly modern.

Unfortunately, there is no free lunch. All of that carbon dioxide given off by burning fossil fuels dramatically changed the climate, quickly halting the cooling trend, and now delivering warmer and warmer temperatures along with the erosion of habitats all across the globe. And did I say “quickly”? Just look at the graph.

Oh, but don’t get too excited about this, say the way James Hansen does in getting himself arrested. Such bad form, you know.

Speaking of global warming—again

March 6, 2013 by davald

I watched Al Gore’s An Inconvenient Truth again, after the DVD had been sitting on a shelf since the last time I viewed it—about a half-dozen years ago. It’s compelling stuff; was then, still is. And that’s a problem, because the world’s political leaders simply shrug while the temperatures rise.

There is a cartoon in Gore’s film that shows the frog placed into two different water-filled beakers. The first is already boiling, so that when the frog is plopped into it, the frog immediately jumps out. In the second, the water is gradually heated from room-temperature to very hot. The problem for the frog is that it doesn’t notice that it’s getting warmer and warmer. It just sits there until it’s too late. (Gore shows a good samaritan plucking out the hapless frog in the nick of time.)

The frog in the warming pot, of course, serves as an apt metaphor for the human species as a whole. The planet is warming, but only by small increments, not enough during each time interval to attract our attention. So we just sit there like the frog, all the while believing that we’re superior to the amphibian—though we surely share a common fate.

Scientists report some more disheartening news. Last year was another record in carbon dioxide emissions. Worse, the emissions rate is accelerating. The AP:

The amount of heat-trapping carbon dioxide in the air jumped dramatically in 2012, making it very unlikely that global warming can be limited to another 2 degrees as many global leaders have hoped, new federal figures show.

David Atkins, writing for Hullabaloo, offers his thoughts here. He concludes:

History will not be a kind judge. But it’s important that the record show that there were voices shouting sanity from the rooftops, even if it only amounts to cries in the wilderness. It’s that or just giving up. And giving up isn’t an option.

The future of our species depends on it.

Have a nice day.

Blogging for free

March 6, 2013 by davald

Matthew Yglesias tells us that if you have something to say, say it. But don’t expect to get paid for it.

He started out in college blogging for nothing, then eventually landed paying jobs, the latest of which is at Slate. He writes:

The fact remains that if you have things to say that you think are worthwhile and nobody is offering to pay you to say them, you ought to say them anyway for free. If enough people agree with you that those things are worthwhile, it just may lead to something.

That worked out fine for him. I presume he’s getting a comfortable paycheck now; enough to buy a new house in the D.C. area. I, however, am under no illusion that the New Yorker will be extending me an offer to write for its magazine. Ain’t gonna happen.

As you may have notice from reading this site, there are no ads. I actually have to pay WordPress for the privilege of being ad-free.

I detest ads, for one thing. So, I make no money researching issues, then expressing my viewpoints. Besides, I’d hate to become dependent on people’s visiting The Observer so that a few mills are sent my way upon each click of the mouse.

And speaking of ads, I often wonder, more so recently, why Google’s stock continues to climb while, for example, Apple’s falters. After all, Google gives away its Android operating system to the likes of Samsung and HTC and a myriad other cellphone manufacturers. So it doesn’t make any money off its OS. The lion’s share of Google’s revenues come from mobile ads, the kind that populate webpages—the good, the bad, and the ugly. It also charges retailers to appear in its search findings. I understand that Google’s price-to-earnings ratio is about three times that of Apple, and Apple makes more profit in a quarter than Google makes in a year.

This article in Bloomberg News offers an explanation for the divergence.

Investors are willing to pay more for each dollar of Google’s earnings relative to Apple amid optimism that Google, with more than 40 percent of the U.S. online-advertising market, will command even more of the $37.3 billion that businesses spend each year to reach Web audiences. [Note: Apple’s last-quarter profits exceeded this annual total.]

But I have to ask: Does anyone actually buy something as a result of seeing a Google ad? I, for one, don’t even notice them. Or perhaps it’s all subliminal, that I’m drawn to make my next car a Jaguar and my next vacation in the Bahamas. But there’s a problem for the advertiser. I will likely never buy another car; the 2nd-generation Prius has low miles and will surely outlast me. Nor do I like to travel, anywhere. The joke around here is that I’ve been to Bothell.

I do understand that it would be easy to join the crowd. I could invite companies to post their ads on my blog. They’d pay Google for the privilege, I guess, in the apparent hope that one out of every 10 readers (about the total thus far) would click on the ad then purchase a whatever.

As I said, I don’t get it. Blogging for free I get. Alms, anyone?
