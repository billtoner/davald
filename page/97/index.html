When journalists make fools of themselves

March 5, 2013 by davald

Joe Nocera reveals his profound ignorance in attacking James Hansen’s opposition to the proposed Keystone pipeline. Yes, he does offer a few, almost dismissive accolades about the noted climate scientist. But Nocera argues that Hansen is undermining that reputation by getting himself arrested or sending out “apocalyptic announcements.”

Few familiar with climate science would doubt that Hansen has a firm, if not the firmest, grasp of the causes and consequences of global warming. (You can read his studies along with his opinions here.) Nocera, on the other hand, knows comparatively little about the subject. So, when Hansen tells us that we should be scared as hell about global warming and its effects, we might want to pay attention. For Nocera, though, it’s all about appropriate style and decorum and venue. Hansen is clearly beyond those considerations. He wants the world to take notice, and he’s been trying for decades to do just that, first with his scientific reports, then with his testimonies (before Congress and elsewhere), then finally with dire warnings. And with each phase of his utterances, the situation has gotten only worse, with more carbon dioxide being emitted, more severe weather, and a constantly rising global temperature.

Take this piece from Nocera’s column:

Yet what people hear from Hansen today is not so much his science but his broad, unscientific views on, say, the evils of oil companies. In 2008, he wrote a paper, the thesis of which was that runaway climate change would occur when carbon in the atmosphere reached 350 parts per million — a point it had already exceeded — unless it were quickly reduced. There are many climate change experts who disagree with this judgment — who believe that the 350 number is arbitrary and even meaningless. Yet an entire movement,350.org, has been built around Hansen’s line in the sand.

Click on the hyperlink ‘who disagree.’ You will be taken to a blog post by a Joe Romm, who writes for the online publication Think Progress. So who are the “many climate change experts who disagree with this judgment” of Hansen’s that pushing beyond CO2 concentrations of 350 ppm poses severe threats to us all? As far as I can tell from Romm’s post, it’s only Romm himself.

For the record, according to Think Progress Romm has the credentials to credibly comment; he received a PhD in physics from MIT. Moreover, he’s very concerned about climate change and advocates measures to halt it. But he’s evidently comfortable with 450 ppm. He suggests that the “bad news” about sticking to 350 ppm, which we exceeded years ago, is three-fold:

  1. An individual wedge is a staggering amount of carbon-free energy [I wrote about wedges here.]
  2. There isn’t political support to do even a single 20-year wedge today.
  3. Doing eight such accelerated wedges simultaneously is far beyond the capability of the market on its own no matter how high a carbon tax you impose.

Hansen et al. strongly disagree. Allowing concentrations to approach, let alone exceed, 450 ppm would doom the planet. By then, we’ve already triggered monumental feedback loops from which there is no escape. Consider this abstract from one of Hansen’s recent scholarly works:

Paleoclimate data help us assess climate sensitivity and potential human-made climate effects. We conclude that Earth in the warmest interglacial periods of the past million years was less than 1°C warmer than in the Holocene. Polar warmth in these interglacials and in the Pliocene does not imply that a substantial cushion remains between today’s climate and dangerous warming, but rather that Earth is poised to experience strong amplifying polar feedbacks in response to moderate global warming. Thus goals to limit human-made warming to 2°C are not sufficient — they are prescriptions for disaster. Ice sheet disintegration is nonlinear, spurred by amplifying feedbacks. We suggest that ice sheet mass loss, if warming continues unabated, will be characterized better by a doubling time for mass loss rate than by a linear trend. Satellite gravity data, though too brief to be conclusive, are consistent with a doubling time of 10 years or less, implying the possibility of multi-meter sea level rise this century. Observed accelerating ice sheet mass loss supports our conclusion that Earth’s temperature now exceeds the mean Holocene value. Rapid reduction of fossil fuel emissions is required for humanity to succeed in preserving a planet resembling the one on which civilization developed.

Carbon dioxide concentrations reached 391 ppm last October. Here’s the trend (Keeling Curve):

 

As greenhouse gas emissions rise so does temperature.

 

 

This is not the first time Nocera has criticized Hansen. Here’s Hansen’s rebuttal to a Nocera column earlier this year.

We should keep in mind that Nocera supports the Keystone pipeline and wants the Obama administration to approve it. Here’s why:

Like it or not, fossil fuels are going to remain the world’s dominant energy source for the foreseeable future, and we are far better off getting our oil from Canada than, say, Venezuela. And the climate change effects of tar sands oil are, all in all, pretty small.

The report he links to under “pretty small,” is by the Congressional Research Service. Its investigators examined existing literature on greenhouse gas emissions for Canadian tar sands and other sources. (The Keystone pipeline would deliver the Canadian-extracted oil to U.S. refineries.) Consider this table from the report:

 

If you begin with the premise that fossil fuels will be with us for the foreseeable future, then it may be an easy leap for Nocera to conclude that the tar-sands oil isn’t that big a deal. The GHG emissions are just a bit more than existing sources.

For Hansen, no emissions are good emissions; we surpassed the dangerous 350 ppm threshold before 1990, and we’ll soon exceed 400. That’s because we continue to rely on fossil fuels, dummy. For Nocera, however, it’s as if we’re choosing between a hot poker and heated frying pan to burn our forearm. One instrument may be only slightly warmer than the other, but both will inflict pain and permanent scarring.

See all those yellow (beige) bars above? Those are “combustion emissions.” For Hansen, they’re all bad, because they contribute to further global warming.

Keystone, if you will, represents a choke point that’s both real and symbolic. Allowing it to be constructed demonstrates a complete disregard for global warming and its catastrophic effects, while enabling greater production of greenhouse gases. In an ideal world, one with sentient journalists and elected officials who truly represent our interests over time, each source of GHG would be terminated, sooner rather than later. That means every coal plant, oil refinery, and petroleum extractor. The carbon must stay in the ground. Period.

Get it, Joe?

Trusting yourself spells ruin

March 5, 2013 by davald

I recently skimmed a review by Cass Sunstein of a new book Against Autonomy: Justifying Coercive Paternalism. Although Mr. Sunstein expressed considerable agreement with the author on key points, including the use of cost-benefit analysis, he ultimately criticized her overreach. But I was struck by the underlying assertion of Sarah Conly, the author: We individuals are often not the best judges of our own interests. Here’s Sunstein, who first references John Stuart Mill’s “harm principle“:

In recent decades, some of the most important research in social science, coming from psychologists and behavioral economists, has been trying to answer it. That research is having a significant influence on public officials throughout the world. Many believe that behavioral findings are cutting away at some of the foundations of Mill’s harm principle, because they show that people make a lot of mistakes, and that those mistakes can prove extremely damaging.

Sunstein offers one example, “present bias.” We tend to ignore the future in favor of the present. Thus, we put off saving money for a rainy day or fail to save at all.

For some people, the future is a foreign country, populated by strangers. Many of us procrastinate and fail to take steps that would impose small short-term costs but produce large long-term gains. People may, for example, delay enrolling in a retirement plan, starting to diet or exercise, ceasing to smoke, going to the doctor, or using some valuable, cost-saving technology. Present bias can ensure serious long-term harm, including not merely economic losses but illness and premature death as well.

This morning the Seattle Times revealed the results of a recent poll. It seems that a majority of Washington voters oppose an increase in both gas and car-tab taxes. I’m shocked, I tell you. Shocked.

The people have never supported higher taxes. Indeed, members of one political party count on that attitude at every election. And it’s a winning formula: just oppose taxes, period, and you’ll garner a huge chunk of the vote.

But are lower taxes in the best interests of society? How do we fix our roads, build commuter rail, or fund even an adequate public school system, let alone a very good one? Do you think taxes would help?

Let’s consider Puget Sound’s transportation mess. Geography prevents road networks that could ease congestion. So hundreds of thousands of motorists join the vehicular goo each day on the interstates to make their way to and from Seattle and other locales. This is collective stupidity, of course, since it is the most inefficient means—driving one’s car—to get to where you need to be, namely work. Yet, each morning and evening brings the same miserable experience—and at considerable personal cost.

It would be far better for nearly everyone if all those people could walk a block or two then climb aboard a train. However, building public transportation requires the yes-vote of millions of citizens to make it happen. Yet, when given that opportunity, we refuse to pool our dollars for the good of all. We’d rather pay a small fortune to own and maintain and fuel our very own car! When hundreds of thousands manifest the same preference we get sprawl and goo.

A relevant bit of paternal coercion would be a carbon tax or a congestion fee. The latter almost immediately reduces congestion, as if by Adam Smith’s “invisible hand.” A carbon tax would eventually curtail private transportation and make it feasible for more of us to jump on trains rather than into our cars. The revenues from either tax could be used to fund public rails.

But we don’t like taxes, remember? So each time we reject taxes at the polls or constrain our legislators’ ability to raise them, we allow the eventual costs that must be borne to escalate.

We could have acquired easements to construct commuter rail lines around Puget Sound. But we kicked the can down the road, again and again. Now that every inch is paved over or built upon, real estate acquisitions become increasingly expensive, making us even less willing to pay the necessary taxes.

Each of us believes that we are meeting our own best interests by keeping more of our income in our pockets. We also insist that we are the best judges of how to spend it—not the government.

However, just look around you. Do you see the triumph of individual choice? Do you love your gooey commute, your grossly underfunded schools, and the consequences of planetary warming? If so, you’re not alone.

Hard to believe that we humans are evolution’s ultimate prize.

Mountains of evidence

March 5, 2013 by davald

During his interview with NBC’s David Gregory, Speaker John Boehner repeatedly trumpeted the stimulative effects of lower taxes. Here’s a pertinent excerpt:

DAVID GREGORY:

But there’s no ironclad evidence that lowering marginal tax rates is going to lead to economic growth.

SPEAKER JOHN BOEHNER:

Oh yes there is. There’s mountains.

DAVID GREGORY:

Bill Clinton raised taxes, President Reagan raised taxes and the economy performed well…

(OVERTALK)

SPEAKER JOHN BOEHNER:

There’s mountains of evidence that if we bring tax rates down, that we will help spur economic growth in our country.

DAVID GREGORY:

That hasn’t been tried before?

SPEAKER JOHN BOEHNER:

Yeah, Ronald Reagan, 1981.

DAVID GREGORY:

Right, he also raised taxes.

SPEAKER JOHN BOEHNER:

And it worked very well.

DAVID GREGORY:

But he raised taxes as well and it didn’t hurt the economy did it?

SPEAKER JOHN BOEHNER:

Listen, he lowered taxes twice, both in 1981 and again out of the 1986 tax reform. When they lowered rates for all Americans, we had this boom in economic growth. Why? Because we got rid of a lot of the silly deductions, brought the rates down, and it helped promote more economic growth in our country.

I confess to being puzzled by the Speaker’s and other Republicans’ oft-repeated mantra that reducing both tax rates and  federal spending cures all economic ills. Is it ideology? Is it religion? Or are there “mountains of evidence” to support their claim?

Let’s look at two charts. First, marginal rates and GDP over time.

While, again, correlation is not causation, we can rather easily see that as the marginal rate fell so did economic growth. Also, we’ll note that when the rate was at its highest—in the 50s and 60s—the economy grew steadily.

Here’s another chart, this one a scatter plot of the same data.

When marginal tax rates have been highest so has GDP growth.

Still looking for those mountains.

Sacramento vs. Seattle and the exclusivity clause

March 1, 2013 by davald

I can fully appreciate Kevin Johnson’s urgent attempts to keep the Kings in Sacramento. After all, he’s the mayor of the capital city and a former NBA all-star, then known simply as “KJ.” So, he’s been scampering behind the scenes to woo deep pockets to mount a counter-offer to the Kings’ majority owners, the Maloof family.

However, I see a couple of almost insurmountable hurdles for Johnson. First, been there and done that. There have been several failed efforts to build a new arena in Sacramento. No dice, with the Maloofs evidently reneging on its initial commitment to put millions into a deal.

Second, and this may be the nail on the coffin lid, the purchase and sale agreement between Chris Hansen’s group and the Maloofs contains a non-compete clause. From the Seattle Times:

The [Sacremento] Bee further reported that the “Maloofs have an exclusive agreement with the Seattle group that prohibits them from entering into negotiations with anyone else offering a competing or counter offer.” Another offer could only be made if the sale to the Hansen-Ballmer group falls through.

In exchange for this provision, the Hansen group paid the Maloofs $30 million, none of it refundable.

The NBA owners will vote on the Hansen proposal this month. Given the checkered history of the Maloofs in Sacramento, combined with solid, well-financed agreements between Hansen, Seattle, and King County (including, of course, money for an arena south of Safeco Field), I’m betting that the Kings will be playing at Key Arena for the next couple of years until the new complex is completed.

Medical costs

February 28, 2013 by davald

A few posts ago I suggested that if the U.S. spent the same percentage of its GDP on health care that Sweden does, we’d save enough each year to wipe out the deficit, give or take a few billions. However, with the cesspool we call ‘Congress,’ that’s not likely to happen any time soon. Instead, our elected officials, those we choose to represent our interests, are instead doing the bidding of the health care industry, which is already huge and growing fatter by the day.

As it happens, Steve Brill conducted an expose of medical costs for Time magazine. It’s worth a read, especially if you like to get angry and spit all over yourself. Will it make a difference?

It wasn’t too long ago that Michael Moore gave us Sicko, a documentary on our stupid and monumentally expensive health care industry. He focused on those Americans who have medical insurance to dramatize the insanity, beginning with a man who was stitching up his own leg to save money, because his insurance company had either capped or declined coverage. One scene that also jumped out: a patient arriving at a UK hospital’s cashier window upon being discharged. The patient was not there to pay a bill but to collect money for the taxi-ride home. Of  course, Sicko did not change America’s sick health insurance industry, though it may have helped effect the passage of Obamacare, a hodge-podge of legislation that will leave the medical corporations richer and richer.

Brill presents some real-life horror stories of individuals experiencing random ailments, from a slip-and-fall in the backyard to a “routine” outpatient procedure to fix an chronic backache. We are introduced to hospitals’ “chargemaster,” a price list for any and all items used in patient care. As you might guess, the chargemaster prices are many times higher than the hospitals’ costs, what they actually pay for pills, gauze, gowns, etc. Those items add up. So much so, that the final bill can quickly explode past a patient’s insurance caps, putting the individual or his or her family in financial jeopardy.

As you might also guess, the health care industry in the U.S. is hugely lucrative, with “not-for-profit” hospital administrators pulling down seven-figure salaries and hospitals themselves realizing record profit margins. Here’s Brill:

The result is a uniquely American gold rush for those who provide everything from wonder drugs to canes to high-tech implants to CT scans to hospital bill-coding and collection services. In hundreds of small and midsize cities across the country — from Stamford, Conn., to Marlton, N.J., to Oklahoma City — the American health care market has transformed tax-exempt “nonprofit” hospitals into the towns’ most profitable businesses and largest employers, often presided over by the regions’ most richly compensated executives. And in our largest cities, the system offers lavish paychecks even to midlevel hospital managers, like the 14 administrators at New York City’s Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center who are paid over $500,000 a year, including six who make over $1 million.

Taken as a whole, these powerful institutions and the bills they churn out dominate the nation’s economy and put demands on taxpayers to a degree unequaled anywhere else on earth. In the U.S., people spend almost 20% of the gross domestic product on health care, compared with about half that in most developed countries. Yet in every measurable way, the results our health care system produces are no better and often worse than the outcomes in those countries.

It doesn’t have to be this way. Brill, who may have not been so disposed prior to his investigation, suggests a Medicare-for-all program, since Medicare does so much better at controlling costs than does the private sector, which never heard of the word ‘efficiency.’ Yet, Medicare could do better.

For example, the lobby-infested Senate and House voted to prevent Medicare administrators from negotiating with drug companies. Congress also protects Big Pharma profits through the elaborate and almost completely unnecessary patent regime, which virtually guarantees that Americans pay orders of magnitude more for prescription drugs than our counterparts in Europe, Canada, and Japan.

This is all very serious stuff. Unless Congress itself gets serious about lowering health care costs, we’ll be paying so much for medical procedures and medicine that bankruptcy will become all too common.

Have we finally killed a zombie?

February 28, 2013 by davald

Washington state’s flim-flam artist, Tim Eyman, who makes his living pushing initiatives, received a message today from the supreme court in Olympia. The two-thirds requirement for new taxes, which voters have routinely passed, cannot bind the legislature. If the people want a super-majority, they’ll have to get it via a constitutional amendment.

The same supreme court held that the legislature is grossly underfunding education. Fix the problem or else. But Eyman’s initiative hamstrung Olympia; it could not establish new taxes by a simple majority vote. And it’s highly doubtful that the state could fulfill its constitutional mandate without new revenues. Today’s ruling removes the impediment.

But not the politics, unfortunately. A small group of rogue Democrats in the senate switched sides, putting the Republicans in control of that body; the house remains solidly Democratic.

Moreover, I expect Eyman to launch yet another campaign, this time to amend the constitution. After all, there’s money in it—for him.
