Why the rant?

June 5, 2012 by davald

My recent post on debt elicited this comment:

Two facts: This is anti-Republican rant spurred by envy, hate and jealousy. No matter what the cause, it happened on u-bam-a’s watch. u-bam-a is responsible PERIOD.

This being said; tax and spend socialism does not work.

And it is your freedom to believe nonsense.

Censorship is evil.

As to the rant charge. Guilty, with no regrets. Yes, I have known and worked with Republicans, and a few have actually been very fine individuals, but their ideology sucks.

However, I really do have to take exception to the commenter’s assumption that I am “spurred by envy, hate and jealousy.” Hardly. I have no desire to be a Republican. Nor am I either jealous or envious. At times I may hate them, as I often hate the Yankees.

Now I find it quite a leap to imply that “u-bam-a” (what’s that all about, anyway?) is a tax-and-spend socialist. He’s nothing of a sort, as this morning’s AP article makes clear. If Obama is a socialist, he’s a very bad one.

While there are socialists in America, and Vermont even elected one to Congress, though he now prefers to be known as an “independent,” socialism as a legitimate political party or constellation of parties gained little traction in the U.S. France just elected a nominal socialist. The Nordic countries’ parliaments have more often than not been run by democratic socialists. I have no explanation as to the different attitudes toward socialism from one side of the pond to the other.

But there may be no stronger epithet in America than “socialist.” It really gets people stirred up, causing them to blabber and spit. And I just don’t get it, since there are so few to be found here, and only one in Congress out of 535. That’s 0.19 percent. You fear this?

Back to my anti-Republicanism, I am mostly concerned about the growing inequality in America and the breakdown of society, which are related. Democrats are more inclined to share this concern; Republicans embrace the divisions.

But, truth be told, neither political party really seems to give a rat’s rectum for the Rest of Us. The Democrats, who used to have a solid Labor core, are now beholden to Wall Street—as the Republicans have been, gleefully so, for a century or more.

And speaking of Labor, in his column this morning Joe Nocera writes about Timothy Noah’s latest book, The Great Divergence, wherein Noah attempts to account for its causes. One of the factors that has changed remarkably since the 50s and even more so beginning in Reagan’s initial term is the decline in union participation rates. Nocera quotes Noah on this: “Draw one line on a graph charting the decline in union membership, then superimpose a second line charting the decline in middle-class income share,” writes Noah, “and you will find that the two lines are nearly identical.”

The above chart, from OECD data, illustrates the strong inverse correlation between union participation rates and income inequality (Gini coefficient). I’ve sorted the countries by union participation rates. Sweden’s rate is 82.2 percent; it’s Gini index is .25 (the lower the number the lower the inequality). The U.S. has the highest Gini index and also the lowest union participation rate.

Republicans, as a rule, loathe unions. The Democrats have gradually distanced themselves from Labor. Unlike other democracies, in America there is no party of Labor. Nocera:

But if liberals really want to reverse income inequality, they should think seriously about rejoining labor’s side.

You’ll have to hurry, though. Labor’s numbers are shrinking rapidly. Wait too long and you won’t find any.

Obama’s debt

June 5, 2012 by davald

It has become a consistent meme amongst the Republicans or idiots—but I repeat myself—that the federal debt under Obama has skyrocketed. And why? Because of runaway government spending. But what if the causation is wrong, terribly so?

Well, debt has risen since Obama assumed office, though GDP, the denominator, even fell in 2009.

But what about that government spending?

The folks at Talking Points Memo have put together a chart.

Note that spending rose in Bush’s second term. It jumped from 2009 to 2010 as stimulus money (almost half in tax cuts) was spent.  Those dollars have dried up, so government expenditures have fallen sharply, declining by almost three percent this year over last.

Republicans want to cut spending even more. They also want to cut taxes further, even though our tax burden is low compared to our European counterparts.

So what will happen to our fiscal accounts should Republicans gain complete control of D.C.? Can you spell d-e-b-t? That’s right. Government deficits will expand and accumulated debt will rise. The reasons are obvious. First, government austerity during a recession retards economic growth, and we have lots of data to prove this. Second, by cutting taxes even further, government receipts will fall, raising deficits.

Should you fear debt as much as the Republicans say they do (along with the New York Times‘ David Brooks) and feel the urge to replace the profligate Obama with Mitt Romney, you are seriously deluding yourself. But, hey, it’s a free country. And all Americans have the freedom to believe nonsense.

Baker beat me to it

June 5, 2012 by davald

I felt compelled to comment on David Brooks’s column this morning about the culture of indebtedness. But economist Dean Baker did the honors. Baker does not mince words:

David Brooks is again prominently displaying his misunderstanding of economics in the New York Times. He told readers in today’s column:

“Every generation has an incentive to borrow money from the future to spend on itself. But, until ours, no generation of Americans has done it to the same extent.”

He then goes on to tell us that we are borrowing because we are more secure, arghhhhh!

Okay, let’s try to put this so that even David Brooks can understand it. First, we are not borrowing money from the future. What does Brooks thinks this means, are we calling up the Ghost of Christmas Future and asking for a loan?

Brooks, always trying to fashion a morality tale, employs the Scott Walker recall to praise his efforts—in roundabout tones—to slash Wisconsin’s state budget on the backs of public employees. Walker, he says, is at least serious about reining in debt. Brooks:

The era of indebtedness began with a cultural shift. It will require a gradual popular shift to reverse. Today’s Wisconsin vote might mark the moment when the nation’s long debt indulgence finally began to turn around.

But never one to miss an opportunity to pour salt in labor’s wound, Brooks has to write this inanity:

A vote to keep Walker won’t be an antiunion vote. It will be a vote against any special interest that seeks to preserve exorbitant middle-class benefits at the expense of the public good.

You teachers and policemen and firemen and all those who work for the state are just a “special interest” expecting “exorbitant…benefits.” My wife’s a school teacher. Trust me. There’s nothing exorbitant about either her current or future benefits. We’re just looking to survive.

And one more thing. Brooks has no concept of the paradox of deleveraging. He hopes that a Walker victory will signal an end of to “the nation’s long debt indulgence.” But if all states and local governments along with their citizens embark on a massive debt-reduction path, we can kiss economic recovery goodbye. Dean Baker:

Brooks may have some magical view of the world where jobs grow up magically in the private sector when the government reduces spending, but in the real world we need a chain of causation. Can anyone tell a story where firms will be motivated to increase spending and hiring when demand drops further due to government cutbacks? There aren’t many business owners who see their demand plummet and then go, “hey, great time to expand.”

A sense of place

June 4, 2012 by davald

Many years ago, whilst a resident of Marysville, Wash., I ran for city council—and lost. As a consolation prize, the mayor appointed me to a new, city-sponsored group: The Marysville Downtown Revitalization Committee.

Now, the irony did not escape me. During my campaign I had referred to the city as “an ugly, dysfunctional burg.” Not exactly a winning expression, however true. I campaigned—loosely defined, since I knocked on no doors nor ran any ads nor raised or spent any money—on a theme of improvement. In particular, I believed then as I do now that cities, however larger or small, need a viable center, a public place that draws people together. (Here is a previous print edition of The Observer focused on this topic.)

Upon entering my apartment you’ll be directly confronted with a large aerial photograph of Manhattan’s Central Park. Twenty years ago, during the Democratic National Convention, I had occasion to walk that park several times. It’s big, incredibly interesting in its variety, and serves as a natural oasis amidst endless acres of concrete, people, and vehicles.

Growing up in the East Bay I spent many days and even weeks traversing San Francisco and Golden Gate Park, which is even larger than New York’s. It is home to the zoo, the aquarium, the Japanese Tea Garden, and lots and lots of grass, trails, ponds, and recreation fields.

The once-small but now-sprawling town in which I was raised featured a legacy of its Spanish heritage: Todos Santos Plaza, a city block of trees, lawn, picnic benches and tables, and a concert venue. It was the first home of the Concord public library.

Marysville has a plaza of sorts, tucked away off State Avenue, between fifth and sixth streets. However, Comeford Park, unlike Todos Santos, say, does not serve as the city’s center. Indeed, that’s one of Marysville’s biggest problem: it has no center, no place that attracts people for rest, conversation, a respite from commercial activities.

In a 1965 essay architect Charles W. Moore argued that “You Have to Pay for the Public Life,” the essay’s title.* He emphasized the importance of monumental. Cities should prominently feature places and objects that mark off public spaces worthy of our attention, enjoyment, and preservation. Regrettably, he noted, most cities, especially in the West, lack such monuments. Moore:

The act of marking is then a public act, and an act of recognition, an expectable public act among members of the society which possesses the place.

I now live in downtown Everett. It has no central park, no monuments that demarcate public space. There are only streets and buildings and small patches of grass. There are no public places to gather. Everything is commercial concrete.

In a society that knows the price of everything but the value of nothing, we abhor anything public. And we’re damn well not going to pay for it. Rather, we’re inclined to view spending on public places and objects as a “waste of taxpayers’ money.” We are worse off for that attitude.

Soros on Europe

June 4, 2012 by davald

By way of Krugman I read George Soros’s commentary on the European crisis. It’s rather heady stuff, invoking the philosopher Karl Popper and including a broad-range discussion of the causes of the problems and required cures. Soros debunks the current morality play that Germany has no soiled hands and that the peripheral nations are solely to blame for their profligacy.

Of particular interest to me is Soros’s explication of economic theory. Though he admittedly stops short of critiquing rational expectations theory and its related assumption of perfect knowledge, he summarily dismisses them as fundamentally flawed and irrelevant; in his financial career, he’s never assumed either, because they have nothing to do with reality.

Ever since the Crash of 2008 there has been a widespread recognition, both among economists and the general public, that economic theory has failed. But there is no consensus on the causes and the extent of that failure.

I believe that the failure is more profound than generally recognized. It goes back to the foundations of economic theory. Economics tried to model itself on Newtonian physics. It sought to establish universally and timelessly valid laws governing reality. But economics is a social science and there is a fundamental difference between the natural and social sciences. Social phenomena have thinking participants who base their decisions on imperfect knowledge. That is what economic theory has tried to ignore.

Bad news. Period.

June 1, 2012 by davald

The unemployment rate in the Eurozone hit a record 11 percent. The U.S. unemployment rate climbed back up to 8.2 percent, after inching downward over the last several months. The employment report was dismal, with the private sector adding only 82,000 in May; the public sector lost 13,000. So the net gain was just 69,000 for the month.

Predictably, the Romney campaign blamed Obama’s “policies” for the downturn. This strategy is part of the end game of the Republican Party: reject any Obama move to improve the economy (e.g., through fiscal stimulus), then tether the dismal news to the White House. Whether or not Americans see through this charade will be answered in November.

Let’s assume that a Keynesian prescription would work and is the only thing that will propel the economy to recovery. The Republican’s stated policies—spending and tax cuts, laxer regulation, etc.—would push the economy further south.

How might we know this? Just wind the clock back about a decade to the reign of G.W. When he assumed office, the federal budget was in surplus about $7 trillion. Bush said that the government should distribute the extra cash to the people, his people. So taxes were cut, and the budget quickly went into deficit. Oh, and Bush added a couple of expensive wars to ensure that the budget exploded. There was no net gain in employment, referred to as “the lost decade.” He left Obama with a financial crisis, mountains of debt, slashed tax revenues, and a very grim recession. (Yes, I think it fair to blame Bush for these problems, because he championed the tax cuts and certainly drove us into war on false pretenses.)

For his part, Obama and a good many of his advisors grossly underestimated the enormity of the inherited problem. Thus, their proffered solution was much too small. His meager stimulus package (almost half of which was in the form of tax cuts) failed to stimulate the economy, and its funding has dried up.

At the time the package was being discussed, several economic experts weighed in, most notably Paul Krugman. He quickly judged ARRA inadequate by a trillion dollars or more. He also correctly foretold the political consequences. The stimulus package would fail to significantly lower the unemployment rate, the Republicans would guarantee no further bites of the apple, argue that fiscal stimulus doesn’t work anyway, then blame Obama for the rotten mess.

Obama fell into the GOP trap by touting the benefits of ARRA: the employment rate would fall, GDP would rise, and all would be well. His big mistake was not recognizing upfront the inadequacy of his stimulus package. Indeed, he should have argued that it was not enough, that it was only a start. Then, as the Republicans played out their scorched earth strategy, they would be blamed for the doldrums.

And speaking of Krugman, in his column today he chides the British government (he’s been in London promoting his new book) for stubbornly adhering to austerity, which is only making things worse. In response the Tory leaders equated the country’s budget with a household’s. If you or I run up our personal debt, we’d better spend less and pay down those credit cards. But governments, Krugman avers, are not like us. Federal debt, in our case, he says, is money owed to ourselves. Moreover, when businesses are reluctant or unable to invest (because of slackened demand, mostly), the federal government can and must increase its spending.

Now here’s the tricky part. Krugman:

So what happens if everyone simultaneously slashes spending in an attempt to pay down debt? The answer is that everyone’s income falls — my income falls because you’re spending less, and your income falls because I’m spending less. And, as our incomes plunge, our debt problem gets worse, not better.

Keynes had a name for it: the paradox of thrift. If everyone reduces spending at the same time, incomes fall, and the economy constricts.

Although consumer spending remains positive, overall private investment has stalled.

Krugman ends his column on a depressing note. He believes that the Cameron government has an ulterior motive:

So the austerity drive in Britain isn’t really about debt and deficits at all; it’s about using deficit panic as an excuse to dismantle social programs. And this is, of course, exactly the same thing that has been happening in America.

Yep, they’re screwing the Rest of Us. If Romney wins and the GOP retakes the Senate, the present participle will be replaced with a fait accompli.

Tough love

May 31, 2012 by davald

Yes, we do have a problem. A very big one, and I have in mind Wall Street and its corrosive influence on politics and government. Because it has amassed so much collective wealth, aided and abetted by a compliant Congress, Wall Street calls the shots.

So, when Wall Street guessed wrong on house prices and the longs and the shorts of trading, the federal government came to its rescue, pouring hundreds of billions of dollars into reserve-starved financial institutions to restore their liquidity. The response to the collapse of Lehman Brothers was as massive as it was quick.

However, instead of gratefulness or newfound humility, the captains of Wall Street have doubled down on attempts to regulate them, seeking to denude Dodd-Frank, itself a toothless product of much compromise, and squelch the Volcker rule, a pale shadow of Glass-Steagall. Meanwhile, these casino magnates are lavishing both the Romney and Obama camps with millions of dollars in contributions.

For his part, Romney promises to gut Dodd-Frank in its entirety, reduce the marginal tax rate, kill the estate tax, and otherwise leave Wall Street to gamble with trillions of dollars while ensuring that any future losses would be covered by the Rest of Us, just as we did under TARP. In Romney’s worldview, these captains of the finance industry are the lifeblood of the U.S. economy. They should be praised rather than pilloried.

Obama chooses to treat the malefactors of great wealth with “tough love.” Writing for the New York Times, Thomas Edsall quotes Obama’s February 2009 remarks to a question about temporarily nationalizing one or more of the then-troubled financial firms.

You know, the scale of the U.S. economy and the capital markets are so vast and the problems in terms of managing and overseeing anything of that scale, I think, would — our assessment was that it wouldn’t make sense. And we also have different traditions in this country. Obviously, Sweden has a different set of cultures in terms of how the government relates to markets and America’s different. And we want to retain a strong sense of that private capital fulfilling the core — core investment needs of this country. And so, what we’ve tried to do is to apply some of the tough love that’s going to be necessary, but do it in a way that’s also recognizing we’ve got big private capital markets and ultimately that’s going to be the key to getting credit flowing again.

Edsall provides us with some truly depressing numbers about Wall Street’s campaign contributions. Wall Street has been the largest source of Romney’s campaign cash, now at $13 million. The pro-Romeny Super PAC, Restore Our Future, has so far received $24 million from financial institutions, almost half of the PAC’s total contributions.

Hedging its bets, Wall Street has also been the Democratic Party’s biggest source of dollars, contributing almost $20 million. Hollywood, famously derided by conservatives as the principal contributor to Democratic candidates, comes in at less than $8 million. Edsall:

Romney has made it very clear how he will help Wall Street if he wins. It’s President Obama who is in a conflicted position. Obama could conceivably take a more aggressive regulatory posture. The more likely scenario, given recent history, is that he would be apprehensive about the consequences of regulating hedge-fund operators and investment bankers. In the aftermath of Citizens United, Obama and the rest of the political community are aware of how dangerous the financial sector can be in the freewheeling world of campaign finance.

The Supreme Court’s awful ruling, equating money with speech, effectively silences the speech of the Rest of Us, since we ain’t got no money. Nor can we look to Obama to take up our cause. We simply don’t count.

The birthers

May 31, 2012 by davald

I haven’t paid all that much attention to the “birther movement,” folks among us who proclaim that Obama was not born where he said he was born. Indeed, the birthers claim, he was born in Kenya, probably raised a Muslim, and now sits in the White House plotting an inside coup based on socialist ideology. State officials have even attempted to bar Obama’s name from the ballot this November, because they continue to believe against all evidence to the contrary, that Obama is illegitimate—only natural born citizens are eligible for president.

So, I happened upon ginandtacos.com this morning, a blog site featuring the content of a semi-anonymous college professor. The professor takes on the birther movement, eventually tossing out the conspiracies in favor of Occam’s Razor, or the principle of parsimony , if you prefer. That is, the simplest, least complicated explanation is more likely to be true than one involving numerous hypotheses, speculation, and random dot-connecting. He concludes:

If a theory this convoluted is necessary to make sense of your predetermined conclusions, there is a good chance that you’re making shit up. That obvious fact is remarkably easy to overlook if you’re 100% convinced that Barack Obama is a fraud. The phrase Unnecessarily Complex does not enter into your thinking. You will develop some theory, find evidence somewhere, and substitute “likely” for “plausible” to make sense of it all. And no matter what Obama says, does, or makes public, this parade of inane conspiracies will never stop.

Hey, this is “Americia” [sic].

Right or left?

May 30, 2012 by davald

Add another item to the category “things I didn’t know.” This one on how countries came to drive on the left or right of highways.

As a consumer of many British video offerings, including stuff on the Tudors, I found this bit interesting:

In feudal Europe, people would travel on the left side of the road because most people were right-handed and wanted the arm that they used for their sword (or other weapon) between them and anyone who approached. As a result, traveling on the left became customary.

Napoleon Bonaparte happened to be left-handed. So he ordered his troops and the countries he conquered to travel on the right.

I don’t know about Japan, though. I think they drive on the left, just as the British do. But the Japanese were not colonized by England. Their samurais must have been right-handed, too.

In praise of idleness

May 30, 2012 by davald

That was the title of one of philosopher Bertrand Russell’s essays. He didn’t have in mind just sitting on one’s butt. Rather, the hustle and bustle of modern living was depriving us of leisure, the opportunity to contemplate, to engage in the arts, to recreate.

We’re obsessed with growth and productivity. As it turns out, these two strategies will be our and the planet’s undoing. Besides, being less productive would allow more of us to work, and there are far too many without jobs.

Such ideas emerge from the keyboard of Tim Jackson, a professor at the University of Surrey. Writing for the New York Times, he tell us:

But the relentless drive for productivity may also have some natural limits. Ever-increasing productivity means that if our economies don’t continue to expand, we risk putting people out of work. If more is possible each passing year with each working hour, then either output has to increase or else there is less work to go around. Like it or not, we find ourselves hooked on growth.

He has an interesting suggestion. One that involves altering incentives.

[The transition to a more sustainable economy and lifestyle] demands careful attention to incentive structures — lower taxes on labor and higher taxes on resource consumption and pollution, for example. It calls for more than just lip service to concepts of patient-centered care and student-centered learning. It requires the dismantling of perverse productivity targets and a serious investment in skills and training. In short, avoiding the scourge of unemployment may have less to do with chasing after growth and more to do with building an economy of care, craft and culture. And in doing so, restoring the value of decent work to its rightful place at the heart of society.

I’m a product of Berkeley in the 60s. So I like what this man is saying. How about you?
